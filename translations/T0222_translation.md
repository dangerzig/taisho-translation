# 光讚經
## Brilliance Praise Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0222

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護).

---

## Fascicle One

### Chapter 1: Mahāprajñāpāramitā, The Brilliance Praise

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of five thousand bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats whose outflows were exhausted, free of all defilements, having attained mastery. Their minds were liberated through wisdom. They had attained benevolence and harmony and served as great guides. All their tasks were accomplished and brought to completion. They had cast off heavy burdens and attained their own benefit, removing the affliction of transmigration. With impartial liberation they had crossed beyond all conceptions, reaching the shore of the boundless, save only the Venerable Ānanda, a learner at the stage of stream-entry. Also present were five hundred bhikṣus, as well as bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, all assembled together.

Also present were bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who had attained dhāraṇī and samādhi, who cultivated the practice of emptiness, who honored signlessness, who did not cling to aspirations, who had attained the patient endurance of equality, who governed the immeasurable, and who had all attained the five supernatural powers. Their speech was swift and without weariness. They had renounced the desire for household profit. Their teaching of the Dharma did not seek offerings. They had reached the profound and wondrous Dharma, crossing to the boundless. They had attained fearlessness and transcended the affairs of Māra, freed from all the hindrances of the aggregates and coverings. They expounded on causes and conditions, following the mind's inclinations. Through innumerable kalpas they had practiced their vows with diligent effort. Their intentions were well directed: they were joyful in greeting and always first to approach others, free from resentful bonds. Entering into countless assemblies, their dignity was majestic and without any fearful difficulty. They could recall the affairs of immeasurable koṭis of kalpas. When they expounded the sūtras and the Dharma, they thoroughly understood the various meanings, knowing them to be like illusions, magical transformations, mirages, the moon reflected in water, dreams, shadows, echoes, and images in a mirror. They were courageous and without peer. With subtle and wondrous wisdom they knew the arising and conduct of all beings' minds. They transcended all discrimination; their intention harbored no harm. They were earnest in patient endurance, complete in their conduct. They clearly understood and faithfully assessed those whom they should liberate. They had taken up the limitless vow of gathering buddha-fields. Constantly in samādhi and concentration, they beheld with their eyes the countless buddha-worlds. Proficient and skillful, they entreated innumerable Buddha World-Honored Ones. Advancing and retreating, they could resolve the various kinds of views and attachments. With settled minds they enjoyed a hundred thousand practices. All the bodhisattvas' virtues were thus.

Their names were: Bhadrapāla Bodhisattva, Ratnākara Bodhisattva, Mahāsubhūti Bodhisattva, Nārada Bodhisattva, Gaukṣita Bodhisattva, Varuṇadatta Bodhisattva, Indra Bodhisattva, Bhadrapāla Bodhisattva, Sūkṣmamati Bodhisattva, Dhṛtimati Bodhisattva, Vardhamānamati Bodhisattva, Amoghadarśin Bodhisattva, Sthitapraṇidhāna Bodhisattva, Parivarta Bodhisattva, Nityodyukta Bodhisattva, Anikṣiptadhura Bodhisattva, Sūryateja Bodhisattva, Nirātman Bodhisattva, Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Jalinīprabha Bodhisattva, Ratnamudrāhasta Bodhisattva, Nityotthitahasta Bodhisattva, Nityāvanatahasta Bodhisattva, and Maitreya Bodhisattva. The assembly of bodhisattvas was thus beyond all limit, an incalculable number of koṭis of hundreds of thousands, all possessed of wondrous virtues, pure and identical in truth.

At that time the World-Honored One, seated upon his natural lion-throne, crossed his legs in the full-lotus posture and sat upright, his mind directed and settled. There was a samādhi called "King of Concentration" (*samādhi-rāja*). At that time, through this samādhi, he naturally entered correct absorption, thereby universally entering all concentrations. He gathered and held them in equality, guiding and directing them. When the Buddha entered samādhi and his mind became peacefully quiescent, he surveyed this world with the eye of the path. His body became luminously still and he smiled. From the soles of his feet he emitted sixty billion hundred-thousand rays of light. From his ten toes he emitted ten billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two sides, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two knees, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two calves, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two shoulders, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two elbows, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his navel, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his head, a billion hundred-thousand rays. From his ten fingers, ten billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two arms, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two eyebrows, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his neck, a billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two eyes, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two ears, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his nose, a billion hundred-thousand rays. From the four sides of his face, four hundred billion hundred-thousand rays. From his forty teeth, forty billion hundred-thousand rays. From the mark between his eyebrows, a billion hundred-thousand rays. From the crown-knot mark, sixty billion hundred-thousand rays. These illuminated the entire trichiliocosm without exception, and universally shone upon as many buddha-fields to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. The south, west, north, the four intermediate directions, above, and below were all likewise. Those beings who encountered this radiance all became tranquil in mind and generated the aspiration for the supreme, correct, and true path.

Thereupon the World-Honored One smiled with delight. From every pore he emitted numerous rays of light, illuminating the entire trichiliocosm, universally pervading the ten directions without omission. In as many buddha-worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, all those beings touched by the light became entirely quiescent, abiding in the supreme, correct, and true path. At that time the World-Honored One displayed the Tathāgata's pure, true, and wondrous radiance of his own nature, illuminating the entire trichiliocosm and reaching in each of the ten directions as many buddha-fields as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. If any person attained this light, they all ultimately reached the supreme, correct, and true path.

Thereupon the World-Honored One extended his tongue-base to cover the entire buddha-field, and emitted innumerable billion hundred-thousand rays of light, illuminating the entire trichiliocosm and pervading in each of the ten directions as many buddha-fields as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. Within that radiance, golden jeweled lotus flowers spontaneously grew. Upon each lotus sat a buddha, cross-legged upon a jeweled lotus, teaching the sūtras and the Dharma, expounding the six pāramitās. In all the ten directions it was likewise. If any being heard this Dharma teaching, all ultimately became firmly established in anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

Thereupon the World-Honored One, seated on his lion-throne, entered a samādhi called "Lion's Sport" (*siṃha-vikrīḍita*). Through this concentration he naturally entered correct absorption, and through his form and appearance universally displayed his majestic radiance, manifesting his supernatural powers. The trichiliocosm trembled in six ways. The periphery also shook, from the center spreading to the edges, gently and softly, out of compassion for all beings, causing them to obtain peace, happiness, and freedom from distress. At that time, in the trichiliocosm, beings in the hells, the realms of hungry ghosts and animals, those in unfavorable conditions and those gripped by terror, were spontaneously freed from the three evil paths. Having been freed, they all came forth and were born among humans, in the Heaven of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, the Yāma Heaven, the Tuṣita Heaven, the Nirmāṇarati Heaven, and the Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven. When those gods were born in the human and heavenly realms, they immediately recognized their former lives and went joyfully to the Buddha. They bowed at his feet, placed their palms together, and took refuge. In all of the ten directions it was likewise, without any difference.

At that time, among the beings of this trichiliocosm, the blind gained eyes and beheld forms and appearances. The deaf gained hearing and heard all sounds. The confused in mind were restored to sanity. Those in delusion immediately attained composure. The naked spontaneously received clothing. The hungry were spontaneously filled. The thirsty had nothing more to seek. The sick were healed. Those with bodily defects had all their faculties made whole. The weary spontaneously found relief, and those long dependent upon others no longer needed support. All beings attained minds of equality. They gazed upon one another as upon a father, a mother, a brother, a sister. Each with a common heart, impartial and without bias, all practiced loving-kindness. All beings cultivated the ten wholesome actions, pure in their brahma-conduct and free of all defilements. All people obtained peace and security, a peace and security like that of a bhikṣu who has attained the third dhyāna. At that time, beings attained wisdom, fully equipped with excellent, joyful, and well-settled concentration, freed from the lowly and having attained refinement.

At that time, the World-Honored One on his lion-throne, present in the trichiliocosm, was supreme in his surpassing supernatural majesty, his radiance blazing and brilliant, without any fear. His holy brightness shone with splendor, his honored countenance complete. It pervaded everywhere, illuminating as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and in the eight intermediate directions, above, and below, as many World-Honored One's lands as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. Like Mount Sumeru towering above all mountains, his brightness reached everywhere.

Thereupon the World-Honored One, carrying out the Tathāgata's command, through his natural holy decree caused all beings in the trichiliocosm to behold him together. At that time, in this world, the Śuddhāvāsa pure-abiding gods, Brahmā, the Paranirmitavaśavartin gods, the Nirmāṇarati gods, the Tuṣita gods, the Yāma gods, the Trāyastriṃśa gods, the gods of the Four Kings, and all people dwelling in the trichiliocosm saw themselves as if personally approaching the Tathāgata. They all spontaneously obtained heavenly flowers and ornaments, heavenly incense, heavenly mixed incense, heavenly powdered incense, heavenly blue lotuses, lotuses, and fresh flowers, with all manner of wondrous heavenly flowers complete with stems and leaves. Each one set out carrying these to the Tathāgata, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and scattered them upon him. The earthly flowers of land and water were likewise each taken in hand and offered to the World-Honored One in worship. The flowers and offerings that the gods and humans scattered rose up into space. The trichiliocosm was transformed into palaces and natural pavilions, from which hung heavenly flowers, silk canopies, banners, and streamers, floating and fluttering, brilliantly displayed. All the flowers and incense adorned the entire trichiliocosm so that its buddha-field became naturally majestic, its forms and appearances like the color of refined purple-gold. In all eight directions, above and below, it was likewise fragrant and luminous. Thereupon the people of all the cities of Jambudvīpa, beholding the Tathāgata's bodily manifestation of incalculable supernatural transformation, each thought in their hearts: "Today the Tathāgata is seated before me." Throughout the entire buddha-field it was likewise: each thought, "Today the Tathāgata sits before me, teaching the sūtras and the Dharma."

At that time, the World-Honored One, on his lion-throne, again smiled with delight and shed his light once more throughout the trichiliocosm, his vast radiance blazing and brilliant. The people of this land all saw together the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, with the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones presently there, together with bodhisattvas and assemblies of śrāvakas. Furthermore, all the beings in as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges also saw from afar this buddha-field, with Śākyamuni Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus and bodhisattvas seated and teaching the sūtras. In all eight directions, above and below, it was likewise: they all saw from afar, equally and without difference.

Thereupon, past as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, at the farthest edge, there was a land called Jeweled Steps, and its buddha was named Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, presently dwelling there and teaching the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* for the benefit of beings. In that buddha-world there was a bodhisattva named Universal Radiance. Seeing the great light and the trembling of the earth, he went to Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata, bowed, and asked: "World-Honored One, for what cause and condition does this great light illumine this buddha-field and the earth greatly tremble? This self-manifest appearance of the Tathāgata's body must have a reason."

That buddha said to Universal Radiance Bodhisattva: "Son of good family, you should know that far to the west there is a world called Endurance, whose buddha is named Śākyamuni Tathāgata. He is presently teaching the Prajñāpāramitā for the bodhisattvas. This is the radiance of his supernatural power."

Universal Radiance Bodhisattva addressed Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata: "World-Honored One, I wish to go there to see Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bow, and pay my respects, as well as the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas and the assembly of pure youths who have attained dhāraṇī and the perfection of samādhi, with concentration and mastery, having crossed to the boundless: Śākyamuni, the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One." Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, said to Universal Radiance Bodhisattva: "Go, good man. You know when the time is right." Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata bestowed upon Universal Radiance Bodhisattva a golden lotus flower with a thousand petals: "Take this, good man, this jeweled lotus, and scatter it as an offering to Śākyamuni Tathāgata. Good man, if you wish to go and practice the conduct of quiescence, know that bodhisattvas born in the land of Endurance encounter much difficulty and are hard to meet."

Universal Radiance Bodhisattva received the golden lotus flower and, together with an immeasurable number of koṭis of hundreds of thousands of bodhisattvas, male and female, great and small, householders and renunciants, first offered worship to the buddhas, gods among gods, in the east, paying reverence, offering flowers, incense, mixed incense, and powdered incense. Then they proceeded to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed at his feet, and stood to one side. Universal Radiance Bodhisattva addressed the World-Honored One: "Great Sage, Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata sends his boundless greetings. He inquires whether you are well, vigorous in practice, and at ease. He also sends this golden lotus flower." The Buddha received the lotus flower and at once scattered it toward the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. The flowers immediately pervaded all the buddha-worlds to the east, and on each one a buddha sat upon a naturally occurring golden lotus flower, teaching the sūtras and the Dharma, also expounding the six pāramitās. All beings who heard this teaching immediately became firmly established in anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Males and females, great and small, all bowed at the Buddha's feet, and each, through their merit, offered worship to the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One.

To the south of this place, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, at the farthest edge, was a buddha-world called Free from All Sorrow, whose buddha was named Sorrowless Chief, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Freed from Grief took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower upon him, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offering worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras.

To the west, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, was a world called Quiescence, whose buddha was named Jeweled Dragon, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Mind-Practice took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offered worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras.

To the north, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, was a world called Supremely Victorious, whose buddha was named Victorious over All Faculties, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Supreme Gift took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offered worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras.

Below, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, was a world called Benevolent Sage, whose buddha was named Sagely Chief, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Lotus-Enthroned took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offered worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras.

Above, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, was a world called Joyful Delight, whose buddha was named Chief of Delight, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Gift of Joy took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offered worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras. In the four intermediate directions, it was likewise, equally and without difference.

At that time, upon this trichiliocosm there rained jeweled flowers, incense, banners, and canopies, naturally adorning fragrant trees and flowering trees, just as in the land called Lotus Steps, the world of the buddha Universal Flower, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, where Samantabhadra Bodhisattva resides. Sthitamati and the other devaputras, along with the remaining great spirits and the assembly of supremely powerful and boundless bodhisattvas bestowed by the World-Honored One, as well as all the gods, people of the world, māras, Brahmā, the śrāvaka assembly, gandharvas, asuras, spirits, and humans, all came together in assembly. These bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, while still in their youth, received food, drink, and merit spontaneously.

At that time the World-Honored One said to the Venerable Śāriputra: "If here there is a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, that one should diligently study the prajñāpāramitā."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, fully endowed and comprehending all dharmas, study the prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, having abided in the prajñāpāramitā and cultivated it without attachment to any location, thereby fulfills the dāna-pāramitā. Without deficiency in giving, one gives without any reluctance. One should fulfill the śīla-pāramitā. Through this cause and condition, one has never dwelt in wrongdoing or non-wrongdoing. One should also fulfill the kṣānti-pāramitā, giving rise to freedom from anger. One should study the vīrya-pāramitā and thereby receive a prediction. Through body and mind, one gives rise to diligent effort without arousing any outflows. One should fulfill the dhyāna-pāramitā. Because of this, one is free of seeking and desire."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva abides in the prajñāpāramitā, that one fulfills the four foundations of mindfulness (*smṛtyupasthāna*), generating nothing to be generated. One should also fulfill the four correct exertions (*samyakprahāṇa*), the four bases of supernatural power (*ṛddhipāda*), the five faculties (*indriya*), the five powers (*bala*), the seven factors of awakening (*bodhyaṅga*), and the eightfold path (*āryāṣṭāṅgamārga*), completely fulfilling the samādhi of emptiness, the samādhi of signlessness, and the samādhi of wishlessness. One should certainly fulfill the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables, the four formless samāpattis, and the eight liberations (*vimokṣa*), gradually fulfilling them and entering correct absorption. Through this as liberation, without any conception, without any internal conception, without a conception of worship, without a conception of light, without a conception of deep red, without a conception of putrefaction, without a conception of blue, without a conception of the eaten, bitten, wounded, and festering, without a conception of disorder, without a conception of dried bones, without a conception of scattered [remains], without a conception of any location. Having left behind all conceptions, one constantly dwells on the Buddha, mindful of the sūtras, mindful of the Saṃgha, mindful of the precepts. The mind is directed to generosity, directed to the gods, to the contemplation of breath, to the contemplation of death, to the conception of impermanence, to the conception of suffering and pleasure, to the conception of non-self, to the conception of transmigration, to the conception that all worlds are without delight, to the conception of habitual tendencies, to the conception of cessation. The wisdom of the path, the wisdom of exhaustion, the wisdom free of fever, the wisdom of non-arising, the wisdom of Dharma. Regarding all sūtras and Dharma, also the wisdom of non-wisdom, also the wisdom of non-self. Without internal wisdom, with subtle wisdom of mind, comprehending all wisdoms, wisdom as it truly is. All these are to be contemplated. What is practiced in samādhi is no different from signlessness, non-conception, and non-active concentration. The faculties differ, and different faculties yield different practices. Furthermore, there are practices that are difficult to attain: the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, great compassion and great loving-kindness. If one wishes to understand all of these, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to fully accomplish wisdom of the path, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to fully accomplish comprehensive wisdom and equip oneself with all supernatural wisdoms, one should practice the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to clearly understand all things, to draw near and remove the defilements, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice the prajñāpāramitā. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva studies the prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha further said to Śāriputra: "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to enter into quiescence, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to cross beyond the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas and abide on the stage of non-retrogression (*avaivartika*), one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to abide in the six supernatural powers, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to know the minds and root-conduct of all beings, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to surpass the wisdom of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to attain the gate of dhāraṇī, and if a good man wishes to encourage and assist the giving of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas and surpass them, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to surpass the moral discipline of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, and to encourage and assist the mind, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to fully accomplish samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge and vision of liberation, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to prominently accomplish dhyāna, samādhi, and samāpatti, and to encourage, assist, and gather together the mindfulness that liberates the mind, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to encourage and assist giving, distinguishing its limitless, immeasurable, and accomplished merits, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to fully accomplish the limitless and immeasurable keeping of precepts, patient endurance, diligent effort, concentration, and wisdom, one should study the prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha further said to Śāriputra: "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to fully accomplish and establish the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, and prajñā-pāramitā practice, and in all future lives to see the buddhas and attain buddhahood, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to accomplish the thirty-two marks and eighty minor characteristics, to fully accomplish the bodhisattva-nature, and while yet a pure youth to establish this stage and never be apart from the Buddha World-Honored Ones, and if whatever one aspires to in roots of virtue and offerings to the Tathāgata are to be fulfilled without fail; and if one wishes to fully satisfy what all beings desire in their hearts, whether food, drink, clothing, chariots, incense, flowers, mixed incense, perfumed ointments, bedding, lamps, towels, shoes, or whatever they should obtain, filling them with all wealth, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to fully equip as many beings as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, establishing them in the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, and dhyāna-pāramitā, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through a single wholesome root, accords with the virtues of the Tathāgata, inexhaustible and without diminishment, until the accomplishment of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva studies the prajñāpāramitā, the buddhas, gods among gods, in all eight directions, above and below, all together praise that person's merits. In the moment of generating the aspiration, if one wishes to travel to the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and in all ten directions, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes, with a single sound, to proclaim to the buddha-fields in all ten directions, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to establish the buddha-fields so that they are never broken off, and wishes to abide in internal emptiness, external emptiness, internal-external emptiness, emptiness of emptiness, great emptiness, ultimate emptiness, emptiness of the existent, emptiness of the nonexistent, conditioned emptiness, unconditioned emptiness, true emptiness, emptiness without worship, emptiness without cause, emptiness of causes, emptiness of the natural characteristic, emptiness of all dharmas, emptiness of non-apprehension, emptiness of nonexistence, natural emptiness, emptiness of the formless and the natural, emptiness of the practices conditioned by causes and supernatural power, and wishes to reach these, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to draw near to all tathāgatas, wishes to comprehend and understand all dharmas, and wishes to understand that all dharmas abide at their original limit, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who wishes to accomplish the prajñāpāramitā should abide in this way.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to count and know the sands, stones, trees, flowers, and all the motes of dust in the trichiliocosm, resolving every doubt, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to know the number of drops in all the great oceans, rivers, streams, springs, and sources of the trichiliocosm, and to cross over the ocean's creatures without harming them, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. Furthermore, Śāriputra, suppose all the fire in the trichiliocosm were to blaze simultaneously as at a kalpa-conflagration, and one wished to extinguish it all at once with a single motion, so that nothing burned, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished, when all the winds of the trichiliocosm blow up and crumble all the Sumeru mountains, reducing them to dust and ashes like burning reeds and straw, to extinguish the fire of the three realms with a single finger, whether a hand-finger or a toe, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished to fill the entire space of the trichiliocosm with one's body, sitting cross-legged pervading all space, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. To transform freely, without near or far, without great or small, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished to take all the Sumeru mountains of the trichiliocosm and lift them with one hand, placing them in extraordinarily many other buddha-worlds, without any thought of going and returning, without increase or decrease, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished to gather at one time all the buddhas, gods among gods, śrāvakas, and pratyekabuddhas in as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and to make offerings to them, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished, with a single robe, incense, flowers, powdered incense, perfumed ointments, silk canopies, banners, and streamers to make offerings to the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones and their śrāvaka assemblies, paying reverence to them all gathered at one time, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished to establish all the beings of the trichiliocosm in the śīla-pāramitā, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge and vision of liberation, in the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, up to the fruit of nirvāṇa without remainder, causing them to enter parinirvāṇa, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā should practice giving as a pāramitā, studying it in this way: 'Such giving obtains great fruit and reward. Such giving causes birth among kings, nobles, and brahmins of great families and clans. Such giving causes birth among the gods of the Four Kings, Trāyastriṃśa, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, and Paranirmitavaśavartin. Such giving, relying on this generosity, contemplates the first dhyāna, the second, the third, and up to the fourth dhyāna, the samāpatti of infinite space in correct absorption, the samāpatti of infinite consciousness, the samāpatti of nothingness, the samāpatti of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Such giving arouses the noble eightfold path and attains the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, the fruit of arhatship, and the realization of a pratyekabuddha.' If one comprehends this, one should study the prajñāpāramitā in this way.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā always gives through skillful wisdom, in order to fulfill the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, and prajñā-pāramitā."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva fulfill the six pāramitās?"

He answered: "The giver has no thought of attachment; the recipient also does not forget the kindness. This is the dāna-pāramitā. One commits no offense and does not adorn oneself through the precepts. This is the śīla-pāramitā. One constantly maintains patient endurance, without anger or resentment toward any being. This is the kṣānti-pāramitā. One is diligent and untiring in wishing to liberate all beings. This is the vīrya-pāramitā. The mind is quiescent and without agitation. This is the dhyāna-pāramitā. One's wisdom comprehends emptiness, without reckoning a self. This is the prajñā-pāramitā. In brief, and to explain further: regarding wrongdoing and non-wrongdoing, and the absence of non-wrongdoing, this is the śīla-pāramitā. Being without anger or resentment is the kṣānti-pāramitā. Being diligent in body and mind without fatigue is the vīrya-pāramitā. Giving rise to non-distraction without any conceptual thought is the dhyāna-pāramitā. Comprehending all dharmas without any attachment is the prajñā-pāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to accomplish and acquire the merit of the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, of the past, future, and present, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to transcend all conditioned and unconditioned dharma-practices, understanding past, present, and future dharmas up to their original limit, where all dharmas arise without departing from their original limit, and wishes to attain this, and to practice all the dharmas of śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and bodhisattvas, and to worship the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and wishes to attain the full retinue of the buddhas and the immeasurable companions, and wishes to attain the associates and party of bodhisattvas, and wishes to bring to purity all the merits and blessings, and wishes to practice giving with a mind that does not receive, without giving rise to the thought of transgressing the precepts, without anger, without laziness, without wishing to generate a distracted mind, and without wishing to generate a foolish mind, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to establish beings in the merit of giving, morality, and wisdom, encouraging them to cultivate these, and wishes to produce whatever merit should be produced, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to produce the five eyes, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. What are the five eyes? The fleshly eye, the heavenly eye, the wisdom eye, the Dharma eye, and the buddha eye. One should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to see the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, in as many worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, in the eight directions, above and below, and to hear with the heavenly ear all the sūtras and Dharma they teach, and also wishes to know the thoughts of the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to hear the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, throughout all ten directions teaching the sūtras and Dharma, hearing it without interruption until anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to see the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones of the past and their buddha-fields, and the present buddhas of the ten directions and all that exists in their lands, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to understand and know the twelve divisions of sūtras taught by the Tathāgata: the connected discourses, analytical discourses, verse discourses, hymn discourses, prompted discourses, discourses spoken on an occasion, birth stories, received discourses, extensive discourses, discourses on unprecedented dharmas, parables, and annotated discourses, which are not heard by the śrāvakas, and wishes to study and recite them all, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to hear the sūtras and Dharma taught by the Tathāgata in the eight directions, above and below, and to remember them without forgetting, and having grasped and retained them, wishes to teach them to the assembly and to others, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to inquire into the meaning of what the buddhas of the past and future have taught, and having heard it, wishes to teach it to others, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to illuminate the buddha-worlds to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, which are dark and gloomy, without the light of the sun and moon, and wishes to illuminate these and the worlds of the ten directions, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to convert beings in the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and throughout the ten directions, who are foolish and shrouded in darkness, who have not heard the name of the Buddha, have not been able to hear the sūtras, and have not seen the Saṃgha, and wishes to convert those beings and establish them in right view, enabling them to see the Buddha, to hear the sūtras and the Dharma, and to encounter the holy assembly, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes that all beings in as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and throughout the ten directions, who are born blind might gain eyes and behold forms, who are deaf might gain hearing, who are deranged might be restored to sanity, who are naked might receive clothing, who are hungry might obtain food, and who are thirsty might obtain water, and wishes, through the power of their vow, to bestow these blessings upon all, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, regarding those beings in this trichiliocosm and throughout the eight directions, above and below, in as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, who are in the evil destinies of the hells, hungry ghosts, and animals, wishes to extend benevolence to these beings so that they may attain their proper place, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to establish all the beings of as many buddha-worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges in the precepts, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge and vision of liberation, in the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, the fruit of arhatship, and the realization of pratyekabuddha, up to the accomplishment of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, and also wishes to cultivate the Tathāgata's deportment and etiquette, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā should contemplate thus: 'Suppose there is what my own body cannot see, yet I wish to perceive it. One should study the prajñāpāramitā.' Suppose my body, from four inches of ground, with a single toe-tip, could pervade everything from the Heaven of the Four Kings through the desire and form realms up to the Akaniṣṭha Heaven, accompanied by an immeasurable retinue of koṭis of hundreds of thousands, going to the bodhi-tree and dwelling at the place of the path. One should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, seated at the bodhi-tree, with the gods of the Four Kings, the higher gods up to the pure-abiding gods equally and without difference, all coming with complete offerings, should accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, and whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down, the ground beneath would become vajra of itself. If one wishes to attain this, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā should contemplate thus: 'On what day shall I leave, renouncing the kingdom and forsaking the household, and on that very day accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, attaining perfect awakening? Having attained perfect awakening, on that very day I shall turn the wheel of Dharma. Having turned the wheel of Dharma, I shall cause immeasurable, incalculable beings to become free from dust and stain, and their Dharma-eye for all dharmas will become pure. Boundless and limitless multitudes of beings will attain the liberation of mind through the exhaustion of outflows, without further arising. Boundless and limitless beings will attain the non-retrogression of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.' Such a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who thinks: 'When I accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and attain perfect awakening, I shall have an immeasurable holy assembly of śrāvaka disciples who are learners. Through a single exposition of the sūtras and the Dharma, in a single sitting, they shall attain arhatship. All the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas shall attain the non-retrogression of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. There shall be an immeasurable, incalculable, boundless assembly of bodhisattvas, whose lifespans are immeasurable, whose light illumines far beyond all limits.' One should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and accomplish perfect awakening, and wishes that in that buddha-field there shall be no sound of the words 'lust,' 'anger,' or 'delusion,' and that all beings there shall have such an appearance as if they had fully accomplished the prajñāpāramitā: 'Excellent, well-tamed and docile! Excellent, wise and well-cultivated in brahma-conduct! And traveling in accord without lingering among beings is excellent!' One should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who vows: 'May I attain the fully accomplished holy understanding, and through the correct Dharma, the concentration of richness without any sound.' One should study the prajñāpāramitā. Thinking in one's mind: 'With my pure sound I shall cause the beings of as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.' One should study the prajñāpāramitā."

### Chapter 2: Conforming to Emptiness

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā and arouses these merits, the Four Heavenly Kings immediately rejoice: 'We shall present four bowls before the Four Kings, offering them as we offered them in the past to the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones, and shall likewise make offerings to one who studies the path of Dharma.' At that time the Trāyastriṃśa gods also rejoice, as do the Yāma, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, and Paranirmitavaśavartin gods: 'We shall all serve and make offerings to this good man. The bodies of the asuras shall diminish, and the bodies of the gods shall increase.' Throughout the trichiliocosm, up to the Akaniṣṭha Heaven, none fail to rejoice: 'We shall entreat and encourage the turning of the Dharma wheel.' Śāriputra, when a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, it increases and benefits and completes the six pāramitās. Good men and good women rejoice with delight: 'We shall be as the loving-kindness of parents, wife, children, relatives, and friends; the loving-kindness of parents, brothers, wife, children, intimate friends and companions, and the affection, respect, and joy of seeing each other.' The gods of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa, the Yāma, the Tuṣita, the Nirmāṇarati, the Paranirmitavaśavartin, up to the Akaniṣṭha, will not allow the bodhisattva to encounter any defilement or desire. With minds directed, they come to serve and bow, thinking: 'We shall also cause this one to attain the pure conduct of Brahmā, the practice free from impurity, without the habit of sensual desire, so as to be born in the Brahmā-heaven.' There is no negligence that engenders negligence. Those who have form cannot progress to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Therefore the bodhisattva, through pure brahma-conduct, forsakes household affairs and then attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. One does not attain the Buddha path through impurity."

The Venerable Śāriputra addressed the World-Honored One: "Must the bodhisattva's dharma necessarily involve having parents, wife, and children, relatives and friends?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattvas who necessarily have parents but should not have wives and children. Some, from the time of their initial aspiration, purely cultivate brahma-conduct, becoming a pure youth, up to the accomplishment of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. There are also bodhisattvas who, through skillful means (*upāya-kauśalya*), partake of the five desires and afterward forsake the household, attaining anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. It is like a clever and skilled magician and his disciple, well-trained in the art of illusion, who conjure the five desires and use these five pleasures for their own enjoyment and amusement. Śāriputra, what do you think: does that magician truly partake of the five desires?"

Śāriputra said: "No, God among Gods."

The Buddha said: "Just so, Śāriputra. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through skillful means, partakes of the five desires in order to convert beings. That bodhisattva-mahāsattva is not stained by the five desires. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through countless means, sometimes praises desire and sometimes reviles desire as a blazing fire, desire as a flaw, desire as an enemy, desire as an adversary. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, for the sake of liberating beings, distinguishes the affairs of the five desires."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "God among Gods, how does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practice the prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not see the bodhisattva, nor does one see the word 'bodhisattva.' One does not see the prajñāpāramitā, nor does one see the words 'practicing the prajñāpāramitā,' nor does one see 'non-practice.' Why? The word 'bodhisattva' is naturally empty. What is empty has no form, no feeling, no perception, no formations, no consciousness, and is not different from the emptiness of form, not different from the emptiness of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. As form is empty, so too feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are empty. What is called emptiness is the emptiness that is form, the emptiness that is feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, being naturally so. Why? What is called 'bodhisattva' is merely a conventional name. What is called 'the path' is also a conventional name. What is called 'emptiness' is also a conventional name. The nature of dharmas does not arise and does not cease, and is without defilement. It has no support and no contention. If there is a bodhisattva whose practice is thus, one does not see any arising, nor does one see any ceasing, nor does one see any support, nor does one see any contention. Why? Words are established through deception, relying on adventitious conceptions. It is because of conceptions that these dharmas come about. From what are words established? They are merely entrusted to empty speech. Understanding thus, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā: one entirely does not see any name or designation. Having nothing to see and not being without seeing, one is without any support. This is practicing the prajñāpāramitā."

### Chapter 3: Practicing Emptiness, Part 1

The Buddha further said to Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, one should contemplate thus: 'What is called "bodhisattva," what is called "buddha," is merely a conventional name. What are called "form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness" are merely conventional names. They all stem from the self. What is called "self" is in fact nothing whatsoever. There is no self, no person, no life-force, no lifespan, no blood-bearing or crawling creature, no mind, no intention. Whatever is done and created, whatever is naturally habituated, undergone, and seen, known, and perceived, all these kinds are unobtainable, empty, and without any attachment. They are all conventional names, merely empty words.' Thus a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā: one does not see beings. If one has nothing to see, one also does not have seeing, nor does one see the words that are entrusted. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva whose practice is thus follows the Tathāgata's teaching and practices the prajñāpāramitā. Having set aside the Tathāgata, this wisdom surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. What one arouses through the practice of emptiness does not bring confusion. Why? Because that person, in what they cultivate, does not see any word upon which to rely. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva whose practice is thus practices the prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha said: "Suppose, Śāriputra, bhikṣus like Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana filled the whole of Jambudvīpa, dense as bamboo, reeds, sugar-cane, rice, or hemp in a thicket, all fully endowed with wisdom. They could never match the bodhisattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā, not a hundredfold, not a thousandfold, not ten-thousandfold, not a hundred-millionfold, not even to begin comparison. Why? The bodhisattva's wisdom seeks to liberate all beings, and it is for this reason.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā, in a single day's practice of wisdom, surpasses all the attainments of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Set aside that Jambudvīpa filled with bhikṣus like Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana. Even if the entire trichiliocosm were filled with bhikṣus like Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana, all their wisdom could not match the bodhisattva-mahāsattva. Set aside that trichiliocosm of Śāriputras and Mahāmaudgalyāyanas. Suppose as many buddha-fields to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, pervading all ten directions, were all filled with bhikṣus like Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana. Even they could not match a single day of the bodhisattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā. Such wisdom surpasses the wisdom of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas by a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a hundred-millionfold, beyond any comparison."

Thereupon the Venerable Śāriputra addressed the World-Honored One: "The wisdom of the śrāvakas, the wisdom of the stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, arhat, pratyekabuddha, bodhisattva, and the tathāgata, worthy one, perfectly awakened one: reckoning all these wisdoms, none can be destroyed, none can be contested, none arise, and they are naturally empty. God among Gods, if what cannot be destroyed, cannot be contested, does not arise, and is naturally empty, how can there be any differentiation to be attained? How can the bodhisattva's single day of practiced wisdom surpass all the wisdom of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "What do you think? Why does the bodhisattva practice the prajñāpāramitā? In a single day, the wisdom that is practiced, the vows that are established, the cultivation of skillful means (*upāya*), and the practice of compassion are all for the sake of all beings. One thoroughly comprehends all dharmas in order to convert beings and bring them to cessation. Do the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas establish such a cause and such wisdom?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods." The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, what do you think? Do the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas have this thought: 'We shall attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening, and convert beings to the realm of nirvāṇa, causing them to attain cessation'?"

He answered: "No."

The Buddha said: "Therefore one should further know that all the wisdom of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, even a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a hundred-thousandfold, a hundred-millionfold wisdom, can never match it. What do you think? Do the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas have this thought: 'We shall practice the six pāramitās, convert beings, purify buddha-fields, fully accomplish the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, and accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, liberating and bringing to cessation immeasurable, limitless, incalculable beings'?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods." The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva generates the thought: 'I shall practice the six pāramitās, fully accomplish all dharmas, and accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, liberating incalculable beings.'"

The Buddha said: "It is like the palace of the sun, which, exerting its radiance, in a single moment universally illuminates the whole of Jambudvīpa without omission. Just so, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the six pāramitās, fully accomplishes the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, and attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, converting and liberating immeasurable, limitless, incalculable beings."

The Venerable Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva surpass the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas and attain the stage of non-retrogression, purely cultivating the Buddha path?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "Herein, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, from the time of the initial aspiration, practices the six pāramitās, crosses beyond the dharmas of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, and thereby surpasses the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, abiding on the stage of non-retrogression."

The Venerable Śāriputra further addressed the Buddha: "How is a bodhisattva-mahāsattva the most blessed among all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, from the time of the initial aspiration, practices the six pāramitās until sitting at the bodhi-tree, always the most blessed among all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Why? When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva appears in the present, the true and wondrous Dharma naturally arises. With it come the ten wholesome actions, the five precepts, the eightfold noble path and the eight precepts of purification, the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables, the four formless samāpattis, the four foundations of mindfulness, the four correct exertions, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, the eightfold path. Also appearing in the world are the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas. All such virtues appear in the world. Through this, the distinctions of noble families and clans, brahmins and great elders, the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven up to the heaven of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, pratyekabuddhas, and tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones are known. It is because of this that such distinctions exist."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva bring blessings to purity and completion?"

The World-Honored One said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva does not bring blessings to purity and completion from within the blessings themselves. Why? Ultimating emptiness is precisely how a bodhisattva-mahāsattva completes blessings. Why? Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva is a giver of gifts. What does one give? One gives the gift of wholesome Dharma, converting beings. What is the wholesome Dharma? The ten wholesome actions, the five precepts, the six pāramitās, the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas. One converts stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, and pratyekabuddhas. Such is the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One's giver of gifts."

Śāriputra further addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what practice does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva follow in practicing the prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "Herein, if a bodhisattva practices the emptiness of form, that is practicing the prajñāpāramitā. If one practices the emptiness of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, that is the practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva comprehends the emptiness of the eye, the emptiness of the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of form, sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of the eye-element, the form-element, the eye-consciousness-element, the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind elements, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of the ear-element, sound, ear-consciousness, nose, smell, nose-consciousness, tongue, taste, tongue-consciousness, body, touch, body-consciousness, mind-element, dharmas, mind-consciousness, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of suffering, the emptiness of origination, the emptiness of cessation, and the emptiness of the eightfold path, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of ignorance, the emptiness of formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, and aging-and-death, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of all dharmas, this is the practice. Whatever is naturally conditioned or unconditioned, if one can comprehend its emptiness, this is the practice. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā comprehends original purity as empty, and one's own nature is likewise. This is the practice. Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā should comprehend these seven emptinesses. This is the true practice. Practicing the prajñāpāramitā through these seven emptinesses, one sees no accordance or non-accordance, no practice or non-practice of form. Without making this contemplation, one does not see feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. One does not see any dharma of form that arises or ceases. One does not see any dharma of feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness that arises or ceases. One does not see any dharma of form with any attachment or any contention. One does not see any dharma of feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness with any attachment or any contention. One does not see oneself as dwelling together with form, nor dwelling together with feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness. One does not see oneself as dwelling together with birth-and-death, nor as not dwelling together with birth-and-death. Why? There is ultimately no dharma that dwells together with anything. All things arise by conditions, and their original nature is empty. Śāriputra, form is empty, and there is no form. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are empty, and there is no consciousness."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "What is empty has no arising and no ceasing. If form is empty, there is no form. If feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are empty, there is no consciousness. If form is empty, there is nothing to be seen. If feeling is empty, there is nothing to be afflicted by. If perception is empty, there is nothing to be conceived. If formations are empty, there is nothing to be created. If consciousness is empty, there is nothing to be discriminated. Why? Śāriputra, form being different is not the same as emptiness. Emptiness is not different. Form is not separate. Form is naturally empty. Form is precisely emptiness. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are not separately different, and emptiness is not different from them. If emptiness is not different, consciousness is not different. Consciousness is naturally empty. Consciousness is precisely emptiness."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "What is empty does not arise and does not cease, has no attachment and no contention, no increase and no decrease, no past, no future, no present. There is no form, feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness. There is no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind. There is no form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharma. Ignorance does not cease; ignorance does not move. There is no consciousness, no name-and-form, no six sense-bases, no contact, no feeling, no craving, no grasping, no becoming, no birth, no aging, no sickness, no death, nor the cessation of birth, aging, sickness, and death. There is no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path. There is no attainment, and there is no time. There is no fruit of stream-entry, no fruit of once-returning, no fruit of non-returning, no fruit of arhatship, no realization of pratyekabuddha. There is no attainment of the path, and there is no Buddha path. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva whose practice of the prajñāpāramitā is thus, this is how the bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā: one does not see the prajñāpāramitā as according or not according, as practicing or not practicing. One does not see giving, precepts, patience, effort, meditation, or wisdom. One does not see the six pāramitās. One does not see form, feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. One does not see the eye as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. Likewise for the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. One does not see form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. One does not see the four foundations of mindfulness as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. Likewise for the four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and eightfold path. One does not see the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and eighteen unique buddha-dharmas as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. One does not see the Tathāgata's sarvajñā-wisdom as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. This is how, Śāriputra, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā. This is the accordance of practice."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā: emptiness does not contend with emptiness; emptiness does not practice with emptiness. Signlessness does not contend with signlessness; signlessness does not practice with signlessness. Wishlessness does not contend with wishlessness; wishlessness does not practice with wishlessness. Emptiness does not correspond to emptiness; signlessness does not correspond to signlessness; wishlessness does not correspond to wishlessness. Why? Emptiness has no practice and no non-practice. Signlessness also has no practice and no non-practice. Wishlessness also has no practice and no non-practice. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice."

The Buddha further said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā comprehends that the natural characteristics of all dharmas cross beyond emptiness. Having crossed beyond emptiness, one does not contend with form and has no practice. One does not contend with feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness and has no practice. One does not contend with past form, nor does one see past form. One does not contend with past form, nor does one see future form. One does not contend with future form, nor does one see present form. One does not contend with present form. Likewise, one does not contend with past feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness, nor with future or present feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness, nor does one see past, future, or present feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not contend with the past regarding the future, does not contend with the future regarding the past, does not contend with the present regarding the past and future, does not contend with the past and future regarding the present. One does not see the three times as practicing emptiness in the prajñāpāramitā. One who practices thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā, whose practice is thus, as it should be practiced: one does not contend with or practice past sarvajñā, nor does one see it. Is there any past sarvajñā, together with the practice and its contention? One does not contend with or practice future sarvajñā, nor is there any practice, nor does one see it. Is there any future sarvajñā, together with the practice and its contention? Nor does one contend with or practice present sarvajñā, nor does one see present sarvajñā. Is there any sarvajñā, together with the practice and its contention? One who practices the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice the sarvajñā of form, nor does one see the sarvajñā of form. One does not practice the sarvajñā of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, nor does one see the sarvajñā of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. One does not practice the sarvajñā of the eye, nor does one see the eye. One does not practice the sarvajñā of the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, nor does one see the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. One does not practice the sarvajñā of form, nor does one see form. One does not see form, one does not practice the sarvajñā of sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas, nor does anything appear."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā also does not follow the sarvajñā of the dāna-pāramitā, nor does one see the dāna-pāramitā, the śīla-pāramitā, the kṣānti-pāramitā, the vīrya-pāramitā, or the dhyāna-pāramitā. Likewise one does not practice the sarvajñā of the prajñāpāramitā, nor does one see the sarvajñā of the prajñāpāramitā. One also does not follow the sarvajñā of the four foundations of mindfulness, nor does one see the sarvajñā of the four foundations of mindfulness. Likewise for the four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and eightfold path. Nor does one follow the sarvajñā of the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, nor does one see them, nor does one see the Tathāgata's sarvajñā of the powers and dharmas. One who practices the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice sarvajñā as the Buddha, nor does the Buddha practice sarvajñā. One does not practice sarvajñā as the path, nor does the path practice sarvajñā. Why? The Buddha is sarvajñā, and sarvajñā is the Buddha. The path is sarvajñā, and sarvajñā is the path. The ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and eighteen unique buddha-dharmas are likewise."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice form as existent, does not practice form as non-existent. One does not practice feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness as existent or non-existent. One does not reckon form as permanent or impermanent. One does not reckon form as suffering or pleasure. One does not reckon form as having a self or being without self. The five aggregates and six sense-gates are likewise. One does not reckon the five aggregates as empty or non-empty, as having characteristics or being without characteristics, as having aspirations or being without aspirations. One practices the prajñāpāramitā thus: 'What I now practice is also without reception. There is no practice, and there is nothing taken. There is practice and yet no practice. There is reception and yet no reception. There is taking and yet no taking.'"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice for the sake of the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, or dhyāna-pāramitā when practicing the prajñāpāramitā. One does not practice the prajñāpāramitā for the sake of the stage of non-retrogression, or for converting beings, or for purifying the buddha-field, or for the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, or eighteen unique buddha-dharmas. One does not practice the prajñāpāramitā for the sake of ultimate emptiness, internal emptiness, external emptiness, internal-external emptiness, emptiness of emptiness, great emptiness, true emptiness, conditioned emptiness, unconditioned emptiness, ultimate emptiness, emptiness without grade, original purity emptiness, natural characteristic emptiness, emptiness of all dharmas, emptiness of non-arising, emptiness of non-ceasing, emptiness of the formless, natural emptiness, emptiness of the formed and formless, or the original foundation, or the dharma-realm, or the original limit. Why? When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, regarding all dharmas there is nothing destroyed and nothing seen."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice for the sake of supernatural powers, nor for the heavenly eye, nor for the heavenly ear, nor for knowing others' minds, nor for recollecting past events. Why? When practicing the prajñāpāramitā, one does not even see the prajñāpāramitā. How much less would one behold the bodhisattva's supernatural powers? One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, when a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, one does not think: 'I shall use supernatural powers to go to the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, to see the tathāgatas and bow to them.' Nor does one think: 'I shall reach the eight directions, above and below.' It is likewise, equally and without difference. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'I shall use the heavenly ear to hear all that the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, have taught.' Nor: 'I shall perceive and see what all beings think in their minds.' Nor: 'I shall recollect the places I have dwelt in past lives.' Nor: 'With the heavenly eye I shall see all beings wherever they are.'"

The Buddha said: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice. Thus, Śāriputra, one who practices thus liberates an immeasurable, incalculable assembly of beings. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can do thus, Māra and his retinue cannot find any opening. Furthermore, even in other world-systems, people hearing of such virtue from afar all bow in respect. Furthermore, the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, in as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and in all eight directions, above and below, all together protect this bodhisattva, who will never fall to the stages of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas. The gods of the Four Kings up to the Akaniṣṭha all together protect this bodhisattva-mahāsattva, so that no one seeking an opening can find one. Whatever one undertakes and whatever one should do, one obtains merit in the present. Why? Because one directs a compassionate mind toward all beings. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā, with only slight effort attains the gate of dhāraṇī and the gate of samādhi. One swiftly draws near the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. In all one's births, one constantly encounters and sees the Buddha, never apart from the buddhas, until attaining anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

The Buddha said: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'Are there any dharmas? Are all dharmas in accordance or not in accordance? Are they equal or not equal?' Why? At that time, the practitioner does not see any dharma as according or not according, as practicing or not practicing, as equal or unequal."

The Buddha said: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'I shall swiftly comprehend the dharma-realm and reach perfect awakening, yet there is no perfect awakening.' Why? One who attains the dharma-realm also has nothing to awaken to. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not see that all dharmas and the dharma-realm have any illness or any quiescence. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'The dharma-realm of all dharmas has various kinds; I do not reckon them as separately different.' One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'Regarding all these dharmas and the dharma-realm, I contemplate or do not contemplate, I see or do not see.' Why? One does not see any dharma that exists and that can be grasped for separate contemplation. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think that the dharma-realm is anxious about the practice of emptiness, nor does the practice of emptiness cause anxiety about the dharma-realm. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think about whether the eye-element is empty. One does not cause the eye-element to be anxious. Form does not cause anxiety about emptiness; emptiness does not cause anxiety about form. The form-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the emptiness-element does not cause anxiety about form. The eye-consciousness-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the consciousness-element does not cause anxiety about the emptiness of eye-consciousness. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas are likewise. The mind-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the emptiness-element does not cause anxiety about mind. The dharma-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the emptiness-element does not cause anxiety about dharmas. The consciousness-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the emptiness-element does not cause anxiety about consciousness."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, this is the foremost practice, namely the practice of emptiness. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice emptiness will not fall to the stages of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas, can purify buddha-fields and convert beings, and will swiftly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. Reckoning all the practices of the prajñāpāramitā, the practice of the prajñāpāramitā is the most honored, the greatest, the highest, without bottom, without peer. Why? The practice of the prajñāpāramitā is the supreme practice: the practice of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice thus and should uphold it in this way, and one will swiftly draw near the stage of receiving a prediction. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices in this accordance liberates and benefits immeasurable, incalculable beings. If one does not think: 'I practice the prajñāpāramitā, and the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, shall give me a prediction.' Nor does one think: 'I shall draw near, receive a prediction, purify the buddha-field, and attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening, and shall turn the wheel of Dharma.' Why? That practitioner does not cling to the dharma-realm, nor is it empty and quiescent, nor does one see any different dharma through which to practice the prajñāpāramitā and receive a prediction from the buddhas, gods among gods, and attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. Why? A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not give rise to the conception of a person, the conception of a self, the conception of a lifespan, the conception of a life-force, the conception of a multitude, or the conception of views and knowledge. Why? Reckoning a self, beings do not arise and do not cease. Reckoning a person, the original nature does not arise and does not cease. That which does not arise and does not cease: what practice of the prajñāpāramitā does it have? A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice thus, with nothing arising in persons, practices the prajñāpāramitā. Beings are empty, beings are unobtainable, beings are quiescent. This is practicing the prajñāpāramitā. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva follows and cultivates emptiness as the foremost practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus surpasses all practices. Set aside what has been described as the practice to be followed. It is the practice of great loving-kindness and the practice of great compassion. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices these will never give rise to a mind of covetous jealousy, nor a mind of broken precepts, nor a mind of anger, nor a mind of laziness, nor a mind of agitation, nor a mind of wrong views."

## Fascicle Two

### Chapter 3: Practicing Emptiness, Part 2

The Venerable Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā: from where did they pass away and come to be born here?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "This bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā passed away from a buddha-field in another region and was born here. Or, having transferred from the Tuṣita Heaven, one was born here in the human realm and swiftly attains the practice of the prajñāpāramitā. One who practices the prajñāpāramitā accomplishes it in the present life, and that person swiftly draws near the gate of the profound and wondrous Dharma. After that, one brings the prajñāpāramitā to its ultimate conclusion, constantly encountering and seeing the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones, never apart from the buddhas in whatever land one is born. There are also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who, having passed away from the Tuṣita Heaven in their final body as one destined to fill the position in a single lifetime, do not lose the six pāramitās. Wherever they go, the gates of dhāraṇī are all fully present, and they swiftly draw near the gates of samādhi." The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, a bodhisattva who passes away from the human realm and is reborn in the human realm is a non-retrogressing bodhisattva. Such a person's faculties are quiescent and settled, but one cannot swiftly attain the practice and concentration of the prajñāpāramitā, nor can one draw near the gates of dhāraṇī or the gates of samādhi."

Śāriputra further asked: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā: when one's life here ends, where will one be born?"

The Buddha said: "When one's life here ends, one travels from one buddha-field to another, wherever the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, presently dwell, never apart from the gods among gods. There are also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who, without skillful means (*upāya-kauśalya*), cultivate the first dhyāna through the fourth dhyāna, practice the six pāramitās, and because of this dhyāna are born in the long-lived heavens. After passing away from there, if they obtain a human body and encounter the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, their faculties are quiescent and settled but they are not sharp-witted."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the first through fourth dhyānas, practice the prajñāpāramitā but without skillful means, and afterward abandon the dhyānas and are born in the desire realm. Such bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have quiescent and settled faculties but are not sharp-witted."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the first through fourth dhyānas without departing from the prajñāpāramitā. They contemplate the wisdom of emptiness and enter concentration, up to the wisdom of consciousness and enter concentration, up to the wisdom of nothingness and enter concentration, up to the realm of neither-perception-nor-non-perception and enter concentration. Transcending these four heavens, they cultivate the four foundations of mindfulness, four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold path. They practice great compassion and possess skillful means. Wherever they are born, they do not follow the dhyāna teachings, nor follow the brahmavihāras of loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity, nor conform to the formless dhyānas. They are born freely wherever they choose, and wherever they are born, they constantly see the present tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones, never apart from the prajñāpāramitā. Within the present kalpa, they shall attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the first through fourth dhyānas, practice the four immeasurables, transcend these four heavens, cultivate the four foundations of mindfulness, four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold path, practice great compassion, and possess skillful means but do not enter dhyāna concentration. Wherever they are born, they do not attain freedom of choice. Such persons are born in the desire realm among nobles, persons of good family and clan, brahmins, and great elders, in order to convert beings and bring them benefit."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the first through fourth dhyānas, practice the four immeasurables, contemplate the wisdom of emptiness, the wisdom of consciousness, the wisdom of nothingness, and the realm of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, transcend these four heavens, cultivate the thirty-seven factors of awakening, practice great compassion and skillful means, do not follow the dhyāna teachings, and wherever they are born, they are born in the Heaven of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, the Yāma Heaven, the Tuṣita Heaven, the Nirmāṇarati Heaven, and the Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven. Born in those places, they convert beings, purify buddha-fields, see the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and never depart from the path-teaching."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the prajñāpāramitā, possess skillful means, cultivate the first dhyāna, and practice the four immeasurables. When their lives end here, they are born in the Brahmakāyika Heaven, the Brahmapurohita Heaven, the Mahābrahmā Heaven, and the Great Brahmā Heaven. Dwelling in the Brahmā and Great Brahmā heavens, they travel from one buddha-field to another. Wherever the buddhas presently dwell, having accomplished anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening and turned the wheel of Dharma, those bodhisattva-mahāsattvas encourage and assist the buddhas to turn the wheel of Dharma."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva destined to fill the position in a single lifetime practices the prajñāpāramitā and, through skillful expedient means, manifests the practice of the first through fourth dhyānas, the samādhis of loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity, up to the wisdom of emptiness, consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception, transcending these four heavens, cultivating the thirty-seven factors of awakening, practicing great compassion, the samādhi of emptiness, the samādhi of signlessness, and the samādhi of wishlessness. The bodhisattva travels freely wherever born. Such a person personally sees the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and in their presence purely cultivates the brahma-conduct and is born in the Tuṣita Heaven. Dwelling there as a guiding teacher, ferrying beings across like a boat, with faculties flawless and constantly quiescent and settled, surrounded by an immeasurable retinue of koṭis of hundreds of thousands of gods, one descends together with them to this world and accomplishes the supreme, correct, and true path, attaining perfect awakening."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has attained the six supernatural powers is not born in the desire heavens, form heavens, or formless heavens, but travels from one buddha-field to another, bowing in reverence and serving all the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has attained the six supernatural powers and delights in them travels from one buddha-field to another. In whatever buddha-field one goes, one does not hear the words 'śrāvaka' or 'pratyekabuddha,' nor even their names."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has attained the six supernatural powers and delights in them travels universally through the ten directions, going from one buddha-field to another. In whatever buddha-land one goes, the lifespans are extremely long, immeasurable beyond counting in kalpas."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has attained the six supernatural powers and delights in them travels from one buddha-world to another. In whatever buddha-land one goes where there is no Buddha-Dharma or holy assembly, one proclaims and explains in verse the virtues of the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha. When beings hear the sounds of 'Buddha,' 'Dharma,' and 'Saṃgha,' their minds are filled with joy, and after their lives end, they are all born in lands where a buddha, World-Honored One, presently dwells."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who, from the time of the initial aspiration, does not attain the first through fourth dhyānas, the four immeasurables, the four formless attainments, the four foundations of mindfulness, the four correct exertions, the four bases of supernatural power, the five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold path, the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, will never be born in the desire realm, form realm, or formless realm. Wherever one is born, one is among beings who seek fame and renown."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās, from the time of the initial aspiration, crosses beyond cessation and quiescence, attains non-retrogression, abides on the stage of the immovable, and shall reach the supreme, correct, and true path, attaining perfect awakening."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, from the time of the initial aspiration, attains the supreme, correct, and true path and perfect awakening, then turns the wheel of Dharma. For immeasurable, incalculable beings, one opens and guides them, bringing benefit and advantage. After that, one passes into the nirvāṇa-realm without remainder and enters parinirvāṇa. After parinirvāṇa, the Dharma then abides for one kalpa or longer.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, from the time of the initial aspiration, practices the prajñāpāramitā. Together with immeasurable koṭis of hundreds of thousands of bodhisattvas, one travels from one buddha-field to another, and wherever one is born, one adorns and purifies that buddha-field."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā attains the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables, and the formless attainments and delights in them. Having attained the first dhyāna, one rises from that dhyāna and enters the attainment of cessation (*nirodha-samāpatti*) in correct absorption. Rising from the attainment of cessation, one enters the fourth dhyāna and contemplates. Rising from the fourth dhyāna, one enters the attainment of cessation. Rising from the attainment of cessation, one enters the samāpatti of infinite space. Rising from the samāpatti of infinite space, one enters the attainment of cessation. Rising from the attainment of cessation, one enters the samāpatti of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Rising from the samāpatti of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, one enters the attainment of cessation. This, Śāriputra, is how a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, manifesting through skillful means the practice of samādhi and correct absorption."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who attains the four foundations of mindfulness, four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, the faculties, powers, factors of awakening, and the eightfold path, the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, does not attain the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, the fruit of arhatship, or the realization of a pratyekabuddha. Practicing the prajñāpāramitā through skillful means and the noble eightfold path, one converts beings and causes them to attain the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, the fruit of arhatship, and the realization of a pratyekabuddha."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The wisdom of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas is comparable to the bodhisattva's attainment of the patient endurance of dharmas. From this one knows that the bodhisattva is non-retrogressing in practicing the prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās and abides in the six pāramitās, dwelling in the Tuṣita Heaven and there completing all the emptinesses: such a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should be known to be among the bodhisattvas of the Fortunate Kalpa who shall accomplish buddhahood."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who attains the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables, the four formless attainments, the four foundations of mindfulness, four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, faculties, powers, factors of awakening, and the eightfold path, the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas: if there are any who practice the Buddha path, they will never establish themselves on the four noble truths. Such a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should be the one destined to fill the position in a single lifetime."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās travels from one buddha-world to another, universally visiting all lands. Wherever one goes, one converts beings and establishes them in the Buddha path. Such a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, after immeasurable, incalculable kalpas, shall attain the supreme, correct, and true path and perfect awakening."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who abides in the pāramitās constantly cultivates diligent effort for the sake of beings, never once giving rise to a thought, speaking a word, or performing an act that is without benefit."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās is constantly diligent in wishing to save beings, traveling from one buddha-field to another, converting the multitudes and causing them to cross beyond suffering, cutting off the three evil destinies of punishment."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who abides in the six pāramitās and practices the dāna-pāramitā, surrounded by a retinue, guides and directs beings toward eternal peace. To the hungry one gives food, to the thirsty one gives drink, to the unclothed one gives clothing, to those without incense one gives incense, mixed incense, and powdered incense, as well as bedding, servants, carriages, gold, silver, and the seven treasures. Whatever they seek for their livelihood, one never refuses, but fulfills their wishes."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā transforms one's own body to appear like a tathāgata. Entering the hells, one teaches the sūtras and the Dharma for those in the hells, as well as expounding the meaning for those in the animal and hungry ghost realms."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās transforms one's own body and mind to appear like a buddha-image, and traverses the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. For all beings, one teaches the sūtras and the Dharma, bows to the tathāgatas, and purifies one's buddha-field. Those who hear the teaching all generate the aspiration for the path. In this way, one universally goes to the buddha-worlds of all ten directions, surveys the buddha-fields, selects superior qualities to purify one's own land, and makes it wonderfully surpass others' buddha-fields in five respects. Such a bodhisattva-mahāsattva perfectly accomplishes the position of one destined to fill it in a single lifetime."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās at once becomes endowed with the thirty-two marks of a great person. With supreme and wondrous faculties, one thoroughly comprehends all things. Through this diligent effort of the faculties, innumerable people who see one feel respect and affection, and incalculable beings are caused to generate minds of joy. Gradually, those who have entered the three evil destinies are guided to attain cessation."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā should purify one's body, speech, and mind."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās, with supreme and wondrous faculties and an upright and dignified form, does not boast of oneself, does not speak of others' faults, constantly examines one's own errors, and does not contest others' shortcomings."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, from the time of the initial aspiration, practices the dāna-pāramitā and the śīla-pāramitā. Having established and abided in these two pāramitās, one attains immeasurable times beyond counting the supreme position of a universal wheel-turning sage-king. Wherever one abides as a wheel-turning king, one sees innumerable hundreds of thousands of buddhas, bows to them in reverence, and serves and makes offerings to the buddhas, World-Honored Ones."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who abides in the six pāramitās, for the sake of all beings, proclaims the radiance of the Dharma. Having illuminated oneself, one uses this Dharma-light without ever losing it, all the way to the supreme, correct, and true path and perfect awakening. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva greatly illuminates the buddha-dharmas. Therefore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā should constantly and diligently cultivate and guard one's body, speech, and mind, so that body, speech, and mind commit no offense."

The Venerable Śāriputra said: "Indeed, World-Honored One! Which bodhisattva-mahāsattva diligently cultivates all practices, guarding body, speech, and mind without committing any offense?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva thinks thus: 'This is what the body does, what the body creates and establishes. This is what speech does. This is what the mind does and establishes.' This is how a bodhisattva-mahāsattva guards body, speech, and mind. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā neither apprehends the body, nor apprehends speech, nor apprehends the mind. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā were to apprehend body, speech, and mind, and cling to body, speech, and mind, there would be a mind of covetous jealousy, a mind of transgressing the precepts, a mind of anger, a mind of laziness, a mind of agitation, and a mind of wrong views."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "One who practices thus should not be called a bodhisattva. It is a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās, purifies the flaws of body, purifies the flaws of speech, purifies the flaws of mind, making them without deficiency. This is what is called a bodhisattva."

The Venerable Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva purify the flaws of body, speech, and mind?"

The World-Honored One answered: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva does not apprehend the body, nor does one apprehend speech or mental thought."

The Buddha said: "Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva purifies body, speech, and mind. If body, speech, and mind have flaws and evil, that is for the sake of gain. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, from the time of the initial aspiration, practices the ten wholesome actions and their corresponding results, never generating the mind of a śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha, thus a bodhisattva-mahāsattva purifies and removes the flaws of body, speech, and mind."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā, wishing to seek the Buddha path, practices the dāna-pāramitā, the śīla-pāramitā, the kṣānti-pāramitā, the vīrya-pāramitā, and the dhyāna-pāramitā."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva seek the Buddha path?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva does not apprehend body, speech, mental thought, the dāna-pāramitā, the śīla-pāramitā, the kṣānti-pāramitā, the vīrya-pāramitā, the dhyāna-pāramitā, the prajñāpāramitā, the śrāvaka, the pratyekabuddha, the bodhisattva, or the Buddha path. Therefore the bodhisattva seeks the Buddha path: because in all dharmas there is nothing to be apprehended."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās reaches everywhere and yet reaches nowhere, and nothing can find an opening."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the six pāramitās reach everywhere and yet reach nowhere, so that nothing can find an opening?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the six pāramitās, one does not think of form, does not think of feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness; does not think of the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind; does not think of form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas; does not think of the eye, form, or eye-consciousness, the ear, sound, or ear-consciousness, the nose, smell, or nose-consciousness, the tongue, taste, or tongue-consciousness, the body, touch, or body-consciousness, the mind, dharmas, or mind-consciousness; does not think of the four foundations of mindfulness, four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, or the eightfold path; does not think of the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, or prajñāpāramitā; does not think of the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, or the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas; does not think of stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, arhatship, pratyekabuddha, the supreme, correct, and true path, or perfect awakening."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices thus is able to fully accomplish and increase the six pāramitās, reaching everywhere and yet reaching nowhere, so that nothing can find an opening."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who abide in the prajñāpāramitā and fully accomplish all the penetrative wisdoms. Through the meaning of what wisdom practices, one will never fall to ruin, will never be despised by others, will never be poor or destitute, and will never take on a defective body. The reason one's body is defective is that it destroys the gods, worldly people, and asuras."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "What is the wisdom of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through the wisdom one relies upon, sees the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones in the buddha-worlds to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. One hears the Dharma they teach and beholds the holy assemblies, sees the buddha-fields and their purifying dharmas. Yet a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through this wisdom, has no conception of a buddha, no conception of a bodhisattva, no conception of a śrāvaka, no conception of a pratyekabuddha, and does not conceive of a buddha-field through one's own wisdom. Why? A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the dāna-pāramitā without apprehending the dāna-pāramitā, practices the śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, and prajñāpāramitā without apprehending them. Through wisdom one attains the four foundations of mindfulness, four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold path, the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas. This is the wisdom of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva. Through this wisdom, one fully accomplishes the root of all dharmas, and regarding all dharmas one has no thought whatsoever."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through the prajñāpāramitā, purifies the five eyes. What are the five eyes? The fleshly eye, the heavenly eye, the wisdom eye, the Dharma eye, and the buddha eye."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "Indeed, World-Honored One! How does a bodhisattva purify the fleshly eye?" The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva with the fleshly eye sees four thousand *li*. Or there are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who with the fleshly eye see eight thousand *li*. There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who with the fleshly eye see the whole of Jambudvīpa. There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who with the fleshly eye see two Jambudvīpas. There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who with the fleshly eye see the four continents. There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who with the fleshly eye see a thousand-world system. There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who with the fleshly eye see two thousand world-systems. There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who with the fleshly eye see the entire trichiliocosm."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "This is how a bodhisattva-mahāsattva attains the purified fleshly eye."

Śāriputra further asked: "What is the purified heavenly eye of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The heavenly eye of all the gods in the Heaven of the Four Kings: the bodhisattva-mahāsattva knows them all. The eyes of the gods in the Trāyastriṃśa, Yāma, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, Paranirmitavaśavartin, up to the Akaniṣṭha heavens: the bodhisattva-mahāsattva knows them all. The bodhisattva's heavenly eye surpasses those from the Four Kings up to the Akaniṣṭha. With this heavenly eye, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva sees the buddha-worlds to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and knows the passing away and rebirth of all beings, extending to the buddha-worlds of all ten directions, seeing all beings' birth and death."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, this is the purified heavenly eye of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva."

Śāriputra further asked: "Indeed, World-Honored One! How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva purify the wisdom eye?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The wisdom eye of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva does not give rise to this thought: 'Dharmas have any existence, whether conditioned or unconditioned, with form or formless, worldly or transcendent, with outflows or without outflows.' The bodhisattva's wisdom eye beholds all dharmas; there is nothing not seen and heard, immeasurable and innumerable. This is the purified wisdom eye of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva."

Śāriputra further asked: "Indeed, World-Honored One! How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva purify the Dharma eye?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through the Dharma eye, makes this distinction: 'This one practices faith, this one practices the Dharma, this one practices emptiness, this one practices signlessness, this one practices wishlessness.' Through this gate of liberation, one attains the five faculties and attains the samādhi of non-apprehension. Having attained the samādhi of non-apprehension, one gives rise to the wisdom that leads to crossing beyond. Having attained this wisdom, one cuts off the three fetters. What are the three? First, attachment to the body; second, doubt; third, transgression of the precepts. These are the three fetters. Able to remove attachment to the body, free from doubt, and not transgressing the precepts, one is then without fetters. One without fetters is a stream-enterer. That person practices the path, and when lust, anger, and delusion have thinned, this is a once-returner. Through this same path, with additional diligence, when lust, anger, and delusion are cut off, this is a non-returner. Through this path, with further diligent practice, when subtle desire for form, desire for the formless, ignorance, and pride are all cut off, this is an arhat. This is one who practices emptiness. Through the gate of emptiness-liberation, one attains the five faculties and attains the samādhi of non-apprehension. Through the samādhi of non-apprehension, one gives rise to the wisdom that leads to crossing beyond and attains the realization of a pratyekabuddha. Furthermore, this person, through the gate of signlessness-liberation, attains the five faculties. In brief, up to the attainment of arhatship. This is the purified Dharma eye of the bodhisattva. If the bodhisattva can discern and understand that all dharmas subject to combination ultimately return to extinction, and sees the exhaustion of all dharmas and attains the five faculties, this is the purified Dharma eye of the bodhisattva.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva discerns thus: 'This is a bodhisattva of the initial aspiration, practicing the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, prajñāpāramitā, and the faculties of faith and diligence. The practices of the faculties are fully endowed with skillful means. One constantly establishes oneself in the root of wholesome virtues. This bodhisattva is born among nobles, persons of good family and clan, brahmins, and great elders, or in the Heaven of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa, Yāma, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, or Paranirmitavaśavartin heavens. Born in those heavens, wherever one abides, one converts beings, causing all sentient creatures to enter into peaceful practice, purifying the buddha-field, encountering the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones, making offerings and serving them, never falling to the stages of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas. Such a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has retrogressed; such a one has not retrogressed, and shall reach the supreme, correct, and true path, attaining perfect awakening.' This is the purified Dharma eye of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva discerns thus: 'Such a bodhisattva has already received a prediction and shall attain the supreme path and perfect awakening. Such a bodhisattva has not yet received a prediction for the supreme, correct, and true path. Such a bodhisattva has established nothing. Such a bodhisattva is non-retrogressing; such a one is not non-retrogressing. Such a bodhisattva's supernatural powers are complete; such a one's are not yet complete. Such a bodhisattva with complete supernatural powers goes to the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, bowing in reverence to the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones, making offerings and serving them. Such a bodhisattva has not yet attained supernatural powers; such a one will attain them. Such a bodhisattva's buddha-field shall be purified; such a one's shall not be purified. Such a bodhisattva shall convert beings; such a one shall not convert them. Such a bodhisattva is praised by the buddhas, World-Honored Ones. Such a bodhisattva shall have the buddhas stand closely before them; such a one shall not have the buddhas appear before them. Such a bodhisattva's lifespan shall be limited; such a one's lifespan shall be immeasurable. Such a bodhisattva's bhikṣu assembly shall be limited; such a one's shall be without limit. Such a bodhisattva, upon attaining the supreme, correct, and true path and perfect awakening, shall have bodhisattvas as the saṃgha. Such a bodhisattva, upon becoming a buddha, shall have no bodhisattva assembly. Such a bodhisattva shall accomplish it through arduous practice; such a one shall accomplish it through peaceful practice. Such a bodhisattva shall bring transmigration to its ultimate end; such a one shall not bring it to its ultimate end. Such a bodhisattva shall sit beneath the bodhi-tree; such a one shall not sit beneath the bodhi-tree. Such a bodhisattva shall be tested by Māra; such a one shall not be tested by Māra.' Such is the bodhisattva. Śāriputra, this is the purified Dharma eye of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva purify the buddha eye?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through the cause of generating the aspiration for the supreme path and the correct absorption of the vajra-like samādhi (*vajropama-samādhi*), fully accomplishes all the penetrative wisdoms: the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, great compassion and great loving-kindness. When the eye of the bodhisattva-mahāsattva universally penetrates all buddha-dharmas, there is nothing unseen, nothing unheard, nothing unmeasured, nothing not penetrated. This, Śāriputra, is when a bodhisattva-mahāsattva attains the supreme, correct, and true path and perfect awakening, and only then can one fully accomplish the purified buddha eye."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Thus a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who wishes to attain the five eyes should practice the six pāramitās. Why? The six pāramitās encompass all wholesome dharmas and completely comprehend the dharmas of śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and bodhisattvas. Therefore, Śāriputra, one who attains the mind of equality and reaches this practice thereby gathers and embraces all dharmas. One should contemplate the prajñāpāramitā, for the prajñāpāramitā is the mother of the five eyes. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who studies these five eyes thereby attains the supreme, correct, and true path and perfect awakening."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who cultivate supernatural powers as a pāramitā. Through immeasurable causes and conditions of supernatural powers, abiding on this stage, with a single body one transforms into countless forms, and returns again to a single body. Through walls, barriers, partitions, mountains, and lofty peaks, one passes through without obstruction, as if through empty space where water courses and clouds form. Like a bird flying through the sky, one enters and emerges from the ground, emerging without openings, entering without gaps, as if entering water. One walks upon water as upon solid ground. The body emits blazing light like a great fire. The sun and moon, though of great radiant power, can hardly match it. With one's hand-palm one touches the sun and moon, grasping their radiance, freely and at will. The body reaches to the Brahmā heaven. One does not become arrogant because of supernatural powers; the mind has no pride and no thought whatsoever. One's supernatural powers have nothing to be apprehended and are without conceit. Arising, there is no conception, no thought. What arises is natural emptiness; natural emptiness is quiescence. The natural itself is without any arising. Furthermore, being thus, one does not generate supernatural powers or the practice of supernatural powers; one only focuses thought on the affairs of penetrative wisdom. This is the bodhisattva's attainment through the prajñāpāramitā of the supernatural-power attestation-wisdom."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva purifies the heavenly ear, surpassing the ears of gods and humans, and hears every single sound, the voices of gods and humans. Yet one does not think of the nature of the heavenly ear, nor does one think: 'I hear their sounds.' There is nothing to be apprehended. Natural emptiness is naturally quiescent; the natural itself is without arising, without apprehension, without thought. One also does not think: 'I have attained the heavenly ear.' One only focuses the mind on the affairs of penetrative wisdom. This is how a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā: the attainment of the heavenly-ear attestation-wisdom supernatural power."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva knows the thoughts of other beings' minds, both truthful and false, and their inclinations. Whether with desire or without desire, with desire-conceptions or without; with anger or anger-conceptions, free from anger or anger-conceptions; with delusion or delusion-conceptions, free from delusion or delusion-conceptions; with attachment or free from attachment; receiving or not receiving; exalted or lowly; with a violent mind or a peaceful mind; with a great mind or a small mind; with a concentrated mind or an unconcentrated mind; with a liberated mind or an unliberated mind; with a defiled mind or one about to be defiled; with defiled conceptions or about to have defiled conceptions. Clearly discerning and distinguishing truth and falsehood, with a supreme mind, thinking of the supreme, one also has no thought and no conception. Why? All minds are without mind; the arising of all minds is without thought. Then the wisdom of recollecting past abodes is what one attests to. The supernatural power of traversing past dwellings is what one attests to, and one uses the supernatural powers to direct this. With a single thought one recalls the affairs of a hundred days, a hundred months, a hundred years, a kalpa, a hundred kalpas, immeasurable kalpas, innumerable hundreds of kalpas, innumerable thousands of kalpas, immeasurable koṭis of hundreds of thousands. One recalls them all: the place where one formerly dwelt, one's name, one's clan, the manner of one's birth, one's food and drink, the duration of one's stay, one's lifespan long or short, suffering or happiness, good or evil. From that place one passed away and was born in such-and-such a place, in such a group, with such a birth. What was spoken there was thus. One can recall immeasurable past dwellings, yet one does not conceive of the supernatural powers one has attained."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the attestation-wisdom of the supernatural power of recollecting past dwellings. This is the practice of the supernatural-power wisdom."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva, with the heavenly eye, sees beings' birth and death, their passing and arising, their good roots and evil roots, their fortune and misfortune, good and evil, their going to peace or going to suffering, the subtle and the flawed, all according to their deeds, and thoroughly knows them. 'These pitiable ones have performed evil deeds of body, evil speech, evil thoughts, complete in evil conduct, slandering the noble sages and holding to wrong views. Because of these conditions, when their body perishes and their life ends, they go toward suffering and fall into the hells. These benevolent and holy beings have performed good deeds of body, good speech, good thoughts, complete in good conduct, not slandering the noble sages and following right views. Because of these conditions, when their body perishes and their life ends, they go toward peace and are born in the heavens.' One sees throughout the eight directions, above and below. The supernatural powers having been fully attained, it is all thus: one sees throughout the ten directions without any obstruction."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva attains the knowledge of others' minds. In one day, a hundred days, one year, a hundred years, one kalpa, a hundred kalpas, a thousand kalpas, ten thousand kalpas, a hundred million kalpas, immeasurable kalpas, immeasurable billions of hundreds of thousands of kalpas, extending to the limitless. In the buddha-fields of the ten directions, what is thought is immeasurable, beyond counting, and the mind has no obstruction. This is the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's attestation-wisdom of the supernatural power of knowing others' minds and past dwellings. This is the practice of the supernatural-power wisdom."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva knows one's own body and from where it has come. One life, a hundred lives, a thousand lives, ten thousand, a hundred million lives, immeasurable lives, one kalpa, a hundred kalpas, a thousand kalpas, ten thousand kalpas, a hundred million kalpas, innumerable billions of kalpas: good and evil, fortune and misfortune, the good and evil one has gone toward, parents, brothers, family, wife, and children, power and nobility, wealth and happiness, poverty and distress, foolishness and wisdom, poverty and success, names, clans. This is the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's attestation-wisdom of the supernatural power of knowing the body's origin and past dwellings. The supernatural-power wisdom-practice, the five paths, natural clairvoyance: one sees throughout the ten directions without any obstruction."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva possesses the wisdom of the exhaustion of outflows as the attestation of supernatural powers. One does not fall to the stages of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas, nor does one conceive of any different dharma, nor does one think: 'I have attained the supreme, correct, and true path and perfect awakening.' Nor does one take the wisdom of the exhaustion of outflows or the wisdom of supernatural powers as grounds for pride or conceit. If one has the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, one comprehends the twelve links of dependent arising as without root and the thirty-seven factors of awakening as without beginning, and converts all. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā fully accomplishes the supernatural powers. Having fully accomplished them, one thereby increases and attains the supreme, correct, and true path and perfect awakening."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the prajñāpāramitā and abide in the dāna-pāramitā. Seeing the ability to adorn and purify the paths of penetrative wisdom and the ultimate true emptiness, from this they arise and receive, and practice benevolent deeds."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the prajñāpāramitā and abide in the śīla-pāramitā, fully adorning and purifying the paths of penetrative wisdom and the ultimate emptiness. Without belief or disbelief, without arising or non-arising, one establishes meaning and is forever without arising."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattvas who practice the prajñāpāramitā and abide in the kṣānti-pāramitā, fully adorning and purifying the paths of penetrative wisdom and the ultimate true emptiness. One gives rise to patient endurance without any resentment, comprehending that there is no root, and all is empty."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the prajñāpāramitā and abide in the vīrya-pāramitā, fully adorning and purifying the paths of penetrative wisdom and the ultimate true emptiness. Body and mind are diligent, focused upon a single meaning. What diligent effort practices is without any outflows, and one establishes this effort."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the prajñāpāramitā and abide in the dhyāna-pāramitā, fully adorning and purifying the paths of penetrative wisdom and the ultimate true emptiness. The mind is undisturbed, neither exalted nor lowly, neither arising nor ceasing, and one establishes this meditation."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice the prajñāpāramitā and abide in the prajñāpāramitā, fully adorning and purifying the paths of penetrative wisdom and the ultimate true emptiness. One removes the mind of wrong views, encourages the unwise, and gives rise to that which has nothing to give."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Thus a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā and abides in the six pāramitās, fully adorning and purifying the paths of penetrative wisdom and the ultimate true emptiness: coming or not coming, there is no going or coming. One gives without receiving, without giving and without miserliness, without precepts and without transgression, without patience and without anger, without effort and without laziness, without meditation and without agitation, without wisdom and without delusion. Whatever one gives, one has no thought and no conception of giving or miserliness, keeping precepts or breaking precepts, patience or anger, effort or laziness, concentration or distraction, wisdom or delusion. One does not think of praise or blame; one does not conceive of conditioned things; there is no arising, no anger, no blame. One does not conceive of anything spoken, nor does one think of existence or nonexistence."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, this is how a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā attains fame and renown that śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas cannot match. Having equipped oneself with these virtues, one converts beings, adorns and purifies buddha-fields, practices great compassion, and attains all the paths of penetrative wisdom."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through the prajñāpāramitā, constantly directs an equal mind toward all beings. Having directed an equal mind toward all beings, one thereby attains equality regarding all dharmas. Having attained equality regarding all dharmas, one thereby establishes beings in equality. All dharmas are then present at once; one is then loved and revered by the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and revered and honored by all bodhisattvas, śrāvakas, and pratyekabuddhas. Furthermore, wherever one is born, the eyes never see anything disagreeable, the ears do not hear unpleasant sounds, the nose does not smell foul odors, the mouth has no bitter taste, the body feels nothing coarse or harsh, and the mind encounters no unwholesome dharmas. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus will never lose the supreme, correct, and true path."

When the Buddha taught this chapter on the prajñāpāramitā, three hundred bhikṣus who were all practitioners took their necklaces and scattered them upon the Buddha, generating the aspiration for the supreme, correct, and true path. At that time the Buddha smiled. The Venerable Ānanda immediately rose from his seat, adjusted his robes, knelt on his right knee, placed his palms together, and addressed the Buddha: "For what cause and condition do you smile? If you smile, there must be a reason."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "These three hundred bhikṣus, after sixty-one kalpas, shall accomplish buddhahood, and their name shall be Great Hero Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, Accomplished in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Tamer, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. When they pass away from here, they shall be born in the land of Akṣobhya Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. Sixty thousand desire-realm gods, at the time of Maitreya Buddha, shall go forth as śramaṇas. Through the Buddha's holy command, in that age they shall each see a thousand buddhas. Their practices shall be among beings, and in the eight directions, above and below, it shall be likewise: each shall see a thousand buddhas and their lands. Furthermore, they shall also see this Endurance World adorned, purified, and without flaw, like the worlds of those buddhas, tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones. At that time, ten thousand people shall each make a vow: 'We shall each generate the aspiration to be present in the buddha-fields of the present buddhas.' The Buddha immediately knew the thoughts in the minds of those good men and smiled again."

Ānanda knelt again and asked the Buddha: "For what cause and condition do you smile? Your smile must have a meaning."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Did you see these ten thousand people establish their vows?"

He answered: "Indeed, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said: "These ten thousand people, when their lives here end, shall be born in buddha-fields, never far from the buddhas and tathāgatas. Afterward they shall become buddhas, named Adorned Purity Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One."

### Chapter 4: Praise of Equality

Thereupon the Venerable Śāriputra, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, Mahākāśyapa, and the rest, together with innumerable other bhikṣus of holy, penetrative, and luminous wisdom, as well as bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, male and female lay-followers, all addressed the Buddha: "Indeed, World-Honored One! Such practice is the great pāramitā of the bodhisattva-mahāsattva, subtle and wondrous, that none can overcome: the supreme pāramitā, that none can surpass; the most honored pāramitā, powerful and renowned, that none can equal; the unsurpassed pāramitā, that none can exceed; the immeasurable pāramitā, that none can transcend; the unmatched pāramitā of the bodhisattva-mahāsattva, the peerless pāramitā, the emptiness that is without limit. Indeed, World-Honored One! This is the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's pāramitā that is already characterized by emptiness; the signless pāramitā that is without thought; the wishless pāramitā that is without attachment. All dharmas are empty, naturally without any existence. The pāramitā of emptiness is naturally empty, and the pāramitā of all virtues complete is without limit. Indeed, World-Honored One! The prajñāpāramitā of all virtues of the bodhisattva-mahāsattva is beyond compare; the pāramitā of non-practice is precisely the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's pāramitā. Through this, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva can equal the unequaled. If one can fully accomplish the pāramitā of equaling the unequaled, one can reach and then return to equality; having attained equality with the unequaled, one reaches anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. The śīla-pāramitā can also bring one to the equality of the unequaled, as can the kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, and prajñāpāramitā. Just as the bodhisattva should practice the dāna-pāramitā in what is appropriate. God among Gods! It is not merely the prajñāpāramitā that can attain equality with the unequaled. Through the true Dharma, with regard to form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, if one turns the wheel of Dharma, equality is without equal. The buddhas, gods among gods, of the past, present, and future have all practiced the prajñāpāramitā and turned the wheel of Dharma that equals the unequaled. Therefore, World-Honored One, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishing to cross beyond the surface of all dharmas should practice the prajñāpāramitā. The bodhisattva-mahāsattva should be honored, and the gods, humans, and asuras should all bow in respect to one who practices the prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha then addressed the immeasurable assembly of śrāvakas and bodhisattva-mahāsattvas: "So it is, so it is! Good men! You should bow to the bodhisattva-mahāsattva. The gods, humans, and asuras, if any practice the prajñāpāramitā, should all come and pay homage."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva comes to appear in the world and manifests in the human realm, or in the heavens, one appears as a noble, a person of good family and clan, a brahmin, or a great elder, or as a wheel-turning sage-king, or in the Heaven of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa, Yāma, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, or Paranirmitavaśavartin heavens, or the Akaniṣṭha Heaven. One appears as a stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, or arhat appearing in the world, or a pratyekabuddha, or a tathāgata, worthy one, perfectly awakened one appearing in the world."

The Buddha said: "Therefore, Śāriputra, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva comes to appear in the world. One is able to obtain and provide food, drink, clothing, bedding, dwellings, lamps, luminous-moon pearls, crystals, lapis lazuli, jade, gold, silver, coral, amber, giant clam shells, and agate, giving them to all beings."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "It is not merely for this that one appears in the world. By upholding these words, one rescues and protects the world and brings it peace. All the music and arts at gatherings of gods and humans are what the bodhisattva-mahāsattva has brought about and caused to appear. Why? When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the six pāramitās and abides in the six pāramitās, wishing to encourage beings to practice giving, one first practices giving oneself. Keeping precepts, patience, effort, concentration, and wisdom are likewise: one encourages the multitudes to practice the prajñāpāramitā. Therefore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva brings peace to all types of beings."

### Chapter 5: Bestowing Predictions

Thereupon the World-Honored One extended his tongue-base to cover the trichiliocosm. From his tongue-base, immeasurable rays of light shone forth, illuminating the buddha-worlds to the east. At that time, in the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, innumerable, incalculable bodhisattva-mahāsattvas saw the light. Each, in their own buddha-land, went to inquire of the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and asked: "God among Gods! By what supernatural power does this light universally illuminate our land?" At that time, the tathāgatas of those lands each told the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas: "Good men! You should know this manifestation. To the west, past as many buddha-worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, there is a buddha-land called the Endurance World. Its buddha is named Śākyamuni Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. He has extended his tongue-base, and its radiance illuminates the buddha-worlds to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, universally shining throughout the ten directions. He does this because he is teaching the prajñāpāramitā for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas." Then the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas of those lands each addressed their buddhas: "We wish to go there to bow to Śākyamuni Tathāgata and to see the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas and hear the prajñāpāramitā." Their buddhas said: "Go, good men! Choose your time, as your wish dictates." The bodhisattvas, having received permission, each took canopies, banners, streamers, incense, flowers, adornments, mixed incense, and powdered incense, and golden and silver flowers, and went to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. They bowed at the Buddha's feet and, taking what they had brought, including canopies, banners, streamers, incense, flowers, adornments, mixed incense, powdered incense, and golden and silver flowers, scattered them upon the Buddha, the bodhisattvas, and the śrāvaka assembly.

In the eight directions, above and below, likewise innumerable, incalculable bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, each in their own land, addressed their World-Honored Ones: "What is the source of this radiance?" The buddhas replied: "There is a buddha named Śākyamuni Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. He has extended his tongue-base, and its radiance illuminates in all ten directions as many buddha-fields as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. This is the source of the radiance." The bodhisattvas each addressed their buddhas: "We wish to go and bow to Śākyamuni and see the bodhisattvas." The buddhas said: "Go, sons of good family! Choose your time, as your wish dictates." The assembly of bodhisattvas each brought offerings and went to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, bowed in reverence, presented their offerings, and sat to one side to hear what the Buddha taught.

At that time the gods of the Heaven of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa, Yāma, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, and Paranirmitavaśavartin heavens, up to the Akaniṣṭha, each brought heavenly flowers, heavenly incense, heavenly powdered incense, heavenly mixed incense, blue lotuses, red lotuses, yellow lotuses, and white lotuses, all with the finest heavenly fragrant flowers, and went to the Buddha. At that time the gods and bodhisattvas each brought incense, flowers, mixed incense, and powdered incense, and each made offerings, scattering them upon the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. The scattered flowers and incense rose up into the air and transformed into palaces in the four directions, floating in space facing all four sides, subtle and radiant, all made of various treasures delightful to all. The billions of hundreds of thousands in the assembly all placed their palms together, took refuge in the Buddha, and asked the World-Honored One: "God among Gods! In future ages, we wish to attain the benefit of the Dharma just as it is now, just as the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones, with their śrāvaka assemblies, teach the sūtras and the Dharma for the assembly, just as it is presently expounded."

At that time the World-Honored One knew the thoughts of those good men's minds: that they saw all dharmas as forever without arising and without practice, that all dharmas have nothing to be attained, and that they had attained the patient endurance of seeing the mind. The Buddha then smiled. The Venerable Ānanda stepped forward and addressed the Buddha: "For what cause does the Buddha smile? Your smile must have a meaning."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The billions of hundreds of thousands of people now in this assembly have all attained the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas (*anutpattikadharmakṣānti*). After sixty-eight billion kalpas, they shall become buddhas named Awakening Flower Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, Accomplished in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Tamer, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. The kalpa shall be named Flower Deeds, and the world shall be called Adorned Flower."

### Chapter 6: Distinguishing Emptiness

At that time the Buddha addressed the Venerable Subhūti: "Are you not capable of expounding the prajñāpāramitā for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, the conditions through which bodhisattva-mahāsattvas are born?"

Thereupon the assembly of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, śrāvakas, gods, and humans each thought to themselves: "Will Subhūti now, through his own eloquence, teach the prajñāpāramitā for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas? Or will he teach through the Buddha's holy authority?"

Subhūti then knew the thoughts of the assembly of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, great śrāvakas, gods, and humans, and said to the Venerable Śāriputra: "Whatever the Buddha's disciples say and expound as the radiance of discernment, all of it is borne by the Tathāgata's awesome virtue. Whatever the Tathāgata has taught, all those dharmas are fundamentally without contention. Good men should study these dharmas, and those who attest to the Dharma shall all accord with the Tathāgata's sphere of wisdom-attestation. The bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who teach the prajñāpāramitā are not in the sphere of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas. Why? When the Dharma is taught at the right time, all rejoice."

Śāriputra said to Subhūti: "This is what is meant by 'bodhisattva.'"

Thereupon Subhūti addressed the World-Honored One: "What is called 'bodhisattva,' God among Gods? What is the meaning of 'bodhisattva' in this Dharma? I can never find any dharma of 'bodhisattva'; what then is a bodhisattva? God among Gods! I can never find the prajñāpāramitā or a bodhisattva. How then shall I teach the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's prajñāpāramitā? By what meaning shall I expound the prajñāpāramitā for the bodhisattvas and guide them?"

The Buddha told the Venerable Subhūti: "What are called 'prajñāpāramitā' and 'bodhisattva' are merely conventional designations. Those names are without names. Those names are not within, not without, and not in between. It is like the word 'person,' Subhūti: it is merely a conventional designation, and that too is without a name. Those dharmas do not arise and do not cease. Through the combination of conditions and following worldly convention, names are given. They are merely sounds and words referring to a self, a person, a lifespan, a life-force, things that creep and crawl and breathe, that wriggle and fly and squirm. Whatever is done and created, whatever is habituated and undergone, seen and known, observed and perceived: all these are dharmas of conventional designation, all of which do not arise and do not cease. What gods and humans express and teach is likewise. Thus, Subhūti, the prajñāpāramitā and the name 'bodhisattva' are all conventional designations; they all do not arise and do not cease. Even the names spoken by the gods among gods are the same, without any difference. It is like, Subhūti, the things within the body that are given the name 'my form.' This too is a dharma of conventional designation, a name given by convention. The dharma of conventional designation does not arise and does not cease. If one wishes to comprehend this combination of conditions, there are merely sounds. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise dharmas of conventional designation. The names given to those dharmas do not arise and do not cease; they are conventional manifestations through conditions, and there are merely sounds. Thus, Subhūti, what is called 'prajñāpāramitā,' the word 'bodhisattva' and the name 'bodhisattva,' are all conventional. Those dharmas do not arise and do not cease. The prajñāpāramitā, the word 'bodhisattva,' and the name 'bodhisattva' have no other support; through conditions and conventional designation there come to be these words. The eye is indistinct and empty; up to the conventional name, the dharma is a given designation, and all of them do not arise and do not cease. Through conditions and conventional designation there come to be sounds. What is called 'the emptiness of the eye': the eye is not within, not without, and not in between. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind are likewise; they are dharmas of conventional designation. Those dharmas do not arise and do not cease; through conditions they are combined and named, and there come to be sounds. What is called 'mind': the mind is not within, not without, and not in between. What is called 'form,' Subhūti, is a dharma of conventional designation. It does not arise and does not cease; it is not within, not without, and not in between. What is called the 'eye-element' is a conventional designation, a name given by convention. The eye-element, form-element, and eye-consciousness-element; the ear-element, sound-element, and ear-consciousness-element; the nose-element, smell-element, and nose-consciousness-element; the tongue-element, taste-element, and tongue-consciousness-element; the body-element, touch-element, and body-consciousness-element; the mind-element, dharma-element, and mind-consciousness-element: these are dharmas of borrowed designation, and there come to be sounds. Those dharmas are not within, not without, and not in between. Thus, Subhūti, what are called 'bodhisattva' and 'prajñāpāramitā' are dharmas of conventional designation. Those designations do not arise and do not cease; they are conventionally named, and there come to be sounds. The prajñāpāramitā and the designation 'bodhisattva': those names are not within, not without, and not in between. It is like, Subhūti, what is within the body is conventionally called 'my body,' and by the designation 'head.' That name is merely a sound. Furthermore, one names the neck and throat, the five aggregates, the two arms, the back, the navel, the sides, the two knees, and the two feet: these are merely conventional designations, given by sound. The designation 'head' is a conventional name by conditions, and reckoning that dharma, it does not arise and does not cease. Reckoning all these, they are all conventional designations with sounds established. Reckoning those names, they do not arise and do not cease; they are not within, not without, and not in between. Thus, Subhūti, the prajñāpāramitā and the name 'bodhisattva' are all conventional designations. Those dharmas do not arise and do not cease; they are all conventional, with sounds established. Those names also do not arise and do not cease; they are not within, not without, and not in between. It is like, Subhūti, the external things such as grass, trees, branches, leaves, flowers, and fruit: reckoning all these, they are all conventional designations with sounds. Those names are without names; those names do not arise and do not cease. They are conventionally named, with sounds established. Reckoning those names, they are not within, not without, and not in between. Thus, Subhūti, the prajñāpāramitā and the name 'bodhisattva' are all dharmas of conventional designation by conditions. Those dharmas do not arise and do not cease; those names are not within, not without, and not in between. It is like, Subhūti, the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, of the past have all had their names transmitted by convention. Those of the future and present are likewise. It is like, Subhūti, the echo of a voice, the reflection in a mirror, an illusion, a magical transformation, a mirage. The Tathāgata teaches that all dharmas are like magical transformations: they are merely conventional designations. Those designations do not arise and do not cease; they are conventionally named with sounds established. Those names are not within, not without, and not in between. Thus, Subhūti, what are called 'bodhisattva' and 'prajñāpāramitā' are merely conventional designations. Those designations do not arise and do not cease; they are not within, not without, and not in between. Thus, Subhūti, what are called 'bodhisattva-mahāsattva' and 'prajñāpāramitā,' through the combination of conditions, are conventionally given empty designations. The designation is a skillful means; the designation is a dharma: all are merely conventional. One should study and practice the prajñāpāramitā thus: not abiding in name-and-form, not seeing anything; not abiding in feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness; not abiding in names; not seeing name-and-form as permanent; not seeing name-and-form as pleasurable; not seeing name-and-form as suffering; not seeing name-and-form as having interiority; not seeing name-and-form as having exteriority; not seeing name-and-form as empty; not seeing name-and-form as undergoing signless transformation; not seeing name-and-form as involving wishlessness; not seeing name-and-form as quiescent; not seeing name-and-form as indistinct; not seeing name-and-form as having desire-dust; not seeing name-and-form as involving contention; not seeing name-and-form as arising; not seeing name-and-form as ceasing. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise. The eye-element, form-element, and eye-consciousness-element; the ear-element, sound-element, and ear-consciousness-element; the nose-element, smell-element, and nose-consciousness-element; the tongue-element, taste-element, and tongue-consciousness-element; the body-element, touch-element, and body-consciousness-element; the mind-element, dharma-element, and mind-consciousness-element: all are the same, formed through the combination of conditions as conventional designations, constituting the five aggregates. Why? A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā and the bodhisattva-practice, together with the designations, are neither existent nor nonexistent, neither abiding in the conditioned realm nor the unconditioned realm, and one sees nothing at all. Why? Subhūti, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā has no thought whatsoever regarding any dharma. There is no accordance or non-accordance; one also does not conceive of practicing the prajñāpāramitā. Abiding in the dharma of non-conception, one cultivates all the foundations of mindfulness. Practicing the prajñāpāramitā, one does not see the prajñāpāramitā, does not see the name of the prajñāpāramitā, does not see the bodhisattva, and does not see the name of the bodhisattva. Who sees the bodhisattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā? A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who clearly discerns the root and characteristics of all dharmas, reckoning the root and characteristics of all dharmas, has no attachment and no contention. Thus, Subhūti, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā does not rely on conventional designation to create names for dharmas. Regarding the Buddha path as well, one does not conventionally designate or rely on borrowed names, but thoroughly comprehends all of this. One does not rely on form, does not rely on feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness; does not rely on the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind. One does not rely on eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, or mind-consciousness. One does not rely on the eye's contact with the five aggregates and has nothing to rely upon. Without arising feeling, without suffering, pleasure, or neither-suffering-nor-pleasure, up to the conditions and contacts of mind-consciousness that give rise to feeling, whether suffering, pleasure, or neither-suffering-nor-pleasure: regarding all of these, one has nothing whatsoever to rely upon. One does not rely on or cling to the conditioned realm, does not rely on or cling to the unconditioned realm, does not rely on or cling to the dāna-pāramitā, does not rely on or cling to the śīla-pāramitā, the kṣānti-pāramitā, the vīrya-pāramitā, the dhyāna-pāramitā, or the prajñāpāramitā. One does not rely on or cling to characteristics, does not rely on or cling to the bodhisattva's body. One does not rely on or cling to the fleshly eye, does not rely on or cling to the heavenly eye, wisdom eye, Dharma eye, or buddha eye. One does not rely on or cling to the prajñāpāramitā, does not rely on or cling to the supernatural powers of mental resolve. One does not rely on or cling to interior or exterior, does not rely on or cling to what is in between. One does not rely on or cling to internal emptiness, external emptiness, formless conditions, natural emptiness, conversion of beings, purification of buddha-fields, or skillful means. Why? Because all dharmas are completely without any existence. Whatever one should cling to, there is nothing to cling to, and nothing whatsoever that can be grasped. Thus, Subhūti, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has no attachment to any dharma thereby practices the prajñāpāramitā. The dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, and prajñāpāramitā thereby increase. Entering quiescence, one attains the bodhisattva path, enters the stage of non-retrogression, and the supernatural powers become complete. When the supernatural powers are complete, one travels to the buddha-fields, converting beings. Having converted beings, one makes offerings to the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and adorns and purifies the buddha-fields. Having adorned and purified the buddha-fields, the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, all see the bodhisattva. At that time the bodhisattva also sees the buddhas, great sages, from afar. Wishing to attain the root of merit and virtue, one then makes offerings to the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and pays homage. One then attains the natural, immeasurable virtue of drawing near to the buddhas, and from them hears the sūtras they teach. Having attained hearing of the Dharma, it is never interrupted, and one attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening, together with the gates of dhāraṇī and the gates of samādhi. Thus, Subhūti, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā-practice clearly comprehends that all dharmas are conventional designations through conditions. What do you think, Subhūti? Is form the bodhisattva? Are feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness the bodhisattva?"

Subhūti answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is the eye the bodhisattva? Are the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Are form, sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

He further asked: "What do you think, Subhūti? Is the eye-consciousness the bodhisattva? Are the ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, and mind-consciousness the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is the earth-element the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Are the water-element, fire-element, wind-element, space-element, and consciousness-element the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is ignorance the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Are consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, craving, feeling, grasping, becoming, birth, old age, sickness, and death the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is there anything different from form that is the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Is there anything different from feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness that is the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Is there anything different from the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind that is the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Is there anything different from form, sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas that is the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Is there anything different from eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, and mind-consciousness that is the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Is there anything different from the twelve links from ignorance to old age, sickness, and death that is the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is the rootlessness of form the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Is the rootlessness of the five aggregates, six sense-objects, eighteen elements, four great elements, and twelve links of dependent arising the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is there anything different from rootlessness that is the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "What do you think, Subhūti? What meaning do you understand and by what observation do you answer?"

The Buddha said: "Form is not the bodhisattva; feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are not the bodhisattva. The six sense-objects, eighteen elements, four great elements, and the twelve links from ignorance to old age, sickness, and death are not the bodhisattva. Their rootlessness is not the bodhisattva, and what is different from rootlessness is also not the bodhisattva."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "God among Gods! A self, a person, and a lifespan are also unobtainable. How then can one name a bodhisattva? How can one name the five aggregates, six sense-objects, eighteen elements, four great elements, and the suffering of transmigration through the twelve links as the bodhisattva? How can one name what is different from form, what is different from feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness as the bodhisattva? How can one name what is different from the six sense-objects, eighteen elements, four great elements, and twelve links of dependent arising as the bodhisattva? How can one name rootlessness as the bodhisattva? How can one name what is different from rootlessness as the bodhisattva? There is no such place."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! Subhūti, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva studying the prajñāpāramitā should contemplate that beings and persons are without any existence and unobtainable, and that the prajñāpāramitā is likewise without any existence and unobtainable. The bodhisattva should study in this way. What do you think, Subhūti? Is what the mouth says about form the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Is what the mouth says about feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"Is reckoning form as permanent, reckoning feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness as permanent, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is reckoning form as impermanent, reckoning feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness as impermanent, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is saying that form is pleasurable, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are pleasurable, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is saying that form is suffering, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are suffering, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is saying that form is mine, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are mine, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is saying that form is not mine, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are not mine, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is saying that form is empty, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are empty, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is saying that form is signless, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are signless, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is saying that form is wishless, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are wishless, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Is saying that form is not empty, not signless, not wishless, and that feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are not empty, not signless, not wishless, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

"What do you think, Subhūti? Are the five aggregates, six sense-objects, eighteen elements, four great elements, and twelve links of dependent arising, as being without any existence, the bodhisattva?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods!"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "By what meaning do you contend that what the mouth says about the five aggregates, six sense-objects, eighteen elements, four great elements, and twelve links of dependent arising, their suffering and pleasure, good and evil, emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, existence and nonexistence, are all not the bodhisattva?"

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "God among Gods! Ultimately seeking form, it is utterly unobtainable. How much less can what the mouth says about form be the bodhisattva! Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise: ultimately seeking permanence, it is unobtainable. How much less can impermanence be obtained and be the bodhisattva! Ultimately, suffering and pleasure are unobtainable. How much less can saying 'suffering and pleasure' be the bodhisattva! Ultimately seeking 'mine,' it is unobtainable. How much less can saying 'mine or not mine' be the bodhisattva! Form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise. Ultimately, the existence of form is unobtainable. How much less can saying 'form is empty' be the bodhisattva! Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise. Ultimately seeking conceptions, they are unobtainable. How much less can saying 'form is signless' be the bodhisattva! Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise. Ultimately seeking wishes, they are unobtainable. How much less can saying 'form is wishless' be the bodhisattva! Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! Subhūti, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishing to study the prajñāpāramitā should study in this way. When what the mouth says about form is unobtainable, and feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness are unobtainable, then one is studying the prajñāpāramitā. As for what you said earlier, Subhūti, 'I can never find in the Dharma anything that is the bodhisattva': Subhūti, you should know that dharmas cannot see dharmas; the dharma-realm cannot see dharmas. The form-realm, the feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness realms are likewise: the form-realm does not see the dharma-realm, the dharma-realm does not see the form-realm. The eye-realm does not see the dharma-realm, the dharma-realm does not see the eye-realm. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind are likewise. The mind-realm does not see the dharma-realm, the dharma-realm does not see the mind-realm. The eighteen element-realms do not see the dharma-realm, the dharma-realm does not see the eighteen element-realms. The conditioned realm does not see the unconditioned realm, the unconditioned realm does not see the conditioned realm. The conditioned realm also cannot be paired. The unconditioned realm also cannot be named; the unconditioned cannot be paired; the conditioned also cannot be named. Thus, Subhūti, one who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, regarding all dharmas, forever sees nothing. Having nothing to see, one does not fear, is not frightened, not alarmed, not terrified; the mind does not falter and has no regret. Why? Subhūti, one does not see form, feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness; does not see the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind; does not see form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas; does not see desire, up to dharma-desire: nothing is seen. One does not see greed, anger, or delusion; does not see ignorance, up to formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, old age, sickness, and death: nothing is seen. One does not see a self, does not see a person or a lifespan. One does not see the desire realm, the form realm, or the formless realm. One does not see śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas; one does not see the bodhisattva-dharma, does not see the Buddha, does not see the Dharma, does not see the bodhisattva assembly. Having seen nothing whatsoever of all dharmas, one does not fear, is not frightened, not alarmed, not terrified, and the mind does not falter."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "Indeed, World-Honored One! For what reason does the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's mind not falter and have no attachment?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva does not apprehend any dharma of mental thought, nor does one see anything at all. Therefore the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's mind does not falter and has no attachment."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva have no fear?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "A bodhisattva does not apprehend a mind of fear, nor does one see anything at all. Therefore the bodhisattva-mahāsattva has no fear."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, regarding all dharmas having nothing to apprehend, practice the prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, in all the practice of the prajñāpāramitā, does not apprehend the prajñāpāramitā, nor does one apprehend the bodhisattva's mind. This is what is now commanded as the bodhisattva's charge."
