# 佛說轉有經
## Sūtra of Transmigrating through Existences, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0576

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Buddhaśānta (佛陀扇多)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling in Rājagṛha, at the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove, surrounded by a great assembly of bhikṣus, one thousand two hundred and fifty in number, together with immeasurable and boundless bodhisattva mahāsattvas.

At that time King Bimbisāra of Magadha came forth from the city of Rājagṛha and went to the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove Monastery. Having arrived before the Buddha, he bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, circumambulated him three times, and sat to one side.

Then the Tathāgata, knowing that King Bimbisāra was seated to one side, said to him: "Great King! Suppose a man in a sleeping dream engages in acts of desire with a jewel-like woman. When that man wakes, he remembers that jewel-like woman. Great King, what do you think? Is the jewel-like woman in the dream real or not?"

The king replied: "No, World-Honored One!"

He asked the king again: "Great King, what do you think? If that man clings to the woman in the dream, can he be called clever and wise?"

The king replied: "No, World-Honored One! Why? Because the woman in the dream ultimately does not exist. How could there be any realm of desire? That man labors in vain."

The Buddha said: "Great King! Just so. All foolish ordinary people, because they have never heard the Buddha's true Dharma, when the eye sees forms, feel delight in their hearts and grasp them as real. Because of grasping, there is bondage. Because of bondage, there is attachment. Because of attachment, karma of greed, anger, and delusion arises: karma of the body, karma of speech, and karma of the mind. Yet the bodily karma that is performed immediately ceases. Having ceased, it does not abide in the east, nor in the south, west, north, above, or below. At the time of death, the consciousness of existence is transferred, and the final mind appears. Great King! That consciousness is not destroyed. Wherever karma is to be exhausted, that karma can manifest, just as the jewel-like woman appears upon waking from a dream. Thus, Great King, when the final consciousness ceases, a subsequent consciousness arises, whether in Naraka, among hungry ghosts, among animals, among asuras, among humans, or among devas. The final consciousness takes up the final birth, and consciousness follows that birth. Thus the mind and consciousness receive retribution according to karma. Yet there is no dharma whatsoever that transmigrates from this world to that world to receive birth.

"Great King! When the subsequent consciousness ceases, this is called cessation. When the initial consciousness arises, as one takes rebirth, this is the subsequent birth. Great King! When the subsequent consciousness is transferred, that dharma does not truly come from there to here. When the initial consciousness arises, it goes nowhere. Why? Because such is the nature of dharmas. Great King! The final consciousness is empty of final consciousness; karma is empty of karma; birth is empty of birth; the initial consciousness is empty of initial consciousness; the place of birth is empty of the place of birth. Yet in that place, the fruits of karma are not lost. Great King! When the consciousness of the final birth ceases there, the mind thereafter is not cut off. The consciousness continues in accordance with its course, and wherever retribution is to be received, it goes there to receive it." Then the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "When the Well-Gone One teaches at last,
> All the words that are spoken
> Are merely conventional designations,
> For they abide in concepts and names.
> Apart from the spoken word, the Dharma
> Has nothing that can be expressed.
> According to whatever words are spoken,
> Those various dharmas are described.
> They are not in that which they describe.
> The Dharma-eye sees: there is no form.
> Those who say they see forms
> Do so because of worldly grasping.
> To say that worldly dharmas are real
> When what is seen is a gathering of what does not exist:
> This is what the Tathāgata teaches;
> This is called the ground of skillful means.
> If one speaks of the truly real,
> The eye does not see forms,
> The mind does not know dharmas:
> This is the most secret truth.
> The world speaks from pride of self,
> But names are originally empty, without names.
> All dharmas have no names;
> They are spoken of through provisional names."

When this Dharma-gate was taught, King Bimbisāra, the great assembly, humans, devas, nāgas, spirits, gandharvas, and others, hearing the Buddha's teaching, were all filled with great joy, and faithfully received and practiced it.
