# 私呵昧經
## The Sthiramati Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0532

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus it was heard.

At one time the Buddha was in the kingdom of Rājagṛha, in the Bamboo Grove, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time there was an elder's son who had taken a vow, named Sthiramati. Together with five hundred disciples, he set out from the great kingdom of Rājagṛha, intending to go to the Bamboo Grove. While still some distance away, he saw the Buddha walking in meditation. The colors and radiance of his body changed in immeasurable ways, beyond what any worldly person could ever hear or see. The five hundred disciples said to one another in praise: "The Buddha is handsome beyond compare. How extraordinary is his spiritual majesty! By what causes and conditions does such a one exist in the world? What kind of practice did he perform? What merit did he accumulate to obtain such a body? We should go and ask him."

The five hundred disciples, all with respectful minds, trembling with reverence, their hair standing on end, went forward and bowed to the Buddha, then withdrew and stood to one side. Sthiramati then came forward and addressed the Buddha: "The Buddha's body is thus: beyond what the world can see. What causes brought this about? What practices were done in the past, and what merit was accumulated?"

The Buddha asked Sthiramati: "What do you see that makes you say, 'The Buddha's body is thus, beyond what the world can see'?"

Sthiramati then spoke in verse before the Buddha:

> "Holding onto perception, one tries to see what cannot be seen.
> When the Honored Among Men walks in meditation,
> lotus flowers appear above and below his feet.
> His form is beautiful; there is nothing lacking.
>
> "His body of empty wisdom can manifest dharmas.
> All the earth shakes and trembles.
> What was sunken becomes level;
> what was high becomes low.
>
> "When he lifts his foot as he walks in meditation,
> and then completes a step upon the ground,
> his body stands upon the ground and turns to the right,
> yet the ground turns without anyone knowing.
>
> "When he sets his foot down upon the earth,
> in the course of his walking meditation it becomes invisible.
> Where his footprints appear, they are like paintings:
> all the marks can be fully seen.
> The wheel-marks have no color,
> yet upon the earth they are fully manifested.
>
> "What is now seen does not exist in the world.
> By this I know the supreme honor.
> No one can see his crown,
> neither to the left nor the right,
> neither before nor behind:
> in every direction it cannot be found.
>
> "By what cause can his mind be known?
> By what condition can his wisdom be understood?
> Because of this my mind marvels.
> Please explain and distinguish this for me.
>
> "How was this body of wisdom obtained?
> What is its root and how was it achieved?
> What dharmas were practiced?
> What must be done to bring it to fruition?
>
> "Please sever my doubts for me
> and resolve what we find doubtful.
> How was the Buddha's wisdom obtained?
> Let us first arouse the aspiration.
> Please explain in order
> what all bodhisattvas should practice,
> so that by their own effort they may attain the Mahāyāna,
> and with supernatural powers reach the ten directions."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! Sthiramati, your question is extremely profound! You have compassion for many and bring peace and security to devas and humans. With pity and care for the ten directions, wishing to bring liberation, you arouse the intentions of bodhisattvas and great beings, causing them all to be diligent." The Buddha told Sthiramati: "I shall explain this for you. Listen carefully and receive it well."

Sthiramati said: "I receive the instruction."

The Buddha said: "If good men or good women wish to practice six things, then even before the bodhisattva-aspiration has arisen, they should seek to arouse it. What are the six? First, relying on the Buddha and abiding. Second, entering the true path and not turning back. Third, inwardly understanding with one's own mind. Fourth, obtaining spiritual friends and relying on them. Fifth, always having great vows. Sixth, having no timidity, and not wearying of wisdom. These are the six."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "If a person relies on the Buddha-Dharma,
> enters the true path without turning back,
> and always associates with spiritual friends,
> from this they obtain great vows.
>
> "When their inner mind has already understood,
> such a person is not timid.
> Fully endowed with wisdom,
> such a one can receive the Dharma."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "For one who has aroused the aspiration, how many kinds of joy are there?"

The Buddha said: "For one who has aroused the bodhisattva-aspiration, there are six kinds of joy. What are the six? First, having attained the joyful mind that does not leave the Buddha. Second, receiving the words of prediction and entering the true path. Third, becoming the king of physicians, healing the birth, old age, sickness, and death of people. Fourth, 'I shall be a general, delivering people from the five destinies and freeing them from birth and death.' Fifth, 'I shall be the great captain of a ship in the ocean, ferrying and liberating people in the currents of the sea.' Sixth, 'I shall be the great lord of light in the darkness, shattering delusion.' These are the six kinds of joy."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Having attained the mind that does not leave the Buddha,
> receiving the words of prediction and entering the true path,
> becoming the king of physicians who heals all:
> practicing thus, one obtains a pleasing mind.
>
> "I, in the world, shall be the honored general
> who wishes to deliver beings from all hardships.
> Birth and death, old age and sickness:
> all that people are attached to.
>
> "I see the people toiling in suffering,
> revolving and falling through the five destinies.
> I shall become the great captain of a ship,
> ferrying and liberating people in the ocean.
>
> "In the darkness I shall be the great light,
> giving sight to all the blind,
> granting wisdom to all the flattering, deceitful, and foolish."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "With this joyful mind, how many kinds of meritorious rest are there?"

The Buddha said: "For one who has aroused the bodhisattva-aspiration, there are six things by which body and mind obtain rest. What are the six? First, obtaining freedom from the toil of the hells, the animal realm, and the hungry ghost realm, and thereby body and mind obtain rest. Second, obtaining freedom from the eight difficult conditions. Third, obtaining freedom from error and never again entering the ninety-six kinds of heterodox paths. Fourth, surpassing the dharmas of the arhat and pratyekabuddha. Fifth, abiding in the foremost vessel of Dharma, never to regress. Sixth, abiding in the Buddha's solemn teaching, never severing the path of the Buddha. These are the six kinds of meritorious rest."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Having been freed from all evil destinies,
> the body is far from the eight difficult conditions.
> All the heterodox paths are not accepted,
> and such are all left far behind.
>
> "Regarding the arhats and pratyekabuddhas,
> and all that people are attached to:
> one surpasses them all, arousing the honored aspiration,
> surpassing all dharmas.
>
> "I am now the vessel of all dharmas,
> of all buddhas and of the Dharma.
> I also do not sever the Buddha's solemn teaching.
> By this I obtain a pleasing mind.
>
> "Though empty space could one day be exhausted,
> and echoes and shadows could be seen,
> it is not so with one of such courageous vigor:
> their practice is boundless and inexhaustible."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "For one who has aroused the bodhisattva-aspiration, what dharmas should be practiced and what should be done?"

The Buddha said: "One who has aroused the bodhisattva-aspiration should practice six things. What are the six? First, one should practice giving. Second, one should keep the precepts. Third, one should practice patience. Fourth, one should be diligent. Fifth, one should practice one-pointed concentration. Sixth, one should practice wisdom. These are the six practices."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The giver makes great offerings.
> In conducting oneself, one should guard the precepts.
> Through patience and diligence,
> surpassing dhyāna and excelling in wisdom,
>
> "receiving the words of wisdom right here,
> one becomes the hero among people.
> This merit is uniquely honored.
> It is what all bodhisattvas should practice.
>
> "One who acts thus, in all things,
> is extraordinary and without a second.
> Wherever one is, one is the honored hero,
> perceived as offering immeasurable worship."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "If a bodhisattva wishes to attain the patience of non-arising, how should it be achieved?"

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva has six practices by which to swiftly attain the patience of non-arising. What are the six? First, not conceiving of a body. Second, not conceiving of a person. Third, not conceiving of a lifespan. Fourth, not conceiving of a form. Fifth, not conceiving of nonexistence. Sixth, not conceiving of permanent existence. These are the six practices. A bodhisattva, through these, swiftly attains the patience of non-arising."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Self, person, and lifespan:
> one also does not conceive of this form.
> The mind does not think of existence or nonexistence.
> The wise should stay far from these.
>
> "What the mouth speaks are dharmas of dependent arising,
> yet those dependent arisings are without any substance.
> All dharmas are without any arising.
> By this one attains the patience of dharmas."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "For a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has already attained the patience of non-arising, how many things lead to omniscience?"

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has already attained the patience of non-arising has six things by which to attain omniscience. What are the six? First, obtaining the power of body. Second, obtaining the power of speech. Third, obtaining the power of mind. Fourth, obtaining the power of supernatural abilities. Fifth, obtaining the power of the path. Sixth, obtaining the power of wisdom."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "What is the power of body?"

The Buddha said: "The power of body is firm and strong like vajra, without flaw or impurity. Fire cannot burn it, and swords cannot cut it. No one can shake it. This is the power of body."

"What is the power of speech?"

The Buddha said: "The power of speech is the six kinds of voice. The sounds spoken by the Tathāgata's mouth pervade three thousand great trichiliocosms. This is the power of speech."

"What is the power of mind?"

The Buddha said: "The power of mind is such that even if a hundred thousand billion māras were to come, they could not disturb a single hair of the Buddha. This is the power of mind."

"What is the power of supernatural abilities?"

The Buddha said: "The power of supernatural abilities is such that pressing down with one toe can cause the three thousand great trichiliocosms to shake, yet the people within them are not alarmed. This is the power of supernatural abilities."

"What is the power of the path?"

The Buddha said: "The power of the path is that the buddhas of the ten directions teach the sūtras and dharmas for all people without any gap, and each person obtains what they need. This is the power of the path."

"What is the power of wisdom?"

The Buddha said: "The power of wisdom is that everything all people's minds know, actions know, thoughts know, and liberation knows is gathered together at one time, and in the snap of a finger, through the power of wisdom, everything that can be known, seen, and learned is completely known, seen, and realized. This is the power of wisdom. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has attained the patience of non-arising attains omniscience through these six things."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The body's courage is beyond measure;
> nothing can destroy it.
> Whether by fire or by sword,
> one can never harm this body.
>
> "All people and all forces,
> whether with staffs or with insults,
> seek to endanger the body but cannot topple it,
> nor can they disturb a single hair.
>
> "The great voice is heard in the Brahmā heavens.
> Ever abiding without fear,
> his spoken Dharma opens up the trichiliocosm.
> None can obstruct these words.
>
> "The mind is honored and noble, difficult to rival.
> The nature of all bodhisattvas is thus.
> Even a hundred million māras seeking to disturb
> can never move his mind.
>
> "His supernatural abilities are fully complete.
> He can move this heaven and earth.
> Having accomplished these supernatural powers,
> he can awaken and become the Honored One.
>
> "Having attained and accomplished the awakening of the path,
> he can awaken and become the honored hero.
> The Buddha bestows all things completely,
> and from this he turns the Dharma wheel."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "Having accomplished omniscience, by how many dharmas does the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One, abide?"

The Buddha said: "Having accomplished omniscience, youth, the Tathāgata abides by six dharmas. What are the six? First, the Buddha's ten powers. Second, the four fearlessnesses. Third, the eighteen distinctive dharmas of a buddha. Fourth, possessing great compassion. Fifth, no one being able to see the crown of the Buddha's head. Sixth, possessing the thirty-two marks of a great being. These are the six dharmas by which he abides."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The ten powers are the Buddha's powers.
> The four fearlessnesses have all been surpassed.
> Passing beyond all dharmas above:
> by this he is the general among people.
>
> "Having attained the uncovered compassion,
> no one can see the crown of the Buddha.
> Neither devas nor nāgas
> nor anyone can see it.
>
> "Such are the marks of courageous vigor,
> distributed throughout the thirty-two.
> All of these have been accomplished,
> and thereby he becomes the supreme among people."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "Having accomplished omniscience, by how many dharmas does the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One, enter parinirvāṇa?"

The Buddha said: "Having accomplished omniscience, youth, the Tathāgata enters parinirvāṇa by six dharmas. What are the six? At the time of parinirvāṇa, youth, the Tathāgata leaves behind five portions that do not perish. What are the five? First, the body of precepts. Second, the body of concentration. Third, the body of wisdom. Fourth, the body of liberation. Fifth, the body of the knowledge and vision of liberation. These five portions are left behind and do not perish, out of compassion for all people. At the time of parinirvāṇa, youth, the Tathāgata, through immeasurable means, praises and extols the merit of the bhikṣu saṃgha, causing people to give generously, out of compassion for all people. At the time of parinirvāṇa, youth, the Tathāgata distributes his bodily relics, making them as small as mustard seeds, out of compassion for all people. At the time of parinirvāṇa, youth, the Tathāgata teaches all the bodhisattvas, saying: 'The reason I sought the unsurpassed true path was solely out of compassion for all people.' At the time of parinirvāṇa, youth, for the sake of the people of the ten directions, the Tathāgata teaches the twelve divisions of sūtras, so that all people each obtain what they need. What are the twelve? First, sūtras that are heard. Second, sūtras that are taught. Third, sūtras that are listened to. Fourth, sūtras that distinguish and analyze. Fifth, sūtras that manifest. Sixth, sūtras of parables. Seventh, sūtras of what was spoken. Eighth, birth-story sūtras. Ninth, vaipulya sūtras. Tenth, sūtras of unprecedented dharmas. Eleventh, sūtras of verse. Twelfth, sūtras of practice. These are the twelve divisions of sūtras. Out of compassionate protection for all people, at the time of parinirvāṇa the Tathāgata broadly teaches the four refuges. What are the four? First, taking only the essential meaning, not mere recognition. Second, taking only the Dharma, not mere recognition. Third, taking only wisdom, not mere form. Fourth, taking only what is correct, not mere words. These are the four refuges. Having accomplished omniscience, youth, the Tathāgata enters parinirvāṇa by these six dharmas."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "When the Buddha is about to enter parinirvāṇa,
> he manifests peace and security for all,
> leaving behind the five portions for the ten directions,
> with compassion for humans and non-humans.
>
> "He places relics in the world,
> distributing and grinding his bones for all,
> as small as mustard seeds in fine portions.
> Whoever obtains them reveres them most highly.
>
> "Those who make offerings to them,
> humans and non-humans, will be exceedingly joyful.
> Among devas and humans,
> wherever they are born, they will be free from toil.
>
> "Seeing me making offerings in person,
> and after parinirvāṇa, to the relics:
> whoever has a mind of purity,
> these two matters are without difference.
>
> "The Buddha entrusts the bhikṣu saṃgha:
> among the honored and noble, there are none above them.
> Giving generously to them,
> one receives merit among devas and humans.
>
> "Leaving behind the twelve divisions of sūtras,
> the Buddha abides by these throughout the ten directions.
> What all bodhisattvas should practice,
> frequently study and arouse the wholesome mind:
>
> "The ten grounds, the three baskets of sūtras,
> and the Prajñā Pāramitā:
> out of compassion for all humans and non-humans,
> in the future he makes these manifest.
>
> "He broadly teaches the four refuges.
> All dharmas hold nothing to be grasped.
> Out of compassion for the world, he teaches these sūtras.
> At that time, the Buddha then enters final parinirvāṇa."

At that time the youth Sthiramati spoke in verse before the Buddha:

> "I too shall attain buddhahood.
> Excellent! How supreme is the unsurpassed wisdom!
> What person, hearing this Dharma,
> would not arouse the bodhisattva-aspiration?
>
> "Now my five hundred disciples
> have all come to this place.
> I shall cause them all to arouse the aspiration,
> exhorting and encouraging the bodhisattva practice.
>
> "Just as one who plants a tree
> has its sprout arise from moisture,
> and by moisture it grows tall
> and produces stems and joints,
> then branches and leaves,
> and from branches and leaves come flowers,
> and from flowers come fruit,
> and after that it grows again:
>
> "The bodhisattva-aspiration is also like this.
> From the six dharmas it arises.
> By this aspiration one can have faith,
> and through this faith the Dharma comes to life.
>
> "This meaning is the truth of this sūtra,
> spoken by all buddhas.
> One then obtains branches and leaves,
> and after that they grow again.
>
> "When such a tree grows tall,
> the bodhisattva-tree is unsurpassed.
> Whoever wishes to rest under this tree
> will be brought to peace and security.
>
> "Such Dharma is the great tree.
> By this one becomes a buddha,
> compassionately caring for all people.
> This is the bodhisattva practice one should do."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "After the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, how many kinds of merit are there that the arhats and pratyekabuddhas cannot match?"

The Buddha said: "Youth, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, there are six kinds of merit that the arhats and pratyekabuddhas cannot match. What are the six? First, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, the relics receive offerings. Devas, nāgas, ghosts and spirits, yakṣas, gandharvas, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, and non-humans all come to make offerings to the relics, bowing in homage without end. Second, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, people escape from the three realms: the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm. Third, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, the fourfold assembly of disciples cultivates merit by making offerings to the bhikṣu saṃgha. Fourth, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, the twelve divisions of sūtras are spread throughout the continent of Jambudvīpa. Fifth, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, in the border regions and great kingdoms where sūtras and dharmas are not understood and where there is no principled reasoning, the dharmas of the various heterodox paths will flourish in their midst. Sixth, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, if people hear of the Buddha's practices, the Buddha's supernatural powers, the Buddha's transformations, and the Buddha's wisdom, many will arouse pure love and devotion, and by this cause and condition they will be reborn in the heavens or among humans, receiving merit. These six kinds of merit are not something the arhats and pratyekabuddhas can match."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Those who make offerings to the relics
> will become devas or humans.
> Those who make offerings to the bhikṣu saṃgha
> will always protect the bhikṣus.
>
> "Abiding in the Dharma and practicing it,
> they will transcend the three realms.
> Whoever hears this essential Dharma
> should practice it at the proper time.
>
> "In the border regions and the various kingdoms,
> people will hear this unsurpassed Dharma.
> Whoever hears of the Buddha's merit
> will immediately arouse the bodhisattva-aspiration."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "How is the unsurpassed true path verified as truth?"

The Buddha said: "There are six dharmas that constitute truth. What are the six? First, the eye being free from form is truth. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind being free from form is truth."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The six: neither ear-sounds nor eye-colors;
> within them there is certainly no form.
> Not seeing each other: this is truth.
> Whoever wishes to learn should be thus.
>
> "Ear and nose are not connected.
> This body, tongue, and mind:
> do not let the mind give rise to such matters.
> Having no thought: this is truth.
>
> "Having no perception: this is truth.
> All attachments to form should be left far behind.
> All that exists is unconnected.
> This is what is called true truth."

At that time Sthiramati spoke in verse before the Buddha:

> "How excellent! The Dharma of no-thought!
> Who could hear it and not wish for it?
> All fears are transcended and liberated.
> In desire there is no attachment."

At that time the Buddha spoke in verse for the youth Sthiramati:

> "Whoever has no reverence for all buddhas,
> no respect for the true Dharma,
> and does not draw near to the Saṃgha:
> hearing this teaching, they will not be pleased.
>
> "If there is a person without faith,
> who is narrow and inferior in precepts,
> who is timid and without diligence:
> for this Dharma they will not be receptive.
>
> "Those who are often angry, covered, hasty, and violent,
> whose aspirations are confused and who cannot be content,
> who are fickle and without wisdom:
> such people will not delight in this.
>
> "If the sons of Māra or Māra's emissaries,
> or those of wrong views and heterodox paths,
> stand firmly in the net of doubt:
> hearing these words, they will not believe or accept them."

Sthiramati said to the Buddha: "Such people are not vessels for the Dharma. I shall make them into vessels for the Dharma. May the Buddha please bestow a prediction upon me!" He then spoke in verse before the Buddha:

> "Just as one who breaks a vessel
> cannot hold the great Dharma,
> it is for the sake of such people
> that I shall become a vessel for the Dharma.
>
> "May the Buddha please grant me a prediction.
> My sincere aspiration arises from within.
> I shall draw near to spiritual friends
> and seek companions among the bodhisattvas.
>
> "The poor I shall make wealthy.
> The faithless I shall cause to believe.
> The wicked I shall cause to keep the precepts.
> For all people I shall offer protection.
>
> "I shall always teach the purity of patience,
> guiding them and leading them to repentance,
> and the Prajñā Pāramitā,
> so that all living creatures may be ferried to liberation.
>
> "With the Dharma of emptiness I shall guide people,
> freeing all from birth and death.
> I shall cause bodhisattvas to arouse the excellent mind,
> practicing what should be done within the Dharma.
>
> "I shall distribute relics everywhere,
> so that sentient beings may attain peace and security.
> I shall leave the sūtras and precepts in the ten directions,
> so that all may always study and practice."

The Buddha told the youth Sthiramati: "All the buddhas of the past have already bestowed predictions upon you. I now also bestow a prediction upon you. The buddhas presently in the immeasurable lands of the ten directions who are turning the Dharma wheel have all also bestowed predictions upon you."

When the youth Sthiramati heard the prediction bestowed by the Buddha, he was greatly joyful and immediately rose into the sky to a height of one hundred and forty zhang. Descending from above, he prostrated with his head at the Buddha's feet, bowing in homage to the Buddha.

At that time, the five hundred disciples, seeing the great transformation, spoke in verse before the Buddha:

> "May you extend your teaching and have compassion for us.
> May the Buddha grant us predictions.
> In the future age of the five turbidities, the evil world,
> we shall uphold the Dharma.
>
> "Though people may admonish and curse us,
> though wicked people may beat us with staves,
> in that age when such people exist,
> we shall teach them to repent and reflect.
>
> "In that age, we
> shall endure all manner of suffering.
> For all humans and non-humans,
> please grant us the essential prediction.
>
> "We shall all give up these decaying bodies.
> We do not covet or cherish our lives.
> We only delight in dwelling in solitude.
> For offerings we have no longing."

The Buddha then spoke in verse for the five hundred disciples:

> "These five hundred disciples
> have all now come to this place.
> In that future age,
> they will all arouse the bodhisattva mind.
>
> "They will still undergo some more toil,
> and their lifespans will be short.
> At that time, wherever they are,
> they will see immeasurable offerings.
>
> "When I first aroused the bodhisattva-aspiration,
> I too endured toil life after life.
> If you practice and learn in this way,
> you will become kings among people.
>
> "Dharma is fundamentally empty, without self or ego.
> Compassionately I teach this again and again.
> At that time, in that world,
> I shall manifest my form for you.
>
> "All the bodhisattvas will rejoice
> and praise, saying the Buddha is ever-present.
> Bringing peace and security to all,
> he manifests the Buddha's form for people.
>
> "In all the lands and the ten directions,
> all the present Dharma Kings:
> the Buddha, for the bodhisattvas' sake,
> will all radiate great light.
>
> "The Invincible One of Great Compassion, the Great Being,
> presently abides in the Fourth Heaven.
> He frequently encourages and delights the bodhisattvas,
> also urging and guiding them to the profound Dharma.
>
> "In that age, those who practice
> will include many who arouse the aspiration.
> As for past-life karma with remaining consequences:
> if the mind is disturbed, the penalty should be completed.
>
> "What the aspiration seeks is without satiation;
> it also cannot satisfy itself.
> It does not delight in doing other things
> but always seeks the bodhisattva practice of the Buddha.
>
> "Disciples, do not grieve.
> Though there is toil, it will not last long.
> After your lives end in this world,
> you will be born above in Tuṣita Heaven.
>
> "You should vow to be born in the Land of Peace and Security,
> before the Dharma King of limitless life.
> In the land of the King of Wondrous Medicine,
> in the teaching place of the Unangered Buddha.
>
> "You should always vow to be born there.
> After your lives end in this world,
> you will attain supernatural powers there,
> and make offerings before all the buddhas.
>
> "Practicing the six dharmas, you will accomplish them.
> Now at the time of the Buddha, all receive predictions.
> They will all be freed from the three evil destinies
> and will be far from the eight difficult conditions.
>
> "All the heterodox paths and the great net:
> these will be torn apart, and they will escape.
> Beyond the unattached pratyekabuddhas,
> they will surpass and transcend all."

At that time the five hundred disciples, hearing the Buddha bestow predictions upon them, were all greatly joyful. They immediately rose into the sky to a height of twenty zhang. Descending from above, they bowed to the Buddha and said: "We and Sthiramati: what appointment and ordination do we receive?"

At that time the Buddha smiled. Lights of immeasurable and varied colors emerged from the Buddha's mouth, illuminating immeasurable buddha-lands. The light circled his body three times, then entered through his crown and was no longer seen.

Then Ānanda rose from his seat, straightened his robes, placed his right knee on the ground, clasped his hands, and prostrated at the Buddha's feet, saying: "For what cause and condition does the Buddha smile? Since the Buddha has smiled, there must be a purpose."

The Buddha then spoke in verse for Ānanda:

> "Sthiramati is at the head,
> the foremost teacher among the disciples.
> They will all share the same kalpa,
> as the honored heroes among people.
>
> "In this Fortunate Kalpa,
> in the future age of the five turbidities and evil,
> within it they will complete their karmic debt,
> and then attain supernatural powers.
>
> "After that, with supernatural powers complete,
> they will fly to hundreds of millions of lands.
> Having made offerings, they will attain buddhahood,
> and that buddha will be named 'Supreme Lotus.'"

At that time the five hundred disciples praised the Buddha in verse before the Buddha:

> "The Buddha now has said that we
> shall receive the appointment of becoming buddhas.
> Teaching all to cultivate merit,
> without thought through immeasurable ages.
>
> "At that time, as Dharma Kings,
> we shall abide in Jambudvīpa.
> The bodhisattvas by the tree of enlightenment
> shall broadly teach the Dharma for all.
>
> "Whoever hears the sūtras and asks
> will immediately arouse the bodhisattva-aspiration.
> We shall all urge the Buddha
> and ask the Honored Hero to grant predictions.
>
> "The Buddha now is the father of all.
> He always has compassion for humans and non-humans.
> For the sake of his children, with loving kindness,
> may the Great Honored General explain and distinguish.
>
> "At that time, regarding this sūtra,
> in the future they will arouse reverence
> and immediately arouse the bodhisattva-aspiration.
> For the sake of people, they will ask about its meaning.
>
> "May the Great Honored Hero explain in full:
> what merit has the bodhisattva-aspiration?
> If a wise person hears this Dharma,
> they will arouse the bodhisattva-aspiration."

The Buddha then spoke in verse for the five hundred disciples:

> "Whoever, hearing this, believes:
> the bodhisattva-tree is the unsurpassed honor.
> I grant predictions to all of them:
> they shall all become kings among people.
>
> "Their aspirations are great and their vigor courageous.
> Arousing the bodhisattva-aspiration, they go straight forward.
> Their aspirations are extremely pure,
> and they will produce pure fruit.
>
> "Transcending the desire for form and the three realms,
> they immediately arouse the bodhisattva-aspiration.
> With this aspiration they cultivate merit,
> and swiftly transcend the three realms.
>
> "All the practices that people perform
> are all attachments within the three realms.
> If one's mind is without any attachment,
> the bodhisattva is unsurpassed.
>
> "If a bodhisattva arouses the aspiration of the sūtras,
> they will teach all the tree of enlightenment.
> With their merit they will guide and lead,
> and with this sūtra they can manifest it.
>
> "The bodhisattva's matter has been fully explained,
> and all that should be taught has been covered.
> The remaining dharmas are countless.
> The Dharma is subtle and beyond expression.
>
> "Immeasurable wisdom is fully complete.
> By this one becomes a buddha,
> compassionately caring for all people,
> always cultivating the bodhisattva practice."

At that time Ānanda said to the Buddha: "What is the name of this sūtra? How should it be upheld?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "This sūtra is named 'The Bodhisattva's Bodhi-Tree Sūtra.' You should recite and uphold it."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Why is it named 'The Bodhisattva's Bodhi-Tree Sūtra'?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "It is like planting a tree. Gradually a sprout appears, then later a stem, joints, branches, leaves, flowers, and fruit. Just so, Ānanda, in this sūtra, a bodhisattva who first arouses the aspiration obtains joy. From joy, body and mind obtain rest. Endowed with the six pāramitās and the transformative wisdom of skillful virtue, one attains the patience of non-arising. Fully endowed with omniscient wisdom, one turns the Dharma wheel and finally enters parinirvāṇa, distributing the relics so that the subsequent Dharma may endure. Therefore, Ānanda, it is named 'The Bodhisattva's Bodhi-Tree Sūtra.'"

When the Buddha had spoken, the youth Sthiramati and the five hundred disciples, all the bhikṣu saṃgha, and the devas, humans, nāgas, ghosts, and yakṣas who heard the sūtra were all greatly joyful. They came forward, prostrated with their heads upon the ground, bowed to the Buddha, and departed.
