# 賢聖集伽陀一百頌
## One Hundred Gāthā Verses Collected from the Worthy Ones and Sages

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1686

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Tianxizai (天息災).

---

> I bow to all who transcend the world, the ocean of merit most honored in the three realms.
> The fire of wisdom burns away the stain of afflictions; to the Correctly Awakened One I now pay homage.
> I bow to the one who pulls us from the peril of birth and death, who dries up the ocean of grief, affliction, greed, and delusion,
> Who shatters the mountain of dust-toil and karmic transgression; I now pay homage to the jewel of the wondrous Dharma.
> I bow to all whom the buddhas have praised, adorned with the practice of the eightfold noble path,
> The unconditioned dhyāna-concentration gradually becoming perfectly bright; I now pay homage to the jewel of the noble saṃgha.

> Inner palace treasure-stores and many-storied pavilions, gold, jade, and maṇi gems of various radiance,
> Pearl necklaces in lavish adornment: hundreds of thousands of heavenly beings constantly walk therein.
> If one can dwell in peace within the heavenly realm, it is the reward of building and adorning buddha-monasteries.
> With a faithful mind, one reveres the Three Treasures and donates wealth and goods to the monasteries.
> Having received the joys of humans and devas, one shall attain the fruit of nirvāṇa and quiescent extinction.

> When monasteries have crumbled with age, one who repairs and adorns them to serve the Buddha and saṃgha
> Shall constantly attain joy and dwell among humans and devas, and shall also attain the fruit of nirvāṇa and quiescent extinction.
> If at a monastery one donates gardens and groves, seat-furnishings, robes, and medicines,
> And further, in an evil age of famine and hardship, gives sentient beings food and drink:
> Such a one obtains boundless merit and also attains the seven kinds of noble wealth of the wise.
> All the sufferings of the evil destinies are eliminated, and one forever enjoys great wealth without end.

> To the places where all buddhas and tathāgatas have walked and stayed, if a person goes and gives rise to reverence,
> Serves and makes offerings while also hearing the Dharma, deeply trusts, studies, and follows the practice of the precepts:
> That person shall obtain a vast and boundless fruit. It is like the great ocean, deep beyond fathoming.
> Measuring it with vessels, one cannot reckon it. Merit and its conditions are likewise.

> If a person builds a buddha-hall where cold, heat, wind, and rain cannot encroach,
> That person shall first attain realization of the Dharma of quiescent extinction and fulfill all wishes as desired.
> If a person sculpts or paints a buddha-image, grief, affliction, and illness shall all be freed.
> Further, one shall be reborn in heaven with a supremely wondrous body, great wisdom, auspiciousness, and nobility.

> If a person repairs a buddha-image, one shall obtain a firm body free of illness.
> Long life, physical strength, and all marks shall be complete, and afterward one attains the quiescent joy of nirvāṇa.
> Grief, affliction, and disputes shall all be eliminated; with heroic eloquence, one is praised by all.
> Subduing the sense-faculties and diligently exerting oneself, adorned in every way, one is honored by the assembly.
> If a person repairs various buddha-images, one departs from faults and is reborn in heaven.
> Wealthy, noble, beautiful, and respected by all, one is fully endowed with every kind of merit.

> If a damaged stūpa or monastery is repaired, that person shall be free of illness with a perfect body.
> All the desirable fruits of the world shall be fully possessed, following one's wishes in every way.

> If one makes a buddha-image or a buddha-stūpa, even if its size is as small as a grain of wheat,
> Not only in heaven and the human realm shall one surely become a king enjoying happiness,
> But rebirth in the form and formless realms shall also be such. Wealth and nobility shall be boundless and immeasurable.
> One shall depart from the suffering of birth, old age, illness, and death, and ultimately attain the complete fruit of bodhi and buddhahood.

> If at a stūpa or monastery one installs śarīra relics and paints buddha-images and makes offerings,
> One shall obtain the light of wisdom that illuminates the great earth, the pure and wondrous buddha-wealth of the Sugata,
> Dwelling peacefully in the boundless realm of sentient beings, entering together into the equal and signless nature.

> If upon a quiet mountain peak one installs a buddha-stūpa with its crowning wheel,
> Even if its size is but the length of a finger-joint, one shall be reborn among all humans and devas,
> Beautiful, greatly wealthy, with physical strength and peace; and afterward one shall become lord of the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven.

> If a person with their hand opens a buddha-stūpa, one shall attain wealth, nobility, and all fulfillment.
> The body shall be radiant and pure, the mind soft and gentle; one shall be wise, pure in nature, and free of anger.

> If a wise person practices the perfection of giving and offers the Buddha fragrant ointment, white sandalwood,
> Kuṅkuma, and other various fragrances, one shall attain a vast and desirable fruit.
> If a person, with faith in the Buddha and giving rise to joy, holds wondrous flowers and incense and music
> And makes various offerings to the buddha and tathāgata, one shall be reborn in heaven and receive a golden jeweled pool.
> With the fine and fragrant pistils of the pool's lotuses, one's whole body is touched and bathed.
> The waters of the māndākinī are clear and pure; those who enter are naturally cleansed of dust and stain.
> Waves surge and spray with cool fragrance; jeweled shores are ringed with blooming red lotuses.
> Devas and others seeing this are always delighted: giving to the Buddha and saṃgha yields this reward.

> Such merit is immeasurable. Therefore the various sūtras conclude with verses of praise:

> Fine garments and superior robes, offered to the Buddha and the saṃgha:
> When later reborn in the heavenly realm, the most supreme heavenly raiments with fragrance,
> Blue, yellow, and various other colors, with hundreds of thousands of maṇi gems
> Used as adornment, wealth and nobility shall be immeasurable,
> And joy also boundless: one obtains this supremely wondrous fruit.
> If a person is born in the world and becomes a king,
> Fully endowed with all marks, with supremely wondrous silk garments,
> Various kinds of surpassing fragrance accompanying the body in constant enjoyment:
> All because of offering robes to the Buddha, this reward is accomplished.
> If one is reborn in the heavenly realm, with various jeweled adornments,
> Wearing pearl necklaces, a jeweled jade crown upon the head,
> Ear-rings, bracelets, and such, with wealth and joy:
> Offering adornments to the Buddha yields a supremely wondrous heavenly fruit.

> If one becomes a great king, the body broadly and beautifully adorned,
> With necklaces of maṇi pearls, pendants tinkling brightly:
> This too is the field of the Three Treasures, through the giving of adornments.
> The wise with a pure mind offer the Buddha flower-garlands and the like.
> In heaven and in the human realm, one constantly attains joy.
> And in the reward of subsequent lives,
> One accomplishes the flowers of the seven factors of enlightenment and is reborn in the mountains and lands of heaven.
> As Lord Indra, together with retainers,
> One sports in gardens and groves. Various flower offerings
> Yield such a fruit. Lapis lazuli, pearls, and jewels,
> Gold, silver, blue-green, and the like adorn parasols and canopies,
> Round and full like the full moon. Whether king or great minister,
> They are used to cover the head. As the Buddha has taught this cause,
> Offering canopies yields this reward. One is freed from greedy grief and affliction,
> Without illness, always in joy. One further becomes a worldly king:
> This too is the reward of offering canopies. If at a buddha-stūpa or monastery
> One offers banners and pennants, one shall become a wheel-turning heavenly king.
> In the world there is none who can surpass. One departs from all transgressions.
> Among all sentient beings, one becomes the foremost,
> Constantly receiving others' offerings. If a person at a stūpa or monastery
> Offers bells and wind-chimes, one shall not be born in places of transgression
> And shall always possess the voice of Brahmā. If a person makes music,
> With harps, lutes, drums, pipes, and the like, to serve the Buddha and the holy ones
> And allow them to hear with delighted minds, one shall obtain the heavenly ear-faculty,
> Adorned and always pure. Further, one obtains vajra wisdom
> That shatters the mountain of afflictions. If with a mind of faith and joy
> One offers wondrous food of fine color, fragrance, and taste,
> Various kinds of fine foods and drinks to all the buddhas and others,
> One shall be reborn in the heavenly realm, with retainers sharing jeweled seats,
> Eating from maṇi jeweled vessels the heavenly realm's finest delicacies.
> In kalpas of famine and kalpas of war, one shall never be reborn.
> If a person with food and drink serves the assembly of saints and worthies,
> One shall be reborn among humans and devas where fine food is always abundant,
> Various kinds being the finest. The wise shall always show respect.
> If with fine and wonderful food one gives to those who have left home,
> One shall constantly attain a wealthy and happy life, with eloquence and long life.
> Physical strength and marks shall all be complete: the reward of giving food is thus.
> If with a compassionate mind one gives sweet honey-water,
> Ghee, milk, curd, broth, and the like to those who are parched and weary,
> They drink it and attain peace and joy. One also receives the aforesaid rewards.
> If with myrobalan, forest vines, and various medicinal herbs
> One makes offerings to the saints and worthies, one shall be reborn among humans and devas,
> Without illness and with long life, constantly attaining bodily peace and joy.

> Furthermore, giving beverages to drink, one shall later be reborn in a heavenly palace
> Where the kalpa-tree's flowers are in rich bloom and whatever one seeks is obtained at will.
> Jeweled vessels of wine and honey and the like: retainers share in the drinking.
> Furthermore, from the kalpa-tree there come forth adornments,
> Music and heavenly melodies that delight the heavenly assembly's minds.
> Sweet dew also follows the body: giving drinks yields this reward.
> If a person prepares a feast-offering, one shall be reborn among humans and devas,
> Far removed from poverty and cold, with long life and abundant wealth.
> If with elephants, horses, and the like, carriages, palanquins, and sedan-chairs,
> One makes offerings to the buddha and tathāgata, one shall attain great supernatural powers.
> If one gives shoes and stockings, one shall always be born in a noble family
> And shall always have elephants, horses, and carriages, forever departing from poverty and hardship.
> If a teacher or elder is ill, and a person diligently serves them,
> Then in every situation whatever is sought shall all be accomplished.
> If one gives seat-furnishings, one shall be reborn in heaven,
> Free from toil and suffering, always sitting on supremely fine seats.
> If one gives bedding as an offering, one shall be reborn in heaven in constant joy.
> One's bodily form shall be broad, upright, and straight; fearless, one shall be praised by all.
> If one cultivates and plants miscellaneous gardens and groves, beautifully adorns various dwellings,
> And gives them to people for their pleasure, bringing joy to body and mind,
> One shall receive the Garden of Delight, where humans and devas stroll,
> Together with heavenly maidens, sporting and receiving joy.
> If in times of burning heat one makes the gift of shade and coolness,
> One shall receive the udumbara tree, the nyagrodha, and the bodhi tree.
> Further, one shall later be reborn in heaven, constantly enjoying the five pleasures.
> Wells, springs, and ponds, repaired and made pure,
> Relieving those who are parched and weary, universally bringing all to satisfaction:
> In subsequent reward one is reborn in heaven, or born in the Brahmā world.
> One receives various kinds of joy and further realizes the fruit of quiescent extinction.
> If with an alms-bowl one gives to the Three Treasures,
> One shall in every place of rebirth enjoy wealth, nobility, and joy.
> In the world one shall be honored and of supreme virtue,
> And further all sentient beings shall constantly make offerings.
> If a wise person gives a knife, one shall be reborn in heaven,
> With wisdom extremely sharp and keen, never encountering war.
> If a person gives a needle, wisdom shall always be keen and sharp,
> Able to sever all afflictions, realizing the path of quiescent stillness.

> The Buddha taught: if a person sculpts or paints a buddha-image,
> Being reborn in the heavenly realm, the body shall be the color of true gold.
> Pure and radiant like the sun, all the heavenly beings,
> Heavenly men and heavenly women, shall constantly pay homage.
> If that wise person is able to give the gift of Dharma-teaching,
> Being born in heaven or in the human realm, wisdom, virtue, and strength shall all be complete.
> One shall constantly receive joy and forever depart from grief and sorrow.
> And in one's subsequent body, one shall realize the path of quiescent extinction.
> If those sentient beings copy the jewel of the wondrous Dharma,
> They shall attain the knowledge of past lives, wealth, nobility, and constant joy.
> All transgressions shall be extinguished, and even through koṭi-kalpas,
> They shall not fall into hell or the realms of ghosts and animals.
> If that wise person sweeps, sprinkles, and prepares the altar,
> Circumambulates, scatters incense and flowers, and constantly makes offerings,
> In subsequent life being born in the human realm and also in heaven,
> One shall constantly enjoy wealth and nobility and further realize quiescent extinction.
> If with a perpetual lamp one makes offerings to the Buddha and the saints,
> Being born among humans and devas, three eyes shall always be pure:
> The wisdom-eye, the heavenly eye, and the fleshly eye.
> Furthermore, that giver of lamps shall always be reborn in heaven.
> The mouth shall not be mute, and the ears and eyes shall be neither deaf nor blind.
> Furthermore, that giver of lamps: not only shall the three eyes be pure,
> But regarding the Dharma of correct awakening, one shall penetrate everything.
> If a wise person gives wealth to serve those of virtue,
> Śramaṇas and brahmins: a small gift yields a great reward.
> One shall broadly obtain wealth that accords with the Way, for long enjoyment.
> Furthermore, that giver, if giving food to animals,
> The merit obtained shall be multiplied a hundredfold.
> If with wealth and food one gives to transgressors,
> The benefit gradually increases further, and the merit becomes a thousandfold.
> If one gives to those who uphold the precepts, the merit is a thousand-hundredfold.
> If one makes offerings to a person without mental activity, the benefit is a hundred koṭi.
> If a person gives to those who are still learning and to those beyond learning,
> The merit obtained surpasses all the foregoing.
> If one gives to the buddha and tathāgata, one shall be reborn in heaven,
> Greatly wealthy, never exhausted, constantly enjoying joy,
> Until the very end of saṃsāra, when one realizes the Dharma of quiescent extinction.

> I have collected all of these gāthā sūtras spoken by the Buddha,
> Briefly clarifying the rewards of merit, universally causing all to give rise to faith.
> Suppose the sun of a thousand rays auspiciously illuminates the great earth,
> And the full moon of the dark hours can open the blue lotus.
> Varuṇa, Vaiśravaṇa, Indra, Nārāyaṇa,
> And other foremost devas all obtained their positions through the practice of giving.
> The sun travels through space, illuminating above and below universally,
> Sustaining all living creatures. But when the time comes, it too is impermanent.
> Indra, the Four Heavenly Kings, and all the heavenly hosts,
> When their merit and lifespan are exhausted, enter the mouth of the enemy Death.
> The fires of grief, sorrow, suffering, and affliction ceaselessly burn them.

> Therefore I exhort all of you: contemplate the impermanent body.
> Depart from the thirst of desire; do not indulge in the joys of humans and devas.
> They last but a moment, not long, and all return to decay.
> I give rise to a pure mind of faith, relinquishing illusory and vain pleasures.
> I constantly go before the Buddha, palms joined, drawing near.
> With my own great bodhi, I shall become the lord of liberation.
