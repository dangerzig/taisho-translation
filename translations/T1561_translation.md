# Sub-commentary on the True Meaning of the Abhidharmakośa

## *Jùshě Lùn Shíyì Shū* (俱舍論實義疏)

Taishō 1561

Composed by the Venerable Sthiramati (*Ānhuì*, 安惠).

*Note: This is a fragmentary sub-commentary (vyākhyā) on Vasubandhu's Abhidharmakośa-bhāṣya by the Yogācāra master Sthiramati (c. 6th century). Only five fascicles survive, covering the first two chapters (Dhātunirdeśa, "Analysis of Elements," and the beginning of Indriyanirdeśa, "Analysis of Faculties"). The commentary explains Vasubandhu's verses and auto-commentary through additional verses, stories (such as the tale of the eighteen-headed sea-fish who was a former Tripiṭaka master punished for verbal abuse), doctrinal clarifications (on the seven perfections of the Buddha, form and formless-realm garments, the six defects in defining unmanifest form, the three types of discrimination, etc.), and citations from sūtras and other treatises. The work includes the famous story of Brahmā requesting the Buddha to turn the Dharma wheel.*

---

> Homage to the Blessed One, the mountain of manifold virtues;
> Homage to the Dharma, the great ocean of wisdom;
> Homage to the Saṃgha, the harmonious assembly.
> I salute the author of this treatise and my own teacher.

> I shall lend my firefly's light to aid the sun's radiance,
> Expounding the Treasury of Higher Dharma according to my ability.
> For the benefit of all beings and the long abiding of the Dharma,
> May the awesome spirits see fit to protect and sustain this work.

## Chapter One: Analysis of Elements (*Dhātunirdeśa*)

### The Seven Perfections

The seven meanings are: perfection of knowledge, perfection of abandonment, perfection of benefiting others, perfection of skillful means, perfection of being worthy of offerings, perfection of aspiration, and perfection of establishing the name [of the treatise].

Through reverence for the Dharma, one always delights in listening. Through listening, the wisdom of hearing arises. Through the wisdom of hearing, the wisdom of reflection arises. Through the wisdom of reflection, the wisdom of cultivation arises. Through the wisdom of cultivation, uncontaminated wisdom arises. Through uncontaminated wisdom, one is able to sever all afflictions. Because all afflictions are severed, one realizes nirvāṇa. This is called "the benefit that is the purpose."

The seven meanings [correspond to the opening verse as follows]: "The extinction of all kinds of darkness" is the perfection of knowledge. "All cessations" is the perfection of abandonment. "Drawing beings out of the mire of birth-and-death" is the perfection of benefiting others. "Homage to such a one" is the perfection of being worthy of offerings. "The teacher of what accords with principle" is the perfection of skillful means, because the Buddha World-Honored One, by supreme skillful means, teaches the doctrine that accords with principle for the benefit of sentient beings. "I shall now explain" is the perfection of aspiration, intending to compose this treatise. "The Treasury of Higher Dharma" is the perfection of establishing the name. These seven meanings are summarized in the verse:

> Knowledge, abandonment, and benefiting others,
> Skillful means, aspiration to compose the treatise,
> And establishing the name "Treasury of Higher Dharma":
> These seven meanings should be understood.

### The Five Categories of Dharmas

Form dharmas, mind dharmas, mental-factor dharmas, dharmas not associated with mind, and unconditioned dharmas: among these five categories of knowable objects, the world is entirely ignorant of them, while only the Buddha alone understands them, having forever severed all afflictions.

"The extremely remote in time" means: beyond three incalculable eons in the past and future, śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas cannot know. How do we know this? Once there was a man who sought ordination from Śāriputra. At that time Śāriputra observed that for two incalculable eons this man had no wholesome roots for going forth, and so refused him. The man then went to the Buddha to seek ordination. The World-Honored One observed that beyond the incalculable eons, this man had a small wholesome root, and so permitted his ordination. He spoke this verse:

> That person's seed of liberation
> I perceive to be extremely subtle,
> Like true gold hidden
> Within various ores.

Maudgalyāyana observed his mother but could not determine where she had been reborn. He went to ask the Buddha. The Buddha told him: "Your mother has been reborn in the Marīci world-system, beyond three thousand great world-systems." In many such kinds of discrimination, the wisdom of śrāvakas and others cannot fully comprehend.

### Self-Benefit and Other-Benefit

> The fruit obtained from cultivating merit and wisdom
> Is entirely for the benefit of others, not for oneself.
> Like the moon's pure light illuminating the ten directions,
> The World-Honored One's compassionate vow is just the same.

And another verse:

> The merit of dedicating gifts, and benefiting oneself,
> The twofold merit of one's own practice and dedicated merit,
> And again the merit of benefiting and delighting sentient beings:
> Such a multitude of accumulated merit and wisdom,
> The fruit-realization is not obtained through another but through oneself.
> The merit of aspiration and the merit of dedication:
> Benefiting both self and others, nothing is in vain.

Though it is said that self-benefit arises from cause and fruit, it is also able to benefit others. There is a verse:

> A wet-nurse eats sweet food to nourish her own body,
> But the child receives comfort, not herself.
> The Buddha cultivated merit and wisdom toward awakening
> Originally for the benefit of beings, not for himself.

A verse says:

> Though seasonal rains fall universally,
> Without seeds, sprouts will not grow.
> Though the Buddha transforms the world,
> When conditions are lacking, the fruit is not obtained.

Another verse:

> Who is a good spiritual friend? The Buddha, who causes wisdom to arise,
> Who causes one to depart from heedlessness and evil conduct.
> Those who go contrary to this are to be left.

### The Meaning of Abhidharma

Higher Dharma (*abhidharma*) is like innate wisdom: it serves as the cause of the wisdom of hearing, the wisdom of hearing as the cause of reflection, the wisdom of reflection as the cause of cultivation, the wisdom of cultivation as the cause of uncontaminated wisdom, and uncontaminated wisdom as the cause of nirvāṇa. For this reason it is called Abhidharma, because it increases the wisdom of hearing and the rest.

Or: all conditioned things are entirely abandoned, and one delights only in nirvāṇa; therefore it is called supreme. The truths of suffering, origination, and the path serve the wisdom of nirvāṇa. The Buddha's teaching is to rely on the Dharma, not on persons. The verse says:

> If one departs from the discernment of dharmas, there is certainly no other
> Supreme skillful means that can extinguish the afflictions.
> Because of afflictions, the world drifts in the ocean of existence.
> For this reason the Buddha taught the Abhidharma.

The treatise says: "If one departs from the discernment of dharmas, there is no supreme skillful means that can extinguish the afflictions." The Buddha told Ānanda: "While I am alive, rely on me. After my passing into nirvāṇa, there is no other refuge. You should rely on the sūtras; do not let them be forgotten and lost."

The Buddha said: "For one who is old and decrepit to go forth and master the three baskets of my teaching is extremely rare." The three baskets are: first, the sūtras; second, the vinaya; third, the miscellaneous basket [Abhidharma].

### The Story of the Eighteen-Headed Fish

In the time of Kāśyapa Buddha, there was a bhikṣu, intelligent and broadly learned, fully versed in the Tripiṭaka, who habitually used abusive speech toward monastics and laypeople, calling them owls, fish, dogs, and various birds and beasts. After death, due to this karmic retribution, he was reborn in the ocean as a great fish. Its body was enormous, with eighteen heads, and it endured manifold sufferings. Even when the World-Honored One appeared in the world, it had not yet been freed.

Later, fishermen numbering a thousand, while struggling to catch fish, snared this creature. They could not haul it out. Afterward, several thousand men together dragged it ashore and saw its strange form with many heads, terrifying in appearance. The World-Honored One observed and knew its retribution was about to end. He then led the great assembly to the seashore, spread a seat, sat down, and called to the fish: "Tripiṭaka! Were you not the Tripiṭaka bhikṣu of a former buddha's time?" The fish, hearing this, wept until its eyes were brimming with tears. At that time the World-Honored One explained the past causes. The fish gave rise to remorse and ceased eating altogether. Because of this, it died and was reborn in heaven. At that time, the assembly who heard the Dharma each obtained great benefit in their respective vehicles.

### The Aggregates, Bases, and Elements

The five aggregates cannot encompass all dharmas; therefore the teaching is not based on the five aggregates alone. First there is the defiled; afterward one seeks the pure. To purify defilement, contaminated dharmas are taught first, and uncontaminated, unconditioned dharmas afterward. The verse:

> When a bhikṣu's mind is tranquil,
> With regard to things, permanent severance is attained.
> One can exhaust the boundary of birth-and-death,
> And never again receive future existence.

## Fascicle Two

The treatise says, as there is a verse:

> The appearance of buddhas is joy;
> The teaching of the true Dharma is joy;
> The harmony of the saṃgha is joy;
> Practicing together with courageous effort is joy.

The treatise states: In Uttarakuru the clothing weighs one liang. In the heaven of the Four Great Kings, the clothing weighs half a liang. In the heaven of the Thirty-Three, clothing weighs one zhu. In the Yāma heaven, clothing weighs half a zhu. In the Tuṣita heaven, clothing weighs one-quarter zhu. In the Nirmāṇarati heaven, clothing weighs one-eighth zhu. In the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven, clothing weighs one-sixteenth zhu.

The clothing of the form-realm heavens cannot be weighed. If those realms have weight, why say it cannot be weighed? The devas of the form realm, according to their body-size, wear garments that are like radiant purity; therefore one cannot speak of them [in terms of weight].

[The text continues with detailed discussions of manifest form, the six defects in defining unmanifest form, the distinction between "earth" and "earth element," the aggregates and bases, and citations of numerous verses from sūtras and the Abhidharma itself.]

## Fascicle Three

The three categories: first, aggregate (*skandha*); second, gate of arising (*āyatana*); third, lineage (*dhātu*). Aggregate means "collection." Arising means "gate": mind and mental factors arise and grow through the gate-meaning; eye consciousness arises with the eye as its gate. The sūtra demonstrates the gate-meaning with six [bases], yet mental factors number twelve. Therefore the sūtra says: "Conditioned by eye and form, eye consciousness arises. The coming together of the three is contact; together with contact arise feeling, perception, and volition." And so on through to: "Conditioned by mind and dharmas, mental consciousness arises, the coming together of the three..."

The lineage-meaning is the element-meaning. Just as within a single mountain there are many lineages of copper, iron, gold, and silver, which are called "many elements," so too within a single body or a single continuum there are eighteen types of dharma-lineages, called the eighteen elements.

> Like a fish dwelling in mud,
> Leaping in the water and making it turbid:
> The inverted perception within ignorance
> Defiles and afflicts in just the same way.

[The text continues with discussions of the eighty thousand dharma-collections, the difference between space-element and consciousness-element, visibility and resistance among the eighteen elements, and the three types of discrimination (intrinsic, deliberative, and recollective).]

## Fascicle Four

[Discusses the momentary nature of dharmas, the eight kinds of views, which elements are "seeing" and which are "non-seeing," the relationships between eye, form, and eye consciousness across different existential levels, and the twenty-two faculties.]

The treatise states: "The sūtra speaks of twenty-two faculties: eye faculty, ear faculty, nose faculty, tongue faculty, body faculty, mental faculty, female faculty, male faculty, life faculty, pleasure faculty, pain faculty, joy faculty, sorrow faculty, equanimity faculty, faith faculty, effort faculty, mindfulness faculty, concentration faculty, wisdom faculty, the faculty of 'I shall come to know the unknown,' the faculty of full knowledge, and the faculty of one who has fully known."

What sūtra teaches this? There was a brahmin named Jānussonin who came to the Buddha, joyfully greeted him, sat to one side, and said to the Buddha: "How many faculties do you establish to encompass all faculties?" The Buddha said: "I teach twenty-two faculties that encompass all faculties. If anyone denies this and claims to teach other faculties, know that such a statement has words but no meaning."

## Fascicle Five: Analysis of Faculties (*Indriyanirdeśa*), Part One

[The text discusses the five physical faculties as having dominance over four functions, the four [mental] faculties over two, and the remaining faculties over various aspects of the path. It includes the famous verse:]

> Just as a person with clear eyes
> Can avoid visible dangers,
> So a wise person in the world
> Can, through wisdom, avoid future suffering.

> Much learning enables one to know the Dharma;
> Much learning enables one to depart from sin.
> Much learning discards what is meaningless;
> Much learning attains nirvāṇa.

[The text discusses nutrition and the life-faculty, male and female entry into the womb, the contaminated mind that drives rebirth, the twenty-two faculties' dominance in their respective domains, the definitions of the five feeling-faculties, the three roots established in relation to the paths of seeing, cultivation, and beyond-learning, and the life-faculty as exclusively karmic-maturation.]

The verse:

> The lifespan of Uttarakuru is definitely one thousand years.
> In the western and eastern [continents], it decreases by half and one day.
> In this continent [Jambudvīpa], the lifespan is indefinite.
> At the end it is ten [years]; at the beginning, it is immeasurable.

[The text concludes with the story of Brahmā requesting the Buddha to turn the Dharma wheel, presented in full narrative detail, ending with Brahmā's verse and the Buddha's acceptance:]

> The World-Honored One's appearance is like the udumbara flower;
> Through vast kalpas it is hard to encounter, yet now we meet it once.
> Universally for the benefit of sentient beings to gain the Dharma's benefit,
> We beg you to open wide the gate of sweet dew.

The World-Honored One, hearing Brahmā's verse of supplication, aroused great compassion and with the eye of the Way surveyed all sentient beings: some with dull faculties, attached to existence; some with middling faculties, slightly weary of birth-and-death; some with sharp faculties, not heedless. Having truly known the world, he accepted Brahmā's request, consented to turn the Dharma wheel, and spoke this verse:

> Great Brahmā King, listen well and carefully.
> The Dharma I have realized is supremely subtle and wondrous.
> I shall now expound it broadly for you and all,
> So that through the long night you may obtain peace and happiness.

[The text ends with discussions of the life-faculty as exclusively karmic-maturation, lifespans across the four continents, and the verse on the holy life.]

> The holy life wondrously established,
> The noble path well cultivated,
> When the lifespan ends, one rejoices,
> Like casting off all manner of disease.
