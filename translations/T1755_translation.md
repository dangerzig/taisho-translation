# 阿彌陀經義記
## Commentary on the Meaning of the Amitābha Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1755

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, taught by Zhiyi (智顗).

---

The supremely sage one, extending compassion, observed the faculties of beings and responded with his manifestation, guiding the six destinies and universally aiding the ten directions. Following conditions, he ascended or descended; according to circumstances, he manifested in pure or defiled realms. Such is the power of skillful skillful means to attract and guide beings toward bodhi. Therefore, the greatly awakened Amitābha, through the power of his former great vows, manifested his form in the Land of Ultimate Bliss, where he presently abides at the site of awakening. Those of the three grades who aspire to be born there all enter the assembly of the assured. His form and appearance are supremely excellent; his lifespan is beyond thought. Jeweled trees and celestial flowers all expound the Dharma; pure winds and flowing waters both proclaim wondrous sounds. Hearing the teaching of suffering and emptiness, one realizes the patience of non-arising. Śākyamuni, the sage-lord, with profoundly deep original vows and tireless compassion, transforms this defiled realm where the five pains blaze and the eight sufferings press hard. He broadly illuminates admonition and exhortation, extending his teaching universally to all beings, revealing the wondrous method of rebirth through ten recollections. The four assemblies practice in accordance with this, cultivating contemplation as taught. There are both extensive and abbreviated expositions, and the times and places differ: at Vulture Peak he proclaimed the three kinds of pure karma; at Śrāvastī he expounded the protective mindfulness of the six directions.

"Amitābha" is a Sanskrit word from India; translated in China it means "Immeasurable Life." The supreme title of the lord of that land establishes this excellent name. "Sūtra" means "constant"; it issues from the mouth of the sage. This was taught by the World-Honored One, speaking of matters concerning the Western Land; hence it is called a sūtra. This single teaching, though concise in text, is truly rich in meaning, speaking in general terms of the Western Land of Peace and Nourishment.

First, explaining the name: from the person the designation is established; by cultivating in accordance with the teaching, one is reborn in that land. Second, distinguishing the substance: the true suchness of the dharma-nature, contemplated with a mind of truth, one realizes the fruit of permanence and bliss. Third, the essential purpose: the conditions and capacities suited to the Pure Land, with wondrous bliss and adornments, and transformation-images that welcome and receive. Fourth, the function and power: destroying attachment to love and views, the afflictions of the five abodes, until both principal and subsidiary defilements are exhausted. Fifth, the teaching classification: combining the Distinct and incorporating the Shared, encompassing both the immature and mature stages of ghee; this constitutes the classification of the teaching.

### Preface Section

The text is divided into three sections: preface, main discourse, and transmission. There is no separate preface here; the general preface contains six elements.

First, "Thus": a word of certainty. The great sage observes beings' capacities. For those of much anger, he teaches briefly the profound and wondrous. For those of much desire, he broadly reveals the secret and esoteric. For those of much ignorance, he teaches in a middle way. His words are never spoken in vain but respond to beings according to their conditions.

"I heard": The Tathāgata's correct Dharma is immeasurable and boundless. What I have heard is like a tiny drop; what I have not heard is like the great ocean.

"At one time": When there is a response to a stimulus, at the moment of capacity and awakening, in that very assembly, the ordinary is transformed into the holy.

"Buddha": This directly designates the lord of the teaching. He is worthy of offerings, omniscient, possessing the four kinds of unobstructed eloquence, the six supernatural powers, the three kinds of clear knowledge, and the eight liberations. With a single voice he expounds the teaching, and each class of being hears and understands according to its kind.

"In the kingdom of Śrāvastī": The name means "Of Wide Renown." It had many renowned worthies and was also rich in precious treasures. Seekers of enlightenment and merchant caravans from all kingdoms gathered there. It is also called "Lacking Nothing."

"The Jeta Grove of the Garden of the Benefactor of Orphans": Prince Jeta donated the trees and first established the gate-pavilions; Sudatta, who relieved the poor, spread gold upon the ground and together they built the vihāra.

"Together with a great assembly of bhikṣus": "Great" has three meanings: they were revered by celestial kings and great persons, hence called "great"; they comprehensively knew both inner and outer scriptures and teachings, hence called "many"; they surpassed the ninety-five other paths, hence called "superior." "Bhikṣu" is here rendered "eliminator of want." At the causal stage it has three meanings: almsman, frightener of māras, and destroyer of evil. At the fruition stage it has three virtues: worthy of offerings, slayer of bandits, and non-arising. "Saṃgha": four or more persons performing karmic procedures in harmony, with affairs and principles unobstructed, upholding and maintaining the correct teaching.

"One thousand two hundred fifty persons": The assemblies of the three Kāśyapas numbered one thousand in all; Śāriputra and Maudgalyāyana had two hundred fifty. They constantly accompanied the sage-lord as the assembly that bore witness to the trustworthiness of the teaching. The elder Śāriputra, foremost in wisdom, is rendered "Son of the Śāri Bird." Mahāmaudgalyāyana, distinguished for supernatural power, is here rendered "Reciter." Mahākāśyapa, also called Pippala, is rendered "Tortoise Clan," and also "Light-Drinker." Mahākātyāyana, skilled in clear discourse, is rendered "Fan-Cord." Mahākauṣṭhila, whose eloquence was unobstructed, is rendered "Great Knee." Revata is rendered "Room-Star," and also called "Constant Sound-Maker." Cūḍapanthaka is translated as "Little Roadside-Born." Nanda is rendered "Joyful." Ānanda is rendered "Joyfully Pleased." Rāhula is rendered "Impediment-Holder." Gavāṃpati is rendered "Ox-Trace." Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja is rendered "Run-and-Shut-the-Door." Kālodāyin is rendered "Great Rough-Dark." Kapphiṇa is rendered "Room-Star Constellation." Bakkula is rendered "Good Appearance." Aniruddha is rendered "As-Wished."

Mañjuśrī is here rendered "Wondrous Virtue." Ajita is rendered "Without the Three Poisons." Gandhahastī is rendered "Fragrant-Intention." "Constant Vigor" is a term from this land. "Such ones as these" summarizes the assembly of bodhisattvas. Śakra Devānām Indra is rendered "Able Heavenly Lord." "And all the hosts of devas" is the general summary of those who heard together. The enumeration of the assembly in the preface is complete.

### Main Discourse

The main discourse has two parts: first, it clarifies the two kinds of fruition, dependent and direct, of that buddha; next, it exhorts beings to seek rebirth.

The first is further divided into two: the earlier section establishes, the later section explains. The earlier section establishes the realm; the later section clarifies the lord of that land. This sūtra addresses Śāriputra throughout; unlike other sūtras, there are no interlocutors. This sūtra was spoken by the Buddha without being asked, and among the twelve divisions of the teaching, it also does not possess all of them, lacking the two kinds of verse. The remaining divisions are also not fully present. The reason it does not address bodhisattvas is that the teaching adapts without fixed method: the intent is to cause ordinary beings and śrāvakas to become weary of this world and aspire to that one.

In establishing the dependent fruition, near and far are first clarified. "From here, passing through ten trillion buddha-lands to the west, there is a land called Ultimate Bliss." Compared to the Worthy-Chief's description, this is still the lower grade, but in contrast to the sahā world it is called "Ultimate Bliss." That land has three names: "Ultimate Bliss" contrasts with suffering; "Peace and Nourishment" is named from its function; "Immeasurable Life" names the land after its person.

Next, the direct fruition is presented: the name Amitābha. In truth his lifespan is measurable, but others cannot count it. Since Avalokiteśvara will succeed him, those of lower stages cannot know; thus "immeasurable" is merely what is taught.

From "Why is that land" onward is the explanation of the dependent fruition. There are three parts: first a general statement, then detailed explanation, then a conclusion. The general statement is as in the text.

From "seven rows of trees" onward is the detailed explanation. There are four parts. First, the jeweled trees, pools, and pavilions of marvelous beauty. Second, celestial flowers and celestial music displaying radiant adornment. Third, the proclamation of the factors of the path. Fourth, the trees performing musical sounds.

The trees are in seven rows, all made of four treasures: gold, silver, lapis lazuli, and crystal. Seven-layered nets, seven rows of trees. When gentle breezes stir them, they produce harmonious sounds; upon hearing these sounds, one immediately enters the correct stage. Waters of eight excellent qualities are full to the brim: light, pure, cool, soft, sweet, and without odor; when drunk they harmonize the body; after drinking, no illness arises. The bottom is spread with golden sand. On the four sides, steps and paths are composed of four treasures. In the pools, lotus flowers are as large as cartwheels, with dazzling colors and subtle, wondrous fragrance and purity. Above are pavilions also adorned with four treasures.

Second, celestial music resounds without being struck; the strings produce immeasurable sounds of Dharma-transformation. Hearing them arouses the mind of compassion; upon hearing, one awakens to the Way. Six times day and night, māndārava flowers rain down. These celestial flowers are supremely wonderful, called māndārava; their colors are incomparably beautiful and their fragrance is richly sweet. Constantly, in the early morning, beings fill their garment-lapels with flowers and make offerings to the buddhas of the other directions, ten trillion in number, returning to their own land just at mealtime. "Garment-lapels" are flower-holding vessels, shaped like a box with a single leg, held in the hands for making offerings.

Third, birds of various colors: white cranes, peacocks, parrots, and mynahs, as well as water-birds of similar kinds. The kalaviṅka has a wondrous, soaring voice that can serve as a simile for the Buddha's voice. The jīvajīvaka, "shared-life bird," has two heads sharing one body; living and dying together equally, hence called "shared-life." These various birds, six times day and night, proclaim the five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and Eightfold Path. Their wondrous sounds are harmonious and elegant; these are the names and meanings of the Dharma-gates within the factors of the path.

The first five faculties are: faith, vigor, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom. Next, the five powers: the names are the same as above, but as the faculties grow and have power they are further designated. The seven factors of bodhi: mindfulness, investigation, vigor, joy, equanimity, concentration, and so forth. The eight factors of the correct path: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration. Through skillful cultivation of the factors of the path, one attains the vision of truth and reaches the stage of no-more-learning. One should not think these birds were born from karmic retribution; they are all transformation-manifestations made by that buddha, who wishes the sounds of the Dharma to flow and circulate.

Fourth, the seven rows of trees and jeweled nets produce wondrous sounds, like a hundred thousand kinds of music performed simultaneously. Naturally, all beings give rise to minds of mindfulness of the Buddha, mindfulness of the Dharma, and mindfulness of the Saṃgha, awakening to the patience of non-arising, blissfully content and at ease.

### The Direct Fruition

Next, the direct fruition is distinguished. The text again has two parts: first, the lord of that land; next, the assemblies. The lord is discussed in two aspects: first, the immeasurability of radiant light; next, the limitlessness of lifespan. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* says: if one wishes to attain immeasurable radiance and limitless lifespan, one should study the prajñā-pāramitā.

Regarding the assemblies, first the śrāvakas are distinguished, then the bodhisattvas. A question arises: it was previously said there are innumerable śrāvakas, yet how can it later say that all sentient beings born in that land are all non-retrogressing? The explanation: in that land, those of the Two Vehicles are also all non-retrogressing. The non-retrogression of the Two Vehicles is for the sake of ordinary beings; the non-retrogression of bodhisattvas is for the sake of śrāvakas.

### Exhortation to Seek Rebirth

From "sentient beings who hear this" onward is the second part, the exhortation to seek rebirth. There are three sections: first, the direct exhortation to seek rebirth; next, citing evidence for the exhortation; third, the concluding exhortation, which itself has two parts: first the direct exhortation, then the demonstration of the method of rebirth.

Within the first, there are again two: first the direct exhortation, then the explanation of the exhortation's meaning.

From "Śāriputra, with few roots of goodness" onward is the demonstration of the method. A question: it first says one cannot be born there with few roots of goodness, yet how can it later say that from one day to seven days, with mind undistracted, one can be born there? The answer: it is not a matter of many or few days, but specifically depends on the depth or shallowness of one's sincerity. If one can maintain an undistracted mind for seven days, at the end of that person's life, Amitābha Buddha, through the power of his former vows, will manifest transformation-buddhas to welcome and receive. With mind unconfused, one immediately attains rebirth. Why? Because at the moment of death, a single thought of earnest and devoted mind is sufficient to attain passage.

"I see this benefit" explains the meaning: one should resolve to make vows and cultivate with single-minded practice. Vows establish the adornment; practice and vows support each other. One will certainly attain rebirth.

From "as I now" onward is the citation of evidence. It is not I alone who praise that buddha and directly exhort beings to seek rebirth. The buddhas of the six directions all encourage and praise, extolling with voices like the sands of the Ganges, each in their own land exhorting rebirth in that country. If men and women hear the name of this sūtra, they are all protected and kept in mind by all the buddhas, and all attain non-retrogression from the mind of unsurpassed bodhi. Therefore you should all trust my words and what the buddhas teach, resolve to make vows, and all will attain non-retrogression and be reborn in that land.

Those buddhas also praise Śākyamuni for being able to accomplish what is difficult: in the sahā world of mixed evil and the five turbidities, he teaches sentient beings the Dharma that is hard to believe, and they attain bodhi. A defiled land has deep obstructions and heavy turbidities of the five kinds. Desire and anger constitute the turbidity of afflictions; the five wrong views constitute the turbidity of views. These two are fundamental. Taking them up as causes, one obtains a life of continuous succession. Upon the five aggregates, the designation "sentient being" is provisionally established. These four, experienced through time, are called the turbidity of the kalpa. In a defiled land, suffering is great and its turbidities are extremely heavy. In a co-inhabited pure land, its turbidities are light. Following the conventions of other realms, these names exist, but without such oppressive torments, it is called "Ultimate Bliss."

### Transmission Section

From "when the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra" onward is the transmission section. The teaching of the sūtra being complete, the four assemblies, devas, humans, asuras, and the like were joyful and faithful in their acceptance. The sage-lord is as rare to encounter as the udumbara flower; how fortunate to see him now! The correct Dharma is hard to hear, yet now it has been heard. What was previously not understood is now understood. Possessing these three joys, they were therefore delighted, and having attained this within their hearts, they leaped with boundless joy.
