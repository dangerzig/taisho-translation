# 佛垂般涅槃略說教誡經
## Sūtra of the Teaching Bequeathed by the Buddha on the Eve of His Parinirvāṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0389

Translated from the Chinese. Yao Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

When the Buddha Śākyamuni first turned the wheel of the Dharma, he liberated Ājñātakauṇḍinya. His final teaching liberated Subhadra. All those who could be liberated had already been liberated. Between the twin śāla trees, he was about to enter nirvāṇa. At that time, in the middle of the night, all was still and silent, and he briefly spoke the essentials of the Dharma for his disciples:

"You bhikṣus, after my passing, should honor and treasure the prātimokṣa as one in darkness encountering light, as a poor person obtaining a jewel. You should know that this is your great teacher. Were I to remain in the world, it would be no different from this. Those who uphold the pure precepts must not engage in buying and selling, acquiring fields and residences, keeping servants, slaves, or livestock. All forms of cultivation and all kinds of wealth should be kept far away, as one would avoid a pit of fire. You must not fell trees and grasses, plow the earth and dig the ground, compound medicines, practice divination about fortune and misfortune, gaze up at the constellations to calculate waxings and wanings, or make calendrical computations. None of these are appropriate. Moderate your body, eat at the proper time, and live in purity. Do not participate in worldly affairs or serve as messengers. Spells, elixirs of immortality, socializing with the powerful, and intimate familiarity: none of these should be done. You should straighten your minds in right mindfulness and seek liberation. Do not conceal faults and flaws, or display strange abilities to delude the multitude. Regarding the four kinds of offerings, know the proper measure and know contentment. Accept offerings as they come and do not accumulate them. This, in brief, describes the marks of upholding the precepts. The precepts are the very foundation of liberation in right order; therefore they are called the prātimokṣa. Relying on these precepts, one gives rise to all dhyāna concentrations and the wisdom that extinguishes suffering. Therefore, bhikṣus, you should uphold the pure precepts and never violate them. If a person can uphold the pure precepts, then they can possess wholesome dharmas. Without pure precepts, no wholesome merit can arise. Therefore you should know: the precepts are the foremost abode of merit, peace, and security.

"You bhikṣus, having already established yourselves in the precepts, should control the five sense-faculties. Do not let them run loose into the five desires. It is like a cowherd who holds a staff and watches over his cattle, not letting them run wild and trample others' crops. If you let loose the five sense-faculties, not only will the five desires become boundless and uncontrollable, but it is also like a vicious horse that without a bridle will drag a person into a pit. If one is robbed and injured, the suffering lasts but a single lifetime; but the harm of the five sense-faculty thieves extends across many lifetimes. The harm is extremely grave, and one cannot be careless. Therefore the wise control them and do not follow them, guarding against them as one would against thieves, not letting them run free. Even if they do run free, before long they will be seen to wear away and perish. The master of these five sense-faculties is the mind. Therefore you should well control the mind. The fearfulness of the mind surpasses poisonous snakes, fierce beasts, hateful enemies, and great fires raging out of control; none of these are sufficient as metaphors. It is like a person holding a vessel of honey who, moving about carelessly, looks only at the honey and does not see the deep pit. It is like a mad elephant without a hook, or a monkey that has found a tree, leaping and bounding, impossible to restrain. You should urgently subdue the mind and not let it run loose. Those who let this mind run loose destroy all wholesome endeavors. If you control it in one place, nothing is impossible. Therefore, bhikṣus, you should diligently strive to subdue the mind.

"You bhikṣus, when receiving food and drink, should regard it as taking medicine. Whether good or bad, do not increase or decrease your response. Take just enough to support the body and remove hunger and thirst. Like a bee gathering from flowers, taking only the nectar without harming the color or fragrance, a bhikṣu likewise, receiving offerings from others, takes only what removes affliction. Do not seek too much and thereby destroy their wholesome minds. Like a wise person who estimates the strength of an ox and the load it can bear, not overloading it to the point of exhaustion.

"You bhikṣus, during the day, diligently cultivate wholesome dharmas and do not waste time. In the first and last watches of the night, also do not neglect practice. In the middle watch, recite the sūtras to refresh yourselves. Do not let sleep cause your entire life to pass in vain with nothing attained. Be mindful that the fire of impermanence burns all the worlds. Seek your own liberation swiftly; do not sleep. The thieves of affliction constantly seek to kill you; they are worse than enemies. How can you sleep and not rouse yourself in alarm? The poisonous serpent of affliction sleeps within your heart, like a black viper sleeping in your room. You should use the hook of upholding the precepts to remove it swiftly. Once the sleeping serpent has been expelled, then you may sleep peacefully. To sleep before it is expelled: this is a person without shame. The garment of shame, among all adornments, is foremost. Shame is like an iron hook that can restrain a person from wrongdoing. Therefore, bhikṣus, you should always have shame and never be without it even briefly. If you depart from shame, you lose all merit. Those who have a sense of shame possess wholesome dharmas. Those without shame are no different from birds and beasts.

"You bhikṣus, even if someone were to dismember you joint by joint, you should control your minds and not give rise to anger. You should also guard your mouths and not utter harsh words. If you indulge in angry thoughts, you obstruct your own path and lose the benefit of merit. The virtue of patience surpasses what holding the precepts and ascetic practice can achieve. One who can practice patience may truly be called a person of great strength. If one cannot joyfully endure the poison of harsh abuse as if drinking sweet nectar, one cannot be called a person of wisdom who has entered the path. Why? The harm of anger can destroy all wholesome dharmas and ruin one's good reputation. In this life and future lives, no one will wish to see such a person. You should know that an angry mind is more fearsome than a raging fire. You should always guard against it and not let it enter. The thief that plunders merit: nothing surpasses anger. For laypeople immersed in desires, who are not practitioners of the path and have no Dharma to control themselves, anger may still be forgiven. But for one who has left home and practices the path, a person without desires, to harbor anger is utterly unacceptable. It is like a thunderclap producing fire from within a cool, clear cloud: this should not be.

"You bhikṣus, you should touch your shaven heads. Having abandoned adornment and fine appearance, you wear dyed robes, hold alms-bowls, and beg for your livelihood. Seeing yourselves thus, if pride arises, you should swiftly extinguish it. To foster pride is improper even for laypeople in the world; how much more so for one who has left home and entered the path, who for the sake of liberation humbles the mind and begs for food?

"You bhikṣus, a flattering and crooked mind is contrary to the path. Therefore you should straighten your minds. Know that flattery is nothing but deception. For one who has entered the path, there is no place for it. Therefore you should correct your minds, taking straightforwardness as your foundation.

"You bhikṣus, you should know that a person of many desires, because they seek much gain, has much suffering. A person of few desires, without seeking or craving, is free from this affliction. The mere practice of few desires is already something to be cultivated; how much more so when few desires can give rise to all wholesome merit? A person of few desires does not flatter or deceive to win others' approval, nor are they led about by the sense-faculties. One who practices few desires has a mind that is open and free from anxiety and fear. In every situation there is surplus and never insufficiency. One who has few desires has nirvāṇa. This is called few desires.

"You bhikṣus, if you wish to be free from all suffering, you should contemplate contentment. The dharma of contentment is the very abode of wealth, happiness, peace, and security. A contented person, though sleeping on the ground, is still at ease. A discontented person, though dwelling in a heavenly palace, is still unsatisfied. A discontented person, though rich, is poor. A contented person, though poor, is rich. A discontented person is constantly led about by the five desires and is pitied by those who are content. This is called contentment.

"You bhikṣus, if you seek the peace and happiness of quiet non-activity, you should leave behind commotion, and alone, dwell in seclusion. Those in quiet places are honored and revered by Śakra and all the devas. Therefore you should abandon your own assembly and others' assemblies, dwell alone in empty and secluded places, and contemplate the root of suffering's cessation. Those who delight in crowds receive the sufferings of crowds, like a great tree where many birds gather, which then faces the calamity of withering and breaking. Bound to the world, one sinks into manifold sufferings, like an old elephant mired in mud, unable to extricate itself. This is called seclusion.

"You bhikṣus, if you are diligently energetic, nothing is difficult. Therefore you should be diligently energetic, like a small stream of water that flows constantly and can eventually pierce through stone. If the practitioner's mind repeatedly slackens and stops, it is like drilling for fire and stopping before heat is generated: though you wish to obtain fire, fire is difficult to obtain. This is called diligent energy.

"You bhikṣus, seek good teachers, seek good assistants, and do not be forgetful. If you are not forgetful, the thieves of affliction cannot enter. Therefore you should always gather your mindfulness and keep it in your heart. If you lose mindfulness, you lose all merit. If the power of mindfulness is firm and strong, though you enter among the thieves of the five desires, you will not be harmed by them. It is like wearing armor and entering battle: there is nothing to fear. This is called not forgetting mindfulness.

"You bhikṣus, if you gather the mind, the mind will be in concentration. Because the mind is in concentration, you can know the marks of arising and ceasing in the world. Therefore you should constantly strive diligently to cultivate and gather all concentrations. If you attain concentration, the mind will not be scattered, just as a household that conserves water well maintains its embankments. The practitioner likewise, for the sake of the water of wisdom, well cultivates dhyāna concentration so that it does not leak away. This is called concentration.

"You bhikṣus, if you have wisdom, you will be free from attachment. Always examine yourselves and do not let faults persist. This is how, within my Dharma, you can attain liberation. If you do not do this, you are neither a practitioner of the path nor a layperson; there is no name for you. True wisdom is the sturdy ship for crossing the sea of old age, sickness, and death. It is the great bright lamp in the darkness of ignorance. It is the good medicine for all illness and suffering. It is the sharp axe for felling the tree of afflictions. Therefore you should increase your own benefit through the wisdom of learning, reflection, and cultivation. If a person has the illumination of wisdom, though they lack the divine eye, they are a person who sees clearly. This is called wisdom.

"You bhikṣus, if you engage in all kinds of idle discussion, your minds will be scattered. Though you have left home, you are still not liberated. Therefore, bhikṣus, you should swiftly abandon the scattered mind of idle discussion. If you wish to attain the happiness of tranquil extinction, you should only well extinguish the affliction of idle discussion. This is called not engaging in idle discussion.

"You bhikṣus, with regard to all kinds of merit, you should always be single-minded. Abandon all negligence as you would leave behind an enemy. Whatever the greatly compassionate World-Honored One wished to benefit, he has already fully accomplished. You need only diligently put it into practice. Whether in the mountains, in empty marshes, under trees, or in quiet rooms in secluded places, be mindful of the Dharma you have received and do not let it be forgotten. Always encourage yourselves and diligently cultivate it. Do not die in vain and afterwards be filled with regret. I am like a good physician who knows the illness and prescribes the medicine. Whether you take it or not is not the physician's fault. I am also like a good guide who shows people the right road. If they hear but do not follow, it is not the guide's fault. If you have any doubts about the Four Noble Truths of suffering and the rest, you may ask about them quickly. Do not harbor doubts without seeking resolution."

At that time the World-Honored One repeated this three times, yet no one asked a question. Why? Because the assembly had no doubts.

At that time, Aniruddha observed the minds of the assembly and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The moon may become hot and the sun may become cold, but the Four Noble Truths the Buddha has taught cannot be altered. The Buddha's teaching that suffering is truly suffering cannot be made into happiness. Arising is truly the cause; there is no other cause. If suffering is extinguished, it is because the cause is extinguished; when the cause is extinguished, the effect is extinguished. The path of suffering's cessation is truly the correct path; there is no other path. World-Honored One! Among the bhikṣus in this assembly, those whose work is not yet accomplished, seeing the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, will naturally feel grief. But those who have newly entered the Dharma, hearing what the Buddha has taught, will all attain liberation, like seeing a flash of lightning in the night and immediately seeing the path. Those whose work is already accomplished and who have crossed the sea of suffering will only think: 'The World-Honored One enters parinirvāṇa. How swift it is!'"

Though Aniruddha spoke these words, all in the assembly had fully comprehended the meaning of the Four Noble Truths. The World-Honored One, wishing to make this great assembly firm and steadfast, with a mind of great compassion spoke again for the assembly:

"You bhikṣus, do not harbor sorrow. Even if I were to remain in the world for a full kalpa, our meeting would eventually end. A meeting without parting can never be. The Dharma that benefits oneself and others is already fully complete. Were I to remain longer, there would be no further benefit. All those who could be liberated, whether among devas or humans, have already been liberated. Those not yet liberated have all been given the causes and conditions for their future liberation. From now on, my disciples, transmitting it from one to another and practicing it: this is the Tathāgata's Dharma-body, always present and never extinguished. Therefore you should know: all in the world is impermanent. Meeting must have parting; do not harbor sorrow. Such is the nature of the world. You should be diligently energetic and seek liberation swiftly. With the light of wisdom, extinguish all the darkness of delusion. The world is truly fragile and without solidity. My present attainment of extinction is like the removal of a severe illness. This is what should be cast off, this evil thing falsely called a body, submerged in the great sea of birth, old age, sickness, and death. What wise person, having removed and extinguished it, as if having slain an enemy, would not rejoice?

"You bhikṣus, you should always be single-minded and diligently seek the way out. All things in the world, whether moving or unmoving, are marked by decay and instability. Now you should stop; do not speak further. The time is about to pass. I am about to enter nirvāṇa. This is my final teaching."
