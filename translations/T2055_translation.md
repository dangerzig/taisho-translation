# 玄宗朝翻經三藏善無畏贈鴻臚卿行狀
## Account of the Conduct of Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha, Scripture-Translator of the Xuanzong Court, Posthumously Granted the Title of President of the Court of State Ceremonial

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2055

Tang dynasty, composed by Li Hua (李華).

---

The Tripiṭaka śramaṇa Śubhakarasiṃha: his complete Sanskrit name should be Śubhākarasiṃha. In the Tang pronunciation, this correctly translates as "Pure Lion." Translated by meaning, his name is Śubhakarasiṃha ("Good and Fearless"). He was a native of the kingdom of Magadha in Central India, residing at Nālandā Monastery in the city of Rājagṛha. He was originally of the kṣatriya class. As a kṣatriya, he renounced worldly glory and honor and left home in accordance with the Buddha's teaching. His spirit was pure and empty, and his undertaking of the Way was vast and distinguished. He was thoroughly proficient in dhyāna and wisdom and wondrously accomplished in the dhāraṇī gate of the Tripiṭaka. With a single mind he traveled through the five regions of India. His fragrant reputation had long been disseminated. Out of great compassion to benefit sentient beings, his conditions led him to journey eastward.

On his way, he reached the territory of North India. His fame resounded to Mahācīna (Great China). Our august sovereign assembled the worthy and good, and dispatched envoys to welcome him. In the fourth year of Kaiyuan (716 CE), a bingshen year, he brought great quantities of Sanskrit manuscripts and arrived at Chang'an. He was first lodged at the South Tower Quarters of Xingfusi.

Subsequently, in the fifth year, a dingsi year, at Bodhisi, he translated the *Sūtra of the Dhāraṇī of the Most Victorious Mind Fulfilling All Wishes of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva, the Method for Seeking Retention through Hearing*, in one fascicle. The śramaṇa Siddha served as translator of the language, and the śramaṇa Wuzhu composed the literary text and served as scribe. The Sanskrit manuscripts that his preceptor had brought were, by imperial decree, all submitted to the palace. Because of this, there was not yet an opportunity to broadly translate the sūtras.

In earlier times, the Preceptor Wuxing had traveled to India to study. Having completed his studies, he spoke of returning, but on his way back he reached North India, where he unfortunately passed away. The Sanskrit manuscripts he had brought were, by imperial decree, escorted back and were kept at Huayansi in the western capital. The Preceptor Śubhakarasiṃha, together with the śramaṇa Yixing, selected from those manuscripts several texts of Sanskrit sūtras, all belonging to the dhāraṇī teaching, which the śramaṇa Yixing had not previously translated.

In the twelfth year (724 CE), he accompanied the emperor to Luoyang and was lodged at Dafuxiansi. The śramaṇa Yixing requested the Tripiṭaka Preceptor to translate the *Mahāvairocana-abhisaṃbodhi-vikurvitādhiṣṭhāna Sūtra* in seven fascicles. The complete Sanskrit text of this sūtra had one hundred thousand verses. What was produced now was an extraction of its essentials. The śramaṇa Baoyue served as translator of the language. Yixing served as scribe and steward of the purport, and also edited and arranged the diction and reasoning. The style was half literary and half plain, wondrously in harmony with the profound meaning.

He further translated the *Subāhu-kumāra-tantra* in three fascicles and the *Susiddhikara Sūtra* in three fascicles.

The Tripiṭaka Master loved tranquility and simplicity by nature. In silent contemplation, he settled his spirit. From time to time he opened instruction in dhyāna contemplation, encouraging and urging beginners. His compassionate thoughts and guiding reception were without deficiency. Whether common people or those who came with questions, he analyzed doubts without obstruction. Whenever there were prayers for rain or its cessation during drought or flood, his skills greatly sheltered the community of monastics. The details are as in the separate record.

Later, he submitted a memorial requesting to return to his homeland. An imperial decree stopped him. On the seventh day of the eleventh month of the twenty-third year of Kaiyuan (735 CE), lying on his right side with feet together, he passed away in tranquility in the meditation chamber. His age was ninety-nine; his monastic rains numbered eighty. The dharma-realm was desolate with grief; the imperial heart was shaken with sorrow. He was posthumously granted the title of President of the Court of State Ceremonial and was buried at the Western Hill of Longmen. The President of the Court of State Ceremonial, Li Xian, together with the Vinaya Master Dingbin, the Saṅgha ceremonial master, supervised the funeral. On the eighth day of the eighth month, he was buried at the Western Hill of Longmen. Tears and mourning filled the capital; the mountains and rivers changed color.

### Stele Inscription with Preface for the Late Central Indian Tripiṭaka Preceptor Śubhakarasiṃha of the Great Holy Goodness Monastery in the Eastern Capital of the Great Tang

The Preceptor was the legitimate Brahmanical descendant of a cakravartin king, named Śubhakarasiṃha ("Good and Fearless"). He was a descendant of Amṛtodana ("Sweetdew-Rice King"), the paternal uncle of Śākyamuni Tathāgata. His ancestors, from Central India, ruled separately over Oḍra. His father was called King Buddhahand (Buddhahasta). Because the Preceptor was born with sacred qualities and early combined virtue and skill, his father tested him at various tasks.

At ten he received the precepts; at thirteen he succeeded to the throne. His elder brothers raised armies in revolt. He had no choice but to campaign against them. Sword-blades struck his body; the battle-wheel wounded his head. His army prevailed by righteousness, and his brothers were spared by love. He then addressed the Queen Mother and announced to the assembled ministers: "The recent campaign was a righteous decision of severing personal bonds. Now that I cede the kingdom, I follow my own aspiration." He thereupon transferred the throne to his brother and firmly sought to enter the Way. The Queen Mother sorrowfully consented and bestowed upon him the jewel of the dynastic succession.

He traveled south to the seacoast and found a supremely excellent monastery. He entered the Lotus Samādhi. He vowed to build stūpas by gathering sand, pledging ten thousand. A black snake bit his finger, but he did not retreat. He committed himself to a merchant ship and went to Central India. He secretly cultivated dhyāna recitation, and white light emerged from his mouth. Without wind, for three days the ship traveled ten thousand leagues. Together with the merchants, he encountered bands of robbers and was on the verge of losing his life. He comforted his companions, silently recited mantras, and the seven-koṭi honored one fully manifested his bodily form. The robbers were then annihilated by another enemy force. The enemy then confessed their crimes and submitted in sincerity, pointing out the terrain's dangers and level areas.

He crossed the utter wilderness and forded poisonous waters. Upon reaching the borders of Central India, he encountered its king. The king's consort was the Preceptor's elder sister. The Preceptor's garments were those of an ordinary person, yet his companions honored him with the rites due a sovereign. The king asked and learned the reason, and sighed in admiration beyond words. The bodhisattva kinsmen all returned together on that day. The cloud of compassion spread its shade, and the entire realm sincerely transformed. Thereupon he opened the treasury of the three vehicles, thoroughly investigated the lineage of all the schools, and recited the chapters and verses without remainder. He explained the doctrines of the Nāga Palace and attained the lion's yawn. His name shook the five regions of India, and he was revered as the foremost.

At Nālandā Monastery, the fountain-source of the semblance Dharma and the capital gathering of the assembly of sages, he placed a precious jewel to adorn the forehead of the great image. By day it was like a crescent moon; by night it radiated brilliance. Among the Saṅgha-treasures there was Dharmagupta ("Dharma-Protector"), who held the secret key of the gate of concentration and bore the secret seal of the Tathāgata. His face appeared as if forty, yet he was already eight hundred years old.

The Preceptor prostrated at his two feet and honored him as his root master. The Preceptor saw that in his master's bowl there was food not of that country. He showed it to a certain dhyāna monk, a Chinese. The dhyāna monk saw that the oil cakes were still warm and the millet rice still hot, and exclaimed in astonishment: "China is one hundred and eight thousand leagues from here! That food was cooked in the morning there and arrived here by midday. How supernaturally swift!" All in the assembly were astounded; only the Preceptor remained silent.

The root master secretly told the Preceptor: "The White Horse Monastery in China has just completed its new upper story. I have just received an offering there and returned. Your ability to keep silent: truly you can learn." He then transmitted to him the supreme dhāraṇī teaching. Nāgas and spirits surrounded him, vivid before his eyes. Immeasurable mudrā-contracts were all received in a single instant. On that very day he received consecration and became a teacher of devas and humans, bearing the title of Tripiṭaka.

The Tripiṭaka has six meanings: internally, concentration, precepts, and wisdom; externally, sūtras, vinaya, and treatises. Through dhāraṇī, all are encompassed. The dhāraṇī is the swift wheel, the ocean of liberation and auspiciousness. The buddhas of the three times are all born from this gate. What wisdom-illumination transmits is but a single lamp. Yet since faculties and natures differ, the lamps too are boundless. From this arise the billions of Śākyamunis and the samādhis like motes of dust. The bodhisattva, through the vajra, encompasses all concentrations. The white moon is mysteriously identical with the Dharma-body. Suddenly ascending the stages, one draws near to great awakening. This is its appearance.

The Preceptor made universal reverence at the sacred sites, traversing the vast wilderness in great circuits. He did not regret the hardships and made each visit three times. He shaved his head for Kāśyapa and received Avalokiteśvara's hand placed upon his crown. He once spent a summer retreat at Vulture Peak. A fierce beast blocked the path. He entered deep into a cave. The cave was bright as day. He saw a standing image of the Muni, with attendants on left and right, their forms and colors as if alive.

Central India suffered a great drought. The king requested the Preceptor to pray for rain. Avalokiteśvara Mahāsattva, within a moon-disc, held a kuṇḍikā in hand and poured water upon the ground. He was deeply moved beneath the twin trees. He inquired about past events from those who had been alive in the Buddha's time: those who would not speak, of ten he heard one.

He forged gold into leaf-shaped pages and wrote the Mahāprajñāpāramitā. He smelted silver and erected a stūpa equal to the Buddha's bodily form. His Queen Mother, believing the Preceptor had died, wept until her tears were exhausted and she lost her sight. When a letter was sent inquiring after her health, her vision was restored as before.

After the extinction of the Great Hero, non-Buddhist paths were like a forest. The ninety-six schools each clung to their own views. The Preceptor, following whatever they grasped, instructed and dispelled their doubts. He released the bonds of wrong views at the gate of emptiness and returned the lost ferry of delusion to the path of awakening. The Dharma-cloud, whether large or small, moistened equally. The water of concentration, whether the vessel was round or square, filled it perfectly. He overthrew the banners and drums of heterodox learning and erected the victory-banner of the Mind-King. He caused them to control madness through mindfulness and see the Buddha within the body itself.

His root master rejoiced and said: "Good man! China has conditions for you. You may now proceed." He then bowed his head in farewell and descended eastward. He passed through the kingdom of Kaśmīra. In the middle of the night, he needed to cross a river. The river had no boat or bridge. He floated through the air to cross. He received hospitality from an elder. An arhat descended and said: "I am a sage of the Lesser Vehicle. The great worthy is a bodhisattva who has attained the stages." He then yielded his seat and deferred in respect. The Preceptor presented him with a fine garment, and the arhat then ascended into the sky and departed.

In the kingdom of Udyāna, a white rat circled around him and daily offered golden coins. He lectured on the Vinaya at the court of the Turks, and the Khatun requested the Dharma. He then sat in meditation beneath a tree. The Dharma appeared as golden letters arrayed in the sky. Among the Turkic palace women, one pressed her breasts with her hand, and three streams of milk flew into the Preceptor's mouth. He joined his palms with composed countenance and said: "This was my mother in a former life."

Someone mistakenly raised a blade and struck him three times. His limbs were unharmed. The striker heard only the sound of copper. At the foot of a snowy mountain, beside a great pool, the Preceptor fell ill and did not recover. His root master arrived from the sky and said: "The bodhisattva's body is the same as the world's. He does not abandon birth-and-death. You have long departed from all marks. How can there be illness?" Having spoken, he soared into the sky. The Preceptor was then instantly healed.

His route passed through Tibet. He stayed at the same encampment as the merchants. The Tibetans, covetous of the goods, led their host to surround them. The Preceptor secretly formed a mind-mudrā, and the Tibetan chieftain came to beg forgiveness. Arriving at the western border of China, at night a spirit appeared, saying: "This eastern land is not the domain of this disciple. Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva protects the central provinces." The spirit prostrated at his feet and vanished.

He loaded the sūtras onto a camel. When crossing a river in the western region, a nāga caused the camel's foot to sink beneath a spring. The Preceptor entered the spring for three days, stayed in the Nāga Palace and converted them. He led the camel out onto the bank; the sūtras had no moisture on them.

Emperor Ruizong, supreme in the Way and abundant in virtue, whose mysterious accord was without limit, decreed the monk Ruona and General Shi Xian to go beyond the Jade Gate to the frontier to await his ceremonial arrival. When the Kaiyuan era continued with renewed brilliance, greatly illuminating the imperial transformation, the sagely sovereign dreamed of meeting a tall monk of extraordinary appearance. He personally took up cinnabar and brush and painted his image on the palace wall. When the Preceptor arrived, he matched the dream precisely. The Son of Heaven, with spiritual delight, revered and rejoiced in him. He adorned the inner chapel and honored him as the master of the teaching. From the ranking princesses on down, all knelt at his seat and held his implements.

He honored the great being in the heavenly palace and connected the Brahmanical feast to the imperial seat. He treated the national teacher with the courtesy of the Way of Guangcheng, and brought the ruler to the vehicle of the Tathāgata. Lofty and magnificent was this Dharma-gate; at this time it was most flourishing.

There was a practitioner of arts who held the contracts of ghosts and spirits, who could participate in the transformations of power. By imperial decree, before the emperor, he displayed his supernatural anomalies. The Preceptor sat calmly without moving, and the practitioner's hands and feet could do nothing. The rest of his secret deeds, the world has not heard.

He repeatedly requested to reside outside the palace. By decree, the various monasteries took turns welcoming him. He accompanied the emperor to the Luo capital, and by decree was lodged at Shanshan Monastery. After leaving the palace, monastics and laypeople came in throngs to pay reverence, rushing from all across China and the surrounding lands. The Preceptor received them all, treating the noble and humble as one. Those who beheld his dignified form saw lotuses bloom before their eyes. Those who received his words felt sweet dew descend upon the source of their minds.

Transcendently self-awakened, he said: "There is such a person." Among his Dharma companions, the one he held in highest esteem was the elder Ratnacitta ("Jewel-Thought"). All others he received with the courtesy due a disciple. The Dhyāna Master Yixing, beyond his concentration and wisdom, had skills that encompassed heaven and earth. When there was something not yet understood, he consulted the Preceptor before proceeding.

The Preceptor was of pure substance and transcendent bearing, harmonious in temperament and concise in speech. He did not abandon the discipline, yet body and mind were free. He did not depart from seated meditation, yet his vow-power was perfectly fulfilled. When approached, he always responded; encountering any situation, he was without obstruction. All manner of wondrous skills and arts were his, encompassing the hundred crafts. His great compassion perfumed all universally, and grasses and trees were as his own child. His extent was unknown.

At his own quarters, he cast a golden-bronze spiritual stūpa, saying: "By this merit, I shall respond to conditions and protect the world." He fashioned the mold with his own hands, wondrously surpassing the work of humans and devas. The monastery assembly melted and cast on a grand scale. The courtyard was deep and narrow, and they feared that if wind arose and the fire grew fierce, the disaster would spread to the precious buildings. He smiled and said: "There is no cause for worry. There will be a sign of its own." On the day of the casting, heavy snow filled the sky. When the spiritual stūpa was complete, auspicious flowers fluttered onto the mats.

Before and after, by imperial decree, he dispelled drought and brought rain, extinguished fires, and reversed winds: this was brilliantly apparent to all eyes and ears. With composed ease he made his requests, greatly sheltering the monastic community. The flourishing of the true Dharma rests upon such dragon-elephants; truly so!

He submitted a memorial requesting to return to his homeland. A gracious decree did not permit it. On the seventh day of the eleventh month of the twenty-third year of Kaiyuan (735 CE), lying on his right side with feet together, he entered nirvāṇa in the meditation chamber. His age was ninety-nine; his monastic rains numbered eighty. The dharma-realm was desolate with grief; the imperial heart was shaken with sorrow. He was posthumously granted the title of President of the Court of State Ceremonial. The Court President Li Xian and the Vinaya Master Dingbin, ceremonial monk, were dispatched to supervise the burial at the Western Hill of Longmen. Tears and mourning filled the capital; the mountains and rivers changed color.

His disciple the monk Baosi was the great-grandson of the Honorable Zheng of the Ministry of Revenue of Xingyang. His disciple the monk Mingsi was of the Langye Wang clan. Both were of eminent family and superior talent, transcendently self-awakened. The eloquence born of their minds constituted joyful expression; the wondrous application was the very essence of dhyāna. Of those who entered the Preceptor's inner chamber, there were only these two.

In the year of Qianyuan (758 CE), when the heavenly governance was restored, the Great Sovereign, whose mind verified the unconditioned compassion, personally practiced the unsparing filial piety. Thereupon Brahmā and Indra attended the imperial retinue, and the celestial nāgas aided the army. The evil defilements were swept away, and humans and spirits were purified. The rank of the transmission of the entrustment was glorified, and the teaching flourished greatly.

Yixing, the second Dhyāna Master, thereupon composed gāthā-verses and had them carved in metal and stone. The Dharma is apart from written words; the Way cannot be named. To comfort the gate-disciples' hearts of yearning admiration, like Yanhui's lamenting sigh, the inscription reads:

> Of the Śākya palace, a noble seed; a nāga supporting his emergence from the pool.
> Renouncing the throne, accomplishing the Way; becoming the teacher of devas and humans.
> Liberating beings like motes of dust; practicing the compassion of sweet dew.
> Wind clears the feverish afflictions; moon dispels the darkness of confusion.
> Dharma is originally unborn; I now have nothing attained.
> Following the direction, he propagated the teaching and came to the central kingdom.
> Emperor and empress received him; heavenly flowers filled his robe.
> In the Garden of Delight, the only fragrance heard was campaka.
> Billions upon billions, he tamed their minds; consecrated from of old.
> Śrāvakas manifest now; the Mountain-King, lofty and wondrous.
> The ocean-moon, full and deep; manifesting cessation, which is not cessation.
> The crane-grove mourns in emptiness. By the Yi River, the western hill;
> In the dark stone chamber, the golden coffin is already closed.
> I gaze at the original sun. The two jewels continue the brightness.
> The teaching is honored, its words secret. Those who return to our Dharma:
> Through the provisional, awaken to the real.

The eleventh year of Zhenyuan (795 CE), the year yihai, the fourth month, the wushu new moon, the seventeenth day jiaying: erected. In the first year of Qianyuan, Duke Guo (Guo Ziyi) memorialized that the stūpa precinct should become Guanghuasi, with a specially appointed inspector. The disciples who were senior monks of this monastery: the monk Shanyi of the Monastery of Good Intention, the abbot Guangxiu, the comptroller Zhiman, the former senior disciple Huizhao and Tanzhen, the abbot Ruzhang, the steward Jianggu, the kitchen-master Daoji. The characters were carved by Ma Zhan of Fufeng and Qu Ben of Hedong.

At that time, the thirteenth day of the eighth month of the third year of Ōan (1281 CE), the copying was ordered and completed.

In the intervening years, searching for the original, I happened to obtain the text. I entrusted two kind persons to copy it. Please refer to this for consultation.

The thirteenth day of the eighth month of the third year of Ōan (1281 CE).

The Acting Junior Master of Monks, Kenpō.

Private note: Dhyāna Master Jingxian of Huishansi on Mount Song received the bodhisattva precept karmavācanā ritual from Tripiṭaka Śubhakarasiṃha and consulted him on the subtle and wondrous essential purport of the Mahāyāna. Dhyāna Master Huijing of Ximingsi compiled it into one fascicle titled *Dhyāna Essentials*. This Jingxian is probably the Dhyāna Master Jing who was a disciple of Dhyāna Master Shénxiù. The *Dhyāna Essentials* was compiled by Dhyāna Master Huijing; perhaps Dhyāna Master Yixing further revised it. From the secret mudrā of the mind-ground, following the text, one may discern its purport.

Pǔjì of Mount Song was posthumously named Great-Illumination Dhyāna Master. See the *Song Biographies of Eminent Monks*. Also, the True-Word master Yixing received the purport of the dhyāna method from Dhyāna Master Dàzhào. See the *Collected Essentials of Ono*. Yixing of Songyang Monastery was a disciple of Śubhakarasiṃha and was of the same body. The purport is set forth in the fifth section of the *Song Biographies of Eminent Monks*, in the entry on Yixing.
