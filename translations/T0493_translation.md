# 佛說阿難四事經
## Sūtra on the Four Things Spoken by the Buddha to Ānanda

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0493

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Kuśinagara, about to enter parinirvāṇa. Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "I think of the devas, nāgas, spirits, emperors, and people who have met with the Buddha and heard the Buddha's teachings and instructions, and none have not rejoiced. According to their aspirations and wishes, some have become śramaṇas and attained arhatship; some are householders who uphold the five precepts and upon death are reborn in the heavens. Now that the Buddha is departing from the world, whom shall the devas, nāgas, spirits, emperors, people, and the four groups of disciples depend upon to obtain merit and liberation? From whom will they receive these?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Ānanda! Your mind is compassionate and greatly merciful. Among devas, humans, and all classes of beings, there are none who do not gain liberation because of you. After I depart, the world will be called the age of five turbidities. People's minds will be confused, defiled and disordered by their own corruption. The world will be full of inversions, despising the good and honoring the wicked. This is truly a cause for worry. Though the world be so, I have my sūtras and scriptures, my earnest and compassionate precepts. If people follow them wholeheartedly, merit will naturally return to them. You need not worry. Though I depart from the world, my scriptures and teachings will continue to exist. The great Dharma of the six pāramitās I do not take away with me. Those who practice will attain liberation; it is not bestowed by divine beings. Do you not understand what I say?"

Ānanda then said: "May you please explain it again."

The Buddha said: "There are four great dharmas by which one can obtain merit and also attain the path. The merit is exactly equal to that of being in the Buddha's presence."

Ānanda further said: "May the Buddha explain the four things for me."

The World-Honored One said: "One should nurture the young and weak with a compassionate mind. When one sees birds, beasts, insects, moths, and lowly creatures that depend upon others for their survival, one should always be compassionate and mindful. Whatever food they need, let them obtain nourishment. One must not inflict upon them with knife or staff, or cut off their lives. With a compassionate and sorrowful heart, one should be like a loving mother. If devas, nāgas, spirits, emperors, and people practice this compassion, the great merit they obtain is exactly equal to serving the Buddha in person. This is the first thing.

"When the world has calamities, when floods and droughts are out of balance, when the five grains fail, when the people suffer famine, are unsettled in their native land, and wish to rebel and flee; if the king, ministers, and people possess granaries full of grain, they should reflect on impermanence, knowing that body and life are hard to preserve. Do not cherish your precious grain; rather, cherish human life. One should give rise to a compassionate mind, open the granaries and lend grain, to relieve the destitute and save their lives, and allow them to settle peacefully in their native land. If one's mind is stingy and one does not wish to give, one should carefully reflect: 'When a person first comes into birth, the soul arrives empty-handed, relying on the desire of the two parents to form one's body. In the mother's womb for ten months one is then born, to the delight of one's parents, and one may preserve one's life. On days of anxiety and resentment, it is abruptly cut short, and after extreme hardship one finally dies. The soul does not perish but seeks another body. Whether noble or lowly, all depends on past deeds. Official rank, salary, and the treasures of the realm: do not be deluded by these, thereby disturbing one's lofty virtue. At the end of one's life, the body and its treasures remain in the world; they do not follow one. One should always be compassionate, practicing and cultivating the path of the sūtras. With the Buddha's clear teaching, one should regard people and things as like illusions and transformations, like dreams and echoes. All is empty and cannot endure. Regard the world as entirely thus; this is the true principle. The people of the world are foolish and confused, their minds are inverted, deceiving themselves and leading themselves astray. It is like paying the price of gold for brass. When the body dies and the soul departs, one will fall into the three evil paths.' Carefully reflect in this way, and urgently give charity, as though competing with one's very life. To the poor, the destitute, the beggars, the feeble and aged, and the sick, give whatever is needed, and do not let their lives be cut short. If one holds to this in one's mind, all the Buddhas of the ten directions, the bodhisattvas and great beings, and the devas, nāgas, and spirits will all be compassionate toward one. At the end of one's life, wherever the soul is reborn, one will always receive nobility and eminence, with body and mind at peace, free from calamity and harm, and one will fully obtain the highest wishes. This is exactly equal, without any difference, to making offerings to the Buddha in person when the Buddha was in the world. This is the second thing.

"When a country has many bandits, floods, fires, calamities, strange occurrences, poisonous vapors spreading, and rampant disease, all of these are the doing of the nāga spirits and demon kings of the sea. From them come these poisons, causing severe illness and suffering. These spirits, nāgas, and demons were all once people who were killed by others through hunting, slaughter, fishing, and poisoning. Their souls fell into the sea to become nāgas, or became powerful spirits and transformation-born beings. They all know their past lives, and in fury over past grudges, they create mist and dew, exhale evil and poisonous vapors, and rain them upon the country. At that time, among the people, some die from the poison, some merely become ill, and some are tainted, all because the people of the world committed acts of cruelty, slaughtering living creatures, generating mutual resentment. Those who killed with their own hands die immediately from the poison. Those who aided and took pleasure in the killing all become severely ill. As for those who are tainted or not tainted, this is due to eating meat, whether one has a share in it or not. The wise and discerning, knowing that the sin of killing pursues a person relentlessly, measure others by themselves, seeing them as exactly equal. Practicing in this way, one upholds the Buddha's great path, cultivating the four immeasurable minds of loving-kindness, compassion, joy, and protection, and merit naturally returns to one. If a household that kills offers one meat, one must be cautious and never eat it. One who does not eat it, even though living in an evil age amid bandits, calamities, and poisonous vapors, though dwelling in the midst of it, will not be tainted or infected. If emperors, people, and the wealthy have granaries full of grain, and there are orphans, the solitary, widows, and widowers whose clothing and food are insufficient, who are severely ill and destitute, with no means to help themselves, one should provide medicine, rice-gruel, and care, to allow them to recover and their lives not to end prematurely. One should understand that these people, in their past lives, committed evil deeds, did not believe in the Three Treasures, turned away from the true and toward the false, and that their stinginess and craving brought this about. The distinction between merit and wrongdoing is clear; be careful not to do evil. One should also, with a compassionate mind, teach the foolish and ignorant with the sūtras and Dharma of the Buddha, causing them to uphold the path of the sūtras. If one saves even one person's life, causing the sick to recover, showing them the good path and causing them to uphold the five precepts, being pure for the rest of their lives: this is exactly equal to serving the Buddha in person. This is the third thing.

"In the world there are those of lofty character, pure and free from desire, whether śramaṇas or brahmacārins, who embrace the sūtras and scriptures, and whose every word accords with the Dharma and precepts. Emperors, ministers, and people should maintain a reverent and respectful mind, going to them to receive the precepts and practices. What these lofty ones speak with their mouths is entirely the transmitted teachings of all the Buddhas, causing people to leave evil and pursue good. Their kindness surpasses that of parents by a hundredfold and more, ensuring that at the end of one's life one does not fall into the three evil paths. One should always be compassionate and reverent toward them. Better to have molten copper poured into one's mouth, or a sharp knife cut one's tongue: be careful never to slander these pure persons. Better to cut off one's own hands than to inflict pain upon them. Better to open one's own belly and take out one's heart and burn it than to become angry at such a person. Even if a foolish person, seeing the sūtras and path of the Buddha, clearly knows the right course, they may yet keep to their company of obstinate fools. But if they run to join the assembly of the wise, receiving the sacred scriptures, they will achieve lofty virtue.

"Śramaṇas and brahmacārins do not engage in buying and selling for profit, which would defile them. Their minds are pure and their conduct is clean, like a luminous maṇi pearl. Protecting and holding their alms-bowls, they labor with their bodies to beg for food. When they have enough to eat, they stop, and do not store surplus. Whether dwelling in monasteries, or in mountains and marshes, under trees, or among tombs, they all know their past lives. Distinguishing truth from falsehood, they compose sūtras and scriptures, serving as bridges for the world, with compassionate and merciful hearts. In sitting and rising, they chant blessings for emperors, ministers, and people, that the country may be peaceful. Such lofty ones, whose virtue instructs the devas, nāgas, and spirits, touching their hearts, do not pursue worldly affairs, and so are not extolled and praised by those who are slaves to desire. If among kings, ministers, and people there are the wise, they should seek out such persons and provide them with proper clothing, food, bedding, and medicine for illness, so that they may dwell in peace and teach the sūtras and precepts, expounding and guiding others, practicing seated meditation and mindful concentration. Through them, some will attain the path, and some upon death will be reborn in the heavens. To clothe and feed a whole country of defiled persons does not compare to wholeheartedly providing for one pure practitioner of the path. The merit of this is vast and great, exactly equal, without any difference, to making offerings to the Buddha in person when the Buddha was in the world. This is the fourth thing.

"Emperors, ministers, and people who regard such persons in this way: the devas, nāgas, and spirits will without exception protect and aid them with joy."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "In my former lives, I practiced these four things, and through the merit they generated, life after life, I brought myself to the attainment of Buddhahood. For this reason I have again taught the four things. Ānanda! You should broadly proclaim them for all the devas, emperors, ministers, and people. The wholesome deeds they perform will bring them their own merit, which will never be in vain. I am about to enter parinirvāṇa; I entrust these four things to you."

When Ānanda heard this sūtra, he was both sorrowful and joyful, and came forward to prostrate at the Buddha's feet.
