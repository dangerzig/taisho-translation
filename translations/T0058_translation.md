# 阿耨風經
## The Anupavana Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0058

Translated by Zhu Tanwulan during the Eastern Jin dynasty

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling in the Vajjian city known as Anupavana. At that time, the World-Honored One arose from his afternoon rest and addressed the Venerable Ānanda: "Come, Ānanda! Let us go together to the Ajitāvatī pool to bathe." "Yes, World-Honored One!" The Venerable Ānanda received the World-Honored One's instruction. Then the World-Honored One, together with the Venerable Ānanda and the accompanying bhikṣus, went to the Ajitāvatī pool. Having arrived at the bank of the Ajitāvatī pool, they removed their robes and placed them on the bank, bathed in the Ajitāvatī pool, emerged from the water, and dried themselves on the bank. Then the World-Honored One addressed the Venerable Ānanda: "Ānanda, the heedless one Devadatta has lost his place and will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue. Have you not heard this said by a certain bhikṣu, Ānanda?"

"I recall that Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue. But why, World-Honored One? Have you not yourself heard this? Indeed, World-Honored One, I heard this said by a certain bhikṣu."

"How is it, Venerable Ānanda? Does the World-Honored One know Devadatta's thoughts and intentions, and his deceitful conduct, through some other means? And yet now the World-Honored One declares without qualification that this Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue. This, Ānanda; the bhikṣu from whom you heard this; whether a senior elder, a young monk, or a junior bhikṣu of little wisdom, may entertain doubt about what the Tathāgata has declared. Why is this? Ānanda, I do not see among devas or in the world, among Māra or Brahmā, among śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas, among the multitudes of devas or humans, anyone concerning whom I would make such a declaration of falling into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue, as I have made concerning Devadatta. Why is this? Ānanda, I declare without qualification that Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue. This, Ānanda: I do not see in Devadatta even a hair's breadth of wholesome dharma. If I did see any, I would not declare without qualification that Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue. Therefore, Ānanda, since I do not see in Devadatta even a hair's breadth of wholesome dharma, I declare without qualification that Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue. Suppose, Ānanda, not far from a town or village there is a great cesspit filled with filth, and a certain person has fallen into it, submerged and invisible. Another person, moved by compassion, wishing to benefit him, wishing to rescue him, wishing to bring him to safety, goes around the edge of that great cesspit looking for him, thinking: 'Is there even a hair's breadth of unsoiled place on this person by which I might grasp him and pull him out?' That person goes around the cesspit searching, but finds not even a hair's breadth of unsoiled place on that person by which he might rescue him. Just so, Ānanda, I do not see in Devadatta even a hair's breadth of wholesome dharma. If there were any, I would not declare without qualification that Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue. Therefore, Ānanda, since I do not see in Devadatta even a hair's breadth of wholesome dharma, I declare without qualification that Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue."

Then the Venerable Ānanda's eyes filled with tears. With joined palms turned toward the World-Honored One, he addressed the World-Honored One: "How extraordinary, World-Honored One! And yet now the World-Honored One declares without qualification that this Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue."

"So it is, Ānanda. So it is, Ānanda. I declare without qualification that this Devadatta will fall into an evil destination, dwelling in the hells for one aeon, beyond rescue. Ānanda, you should listen to the Tathāgata discern the spiritual faculties of great persons; your confidence and delight in the Tathāgata will thereby increase, and joy will arise in your mind."

Then the Venerable Ānanda, with joined palms turned toward the World-Honored One, addressed the World-Honored One: "Now is the time, World-Honored One! This is the fitting moment. We pray, World-Honored One, that you would set forth for the bhikṣus the discernment of the spiritual faculties of great persons. Having heard it from the World-Honored One, these bhikṣus will retain it."

"Ānanda, listen. Attend to it well, and I shall explain."

"Yes, World-Honored One!" The Venerable Ānanda received the World-Honored One's instruction.

The World-Honored One declared: "This, Ānanda: the Tathāgata knows the thoughts and intentions of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by wholesome dharmas and accompanied by unwholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his wholesome dharmas have diminished and unwholesome dharmas have arisen; his wholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess unwholesome dharmas. Yet the wholesome roots he already has are not severed; through this wholesomeness he will again attain wholesomeness. Thus this person proceeds toward the dharma of mental purification. Suppose, Ānanda, as the sun is about to rise, all darkness is extinguished and great light appears. Ānanda, what do you think; after the sun has risen, approaching the time of the meal, would all darkness be extinguished and there be full brightness?"

"Yes indeed, World-Honored One."

"Just so, Ānanda. The Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by wholesome dharmas and accompanied by unwholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his wholesome dharmas have diminished and unwholesome dharmas have arisen; his wholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess unwholesome dharmas. Yet the wholesome roots he has are not severed; he will again attain wholesome dharmas. Thus this person proceeds toward the dharma of mental purification. Suppose, Ānanda, there are seeds that are not damaged, not broken, not rotted, not split, not injured by wind, but kept safely in a vessel. A farmer thoroughly levels his field, and having plowed and tilled it, plants the seeds therein, and heaven sends timely rain to water them. Ānanda, what do you think; would a great yield of seeds be obtained?"

"Yes indeed, World-Honored One."

"Just so, Ānanda. The Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by wholesome dharmas and accompanied by unwholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his wholesome dharmas have diminished and unwholesome dharmas have arisen; his wholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess unwholesome dharmas. Yet the wholesome roots he has are not severed; through that wholesomeness he will again attain wholesomeness. Thus this person proceeds toward the dharma of mental purification. Such, Ānanda, is the Tathāgata's discernment of the spiritual faculties of great persons; thus the Tathāgata fully knows the arising of all dharmas in accordance with dharma.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, the Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by wholesome dharmas and accompanied by unwholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his wholesome dharmas have diminished and unwholesome dharmas have arisen; his wholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess unwholesome dharmas. The wholesome roots he has, which are not yet severed, will all be severed. Thus this person is one whose dharmas will be severed. Suppose, Ānanda, in the late afternoon as the sun is about to set, all light is extinguished and darkness arises. Ānanda, what do you think; when the sun sets, which is not the time of the meal, would all brightness be extinguished and darkness arise?"

"Yes indeed, World-Honored One."

"Just so, Ānanda. The Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by wholesome dharmas and accompanied by unwholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his wholesome dharmas have diminished and unwholesome dharmas have arisen; his wholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess unwholesome dharmas. The wholesome roots he has, which are not yet severed, will all be severed. Thus this person is one whose dharmas will be severed. Suppose, Ānanda, there are seeds that are not damaged, not broken, not rotted, not split, not injured by wind, but kept safely in a vessel. A farmer thoroughly levels his field and thoroughly plows and tills it and plants the seeds therein, but heaven does not send timely rain to water them. Ānanda, what do you think; would a great yield of seeds be obtained?"

"No, World-Honored One."

"Just so, Ānanda. The Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by wholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his wholesome dharmas have diminished and unwholesome dharmas have arisen; his wholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess unwholesome dharmas. The wholesome roots he has that are not yet severed will all be severed. Thus this person is one whose dharmas will be severed. Such, Ānanda, is the Tathāgata's discernment of the spiritual faculties of great persons; thus the Tathāgata knows with certainty and in full the arising of dharmas in accordance with dharma.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, the Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; I do not see in this person even a hair's breadth of wholesome dharma. This person is utterly filled with evil unwholesome dharmas bound by fetters, and will in turn receive a retribution of suffering and torment, experiencing birth, old age, sickness, and death. Thus, when this person's body breaks apart at death, he will be reborn in the hells. Suppose, Ānanda, there are seeds that are broken, cracked, split, damaged by wind, and not kept safely in a vessel. A farmer does not plow his land well, does not level it well, but plants the seeds there, and heaven sends timely rain. Ānanda, what do you think; would a great yield of seeds be obtained?"

"No, World-Honored One."

"Just so, Ānanda. The Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; I do not see in this person even a hair's breadth of wholesome dharma. This person is utterly filled with evil unwholesome dharmas bound by fetters, and will in turn receive a retribution of suffering and torment, experiencing birth, old age, sickness, and death. Thus, when this person's body breaks apart, he is reborn in the hells. Such, Ānanda, is the Tathāgata's discernment of the spiritual faculties of great persons; thus the Tathāgata fully knows the arising of all dharmas in accordance with dharma."

Then the Venerable Ānanda, with joined palms turned toward the World-Honored One, addressed the World-Honored One: "World-Honored One, we have now received these three types of persons. Might there be yet another three types of persons that could be described, or might there not?"

"Ānanda," the World-Honored One said, "this, Ānanda: the Tathāgata may know the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by unwholesome dharmas and accompanied by wholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his unwholesome dharmas have diminished and wholesome dharmas have arisen; his unwholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess wholesome dharmas. Yet the unwholesome roots he has are not yet severed; through these wholesome dharmas he will again attain further wholesome dharmas. Thus this person is one whose dharmas will be extinguished. Suppose, Ānanda, there is a fire burning, blazing of its own accord, and someone adds dry grass and wood to it. Ānanda, what do you think; would the fire be greatly increased?"

"Yes indeed, World-Honored One."

"Just so, Ānanda. The Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by unwholesome dharmas and accompanied by wholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his unwholesome dharmas have diminished and wholesome dharmas have arisen; his unwholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess wholesome dharmas. Yet the unwholesome roots he has are not yet severed; through these unwholesome dharmas he will again attain further unwholesome dharmas. Thus this person is one whose dharmas will be extinguished. Such, Ānanda, is the Tathāgata's discernment of the spiritual faculties of great persons; thus the Tathāgata knows with certainty and in full the arising of dharmas in accordance with dharma.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, the Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by unwholesome dharmas and accompanied by wholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his unwholesome dharmas have diminished and wholesome dharmas have arisen; his unwholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess wholesome dharmas. The unwholesome roots he has that are not yet severed will all be severed. Thus this person proceeds toward the dharma of ultimate purification. Suppose, Ānanda, there is a fire burning, blazing of its own accord, and someone carries the fire and places it on dry ground or on stone. Ānanda, what do you think; would the fire burn greatly?"

"No, World-Honored One."

"Just so, Ānanda. The Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; a person who is accompanied by unwholesome dharmas and accompanied by wholesome dharmas. The Tathāgata then knows the subsequent thoughts and conduct of this person: his unwholesome dharmas have diminished and wholesome dharmas have arisen; his unwholesome dharmas have diminished and he has come to possess wholesome dharmas. The unwholesome roots he has that are not yet severed will all be severed. Thus this person proceeds toward the dharma of ultimate purification. Such, Ānanda, is the Tathāgata's discernment of the spiritual faculties of great persons; thus the Tathāgata fully knows the arising of dharmas in accordance with dharma.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, the Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; I do not see in this person even a hair's breadth of dark conduct. This person is utterly filled with wholesome dharmas, wholesome conduct, and wholesome retribution; his person accords with wholesome destinies. Thus this person should attain parinirvāṇa in this very life. Suppose, Ānanda, a fire has been extinguished, cool, cold, and without heat, and someone places dry grass and wood upon it. Ānanda, what do you think; would fire be obtained?"

"No, World-Honored One."

"Just so, Ānanda. The Tathāgata knows the thoughts and conduct of a certain person; I do not see in this person even a hair's breadth of dark conduct. This person is utterly filled with wholesome dharmas, practicing wholesome conduct with wholesome retribution. Thus this person should attain parinirvāṇa in this very life. Such, Ānanda, is the Tathāgata's discernment of the spiritual faculties of great persons; thus the Tathāgata fully knows the arising of dharmas in accordance with dharma.

"This, Ānanda: among the first three types of persons, the first type has the dharma of purification, the second type has the dharma of cessation, and the third type, when the body breaks apart, falls into an evil destination and is reborn in the hells. Among the latter three types of persons, the first type has the dharma of cessation, the second type has the dharma of purification, and the third type should attain parinirvāṇa in this very life.

"This, Ānanda: I have now explained the discernment of the spiritual faculties of great persons. As the World-Honored One should do out of compassion and for the benefit of his disciples, I have explained this to you. Now you should dwell in a secluded place, sitting at the foot of a tree, practicing dhyāna. Do not be heedless. Do not later come to regret it. This is what I have said; this is my instruction."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The bhikṣus, having heard what the World-Honored One said, rejoiced and undertook to practice it.
