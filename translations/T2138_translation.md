# T2138 勝宗十句義論

# Treatise on the Ten Categories of the Vaiśeṣika School

*Note: This treatise was composed by Candramati (Huìyuè 慧月) and translated by Xuanzang (Xuánzàng 玄奘) during the Tang dynasty. It is the most complete Chinese presentation of the Vaiśeṣika (Shèngzōng 勝宗) philosophical system, which classifies all reality into ten categories (daśapadārtha): substance (shí 實), quality (dé 德), action (yè 業), universality (tóng 同), particularity (yì 異), inherence (héhé 和合), potency (néng 能), impotency (wúnéng 無能), co-sharedness (jùfēn 俱分), and non-existence (wúshuō 無說). The nine substances are: earth, water, fire, wind, space, time, direction, self, and mind. The twenty-four qualities include color, taste, odor, tangibility, number, measure, distinction, conjunction, disjunction, remoteness, proximity, cognition, pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, effort, heaviness, fluidity, viscidity, disposition (saṃskāra), merit (dharma), demerit (adharma), and sound. The five actions are: lifting, throwing, bending, extending, and moving. The text systematically defines each category, enumerates their instances, and analyzes their interrelationships through detailed classifications of which substances possess which qualities, which qualities are perceptible, which are produced or unproduced, and how causation operates within the system. This is a key primary source for understanding the Indian Vaiśeṣika school as received in the Chinese Buddhist tradition.*

---

There are ten categories. First, substance (shí 實); second, quality (dé 德); third, action (yè 業); fourth, universality (tóng 同); fifth, particularity (yì 異); sixth, inherence (héhé 和合); seventh, potency (néng 能); eighth, impotency (wúnéng 無能); ninth, co-sharedness (jùfēn 俱分); tenth, non-existence (wúshuō 無說).

## I. Substance

What is the category of substance? Nine kinds of substance are called the category of substance. What are the nine? (1) Earth, (2) water, (3) fire, (4) wind, (5) space, (6) time, (7) direction, (8) self (ātman), and (9) mind (manas). These are the nine substances.

**Earth:** What is earth? That which possesses color, taste, odor, and tangibility is earth.

**Water:** What is water? That which possesses color, taste, tangibility, and the quality of flowing moisture is water.

**Fire:** What is fire? That which possesses color and tangibility is fire.

**Wind:** What is wind? That which possesses only tangibility is wind.

**Space:** What is space? That which possesses only sound is space.

**Time:** What is time? That which is the notional cause for expressions of "that," "this," "simultaneous," "non-simultaneous," "slow," and "fast" is time.

**Direction:** What is direction? That which is the notional cause for expressions of "east," "south," "west," "north," and so forth is direction.

**Self:** What is self? That which has cognition, pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, effort, disposition, merit, demerit, and so forth, whose nature is such that it is the cause of cognition arising from the conjunction of these: this is self.

**Mind:** What is mind? That which has cognition, pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, effort, merit, demerit, disposition, and is the cause of cognition arising from the non-conjunction of these: this is mind.

## II. Quality

What is the category of quality? Twenty-four qualities are called the category of quality. What are the twenty-four? (1) Color, (2) taste, (3) odor, (4) tangibility, (5) number, (6) measure, (7) distinction, (8) conjunction, (9) disjunction, (10) remoteness, (11) proximity, (12) cognition, (13) pleasure, (14) pain, (15) desire, (16) aversion, (17) effort, (18) heaviness, (19) fluidity, (20) viscidity, (21) disposition, (22) merit, (23) demerit, (24) sound. These are the twenty-four qualities.

**Color:** What is color? That which is apprehended only by the eye, inhering in a single substance, is called color.

**Taste:** What is taste? That which is apprehended only by the tongue, inhering in a single substance, is called taste.

**Odor:** What is odor? That which is apprehended only by the nose, inhering in a single substance, is called odor.

**Tangibility:** What is tangibility? That which is apprehended only by the skin, inhering in a single substance, is called tangibility.

**Number:** What is number? The notional cause for expressions such as "one" or "non-one" regarding the conjunction of all substances is called number; "one-substance" and so forth are the names for number.

**Measure:** What is measure? The minute-substance, the great-substance, the short-substance, the long-substance, the round-substance, and so forth are called measure.

The minute-substance: taking the product of two atoms as the conjunctive cause, produced from two substances, the notional cause for expressing "one substance is minute" is called the short-substance. The long-substance: caused by the accumulation and differentiation of many long substances, the conjunctive product of three atoms and beyond, the notional cause for expressing "one substance is great" is called the great-substance.

The short-substance: taking the product of two atoms as the conjunctive cause, produced from two substances, the notional cause for expressing "one substance is short" is called the short-substance. The long-substance: caused by the accumulation and differentiation of many long substances, the conjunctive product of three atoms and beyond, the notional cause for expressing "one substance is long" is called the long-substance.

The round-substance has two kinds: the atomic and the maximal. The atomic: the conjunction inherent in an atom, the notional cause for expressing "one substance is atomically round," is called the atomic. The maximal: the conjunction of substances such as space, time, direction, and self, the notional cause for expressing "one substance is maximally round," also called "all-pervading," is called the maximal.

**Distinction:** What is distinction? The notional cause for expressions of "one distinct substance" or "non-one distinct substance" regarding the conjunction of all substances is called distinction; "one distinction" and so forth constitute the name.

**Conjunction:** What is conjunction? When two things not in contact come into contact, this is called conjunction. It has three kinds: (1) arising from one's action alone, (2) arising from the actions of both, and (3) arising from conjunction. "Arising from one's action alone" means: produced from one having motion and one without motion. "Arising from the actions of both" means: produced from both having motion. "Arising from conjunction" means: when a substance without motion is produced involving many substances, it conjoins with space and such.

**Disjunction:** What is disjunction? When two things in contact lose contact, this is called disjunction. It has three kinds: (1) arising from one's action alone, (2) arising from the actions of both, and (3) arising from disjunction. "Arising from one's action alone" and "arising from the actions of both" are as explained above for conjunction. "Arising from disjunction" means: when an already-produced resultant substance, through another cause, undergoes disjunction; awaiting the destruction of the resultant substance, it disjoins from space and such.

**Remoteness:** What is remoteness? That which belongs to one time and so forth, is dependent on the cognition of "far," is produced from a single substance, and is the notional cause for expressing "remote": this is called remoteness.

**Proximity:** What is proximity? That which belongs to one time and so forth, is dependent on the cognition of "near," is produced from a single substance, and is the notional cause for expressing "proximate": this is called proximity.

**Cognition:** What is cognition? It is the apprehension of all objects. It has two kinds: (1) direct perception (pratyakṣa) and (2) inference (anumāna).

Direct perception: when the sense-faculties and such are in conjunction with a real substance such as color, and a cognition with the characteristic of apprehension arises, this is called direct perception.

Inference has two kinds: (1) inference from seeing something similar, and (2) inference from not seeing something similar.

Inference from seeing something similar: having seen a mark, relying on the recollection of the relationship between mark and marked, and through conjunction of self and mind, a cognition arises regarding the unseen marked object. This is called inference from seeing something similar.

Inference from not seeing something similar: having seen the relationship between cause and effect, and through the conjunction and opposition of a single meaning, relying on the recollection of that relationship, and through conjunction of self and mind, a cognition arises regarding an object that is forever not directly perceived. This is called inference from not seeing something similar.

**Pleasure:** What is pleasure? That quality of self inhering in a single substance whose intrinsic nature is satisfaction and delight is called pleasure.

**Pain:** What is pain? That quality of self inhering in a single substance whose intrinsic nature is affliction and distress is called pain.

**Desire:** What is desire? The conjunction of self with a single substance, seeking color and other objects, is called desire.

**Aversion:** What is aversion? The conjunction of self with a single substance, seeking to harm color and other objects, is called aversion.

**Effort:** What is effort? The conjunction of self with a single substance, depending on desire, aversion, and the conjunction of self and mind, the exertion that is produced is called effort.

**Heaviness:** What is heaviness? The conjunction of the substances earth and water with a single substance, the cause of falling and sinking, is called heaviness.

**Fluidity:** What is fluidity? The conjunction of the substances earth, water, and fire with a single substance, the cause of flowing and streaming, is called fluidity.

**Viscidity:** What is viscidity? The conjunction of the substance water with a single substance, the cause of earth and other substances cohering, is called viscidity.

**Disposition:** What is disposition? It has two kinds: (1) the cause of memory, and (2) the cause of action. The cause of memory: the conjunction of self with a single substance, the differentiation arising from repeated practice of perception and inferential cognition as conducted. This is called the cause of memory. The cause of action: the momentum (vega) that is produced from actions such as throwing and casting, depending on a single substance with material resistance, is called the cause of action. "Disposition" means momentum.

**Merit:** What is merit? It has two kinds: (1) that which enables progression, and (2) that which enables return. That which enables progression: the conjunction of self with a single substance, the cause of pleasure in a desirable body and such, which is not opposed to the fruit. This is called that which enables progression. That which enables return: the conjunction of self with a single substance, abandoning the conditions of defilement, the cause of correct knowledge and joy, which is not opposed to the fruit. This is called that which enables return.

**Demerit:** What is demerit? The cause of pain and wrong knowledge in an undesirable body and such, the conjunction of self with a single substance, which is not opposed to the fruit. This is called demerit.

**Sound:** What is sound? That which is apprehended only by the ear, inhering in a single substance, is called sound.

## III. Action

What is the category of action? Five kinds of action are called the category of action. What are the five? (1) Lifting, (2) throwing, (3) bending, (4) extending, and (5) moving.

**Lifting:** What is lifting? In the directions above, below, and to the sides, at places in space and so forth, the cause of conjunction and disjunction of atoms and such, inhering in a single substance, is called lifting.

**Throwing:** What is throwing? In the directions above, below, and to the sides, at places in space and so forth, the cause of disjunction and conjunction of atoms and such, inhering in a single substance, is called throwing.

**Bending:** What is bending? In a great or long substance, inhering in a single substance, at a near place there is conjunction and at a far-and-near place there is disjunction: the cause of conjunction is called bending.

**Extending:** What is extending? In a great or long substance, inhering in a single substance, at a near place there is disjunction and at a far-and-near place there is conjunction: the cause of disjunction is called extending.

**Moving:** What is moving? The conjunction of all material substances with a single substance, the cause of conjunction and disjunction, is called moving.

## IV. Universality

What is the category of universality? It is "existence" (bhāva). What is existence? That which is conjoined with all substances, qualities, and actions of the categories, is apprehended by all sense-faculties, and is the notional cause for cognition of "existence" regarding substance, quality, and action: this is called existence.

## V. Particularity

What is the category of particularity? That which is permanent, which operates regarding substances, inhering in a single substance, and which is the cause of the cognition "this is excluded" as well as the cause of the cognition "this is indicated": this is called the category of particularity.

## VI. Inherence

What is the category of inherence? That which causes substances and other things to be inseparable from each other, the notional cause for this cognition: furthermore, its nature is one. This is called the category of inherence.

## VII. Potency

What is the category of potency? That which is the determinate requisite for substances, qualities, and actions in conjunction, whether collectively or individually, each producing their respective results: this is called the category of potency.

## VIII. Impotency

What is the category of impotency? That which is the determinate requisite for substances, qualities, and actions in conjunction, whether collectively or individually, not producing other results: this is called the category of impotency.

## IX. Co-sharedness

What is the category of co-sharedness? Substance-ness, quality-ness, action-ness, as well as the conjunction of their single meanings, earth-ness, color-ness, lifting-ness, and so forth: these are called the category of co-sharedness.

Substance-ness: that which is conjoined with all substances, is the notional cause for the expression "substance" regarding all substances, does not operate regarding quality and action, and is apprehended by eye and tangibility. This is called substance-ness.

Quality-ness: that which is conjoined with all qualities, is the notional cause for the expression "quality" regarding all qualities, does not operate regarding substance and action, and is apprehended by all sense-faculties. This is called quality-ness.

Action-ness: that which is conjoined with all actions, is the notional cause for the expression "action" regarding all actions, does not operate regarding substance and quality, and is apprehended by eye and tangibility. This is called action-ness.

Earth-ness and so forth are likewise.

## X. Non-existence

What is the category of non-existence? Five kinds of non-existence are called the category of non-existence. What are the five? (1) Prior non-existence, (2) posterior non-existence, (3) mutual non-existence, (4) non-conjunction non-existence, and (5) absolute non-existence. These are the five kinds.

**Prior non-existence:** When the causal conditions of a substance, quality, or action have not yet come together and it has not yet come into being: this is called prior non-existence.

**Posterior non-existence:** When a substance, quality, or action, either through the exhaustion of its causal force or through the arising of an opposing condition, though having arisen, is destroyed: this is called posterior non-existence.

**Mutual non-existence:** When various substances and such are mutually absent from each other: this is called mutual non-existence.

**Non-conjunction non-existence:** When existence, substance, and such, at whatever place where there is no conjunction and no inherence: this is called non-conjunction non-existence.

**Absolute non-existence:** When there is no cause, and through the three times something never arises and ultimately never comes into being: this is called absolute non-existence.

## Systematic Analysis of the Nine Substances

Of the nine substances, how many have motion and how many are motionless? Five have motion: earth, water, fire, wind, and mind. Four are motionless: the remaining substances. Just as with motion and motionlessness, the same analysis applies to material and immaterial, having momentum and lacking momentum, having remoteness-and-proximity and lacking remoteness-and-proximity.

Of the nine substances, how many have qualities and how many lack qualities? All have qualities; there is no substance without qualities. Just as all have qualities, so too all have conjunctive causal conditions, have substance-ness, have particularity, are not opposed to their results, and have dependent causes.

Of the nine substances, how many have tangibility and how many lack it? Four have tangibility: earth, water, fire, and wind. Five lack tangibility: the remaining substances. Just as with tangibility and non-tangibility, the same applies to being a producing-substance, being the cause of quality and action in substance, and being collective or non-collective.

Of the nine substances, how many have color and how many lack it? Three have color: earth, water, and fire. Six lack color: the remaining substances. Just as with color and colorlessness, the same applies to visibility and invisibility, being opposed to the eye and not opposed to the eye.

Of the nine substances, five are permanent and four are differentiated. Among the four, what is not produced is permanent; what is produced is impermanent. Just as with permanence and impermanence, the same applies to having and not having real substance, having and not having subtle parts, being opposed and not opposed to causation, having particularity at the boundary and having particularity not at the boundary, and being round or not round.

Of the nine substances, five are sense-faculties and four are not. What are the five? Earth, water, fire, wind, and space are sense-faculties. Of these five: the nose-faculty is earth, the taste-faculty is water, the eye-faculty is fire, the skin-faculty is wind, and the ear-faculty is space.

## Qualities Attributed to Each Substance

**Earth** is said to have qualities by virtue of fourteen: (1) color, (2) taste, (3) odor, (4) tangibility, (5) number, (6) measure, (7) distinction, (8) conjunction, (9) disjunction, (10) remoteness, (11) proximity, (12) heaviness, (13) fluidity, (14) disposition.

**Water** is said to have qualities by virtue of fourteen: (1) color, (2) taste, (3) tangibility, (4) number, (5) measure, (6) distinction, (7) conjunction, (8) disjunction, (9) remoteness, (10) proximity, (11) heaviness, (12) fluidity, (13) viscidity, (14) disposition.

**Fire** is said to have qualities by virtue of eleven: (1) color, (2) tangibility, (3) number, (4) measure, (5) distinction, (6) conjunction, (7) disjunction, (8) remoteness, (9) proximity, (10) fluidity, (11) disposition.

**Wind** is said to have qualities by virtue of nine: (1) number, (2) measure, (3) distinction, (4) conjunction, (5) disjunction, (6) remoteness, (7) proximity, (8) tangibility, (9) disposition.

**Space** is said to have qualities by virtue of six: (1) number, (2) measure, (3) distinction, (4) conjunction, (5) disjunction, (6) sound.

**Time** is said to have qualities by virtue of five: (1) number, (2) measure, (3) distinction, (4) conjunction, (5) disjunction. Direction likewise.

**Self** is said to have qualities by virtue of fourteen: (1) number, (2) measure, (3) distinction, (4) conjunction, (5) disjunction, (6) cognition, (7) pleasure, (8) pain, (9) desire, (10) aversion, (11) effort, (12) merit, (13) demerit, (14) disposition.

**Mind** is said to have qualities by virtue of eight: (1) number, (2) measure, (3) distinction, (4) conjunction, (5) disjunction, (6) remoteness, (7) proximity, (8) disposition.

## Perceptibility of the Twenty-Four Qualities

Of the twenty-four qualities, how many are objects of direct perception and how many are not? Color, taste, odor, and tangibility are sometimes objects of direct perception and sometimes not. When are they objects of direct perception? When they inhere in a large, non-unitary substance. When are they not? When they inhere in an atom or in the product of two atoms. Sound is always an object of direct perception. Like color, taste, odor, and tangibility, number, measure, distinction, conjunction, disjunction, remoteness, proximity, fluidity, viscidity, heaviness, and momentum are likewise. Cognition, pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, and effort are objects of direct perception of the self. Merit, demerit, and disposition are only non-objects of direct perception.

## Produced and Unproduced Qualities

Among these qualities, how many are produced and how many are unproduced? Cognition, pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, effort, merit, demerit, disposition, disjunction, remoteness, proximity, and sound are always produced. The rest are sometimes produced and sometimes not. Color, taste, odor, and tangibility belonging to earth are all produced. Color, taste, tangibility, fluidity, and viscidity that are in conjunction with atoms are unproduced. Those in conjunction with the product of two atoms and above are produced. Heaviness is likewise. As with the unproduced and produced, so with the permanent and impermanent. The same applies to those belonging to water, those belonging to fire, and tangibility belonging to wind. Fluidity belonging to earth and fire is entirely produced.

The number "one," the distinction "one distinct," being produced or unproduced, follow whatever substance they conjoin with. Numbers of "two" and above, distinctions of "two" and above, are all produced. Great-substance, minute-substance, short-substance, and long-substance are all produced. Round-substance is entirely unproduced.

Conjunction of all material substances and material-immaterial substances is produced. As with produced and unproduced, so with permanent and impermanent.

## Causation of the Twenty-Four Qualities

Among these qualities, what serves as the cause for what? Color, taste, odor, and tangibility have their own kind as cause: this applies to conjunction with the product of two atoms and above. Having conjunction with fire as cause applies to the color, taste, odor, and tangibility of atoms belonging to earth, the fluidity belonging to earth and fire, the heaviness belonging to earth and water, and the fluidity and viscidity belonging to water. Those in conjunction with the product of two atoms and above have their own kind as cause.

The number "one" and the distinction "one distinct" in conjunction with the product of two atoms and above have their own kind as cause. Numbers of "two" and above and distinctions of "two" and above have both same-kind and different-kind causes. The distinction "one" and "one distinct" have the cognition of "that" as their cause.

Great-substance and long-substance have as their cause the accumulation and differentiation of many great-substances and long-substances. Minute-substance and short-substance have "two-substance" as their cause.

Conjunction and disjunction have as their cause: one's action, both actions, conjunction, and disjunction. Remoteness and proximity have as their cause: belonging to one time and such, the relationship of dependence on the cognitions of "far" and "near."

## Four Kinds of Cognition

Cognition has two kinds: direct perception and inference. Direct perception has four kinds: (1) doubtful cognition, (2) decisive cognition, (3) erroneous cognition, and (4) correct cognition.

**Doubtful cognition:** what is its cause? Having non-unitary similar dharmas as the prior direct perception, depending on the recollection of their respective particularities, with the conjunction of self and mind as cause.

**Decisive cognition:** what is its cause? Having doubtful cognition as prior, depending on the recollection of the respective particularities and their confirmation, with the conjunction of self and mind as cause. The cognition "this is definitely this" is called decisive cognition.

**Erroneous cognition:** what is its cause? Having non-unitary similar dharmas as the prior direct perception, depending on the recollection of their respective particularities and seeing, with the conjunction of self and mind as cause. A cognition of dark determination is called erroneous cognition.

**Correct cognition:** what is its cause? Having non-unitary similar dharmas as the prior direct perception, depending on the respective particularities and direct perception, with the conjunction of self and mind as cause. A cognition without error is called correct cognition.

As with direct perception, so with inference.

## Types of Direct Perception

Direct perception has three kinds: (1) that produced from four conjunctions, (2) that produced from three conjunctions, and (3) that produced from two conjunctions.

**Four-conjunction direct perception:** the apprehension of real substances such as color, taste, odor, tangibility, number, measure, distinction, conjunction, disjunction, remoteness, proximity, heaviness, fluidity, viscidity, momentum; the substances earth, water, and fire; actions such as lifting; existence; and also, excluding sound, the conjunction with potency, impotency, and sound-ness regarding co-sharedness, and the cognition regarding potency and impotency of sound: all these have self, sense-faculty, mind, and object as the four-conjunction cause.

**Three-conjunction direct perception:** the cognition regarding sound and its conjunction with potency, impotency, sound-ness, and existence: this has self, sense-faculty, and mind as the three-conjunction cause.

**Two-conjunction direct perception:** the cognition regarding pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, effort, and their potency, impotency, co-sharedness, and existence: this has self and mind as the two-conjunction cause.

Inference: the cognition produced from the prior cognition of conjunction, single-meaning conjunction, and opposition, depending on the recollection of the relationship of conjunction and such, with the conjunction of self and mind as cause.

Pleasure and pain have merit and demerit as cause, with four, three, or two conjunctions as cause. Desire and aversion have as their cause the recollection of pleasure and pain, erroneous cognition, and the conjunction of self and mind. Effort has as its cause desire, aversion, and the conjunction of self and mind, as well as the life-condition as cause. Without desire, it serves as the cause of actions such as breathing in and out. Momentum has as its cause the momentum of throwing and casting and produced actions.

Merit and demerit have desire and aversion as prior, depending on hearing, recollection, and the distancing from merit and demerit, with the conjunction of self and mind as cause. The disposition that is the cause of memory has direct perception and inferential cognition as prior, depending on the conduct of self and mind in conjunction as cause. Sound has three kinds: (1) produced from conjunction, (2) produced from disjunction, and (3) produced from sound. Produced from conjunction: the conjunction of material substances, together with the conjunction of material substances in space. Produced from disjunction: the disjunction of material substances, together with the disjunction of material substances in space. Produced from sound: the conjunction and disjunction of material substances, depending on unobstructed space, with sound as cause.

## Further Analysis

Of the twenty-four qualities, how many inhere in one substance and how many in more than one? Color, taste, odor, tangibility, measure, remoteness, proximity, cognition, pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, effort, merit, demerit, disposition, heaviness, fluidity, viscidity, momentum, and sound: these twenty-one all inhere in one substance. Conjunction and disjunction inhere in two substances. Number sometimes inheres in one substance and sometimes in more than one: the number "one" inheres in one substance; the number "two" and above inheres in more than one. As with number, so with distinction.

Of the twenty-four qualities, how many pervade their substrate and how many do not? Color, taste, odor, tangibility, number, measure, distinction, remoteness, proximity, fluidity, viscidity, heaviness, and momentum pervade their substrate. The rest do not.

Of the twenty-four qualities, which are mutually opposed? Conjunction, disjunction, produced sound, the producing sound, and all sound-results are mutually opposed. Merit is opposed to the results of pleasure and correct cognition. Demerit is opposed to the results of pain and erroneous cognition. All cognition and disposition are opposed to each other as results. The discriminating cognition and all cognition-and-disposition are opposed as results. The final sound is opposed to all causes. The final quality of self is likewise. Pleasure and pain are opposed to desire and aversion as results. Merit and demerit are opposed to them as causes. Desire and aversion are opposed to effort as results, and pleasure and pain are opposed as causes. Intermediate sounds are likewise.

Of the quality of self, effort, pain, and the conjunction of material substances: these two are not opposed as result and cause. The disposition of self, the disposition that is the cause of memory and pain: these are not opposed as result and cause. The disposition that is the cause of memory is opposed as result. The disposition that is the cause of action is not opposed to the conjunction of material substances as result. Numbers of "two-ness" and above, and cognitions of "two" and above, are not opposed as results. As with numbers of "two" and above, so with distinctions of "two" and above, remoteness, and proximity.

The colors, tastes, odors, and tangibles belonging to earth that are in conjunction with atoms are opposed to the great conjunction as non-result and cause. Conjunction and disjunction are mutually opposed as non-result and cause. The colors and such of a single-substance atom and the produced same-kind colors and such of two atoms and above are not opposed as result. The colors and such of the final composite substance and the colors and such of the causal same-kind are not opposed. The intermediate composite-substance colors and such are not opposed to the same-kind result-and-cause colors and such. The colors and such of a single substance are mutually not opposed as non-result and non-cause.

All qualities are not opposed to substance.

Of the twenty-four qualities, how many have real substance and how many do not? All have real substance. As having real substance, being without quality, without motion, not being the conjunctive causal condition: this is the mark of being a quality of a real substance. Being immaterial and without subtle parts is likewise.

Of the five actions, how many have real substance and how many do not? All have real substance. As having real substance, inhering in one substance, being immaterial, without quality, without subtle parts, being the cause of conjunction and disjunction, being the agent and the acted-upon, not accumulating, and being the mark of substance: being the cause of momentum that is dependent on throwing and casting, and not having same-kind as cause. This is likewise.

Of the five actions, which inheres in which substance? Lifting action has all earth, water, fire, wind, and mind as its substrate. As with lifting, so with throwing and moving. Bending has as its substrate the great or long substance that is the differentiated result of the extension and relaxation of the most-subtle parts arranged together. As with bending, so with extending.

Of the five actions, how many pervade their substrate and how many do not? All pervade their substrate. Some say: those inhering in atoms and mind pervade their substrate; those inhering in the product of two atoms and above do not.

Among the various actions, those that are internal have the body and its causal conditions, the nose, taste-faculty, skin, eye-faculty, and mind conjoined with the body as the conjunctive causal condition. Among these, the first bodily action has desire as prior, with the conjunction of self and effort as the non-conjunctive causal condition. The second and subsequent actions also have disposition as the non-conjunctive causal condition. As with bodily action, so with actions in mind and in subtle parts. The actions of nose, taste-faculty, skin, and eye-faculty have the conjunction of self and effort and the conjunction of the body as the non-conjunctive causal condition in the first instance. The second and subsequent have disposition also as the non-conjunctive causal condition. As with the actions of nose and other faculties, so with actions in a pestle and other implements, and in garlands, ornaments, perfumes, and such attached to the body.

The falling action of a sleeping person's body has heaviness as the non-conjunctive causal condition in the first instance. The second and subsequent have heaviness and disposition as the non-conjunctive causal condition. The breathing action of a sleeping person, or when a sleeping person is unwilling: the first has the life-condition as prior, with effort and the conjunction of self as the non-conjunctive causal condition. The second and subsequent also have disposition as the non-conjunctive causal condition. As with the downward flow of water, the first has fluidity as the non-conjunctive causal condition. The second and subsequent have fluidity and disposition. The upward burning of fire and the sideward blowing of wind have merit, demerit, and the conjunction of self as the non-conjunctive causal condition in the first instance. The second and subsequent are as previously stated.

The first action of the four great-element atoms in producing the body has merit, demerit, and the conjunction of self as the non-conjunctive causal condition. The second and subsequent are as previously stated. As for producing the body, so for producing trees and such, transformation, and actions in the product of two atoms and above.

The approaching and departing actions of mind have merit, demerit, and the conjunction of self as the non-conjunctive causal condition in the first instance. The second and subsequent are as previously stated. The foot-action of earth, manifesting the benefit and non-benefit and karmic ripening of sentient beings, has merit, demerit, and the conjunction of self as the non-conjunctive causal condition in the first instance. The second and subsequent are as previously stated.

Actions of striking and throwing in earth, water, and fire have conjunction, heaviness, fluidity, effort, and momentum as the non-conjunctive causal condition. As appropriate, there are actions of lifting and such. In fire, heaviness is excluded. In wind, fluidity is excluded. In mind, striking and throwing are excluded.

## Final Analysis

Is existence produced or unproduced? It is certainly unproduced. As with the unproduced, it is permanent, without quality, without motion, and without subtle parts. It has real substance, quality, and action, and excluding universality, potency, impotency, co-sharedness, and particularity, what it conjoins with is one; it is the cause for a cognition of "same."

Particularity is distinct from substance; it operates regarding substance, inhering in one substance, and is the cause of the cognition "this is excluded" and the cause of the cognition "this is indicated." It operates in space, direction, and time. The cause of the conception of space and such is permanent, unproduced, without quality, without motion, and without subtle parts. Excluding existence, potency, impotency, co-sharedness, and particularity, what it conjoins with is not one.

Inherence is one, permanent, unproduced, without subtle parts, and immaterial. The cause of cognition that substance, quality, action, universality, particularity, potency, impotency, and co-sharedness arise and remain together.

Is potency produced or unproduced? It is certainly unproduced. As with the unproduced, it is permanent, without quality, without motion, without subtle parts, and immaterial. Regarding substance, quality, and action, each excluding universality, potency, impotency, co-sharedness, and particularity, what it conjoins with is not one; it is the cause for a cognition of "same." This is called potency. Impotency is likewise.

The co-sharedness of substance-ness pervades all substances in conjunction, is one, immaterial, without subtle parts, without motion, without quality, permanent, and unproduced. All substances are mutually collective in this, and it is different from quality and action. Quality-ness, action-ness, earth-ness, and so forth are likewise.

## The Five Kinds of Non-existence

Of the five kinds of non-existence, how many are permanent and how many are impermanent? Prior non-existence is impermanent, being opposed to the arising of substance, quality, and action. Posterior non-existence, mutual non-existence, and absolute non-existence are all permanent, not being opposed to substance and such. Non-conjunction non-existence is partly permanent and partly impermanent. How is it permanent? When, for example, a substance of earth and such is not conjoined with a certain quality; or when substance-ness and such, universality, particularity, potency, and impotency, excluding their own substrate, are not conjoined with other places; or when existence is not conjoined with universality and such. How is it impermanent? When a substance, though not yet in relation with another substance, will necessarily come into relation; and the substance, quality, and action that will necessarily conjoin with a real substance are currently absent from it.

Of the five kinds of non-existence, how many are objects of direct perception and how many are not? All are non-objects of direct perception. They also do not operate dependent on other things; they are all objects of inference.

Of the ten categories, how many are objects of knowledge and how many are not? All are objects of knowledge, and precisely this is the cause of cognition.

This completes the Treatise on the Ten Categories of the Vaiśeṣika School, composed by Candramati, translated by the Tripiṭaka Master Xuanzang of the Great Tang.
