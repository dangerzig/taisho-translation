# 佛說大乘戒經
## Sūtra on the Mahāyāna Precepts Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1497

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time the Buddha was dwelling at Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada.

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus, saying: "There are those who destroy their precepts and squander their lifespan, and those who sever their wholesome roots. Renunciation is difficult to encounter; arouse a mind of diligence and firmly protect the precepts. If any bhikṣus seek liberation within the Buddha-Dharma, they must distance themselves from all evil and suffering. As the Buddha has said: it is better to give up one's body and life and face impermanence than to indulge the mind and transgress the precepts. If a person gives up their life, only one lifetime is ruined; but if one breaks the precepts, it causes hundreds of thousands of lifetimes of sinking into the evil realms. If a person upholds the precepts, they shall come to see the Buddha. The precepts are the supreme adornment; the precepts are the supreme wondrous fragrance; the precepts are the supreme cause of joy. The substance of the precepts is pure, like cool and clear water, capable of removing the affliction of heat. The Dharma of the precepts is supreme; the incantations of the world and the poisons of dragons and serpents cannot penetrate it. Upholding the precepts brings renown; upholding the precepts brings peace and happiness; when life thus comes to its end, one is reborn in the heavens."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! One who transgresses the rules of conduct is like a blind person who cannot see the many colors, and also like one without feet who cannot travel the path, they are far removed from nirvāṇa and cannot reach the other shore. A person who upholds the precepts accomplishes all the treasures of the Dharma; they are like a fine vessel, perfectly round and solid, capable of holding all precious jewels. When such a vessel is cracked and damaged, the precious jewels are scattered and lost; likewise, when one breaks the rules of conduct, all wholesome dharmas are abandoned. To have previously transgressed the precepts and then wish in one's heart to seek nirvāṇa is like removing one's eyes and ears and then holding a mirror up to one's face, what could one possibly accomplish?"

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Women are without trustworthiness and should not be approached. Though royal favor may be excellent, it should not be relied upon. Foam upon water has no substance and cannot be grasped. Wealth and status are impermanent and cannot long remain. Appearances are like flowers, changing in an instant. Life is like ripe fruit and cannot long endure; like crossing a swift current by boat, like temporarily dwelling in a crumbling house. It is better to eat poison than to drink wine; it is better to enter a great fire than to indulge in desires."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, the bhikṣus and all the bodhisattvas present were filled with great joy, received it with faith, and put it into practice.
