# 大堅固婆羅門緣起經
## 大堅固婆羅門緣起經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0008

Translated from the Chinese. 宋 施護等譯

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling on Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain near Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly. At that time, a gandharva prince with five topknots, in the period past midnight approaching the dawn, came to where the Buddha was. His bodily radiance was vast and brilliant, illuminating the entirety of Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain in a single blaze of light. Having arrived at the Buddha's presence, he prostrated with his head at the Blessed One's feet, withdrew to stand to one side, and addressed the Buddha, saying: "Bhagavān! At a certain time I was in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven and witnessed the lord Śakra, the Great Brahmā king, and the assembly of the Sudharmā devas gathered together, proclaiming something. I heard it with my own ears; I received it with my own understanding. What is the meaning of this? I beseech the Bhagavān to instruct me, so that I may comprehend."

The Buddha addressed the gandharva prince with five topknots, saying: "Whatever you heard and received at the gathering of Śakra, lord of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, the Great Brahmā king, and the Sudharmā deva assembly, I shall now explain to you as is fitting, so that you may comprehend."

Then the gandharva prince with five topknots again addressed the Buddha, saying: "Bhagavān! At a certain time I was in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven where Śakra, lord of the heaven, the Great Brahmā king, and the Sudharmā deva assembly were gathered together. At that time, there were devaputras who, by reason of their conditions, were newly born in that heaven. At the same time, certain devaputras who had been born there previously, upon seeing those newly born, gave rise to five kinds of extreme delight, namely: long life, physical form, renown, auspiciousness, and retinue. Bhagavān! Among them, certain devaputras spoke thus: 'O devaputras! Observe this newly born devaputra — the devaputras born previously give rise to five kinds of extreme delight: long life, physical form, renown, auspiciousness, and retinue.' At that same time, other devaputras spoke thus: 'O devaputras! This newly born one practiced the holy life in the Dharma of the Buddha Bhagavān, a Śrāvaka; when his body perished and life ended, he received the reward of a fortunate destiny and was born here in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. At the same time, the devaputras born previously give rise to five kinds of extreme delight.' At that same time, still other devaputras spoke thus: 'Excellent, O devaputras! There are four Buddhas, Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas, who appear in the world, proclaim the Dharma, benefit gods and humans, diminish the host of asuras, and augment the host of devas.' At that time, yet other devaputras spoke thus: 'Stop, O devaputras! There are not four Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas appearing in the world. Excellent, O devaputras! There are three Buddhas, Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas, who appear in the world, proclaim the Dharma, benefit gods and humans, diminish the host of asuras, and augment the host of devas.' At that time, yet other devaputras spoke thus: 'Stop, O devaputras! There are not three Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas appearing in the world. Excellent, O devaputras! There are two Buddhas, Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas, who appear in the world, proclaim the Dharma, benefit gods and humans, diminish the host of asuras, and augment the host of devas.' Such were these matters; I beseech the Buddha to explain them."

At that time, Śakra, lord of the heaven, and the Great Brahmā king were in the Buddha's assembly. The Buddha addressed Śakra and the deva assembly concerning this matter, saying: "You should know that within a single time there is no place to accommodate two Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas appearing in the world to proclaim the Dharma." When Śakra and all the devas heard the Buddha's words, they were all filled with joy and their minds were gladdened.

Then the Bhagavān, knowing that Śakra and all the devas were filled with joy, addressed the assembly, saying: "When a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world, he is endowed with eight rare and extraordinary qualities. If you wish to hear of them, you should surpass your previous joy and arouse minds of delight and gladness." The Buddha then addressed Śakra, saying: "Kauśika! Now, for the sake of this deva assembly, explain as is fitting and with delight the eight rare qualities of a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha."

Then Śakra, receiving the Buddha's instruction, proclaimed the eight rare qualities of the Bhagavān: "O devaputras! Whenever a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world, he invariably diminishes the host of asuras, augments the host of devas, and is able to bring benefit and happiness to many beings. Such benefit and happiness is rare.

"Furthermore, O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard. Therefore, when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world and proclaims the teaching of the Dharma for the benefit of gods and humans — namely, the Dharma that destroys wrong views, the Dharma that removes defilement, the Dharma of following right contemplation, the Dharma of purity, the Dharma of comprehending all feelings, the Dharma that removes conceit, the Dharma that tames the torrent of craving, the Dharma that destroys ignorance, the Dharma that severs clinging, the Dharma that abandons attachment, the Dharma of cessation, the Dharma of nirvāṇa — the proclamation of such Dharma is rare.

"Furthermore, O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard. Therefore, when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world and instructs his śrāvakas in the training, namely in all the practices of non-contention that ought to be cultivated — for this reason the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha repeatedly instructs: 'Practitioners should cultivate the practice of non-contention in remote wilderness places and other peaceful locations, whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down; they should withdraw from clamor and tumult, and relying on themselves, in accordance with their own form and their own delight and their own preference, without mixing with others, they should conduct themselves as befits themselves' — such instruction is rare.

"Furthermore, O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard. Therefore, when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world, though he receives and partakes of food and drink in accordance with circumstances, the Tathāgata always obtains the supreme flavor among foods, the right flavor, the foremost flavor, the flavor of non-dispersal. Moreover, when the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha receives food and drink, he is far removed from arrogance; he neither abides in it nor is attached to it; he always departs from fault, arises in right wisdom, and always aspires to liberation. He further instructs all beings in this Dharma. Such is rare.

"Furthermore, O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard. Therefore, when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world, being endowed with supernatural powers, he explains the Dharma of supernatural powers to his śrāvakas, instructing, opening, and guiding them to cultivate it. Such instruction is rare.

"Furthermore, O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard. Therefore, when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world, he is free from all doubt and all disputation; in virtuous Dharma he attains fearlessness. Such freedom from doubt is rare.

"Furthermore, O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard. Therefore, when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world, he is able to act in accordance with what he teaches, and to teach in accordance with how he acts; moreover he instructs, opens, and guides all beings to cultivate the various Dharmas. Such instruction is rare.

"Furthermore, O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard. Therefore, when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world and instructs beings in nirvāṇa and the path to nirvāṇa, it grows and fills without exhaustion. Just as the waters of the Gaṅgā and the Yamunā flow into the great ocean and increase without end, so too the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha instructs beings in nirvāṇa and the path to nirvāṇa, skillfully proclaiming all the Dharmas of nirvāṇa and skillfully establishing beings, enabling them to cultivate and increase without end. Such instruction is rare.

"O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world endowed with these eight rare qualities, I see no difference between past and present in this regard."

When the assembly of devaputras heard this teaching, they were once again surpassingly filled with joy, their minds gladdened, and they all addressed Śakra, saying: "O lord of the devas! We pray that you proclaim once more those eight rare qualities of the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha."

Then Śakra, for the sake of that deva assembly, proclaimed the eight rare qualities of the Tathāgata a second time:

"Furthermore, O devaputras! Whenever a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world, he invariably diminishes the host of asuras, augments the host of devas, and is able to bring benefit and happiness to many beings. Such benefit and happiness is rare. O devaputras! Therefore, when the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard — as has been fully explained above — down to: when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world and instructs beings in nirvāṇa and the path to nirvāṇa, it grows and fills without exhaustion. Just as the waters of the Gaṅgā and the Yamunā flow into the great ocean and increase without end, so too the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha instructs beings in nirvāṇa and the path to nirvāṇa, skillfully proclaiming all the Dharmas of nirvāṇa and skillfully establishing beings, enabling them to cultivate and increase without end. Such instruction is rare. O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world endowed with these eight rare qualities, I see no difference between past and present in this regard." When this was said, the assembly of devaputras was once again surpassingly filled with joy, their minds gladdened.

Then the Bhagavān, knowing that the assembly of devaputras was once again surpassingly filled with joy, again addressed Śakra, saying: "Kauśika! Now proclaim once more the eight rare qualities of a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha."

Then Śakra, receiving the Buddha's instruction, proclaimed the eight rare qualities a third time:

"Furthermore, O devaputras! Whenever a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world, he invariably diminishes the host of asuras, augments the host of devas, and is able to bring benefit and happiness to many beings. Such benefit and happiness is rare. O devaputras! Therefore, when the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world, I see no difference between past and present in this regard — as has been fully explained above — down to: when a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha appears in the world and instructs beings in nirvāṇa and the path to nirvāṇa, it grows and fills without exhaustion. Just as the waters of the Gaṅgā and the Yamunā flow into the great ocean and increase without end, so too the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha instructs beings in nirvāṇa and the path to nirvāṇa, skillfully proclaiming all the Dharmas of nirvāṇa and skillfully establishing beings, enabling them to cultivate and increase without end. Such instruction is rare. O devaputras! When the Tathāgata, the great Teacher, appears in the world endowed with these eight rare qualities, I see no difference between past and present in this regard." When this was said,

the Great Brahmā king, knowing that the deva assembly was once again surpassingly filled with joy and their minds gladdened, then spoke a gāthā:

> Śakra and the host of devas all give rise to such joy,
> Taking refuge, they praise the Buddha Tathāgata,
> Who has well proclaimed the Tathāgata's rare qualities.
> Aforetime they witnessed those newly born in heaven,
> Endowed with form and majesty and radiance —
> Having long cultivated the holy life,
> They attained birth there, possessed of surpassing power.

When the assembly of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven heard this gāthā, they were once again surpassingly filled with joy, their minds gladdened.

Then the Great Brahmā king, knowing that the deva assembly was once again surpassingly filled with joy, addressed them, saying: "If you wish to hear how the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, possessed of great wisdom, has brought benefit and happiness throughout the long night, you should surpass your previous joy and arouse minds of delight and gladness." The deva assembly all addressed the Great Brahmā king, saying: "Excellent, O Great Brahmā king! We pray that you fully explain how the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, possessed of great wisdom, has brought benefit and happiness throughout the long night."

Then the Great Brahmā king fully explained the former causes and conditions of the Tathāgata's great wisdom. The Brahmā king said: "Bhagavān! In a time long past, there was a king named Yojana-pati. At that time there was a brahmin named Dṛḍha, who held the position of chief minister, serving as the king's preceptor; he was wise and greatly intelligent, endowed with great ability and discernment, and skillful in governing affairs of state. The king had a crown prince named Reṇu, deeply beloved by the king, wise and greatly intelligent, also possessed of great ability, and knowledgeable in all matters. Bhagavān! This crown prince Reṇu had six other kṣatriya youths as his companions and associates, who regularly gathered together and played at piling sand. The chief minister Dṛḍha the brahmin also had a son named Jyotiṣprabha, deeply beloved, sharp and intelligent in talent and wisdom, versed in all matters without exception. Bhagavān! After the chief minister had participated in affairs of governance for a considerable period of years, he suddenly passed away at one time. The king heard that his chief minister Dṛḍha the brahmin had passed away, and he was overcome with grief and anguish, his tears flowing in sorrow; striking his knees in distress, he was dazed and stupefied as if beside himself, and said: 'This chief minister of mine had great talent and wisdom; he participated in governing the realm and was truly an excellent counselor; moreover he always shared my pleasures with me, yet he has suddenly died — I am deeply grieved.' At that time, the crown prince heard that his royal father was lamenting because the chief minister Dṛḍha the brahmin had passed away, and he too was overcome with grief and anguish, his tears flowing in sorrow. The crown prince immediately went to where the king was and, having arrived, said: 'Royal Father, there is no need for grief, no need for weeping — why do you harm yourself in such dazed stupefaction? Why so? Royal Father, you should know that Dṛḍha the brahmin has an eldest son named Jyotiṣprabha, possessed of talent and wisdom, sharp and intelligent; if he succeeds to his father's position, he can comprehend affairs of governance — whatever his father understood, this son knows as well. Now that this person is available, why should Your Majesty grieve? Your Majesty should summon him quietly, instruct him as the situation requires, and confer upon the son the office held by his father.'

"The king, having heard this, immediately dispatched a messenger, saying to him: 'Go to the youth Jyotiṣprabha and convey this message: "The king summons you now; you should come here with all speed."' The messenger received the command and immediately went to where the youth Jyotiṣprabha was. Having arrived, he fully conveyed the royal command: 'You are now summoned; you should go there with all speed.' The youth Jyotiṣprabha, hearing the messenger's words, immediately came to where the king was. Having arrived, he offered his respects and was given a seat to one side. The king, with pleasure, repeatedly comforted and reassured him, saying: 'I now tell you truly: though your father has passed away and I have been grieved, I now wish you to succeed to your father's position and serve as chief minister, and to govern the realm together with me.' The youth Jyotiṣprabha received the king's command and succeeded to his father's position, becoming chief minister, governing the realm jointly as his father had done; in all appropriate matters he performed exactly as his father had, without deviation or failure. At that time, the brahmins, householders, commoners, and people throughout the realm, learning of this, all said: 'Excellent, youth Jyotiṣprabha! Your father was formerly called Dṛḍha; now you succeed to his position and carry forward and expand upon his former achievements — we shall call you Mahādṛḍha.' His original name Jyotiṣprabha had been given on account of the circumstances of his birth from fire; henceforth he was called Mahādṛḍha.

"Bhagavān! Not long after the chief minister Mahādṛḍha the brahmin had become minister, he went to where the six kṣatriya youths were, and having arrived, said to them: 'You youths should go to crown prince Reṇu and tell him: "Prince! In suffering we share it together; in happiness we celebrate together; where you take refuge, there too do we take refuge. Now your royal father is advanced in years, his movements have become feeble — that which is hard to know in this world is the span of life — should he one day depart this world, where shall we turn? You should now know that the various ministers and counselors, deliberating among themselves, intend after the king's passing to bestow upon you the royal consecration. When you ascend to the throne, you should divide the kingdom and share governance with us."' At that time, the six youths, having heard the chief minister Mahādṛḍha the brahmin's words, immediately went to where crown prince Reṇu was, and having arrived, said exactly what had been described above. The prince said: 'Young men! On the day of my new birth, should the ministers and counselors install me in the succession and bestow upon me the royal consecration, I shall not at that time forget you; I shall share governance of the kingdom with you and share equally whatever pleasures arise.' Afterward, King Yojana-pati, after a further long time, suddenly passed away. Then the various ministers and counselors went to the crown prince and, having arrived, said: 'Crown Prince! You should know that we ministers bestow upon you the royal consecration — now is the time for you to succeed to the royal throne.' The crown prince Reṇu said to the ministers and counselors: 'If you are able to install me in the succession, and it is appropriate to do so, now is indeed the time.' Then the ministers and counselors immediately arranged a magnificent lion throne; the crown prince took his seat upon it. They poured fragrant water upon his crown, saying: 'Devaputra! Now is the time for you to succeed to the royal throne — we ministers offer the royal consecration to the king.' Having received the royal consecration, he governed the realm well.

"Bhagavān! Not long after King Reṇu received the royal consecration, he freely indulged in the five pleasures and sported at will. Then the chief minister Mahādṛḍha the brahmin went to where the six youths were and, having arrived, said to them: 'You should know that crown prince Reṇu has received the royal consecration, has taken his place on the throne, and not long since has been freely indulging in the five pleasures and sporting at will. The king once promised to share governance of the kingdom with you. Now is the time for you to go to King Reṇu and say: "The king formerly promised us a division of the land to govern. Does Your Majesty remember this or not?"' The six youths, having heard the chief minister Mahādṛḍha the brahmin's words, immediately went together to where King Reṇu was and said exactly what had been described above.

"Bhagavān! King Reṇu said to the six youths: 'As was agreed formerly, I remember those words. I shall now divide this kingdom equally into seven portions and give one to each of you six, for each to govern separately.' Having said this, the six youths all addressed the king: 'If Your Majesty remembers those words, this is most excellent. We pray that Your Majesty quickly summon the chief minister Mahādṛḍha the brahmin and have him, according to Your Majesty's instructions, survey and demarcate the borders. That man is sharp and wise and clear in understanding, and can serve as the standard.' Then King Reṇu dispatched a messenger, saying to him: 'Go to the chief minister Mahādṛḍha the brahmin and say: "The king summons you now; you should come to the king's presence with all speed."' The messenger received the command and immediately went to where the chief minister Mahādṛḍha the brahmin was. Having arrived, he duly conveyed the king's words: 'You should now come to the king's presence with all speed.'

"Then the chief minister Mahādṛḍha the brahmin immediately came to where King Reṇu was, and having arrived, offered his respects and withdrew to sit to one side. The king, pleased, looked upon him, gave words of comfort and reassurance, and the chief minister responded with respectful deference. The king said: 'Mahādṛḍha! Now on my behalf survey this kingdom and divide it into seven portions, so that I and the six youths may each govern one separately.' Then the chief minister, having received the king's command, immediately surveyed and measured, dividing the territory. The northern region was broad and expansive; the southern region was narrow and contracted, like the shape of a cart. The central territory, where many people gathered, was where King Reṇu resided. The kingdoms thus apportioned were: the kingdom of Kaliṅga, with the city of Naṭapura; the kingdom of Aśmaka, with the city of Potana; the kingdom of Avanti, with the city of Māhiṣmatī; the kingdom of Sūvīra, with the city of Roruka; the kingdom of Videha, with the city of Mithilā; the kingdom of Magadha, with the great city of Campā; and the kingdom of Vārāṇasī, with the great city of Kāśi. These seven kingdoms having each been given their boundaries, the six youths in their respective places received the royal consecration and each became king, ruling their own territory. From that time on there were seven kings: King Reṇu, King Vighna, King Brahmadatta, King Jitaśatru, King Priyavardhana, King Rāṣṭrapāla, and King Mahārāṣṭrapāla — these seven kings each governing their own portion. Afterward, the six kings again gathered together and went to the brahmin chief minister, and having arrived, said to him: 'Mahādṛḍha! With the wisdom, resourcefulness, and ability you have, assisting and supporting King Reṇu the Great — we six kings also wish you to assist and support us in the same manner.' The brahmin chief minister, having heard these words, jointly assisted all seven kings, participating in counsel in all matters.

"At that time, the brahmin chief minister further instructed seven thousand brahmins in chanting those scriptures, and taught seven thousand brahmins in reading those scriptures. Then the brahmin householders, commoners, and people of the realm, all knowing and witnessing the brahmin chief minister's such talent and wisdom, discussed it among themselves: 'This Mahādṛḍha is truly a great brahmin; he is further able to instruct the brahmin community in reading and chanting the Vedic texts.'

"At that time, the brahmin chief minister, having heard the people's discussion, reflected thus: 'These brahmins, householders, commoners, and people gather here and there to deliberate, and groundlessly praise me, speaking of my talent and wisdom, and furthermore regard me as a truly great brahmin. This is not appropriate for me. Let me examine myself — truly I am not a genuine great brahmin. I shall no longer instruct the brahmins in reading and chanting the Vedic texts. Even if one's learning is broad, this is genuinely not my excellence; moreover, in this world my physical form will not long endure. I once heard the elders, the wise great brahmins of former times, say that in brahminical practice, during the four months of summer, one should dwell quietly in one place and cultivate the meditation of compassion; if that meditation succeeds, the Great Brahmā king will appear in person and grant the wishes one seeks. If such a thing should occur, this is what I desire — I should cultivate this meditation as has been described.' Having thus reflected, the brahmin chief minister, wishing to dwell quietly in one place during the four months of summer and cultivate the meditation of compassion, went to where King Reṇu was and, having arrived, said: 'Great King! I now desire, during the four months of summer, to dwell quietly in one place and cultivate the meditation of compassion. I pray that Your Majesty grant me permission.' King Reṇu said: 'Mahādṛḍha! As you wish — now is indeed the time.'

"Then the brahmin chief minister, having received the king's permission, went to a secluded place and with focused and concentrated mind cultivated the meditation of compassion during the four months of summer. When the four months of summer had passed, on the fifteenth day of the bright fortnight, the day of the monks' Poṣadha, he performed in that place, according to brahminical practice, the following: with fresh cow dung he first plastered the ground, then constructed a four-sided fire altar; in the center of the altar he also made a fire pit. The brahmin chief minister bathed his body, donned clean new garments, approached from the north to the southern boundary of the altar, scattered auspicious grass to cover the altar floor completely, sat facing north, took up the ladle, performed the fire rite, and made offerings to the Brahmā heaven. Not long after the brahmin chief minister had begun the rite, suddenly from the north there appeared a great luminous sign. Upon seeing this light, the brahmin chief minister gave rise to a sense of the extraordinary; the hairs of his body stood up in delight; he became even more reverently composed and dwelt with focused mind. The radiance blazed brilliantly, the like of which he had never before seen. Not long after the Great Brahmā king revealed this light, he came from the north and hovered in the midst of space. The brahmin chief minister, with wholehearted joy, gazed up into space and beheld the Great Brahmā king dwelling in the sky. Immediately joining his palms and bowing his head in reverence, he spoke a gāthā:

> 'Endowed with divine power, form, and brilliant radiance,
> What holy one now appears in space?
> Though I now see you, I cannot know —
> I pray you tell me truly as it is.'

"Then the Great Brahmā king spoke a gāthā in reply to the brahmin chief minister:

> 'Those of pure conduct all know
> That I dwell constantly in the Brahmā realm;
> Moreover, the devas know my name —
> You, brahmin, should reflect upon this yourself.'

"The brahmin chief minister again spoke a gāthā:

> 'Pure water and a seat are provided,
> With butter, honey, milk-porridge, finest in flavor —
> These I offer first with concentrated mind;
> I pray the Brahmā king compassionately receive them.'

"The Great Brahmā king again spoke a gāthā:

> 'Pure water and a seat are provided,
> With butter, honey, milk-porridge, finest in flavor —
> You, brahmin, have offered these first;
> I now receive them for you as is fitting.'

"The brahmin chief minister spoke a gāthā:

> 'The domain of the five pleasures is called this world;
> Birth in the Brahmā realm is called the other world.
> I rejoice in this meaning and begin my inquiry;
> I pray the Brahmā king grant me leave to ask.'

"The Great Brahmā king spoke a gāthā:

> 'Of the two meanings, this world and the other world,
> Ask freely as you please.
> I now grant you leave without hesitation —
> Whatever you would ask, speak it quickly.'

"Then the brahmin chief minister reflected thus: 'At this time, wishing to resolve my doubts, which meaning should I first ask the Brahmā king? Should I ask about the arising of this world? Or should I ask how to attain birth in the other world?' The brahmin chief minister further considered: 'As for the meaning of this world — it arises through the five pleasures; this should not be asked. I shall now ask the Brahmā king about the meaning of attaining birth in the other world.' Having so reflected, he then asked the Great Brahmā king: 'O valiant and pure one, Great Brahmā king! I now ask you; I pray you resolve my doubts. Among the great brahmins, if one wishes to attain birth in the peaceful Brahmā realm, what practice must one cultivate in order to attain birth there?'

"Then the Great Brahmā king spoke a gāthā in reply to the brahmin chief minister:

> 'One who cultivates non-self practices pure conduct;
> The mind dwelling in one-pointedness is liberated through compassion.
> Removed from the defilements of desire, afflictions eliminated —
> Such persons attain birth in the Brahmā realm.'


The minister brahmin then addressed Mahābrahmā: "As the Great Brahmā has stated in his verse, 'one who cultivates non-self is one who practices pure conduct'—I have now come to understand this meaning. It refers to a certain class of persons who arouse sincere faith, cultivate the dharma of renunciation, shave off beard and hair, wear the kāṣāya robe, and relinquish all wealth and pleasures, whether few or many. Their wisdom follows in accordance. Whether born in a high lineage or a low lineage, their minds are equanimous, free from grasping and attachment. They keep only the three robes and one bowl, possessing nothing beyond these. In the various trainings, they instruct others in the methods of training; their bodily, verbal, and mental karma are completely pure; they sustain themselves through pure livelihood, free from all transgression. This is what is called 'one who cultivates non-self.'

"Furthermore, as the Great Brahmā has stated, 'the mind abides in a single object'—I have heard these words and also understand this meaning. It refers to a certain class of those who cultivate meditative absorption. Inwardly pure of mind, abiding in the nature of a single object, without applied thought or sustained thought, joy and bliss born of concentration—they attain the second dhyāna in its fullness of practice. This is what is called 'the mind abides in a single object.'

"Furthermore, as the Great Brahmā has stated, 'the liberation of compassion'—I have heard these words and also understand this meaning. It refers to a certain class of those who cultivate compassion. With the mind arising simultaneously with compassion, first in the eastern direction, they extend compassion extensively; their mind is vast, complete in its practice, equal and non-dual, likewise without limit, free from enmity and affliction. Having thus practiced in the eastern direction, in the southern, western, and northern directions, in the four intermediate directions, above and below, throughout all the world, they extend compassion extensively, complete in their practice, and it is likewise. This is what is called 'the liberation of compassion.'

"Furthermore, as the Great Brahmā has stated, 'one who is free from the stain of desire, whose afflictions are removed'—I have heard these words and do not understand this meaning. Great Brahmā, what are the afflictions? How can one among human beings cause the afflictions to become purified? The ocean of afflictions overflows in abundance—amid this, how can a practitioner come to be reborn in that tranquil Brahma realm?"

Then Mahābrahmā spoke a verse in reply to the minister brahmin:

> Greed, aversion, delusion, conceit, doubt, anger, and concealment,
> vexation, harm, deceit, falsehood, miserliness, and envy—
> arising through these defiling dharmas and slandering others:
> these are called the afflictions.
> Far removed from all such afflictions,
> one attains purity among humankind;
> sealing the source of the ocean of afflictions,
> one is reborn in the tranquil realm of Brahma.

The minister brahmin then addressed Mahābrahmā: "As the Great Brahmā has spoken of the dharmas of affliction—I have heard these words and fully understand this meaning. Were I to remain a householder, I would be wholly entangled. Were I to become a renunciant, I would be wholly free from transgression, and I should cultivate the pure and immaculate brahmacarya. Why so? All that is born must perish; human life is brief; if one does not awaken to this, one falls at death into the evil destinies. Therefore I now, knowing this of myself and awakening to it myself, should practice well and conduct myself in right brahmacarya, no longer creating evil karma in the world. Great Brahmā, I now relinquish the household life and seek the homeless life—I pray that the Great Brahmā knows my heart and mind." Mahābrahmā replied: "As you wish, now is just the time."

Having thus spoken, Mahābrahmā, who had appeared in the sky, concealed himself and was no longer seen.

Furthermore, in the assembly, Pañcaśikha, son of the gandharva king, came forward and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, today I have heard this Brahma king recount before the World-Honored One the story of causes and conditions, and it has suddenly occurred to me: was not that prime minister, the great Mahādṛḍha brahmin, none other than the World-Honored One himself?"

The Buddha told Pañcaśikha, son of the gandharva king: "Just so, just so. That prime minister, the great Mahādṛḍha brahmin, was indeed myself. I recall how in the past that prime minister, the great Mahādṛḍha brahmin, left the household life and so forth—have you not heard of this before?"

Pañcaśikha replied: "No, World-Honored One. I have not heard of it before."

The Buddha said: "Pañcaśikha, I shall now tell you in sequence. Pañcaśikha, at that time, after the prime minister, the great Mahādṛḍha brahmin, had completed the fire rite, he went to where King Reṇu was. Having arrived, he knelt in obeisance and respectfully facing the king, spoke a verse:

> I have a wish I would now declare
> to Reṇu, great king, lord of the realm:
> I relinquish my ministerial office to seek the homeless life—
> may the king govern the affairs of state himself.

At that time King Reṇu spoke a verse in reply to the minister:

> If you lack any of your needs,
> whatever you desire, I shall provide;
> if someone troubles you, tell me at once—
> I shall punish them according to the law of kings.
> You are as my father and I as your son;
> we support each other—how could we be parted?
> Though you serve as minister, you are also my teacher—
> why do you now speak these words?

The minister brahmin spoke a verse:

> In all my needs I lack for nothing,
> nor has any other troubled or vexed me;
> it is only that I have heard the words of truth,
> and my heart's resolve to leave the world will not be turned.

King Reṇu spoke a verse:

> What non-human has spoken, and what is this truth?
> Why do you hearken and believe such words?
> Do not take these words as true
> and abandon your ministerial post to seek the homeless life.

The minister brahmin spoke a verse:

> O Son of Heaven, I first performed the fire rite,
> boldly arousing a pure and concentrated mind;
> I laid out the altar according to the rite, offering fire to the devas,
> employing auspicious grass in the ceremony.
> The great Mahābrahmā, the great sage and holy one,
> responding to my entreaty, manifested in person;
> I heard him speak the words of truth—
> therefore my resolve to leave the world stands firm.

King Reṇu spoke a verse:

> As you, my minister, have well spoken,
> I now find in myself complete faith and understanding;
> since you have heard the words of the ancient sages,
> how could this resolve to leave the world be turned?
> Your mind is pure as empty space,
> and again like a jewel of pure and wondrous vaiḍūrya;
> as you practice, so shall I follow—
> through you I have attained awakening.

When King Reṇu had finished speaking this verse, he also said: "Great Mahādṛḍha, your mind is pure and you delight in cultivating good conduct. Do as you wish—you have found a refuge, and I too have found a refuge."

Then the minister brahmin spoke another verse, addressing the king:

> O King, you should renounce all objects of desire—
> one who clings to them is but a fool;
> you should arouse a firm mind free from attachment,
> endowed with samāhita and the power of forbearance.
> What is here awakened to is the pure vehicle;
> this pure path is truly and permanently abiding;
> this is the gate of the true Dharma that is proclaimed—
> through this one is reborn in the realm of Brahma.

Pañcaśikha, the six kings who administered the various kingdoms, having heard that the minister brahmin was relinquishing his ministerial post and delighting in seeking the homeless life, assembled all six kings together in one place. Then the minister brahmin himself went to where the six kings were. Having arrived, he knelt in obeisance and addressed the kings: "Your Majesties, you should know that I now wish to relinquish that ministerial post. I pray that each of the kings will separately seek someone to assist with affairs of state; if there be instruction to be given, let them rely separately on teachers and exemplars. I now delight in wishing to leave the household life to cultivate the path. Why so? At the place of Mahābrahmā I heard the words of truth—namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned. From this time forth, I no longer delight in the household life with its complete entanglement. Were I to become a renunciant, I would be wholly free from transgression, and I should cultivate the pure and immaculate brahmacarya. Why so? All that is born must perish; human life is brief; if one does not awaken to this, one falls at death into the evil destinies. Therefore I now, knowing this of myself and awakening to it myself, should practice well and conduct myself in right brahmacarya, no longer creating evil karma in the world."

At that time the six kings all deliberated together: "Why is this minister brahmin forsaking wealth and rank to seek the homeless life? Among brahmins there are also those who love wealth and rank. We should encourage this man with wealth and rank so that he does not leave the household life." Having thus deliberated together, the six kings all said to the minister brahmin: "We six kings, with all that is desired by way of wealth and rank, implore you. Now the wealth and rank we possess were all obtained through the Dharma." Having said this, they brought forth abundant wealth and jewels and all the implements of affluence, and presented them to the minister brahmin.

The minister brahmin then addressed the six kings: "Your Majesties, all these wealth, jewels, and implements of affluence I already possess in full abundance, and what I possess was likewise obtained through the Dharma. What I myself possess, I have already fully relinquished—how much the less would I now accept the gifts of the kings? I am now firmly resolved to seek the homeless life. Why so? At the place of Mahābrahmā I heard the words of truth—namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned, and so forth as previously spoken in full."

Pañcaśikha, the six kings then deliberated again among themselves: "Among brahmins there are also those who love beautiful dancing girls—we should present some to him to receive." Having thus deliberated together, the six kings presented beautiful dancing girls to the minister brahmin. The kings said: "These dancing girls of ours are extraordinary in their appearance, full and fine of form, pleasant in their bearing and conduct, and moreover possessed of many accomplishments. You should accept them—do not leave the household life."

The minister brahmin then addressed the six kings: "Your Majesties, in my own home I already have forty wives, extraordinary in their appearance, full and fine of form, pleasant in their bearing and conduct, perfectly equal in their comeliness. Though I already possess them, I have fully relinquished them—how much the less would I now accept the gifts of the kings? I am now firmly resolved to seek the homeless life. Why so? At the place of Mahābrahmā I heard the words of truth—namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned, and so forth as previously spoken in full."

Pañcaśikha, the six kings all then said to the minister brahmin: "Since you are firmly resolved to seek the homeless life, wait yet a while—after seven years, when our princes, sons, and younger brothers have each been established, we too shall follow you into the homeless life. O Great Mahādṛḍha, if you have found a refuge, we kings too have found a refuge."

The minister brahmin then addressed the six kings: "If I must wait seven years, that is far too long. My resolve is firm and I wish to leave the household life without delay. Why so? At the place of Mahābrahmā I heard the words of truth—namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned, and so forth as previously spoken in full."

The kings said again: "O Great Mahādṛḍha, if that cannot be, then wait six more years, or five years, or even one year."

The minister replied: "If I must wait even one year, that is far too long. My resolve is firm and I wish to leave the household life without delay."

The kings said again: "If that cannot be, then wait seven more months."

The minister replied: "If I must wait seven months, that is far too long. My resolve is firm and I wish to leave the household life without delay."

The kings said again: "If that cannot be, then six months, or even half a month."

The minister replied: "If I must wait half a month, that is far too long. My resolve is firm and I wish to leave the household life without delay."

The kings said again: "If that cannot be, then wait seven days."

The minister replied: "Your Majesties, if I must wait seven days, that would be acceptable. Leaving the household life means abandoning suffering for joy—now is just the time."

Pañcaśikha, the minister brahmin then went to where the seven thousand brahmins who chanted the scriptures and the seven thousand brahmins who read the scriptures were. Having arrived, he addressed in full all fourteen thousand brahmins: "Welcome, welcome, assembly of brahmins! As for the Vedic texts you possess—whether reading them or reciting them—from this time forth, each of you should separately seek a teacher to instruct you. I am now leaving the household life and cannot teach you. Why so? At the place of Mahābrahmā I heard the words of truth—namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned. From this time forth, I no longer delight in the household life with its complete entanglement. Were I to become a renunciant, I would be wholly free from transgression, and I should cultivate the pure and immaculate brahmacarya. Why so? All that is born must perish; human life is brief; if one does not awaken to this, one falls at death into the evil destinies. Therefore I now, knowing this of myself and awakening to it myself, should practice well and conduct myself in right brahmacarya, no longer creating evil karma in the world."

At that time the assembly of fourteen thousand brahmins all addressed the minister brahmin: "Our teacher is a man of wisdom—it would not be right for him to leave the household life. Why so? As for the homeless life, it has little benefit, little authority, and little renown. But as for a brahmin, he has great benefit, great authority, and great renown."

The minister brahmin then told those fourteen thousand brahmins: "You brahmins, do not say this! Do not say this! You should know that the homeless life has great benefit, great authority, and great renown; whereas a brahmin has little benefit, little authority, and little renown. Whatever you brahmins know and understand, all of it arises through the condition of transmission by teachers—therefore, do not harbor differing views."

At that time the assembly of fourteen thousand brahmins all addressed the minister brahmin: "As our teacher has said—just so, just so. The homeless life has great benefit, great authority, and great renown. Indeed, whatever we know and understand, all of it arises through the condition of transmission by teachers. If our teacher now has found a refuge, we too have found a refuge."

The minister brahmin then again told the fourteen thousand brahmins: "Leaving the household life means abandoning suffering for joy—now is just the time."

The minister brahmin then returned to his own home to where his forty wives were, and said to them: "Welcome, welcome! Each of you should go to your respective kinsmen, or if you prefer, remain among another brahmin household. I now relinquish you, with my heart set on leaving the household life. Why so? At the place of Mahābrahmā I heard the words of truth—namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned. From this time forth, I no longer delight in the household life with its complete entanglement. Were I to become a renunciant, I would be wholly free from transgression, and I should cultivate the pure and immaculate brahmacarya. Why so? All that is born must perish; human life is brief; if one does not awaken to this, one falls at death into the evil destinies. Therefore I now, knowing this of myself and awakening to it myself, should practice well and conduct myself in right brahmacarya, no longer creating evil karma in the world."

The forty wives then all addressed the minister brahmin: "O Great Mahādṛḍha, when you should be a teacher and venerable one, you are just that; when you should be a husband and master, you are just that; when you should be a good friend, you are just that. Now do as you wish—you have found a refuge, and we too have found a refuge."

The minister brahmin then again said to the forty wives: "Leaving the household life means abandoning suffering for joy—now is just the time."

Pañcaśikha, after the minister brahmin had conveyed his message to all who needed to be informed, within seven days, with firm and unwavering faith, he took refuge in the Buddha and left the household life. His beard and hair fell away of themselves, the kāṣāya robe appeared on his body, and he became possessed of the marks of a bhikṣu, complete in dignified bearing.

After the minister brahmin had left the household life, the seven kings all relinquished their kingdoms and likewise followed him into the homeless life; the seven thousand brahmins who chanted the scriptures likewise followed him into the homeless life; his forty wives likewise followed him into the homeless life. At that time, countless hundreds of thousands of people each rejoiced and all delighted in leaving the household life.

Pañcaśikha, the prime minister, the great Mahādṛḍha brahmin, having gone far from all sensual pleasures, attained the fruit of arhatship. Having attained the holy fruit, he further expounded for those who practiced brahmacarya alongside him the various gateways of the Dharma for the class of śrāvakas. Having heard the Dharma, they comprehended its meaning and were to be reborn in the Brahma realm.

At that time, the śrāvakas of Mahādṛḍha further expounded for those who cultivated brahmacarya alongside them the various gateways of the Dharma for the class of śrāvakas. Having heard the Dharma, they comprehended its meaning and were reborn in the Heaven of the Four Great Kings within the realm of desire. There was another class of those who practiced brahmacarya: having heard the Dharma and comprehended its meaning, they were reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven; there was another class who were reborn in the Yāma Heaven; there was another class who were reborn in Tuṣita Heaven; there was another class who were reborn in the Nirmāṇarati Heaven; there was another class who were reborn in the Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven.

Pañcaśikha, among those in that assembly—men and women, and those who practiced brahmacarya alongside him—those who harbored thoughts of transgression toward the śrāvaka Mahādṛḍha: at the dissolution of their bodies at death, they fell into the hells. Among those in that assembly—men and women, and those who practiced brahmacarya alongside him—those who harbored thoughts of pure faith toward the śrāvaka Mahādṛḍha: at the dissolution of their bodies at death, they were reborn in the heavenly realms.

Pañcaśikha, the śrāvaka Mahādṛḍha traveled throughout cities, towns, and villages, extensively teaching and benefiting all—kings, ministers, elders, brahmins, and even ordinary people of every station—causing them to abandon wrong paths. At that time, the kings, ministers, elders, and brahmins throughout the realm, those who cultivated brahmacarya, and the householders, and all ordinary people of every station, all said: "We take refuge in the holy one Mahādṛḍha, minister of the seven kings. How excellent! Today we have gained great good fortune."

Having thus concluded his recounting of the causes and conditions of the past, the World-Honored One saw that Pañcaśikha, son of the gandharva king, experienced joy arising in his mind; far from dust and defilement, he attained purity of the Dharma eye.

When the Buddha had finished expounding this sūtra, Pañcaśikha, son of the gandharva king, and the entire great assembly, having heard what the Buddha had taught, all experienced great joy, and with faith received and upheld it.
