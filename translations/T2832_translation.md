# 南天竺國菩提達摩禪師觀門
## Gates of Contemplation by Chan Master Bodhidharma of South India

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2832

Attributed to Bodhidharma (菩提達摩) of South India (南天竺國)

---

Someone asked: What is called dhyāna-samādhi?

Answer: Dhyāna is the non-arising of a disturbed mind. No movement, no thought; this is dhyāna-samādhi. Rectifying the mind and stilling thought; no arising, no ceasing; no going, no coming; serene and immovable; this is called dhyāna-samādhi.

What is called dhyāna-contemplation?

Answer: The mind-spirit being clear and pure is called dhyāna. Illuminating principle with clarity is called contemplation. When dhyāna-contemplation is realized spontaneously, without error; therefore it is called dhyāna-contemplation. The mind-spirit being clear and pure, neither arising nor ceasing, neither coming nor going, tranquil and immovable; this is called dhyāna.

Someone asked: What is called dhyāna-samādhi?

Answer: Dhyāna-samādhi is a Sanskrit term. In Chinese it is called "the grove of accumulated merit." All the Buddhas of the three realms speak of sitting in dhyāna. Therefore it is called "the grove of accumulated merit."

Furthermore it was asked: What is called the Dharma of dhyāna?

Answer: The Dharma of dhyāna proceeds in sequence through penetration. For one beginning the study, from start to finish there are seven gates of contemplation. The first is the Gate of Settling the Mind. The second is the Gate of Emptying the Mind. The third is the Gate of No-form. The fourth is the Gate of Liberation of the Mind. The fifth is the Gate of Dhyāna-Samādhi. The sixth is the Gate of True Wonder. The seventh is the Gate of Wisdom.

As for the Gate of Settling the Mind: this refers to the mind being scattered and active, grasping at conditions without settling. One singularly gathers and focuses, keeping mindfulness settled, with no further wandering or agitation. Therefore it is called the Gate of Settling the Mind.

As for the Gate of Emptying the Mind: this refers to watching the mind as it turns and pursues, awakening to the mind as empty and still; no going, no coming, no place of abiding, a mind with nothing to rely upon. Therefore it is called the Gate of Emptying the Mind.

As for the Gate of the Signless Mind: this refers to the mind being clear and pure, without any appearance or characteristics; neither blue nor yellow, neither red nor white; neither long nor short, neither great nor small; neither square nor round, tranquil and immovable. Therefore it is called the Gate of No-form.

As for the Gate of Liberation of the Mind: one knows the mind to be unfettered and unbound. All afflictions (kleśas) do not come to oppress the mind. Therefore it is called the Gate of Liberation of the Mind.

As for dhyāna-samādhi: it is a Sanskrit term from the Western Regions. In Chinese it means "serene contemplation." One awakens to the mind's stillness and serenity; whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down, in all cases the mind is serene and still, without dispersion or agitation. Therefore it is called "serene."

As for the Gate of True Suchness (tathatā): one awakens to the mind as no-mind, equal and co-extensive with empty space; pervading and encompassing the entire Dharma-realm, equal and non-dual; beyond all multiplicity, without change. Therefore it is called the Gate of True Suchness.

As for the Gate of Wisdom: understanding and comprehending all things is called wisdom (jñāna). Conforming to and penetrating the source of emptiness is called discernment (prajñā). Therefore it is called the Gate of Wisdom. It is also called the Ultimate Path. It is also called the Mahāyāna Gate of Signless Dhyāna-Contemplation. This is because it constitutes the practice of dhyāna and the study of the Path. Thus dhyāna has seven gates of contemplation.

Reciting the Buddha's name aloud yields ten kinds of merit. First, one does not hear evil sounds. Second, recollection of the Buddha is undispersed. Third, drowsiness and sleep are expelled. Fourth, one is vigorous and energetically diligent. Fifth, the gods (devas) are joyful. Sixth, the armies of Māra are filled with dread. Seventh, the sound reverberates throughout the ten directions. Eighth, the sufferings of the three evil destinies are stilled. Ninth, samādhi appears before one. Tenth, one attains rebirth in the Pure Land.
