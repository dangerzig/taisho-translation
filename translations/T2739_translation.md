# Interlinear Notes on the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra

## *Jīngāng Bōrě Jīng Jiāzhù* (金剛般若經挾註)

Taishō 2739

Author unknown.

*Note: This is a fragmentary text presenting the latter portion of the Diamond Sūtra (Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā, T235, Kumārajīva's translation) with brief interlinear annotations (jiāzhù) inserted between the sūtra phrases. Such interlinear note editions were a common format in Chinese Buddhism, where short glosses, section markers, or explanatory phrases were written in smaller characters between the lines of the main text. In the present extraction, the annotations and sūtra text are merged together, making them difficult to distinguish. The text covers roughly the second half of the Diamond Sūtra, beginning from the discussion of "seeing all characteristics as non-characteristics" and proceeding through the major themes: the four fruits of the śrāvaka path (stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, arhat), the adornment of buddha-lands, the superiority of the sūtra's merit over material giving, the famous verse on not seeking the Tathāgata through form or sound, the teaching that all dharmas are buddha-dharmas, the five eyes of the Tathāgata, the unreality of past, present, and future mind, and the final teaching that the Tathāgata does not liberate any sentient being. The text breaks off abruptly near the end.*

---

[The text begins mid-sūtra:]

"To see all characteristics as non-characteristics is to see the Tathāgata." Subhūti addressed the Buddha, saying: "World-Honored One." [Section marker.] "... can they give rise to true faith?" "Do not speak thus. Those who maintain precepts and cultivate merit, with regard to these phrases, can give rise to faith. You should know that such persons have not planted wholesome roots with just one buddha or two buddhas, but have planted various wholesome roots with immeasurable thousands of myriads of buddhas. With regard to these phrases, if they give rise to even a single thought of pure faith, the Tathāgata fully knows and fully sees. These sentient beings obtain [immeasurable merit]."

"Why? Because these sentient beings have no mark of self, no mark of person, no mark of sentient being, no mark of life-span. They have no mark of dharmas, nor any mark of non-dharmas. Why? If they grasp the mark of dharmas, that is attachment to self, person, sentient being, and life-span. If they grasp the mark of non-dharmas, that is also attachment to self, person, sentient being, and life-span. Therefore, one should not grasp dharmas, nor should one grasp non-dharmas. The Tathāgata always says: 'You bhikṣus, know that the Dharma I teach is like a raft. Even the Dharma should be relinquished; how much more so what is not the Dharma.'"

"Subhūti, what do you think? Has the Tathāgata attained anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi?" Subhūti [said]: "There is no fixed dharma called anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi that the Tathāgata could teach." "Why? Because the Dharma taught by the Tathāgata cannot be grasped and cannot be spoken. Why? All worthies and sages are distinguished by the unconditioned dharma."

"Subhūti, what do you think? If a person were to fill the three-thousand great-thousand world-system with the seven treasures and use them for giving, would the merit obtained by that person be great or not?" Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One. Why? This merit is not the nature of merit itself. Therefore the Tathāgata says the merit is great."

"If again there were a person who, within this sūtra, received, upheld, and even so much as a four-line verse, and explained it for others, that person's merit would surpass the former. Why? Subhūti, all buddhas, and all buddhas' dharma of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi, all come forth from this sūtra. Subhūti, what is called 'buddha-dharma' is not buddha-dharma."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can a stream-enterer (*srotāpanna*) think, 'I have attained the fruit of stream-entry'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One. Why? The stream-enterer is called 'one who enters the stream,' yet there is nothing entered. Not entering form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas: this is called a stream-enterer."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can a once-returner (*sakṛdāgāmin*) think, 'I have attained the fruit of once-returning'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One. Why? The once-returner is called 'one who comes and goes once,' yet in reality there is no coming or going. This is called a once-returner."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can a non-returner (*anāgāmin*) think, 'I have attained the fruit of non-returning'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One. Why? The non-returner is called 'one who does not come,' yet in reality there is no coming. Therefore it is called a non-returner."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can an arhat think, 'I have attained the way of arhatship'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One. Why? There is in reality no dharma called 'arhat.' World-Honored One, if an arhat were to think, 'I have attained the way of arhatship,' that would be attachment to self, person, sentient being, and life-span. World-Honored One, the Buddha has said that I have attained the samādhi of non-contention and am foremost among people, the foremost arhat free from desire. Yet I do not think, 'I am an arhat free from desire.' World-Honored One, if I were to think, 'I have attained the way of arhatship,' the World-Honored One would not say that Subhūti delights in the practice of araṇya (*āraṇyaka*). Because Subhūti in reality has no practice, he is called Subhūti who delights in the araṇya practice."

"The Buddha told Subhūti: 'What do you think? When the Tathāgata was formerly with Dīpaṃkara Buddha, did he obtain any dharma?' 'World-Honored One, when the Tathāgata was formerly with Dīpaṃkara Buddha, in regard to dharma there was truly nothing obtained.'"

"Subhūti, what do you think? Does a bodhisattva adorn buddha-lands?" "No, World-Honored One. Why? To adorn buddha-lands is not adornment; it is called adornment." "Therefore, Subhūti, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva should give rise to a pure mind in this way: one should not abide in form to give rise to mind, nor should one abide in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas to give rise to mind. One should give rise to a mind that abides nowhere."

"Subhūti, suppose there were a person whose body was like Mount Sumeru, the king of mountains. What do you think? Would that body be great?" Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One. Why? The Buddha says 'not-body'; this is called a great body."

"Subhūti, as the sands of the Ganges: as numerous as those sands, so many Ganges Rivers there would be. What do you think? Would the sands in all those Ganges Rivers be many?" Subhūti said: "Very many, World-Honored One. Even the Ganges Rivers alone would be innumerable; how much more so their sands." "Subhūti, I now tell you truthfully: if a good man or good woman were to fill as many three-thousand great-thousand worlds as there are sands in all those Ganges Rivers with the seven treasures and give them in charity, would the merit obtained be much?" Subhūti said: "Very much, World-Honored One." The Buddha told Subhūti: "If a good man or good woman, from this sūtra, receives, upholds, and practices even as few as four lines of verse, and explains them for others, this merit surpasses the former."

"Furthermore, Subhūti, wherever this sūtra is taught, even as few as four lines of verse, know that this place should be revered by all the world, by devas, humans, and asuras, as a buddha-stūpa. How much more so a person who can fully receive, uphold, read, and recite it. Subhūti, know that such a person achieves the most supreme and rare dharma. Wherever this sūtra is found, there is the Buddha, and the revered disciples."

At that time, Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what should this sūtra be called? How should we receive and uphold it?" The Buddha told Subhūti: "This sūtra is called the Diamond Prajñāpāramitā. By this name you should receive and uphold it. Why? Subhūti, the Buddha says prajñāpāramitā is not prajñāpāramitā."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Does the Tathāgata have any Dharma to teach?" Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the Tathāgata has nothing to teach."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Are the minute particles in the three-thousand great-thousand worlds many?" Subhūti said: "Very many, World-Honored One." "Subhūti, all those minute particles, the Tathāgata says are not minute particles; they are called minute particles. The Tathāgata says a world is not a world; it is called a world."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can one see the Tathāgata by means of the thirty-two marks?" "No, World-Honored One. Why? The Tathāgata says the thirty-two marks are non-marks; they are called the thirty-two marks."

"Subhūti, if a good man or good woman gives in charity as many lives as there are sands of the Ganges, and if another person, from this sūtra, receives, upholds, and practices even four lines of verse, and explains them for others, that person's merit is far greater."

At that time, Subhūti, hearing this sūtra taught, deeply understood its meaning and import, and wept with tears, addressing the Buddha: "How rare, World-Honored One! The Buddha teaches such a profound sūtra. Since the past, when I first obtained the wisdom-eye, I have never heard such a sūtra. World-Honored One, if again there is a person who, hearing this sūtra, gives rise to pure faith, that person will realize the true characteristic. Know that such a person achieves the foremost and rarest merit. World-Honored One, this true characteristic is non-characteristic; therefore the Tathāgata calls it the true characteristic. World-Honored One, that I now hear such a sūtra and believe, understand, receive, and uphold it is not difficult. If in the future, in the last five hundred years, there are sentient beings who hear this sūtra and believe, understand, receive, and uphold it, such persons are the foremost and rarest. Why? Such persons have no self-characteristic, no person-characteristic, no sentient-being-characteristic, and no lifespan-characteristic. Why? The self-characteristic is non-characteristic; the person-characteristic, sentient-being-characteristic, and lifespan-characteristic are all non-characteristics. Why? To be free from all characteristics is to be called 'all buddhas.'" The Buddha told Subhūti: "So it is, so it is. Furthermore, if a person hears this sūtra and is not alarmed, not frightened, not afraid, know that such a person is exceedingly rare. Why? Subhūti, the Tathāgata says the first pāramitā is not the first pāramitā; it is called the first pāramitā."

"Subhūti, the pāramitā of patience: the Tathāgata says it is not the pāramitā of patience. Why? Subhūti, in a former life, when King Kalirāja cut my body to pieces, at that time I had no self-characteristic, no person-characteristic, no sentient-being-characteristic, and no lifespan-characteristic. Why? At that time in the past, when my limbs were severed joint by joint, if I had had a self-characteristic, person-characteristic, sentient-being-characteristic, or lifespan-characteristic, I would have given rise to anger and resentment. Subhūti, I further recall that in the past, for five hundred lifetimes, I was the sage of patience. In all those lifetimes, I had no self-characteristic, no person-characteristic, no sentient-being-characteristic, and no lifespan-characteristic. Therefore, Subhūti, a bodhisattva should be free from all characteristics and generate the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. One should not abide in form to generate the mind; one should not abide in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas to generate the mind. One should generate a mind that abides nowhere. If the mind has an abiding, it is not true abiding. Therefore the Buddha says a bodhisattva's mind should not abide in form when practicing generosity."

"Subhūti, a bodhisattva, for the benefit of all sentient beings, should practice generosity in this way. The Tathāgata says all characteristics are non-characteristics. He also says all sentient beings are non-sentient-beings."

"Subhūti, the Tathāgata is a speaker of truth, a speaker of reality, a speaker of what accords, a non-deceiver, a speaker without contradiction."

"Subhūti, the Dharma the Tathāgata has obtained is neither real nor unreal. Subhūti, if a bodhisattva's mind abides in dharma when practicing generosity, it is like a person entering darkness who cannot see anything. If a bodhisattva's mind does not abide in dharma when practicing generosity, it is like a person with eyes in sunlight who can see all kinds of forms."

"Subhūti, in ages to come, if a good man or good woman can receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra, the Tathāgata, with the Buddha's wisdom, fully knows and fully sees that such a person will attain immeasurable, boundless merit."

"Subhūti, if a good man or good woman, in the morning, gives bodies in charity equal to the sands of the Ganges; at midday, again gives bodies equal to the sands of the Ganges; and in the evening, again gives bodies equal to the sands of the Ganges; and does so for immeasurable hundreds of thousands of millions of kalpas; yet if another person hears this sūtra and does not reject it in their faith, that person's merit surpasses the former. How much more so one who copies, receives, upholds, reads, recites, and explains it for others."

"Subhūti, this sūtra has inconceivable, immeasurable, boundless merit. The Tathāgata speaks it for those who have set out on the great vehicle, for those of the supreme vehicle. If there is a person who can receive, uphold, read, and recite it, and broadly explain it for others, the Tathāgata fully knows and fully sees that such a person achieves immeasurable, indescribable, boundless, inconceivable merit. Such persons bear the Tathāgata's anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Why? Subhūti, if one delights in lesser dharmas and is attached to the view of self, person, sentient being, and lifespan, one cannot hear, receive, read, or recite this sūtra, or explain it for others."

"Subhūti, wherever this sūtra is found, all the world, devas, humans, and asuras should make offerings to it. Know that such a place is a stūpa. All should reverently pay homage, circumambulate it, and scatter flowers and incense there."

"Furthermore, Subhūti, if a good man or good woman receives, upholds, reads, and recites this sūtra, and yet is disparaged by others, this person's prior karmic offenses from past lives should have led to rebirth in the evil paths; but because people in this present life disparage them, those prior karmic offenses are thereby eliminated, and they shall attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

"Subhūti, I recall in the past, innumerable asaṃkhyeya kalpas ago, before Dīpaṃkara Buddha, I encountered eight hundred and forty thousand million nayutas of buddhas, and made offerings and attended to every one of them without exception. If there is a person in the latter age who can receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra, the merit obtained is such that all the merit of my offerings to those buddhas does not amount to one hundredth, one thousandth, one ten-thousandth, one hundred-millionth; no calculation or simile can approach it."

"Subhūti, if a good man or good woman in the latter age receives, upholds, reads, and recites this sūtra, the merit obtained, were I to explain it fully, might cause some who hear it to become bewildered and doubtful. Subhūti, know that the meaning of this sūtra is inconceivable; its retribution is also inconceivable."

At that time, Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if a good man or good woman generates the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, how should they abide? How should they subdue their minds?" The Buddha told Subhūti: "A good man or good woman who generates the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi should give rise to this thought: 'I should bring all sentient beings to final nirvāṇa. Yet having brought all sentient beings to final nirvāṇa, in truth not a single sentient being has been brought to final nirvāṇa.' Why? If a bodhisattva has the characteristic of self, person, sentient being, or lifespan, that one is not a bodhisattva. Why? Subhūti, in truth there is no dharma that generates the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

"Subhūti, what do you think? When the Tathāgata was with Dīpaṃkara Buddha, did he obtain any dharma of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?" "No, World-Honored One. As I understand the meaning of what the Buddha has taught, the Buddha obtained no dharma of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi from Dīpaṃkara Buddha." The Buddha said: "So it is, so it is. Subhūti, in truth there is no dharma by which the Tathāgata obtained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Subhūti, if there were a dharma by which the Tathāgata obtained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, Dīpaṃkara Buddha would not have given me the prediction: 'In a future life you shall become a buddha named Śākyamuni.' Because in truth there is no dharma by which one obtains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, Dīpaṃkara Buddha gave me the prediction, saying: 'In a future life you shall become a buddha named Śākyamuni.' Why? 'Tathāgata' means the suchness of all dharmas. If someone says the Tathāgata obtained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, Subhūti, in truth there is no dharma by which the Buddha obtained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Subhūti, the anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi the Tathāgata has obtained is, in this, neither real nor unreal. Therefore the Tathāgata says all dharmas are buddha-dharmas. Subhūti, what are called 'all dharmas' are not all dharmas; therefore they are called 'all dharmas.'"

"Subhūti, it is like a person with a great body." Subhūti said: "World-Honored One, the Tathāgata says a person with a great body is not a great body; it is called a great body." "Subhūti, a bodhisattva is likewise. If one says, 'I shall bring immeasurable sentient beings to final nirvāṇa,' one is not called a bodhisattva. Why? Subhūti, in truth there is no dharma called 'bodhisattva.' Therefore the Buddha says all dharmas are without self, without person, without sentient being, without lifespan. Subhūti, if a bodhisattva says, 'I shall adorn buddha-lands,' that one is not called a bodhisattva. Why? The Tathāgata says to adorn buddha-lands is not adornment; it is called adornment. Subhūti, if a bodhisattva penetrates the dharma of non-self, the Tathāgata says that one is truly a bodhisattva."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Does the Tathāgata have the fleshly eye?" "Yes, World-Honored One. The Tathāgata has the fleshly eye." "Subhūti, what do you think? Does the Tathāgata have the heavenly eye?" "Yes, World-Honored One. The Tathāgata has the heavenly eye." "Subhūti, what do you think? Does the Tathāgata have the wisdom eye?" "Yes, World-Honored One. The Tathāgata has the wisdom eye." "Subhūti, what do you think? Does the Tathāgata have the dharma eye?" "Yes, World-Honored One. The Tathāgata has the dharma eye." "Subhūti, what do you think? Does the Tathāgata have the buddha eye?" "Yes, World-Honored One. The Tathāgata has the buddha eye."

"Subhūti, what do you think? As the sands in the Ganges River, does the Buddha say they are sands?" "Yes, World-Honored One. The Tathāgata says they are sands." "Subhūti, what do you think? If there were as many Ganges Rivers as the sands in one Ganges River, and as many buddha-worlds as the sands in all those rivers, would that be many?" "Very many, World-Honored One." The Buddha told Subhūti: "In all the lands of those worlds, all the various kinds of minds of all the sentient beings therein, the Tathāgata fully knows. Why? The Tathāgata says all minds are non-minds; they are called minds. Why? Subhūti, the past mind cannot be obtained, the present mind cannot be obtained, and the future mind cannot be obtained."

"Subhūti, what do you think? If someone were to fill the three-thousand great-thousand worlds with the seven treasures and give them in charity, would that person, by this cause, obtain much merit?" "Yes, World-Honored One. This person, by this cause, obtains very much merit." "Subhūti, if merit had substance, the Tathāgata would not say that one obtains much merit. Because merit is without substance, the Tathāgata says one obtains much merit."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can one see the Buddha through the perfection of his form-body?" "No, World-Honored One. One should not see the Tathāgata through the perfection of his form-body. Why? The Tathāgata says the perfected form-body is not the perfected form-body."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can one see the Tathāgata through the perfection of his various marks?" "No, World-Honored One. One should not see the Tathāgata through the perfection of his various marks. Why? The Tathāgata says the perfection of the various marks is not perfection; it is called the perfection of the various marks."

"Subhūti, do not think the Tathāgata has the thought, 'I have a Dharma to teach.' Do not have such a thought. Why? If someone says the Tathāgata has a Dharma to teach, that person slanders the Buddha and cannot understand what I teach. Subhūti, to teach the Dharma means there is no dharma to teach; this is called teaching the Dharma."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, when the Buddha attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, was nothing obtained?" "So it is, so it is. Subhūti, regarding anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, I did not obtain even the slightest dharma. This is called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

"Furthermore, Subhūti, this Dharma is equal, without high or low. This is called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. By practicing all wholesome dharmas without self, without person, without sentient being, and without lifespan, one attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Subhūti, what are called 'wholesome dharmas,' the Tathāgata says are non-wholesome dharmas; they are called 'wholesome dharmas.'"

"Subhūti, if, among all the Mount Sumerus in the three-thousand great-thousand worlds, there were such heaps of seven treasures, and a person used them for charity; and if another person, from this Prajñāpāramitā sūtra, received, upheld, and practiced even four lines of verse, and explained them for others, the former merit would not amount to one hundredth, one thousandth, one hundred-millionth; no calculation or simile could approach it."

"Subhūti, what do you think? You should not think the Tathāgata has the thought, 'I should liberate sentient beings.' Subhūti, do not have such a thought. Why? In truth there are no sentient beings for the Tathāgata to liberate. If there were sentient beings for the Tathāgata to liberate, the Tathāgata would have self, person, sentient being, and lifespan. Subhūti, the Tathāgata says 'having a self' is not having a self; yet ordinary people take it to be having a self. Subhūti, ordinary people, the Tathāgata says, are not ordinary people."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can one see the Tathāgata through the thirty-two marks?" Subhūti said: "Yes, yes. One sees the Tathāgata through the thirty-two marks." The Buddha said: "Subhūti, if one sees the Tathāgata through the thirty-two marks, then a wheel-turning sage-king would be a Tathāgata." Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, as I understand the meaning the Buddha has taught, one should not see the Tathāgata through the thirty-two marks." At that time, the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "If one seeks me through form,
> Or seeks me through sound,
> This person walks the wrong path
> And cannot see the Tathāgata."

"Subhūti, if you have the thought that the Tathāgata did not attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi by means of the perfection of marks, Subhūti, do not have such a thought. The Tathāgata did not fail to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi by means of the perfection of marks."

"Subhūti, if you have the thought that one who generates the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi teaches the annihilation of all dharmas, do not have such a thought. Why? One who generates the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi does not teach the characteristic of annihilation regarding dharmas."

"Subhūti, if a bodhisattva fills as many worlds as the sands of the Ganges with the seven treasures and gives them in charity, and if another person knows that all dharmas are without self and attains the patience of non-arising, this bodhisattva surpasses the former bodhisattva's merit. Subhūti, because all bodhisattvas..."

The text breaks off here.
