# 聖無動尊安鎮家國等法
## Methods of the Holy Acala for Pacifying and Protecting the Home and State

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1203

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

At that time, Vairocana Tathāgata, out of compassion for all sentient beings constantly adrift in the bitter sea of afflictions, transmigrating through birth and death without escaping the three realms, suffering manifold torments and continually creating immeasurable painful karma without cease, spoke thus:

"In order to rescue all sentient beings, within the three realms I manifest a body of majestic radiance and sovereign power, called Acala Vajra Vidyā-rāja, who can bestow peace and benefit upon devas and humans and pacify and protect the home and state, free from decline and calamity. If there is a land beset by many disasters, where the sun and moon lose their courses, where baleful stars produce strange portents and encroach upon one another, where floods and droughts are out of balance, where the merit of sentient beings is thin and the nāgas and devas have departed, then at such a time inauspicious events arise in rivalry without respite: grain becomes dear, people freeze and starve, ghosts and spirits spread abroad, pestilence and malign influences rage, tigers and wolves wreak havoc, violent storms bring frost and hail, venomous insects and locusts swarm, enemies arise in quick succession, the sovereign's ministers rebel, bandits multiply, and military campaigns are repeatedly launched, with calamities continuing without end.

"When the benevolent king of that land learns that there exists such a holder of the vidyā, he should rouse the great mind of compassion, have pity on all sentient beings throughout his realm, and carry out the secret method of the great Vidyā-rāja's majestic power, causing all sentient beings to increase in merit, to give rise to a sense of conscience and shame, and to abandon all painful karma. At that time the king should generate a mind of great faith and firm resolve, summon such a great master who holds the vidyā, and offer him wealth, cloth, garments, precious playthings, gardens, houses, elephants, horses, carriages, servants, attendants, and various supremely wonderful auspicious things, saying to him: 'I act for the sake of all sentient beings and for the security of the land. May you, Venerable One, accomplish this method on my behalf. I have heard that the power of this method is inconceivable. You should, for my sake and for all sentient beings, swiftly establish this supreme method and eliminate all calamities. Whatever things you need, take them as you wish; I shall never deny you.' When the vidyā-holder receives this commission, he should not give rise to weariness.

"Following the teaching, he should practice it. He must make a pure image of Acala Vidyā-rāja, together with the eight directional deva-kings and their retinues. They should be fashioned according to the method and according to one's means, whether in gold, silver, or even copper, iron, clay, wood, or paint. The body should be eight finger-breadths tall. The two-armed form has a kumāra appearance; the body is full and robust, golden in color, with hair hanging to the left, an expression of extreme wrath. The right hand holds a wisdom-sword, the left grasps a lasso. He sits upon a golden rock, blazing with fierce flames. Many of the flames take the form of garuḍas. This image should be placed in the royal palace, and in the residences, lodgings, and chambers of all officials and common people alike, a small portion of the central area should be set aside to install this image.

"For the eight directional emplacement sites, make instead the four-armed form of great and awesome wrath: the body is dark blue, full and dignified, with eyes and mouth wide open and sharp fangs protruding upward. The right hands hold a sword and lasso; the upper two arms, flanking the mouth, form the wrathful mudrā. The body is set within an eight-spoked vajra-wheel. The inner wheel has four spokes; the outer rim displays eight triple-pronged vajra heads. The wheel also has garuḍa flames. He sits atop four great jeweled Mount Sumerus. Together with the eight directional devas, their retinues, and the cast metal wheels, there are separate diagrams for these. From the king's palace down to the common people, all should carry out this method. If it is not universally practiced, there will be no effect. All should install the image in their dwelling places and not give rise to fear. My original vow is to never abandon sentient beings. I constantly dwell in each place, mingling with defilement and impurity, causing all to become pure. Even leftover food offered to me serves as an offering. One should also receive the five-syllable heart mantra of Mahāvairocana. Troops and soldiers among the people should also wear this mantra and offer leftover food, causing their merit to increase.

"In the royal palace, on the first and fifteenth day of each month, offerings should be made according to the method, with various incenses, flowers, superior banners, jeweled nets, ghee lamps, arghya water, jeweled vases, jeweled vessels, fine paste-incense, superior delicious foods and medicinal drinks, accompanied by the performance of various excellent music. At that time the king should set aside his pride and generate a humble mind, personally supplicating on behalf of all sentient beings throughout his realm with compassion, reverently making offerings, circumambulating, and bowing, earnestly praying and making supreme vows, increasing the power of merit so that the people may dwell in peace and not suffer calamity. Each year in spring and autumn, perform the homa according to the teaching, making offerings according to the method to express the king's sincere commitment, not growing weary for the sake of all sentient beings, seeking supreme merit for the realm. After such emplacement in the eight directions, that land shall be forever free from all calamities. The nāgas and devas shall rejoice and take pity on all beings, and all buddhas shall constantly guard and protect all sentient beings. Through the power of this empowerment, all shall cultivate conscience and shame, together practice the ten wholesome deeds, and the realm shall naturally become peaceful, prosperous, and abundant. The people shall enjoy all happiness. The merit of the benevolent king's transforming influence shall increase and continue without end; his life shall be prolonged, the land shall flourish, and he shall possess great majestic power like a lion-king, whom none can overcome."

"Next, the method of sequential emplacement. When about to emplace, begin from the east. In the east, select superior ground free from all defilement, establish boundaries and empower it. At the emplacement site, dig a pit three cubits deep and three cubits wide, coat it with cow dung, and prepare paste-incense, arghya water, lamps, food and drink, ghee, milk, and fruit. Separately make a maṇḍala one cubit square. Upon this altar place the image of the presiding deity, adorned with banners and flowers, and make extensive offerings. At midnight, perform the homa. In the pit, place gold, silver, lapis lazuli, coral, agate, pearls, amber, and the seven jewels atop the wheel. At the center of the wheel, drive in an iron vajra-stake, empowering it with 1,080 recitations, one hammer-blow per recitation, until the full count is complete. At the four corners, scatter the five grains, then fill the pit with earth and level it. When emplacing, from after noon until before midnight, select an auspicious time and complete the emplacement. After midnight, perform the homa. On the four sides of the altar, use silk cloths and coins of the five directional colors to complete the emplacement. At dawn, complete the merit-making. Above the emplacement site, place a white image of Acala Vidyā-rāja in the four-armed form, together with the deva of that direction and retinue, each holding their own seal. Offer incense and flowers toward the deity. The white image should be made to the height of the patron's body. When the work is not yet complete, first open the image's eyes, make extensive offerings, and perform great music, making everything supremely excellent. Having emplaced in this manner, do the same all around the eight directions as described above. Then in the royal palace, install the two-armed root form of Acala Vidyā-rāja. Having established and installed it according to the method and teaching, the king should then issue a decree to all four quarters and all jurisdictions: from ministers and officials above down to common people below, all should install the image of Acala in their dwelling chambers as described, and also recite and practice the five-syllable heart mantra. The decree should be strict, with further admonitions regarding the precepts. Offerings of leftover food must not be allowed to cease, so that together the realm may be pacified and protected universally.

"Even if someone before this great holy lord should sing, dance, play, laugh, or generate a mind of disrespect, this great Vidyā-rāja will not become displeased or depart. I bestow supreme merit. If any sentient being offers even a little leftover food as an offering to me, it is equal to offering immeasurable supremely wonderful treasures, a hundred flavors of food and drink, garments and bedding, supreme medicinal drinks, palaces of the seven jewels, elephants, horses, carriages, and servants throughout the ten directions. Indeed it surpasses this a hundred thousand myriad times over, constituting a true and vast offering. When performing offerings, the most auspicious thing is to wear red-colored garments.

"Next, the method of constructing the homa hearth and the materials used. When performing the homa, before the Vidyā-rāja construct a homa hearth one cubit wide and half a cubit deep. Take water flowing in the proper direction from the rivers around the city or town of that place, and take earth from that river mixed with various incenses. Empower and knead it into clay, and use this clay for the hearth altar. Then coat it with cow dung. In the center, mark the sword seal. On the practitioner's left is the patron's seat, then the minister's seat, then the general's seat, then the landowner's seat. They should enter the practice hall in person with a mind of utmost sincerity and sit in their proper positions within the hall. No more than three persons may be inside the altar. Outside the altar on all four sides, seven paces away, armored soldiers should stand bearing weapons in full array. Various excellent music should also be played, extensive offerings made, and the king's decree broadly proclaimed.

"Before the great holy lord, for the sake of all sentient beings, install the image of the directional deva of that quarter and retinue according to the method. When performing the merit-making, one should observe the fire's appearance. If there are inauspicious signs, dispel them according to the teaching. Once the emplacement is complete, at that site summon three persons capable of practice and commission them to maintain it, offering incense three times daily and sweeping and making offerings. On the first and fifteenth of each month, make offerings of lamps, food, and various music according to the method. The devas will rejoice and protect that territory, and there shall be no decline or calamity, only universal peace.

"For the homa, use four catties or two catties of cow ghee, the five grains, sesame, milk-curd, food and drink, fruit, beeswax candles, mung beans, paste-incense, five-flavored agarwood, sandalwood, storax, camphor, turmeric, benzoin resin, frankincense, white mustard seeds, and honey. According to the patron's age, the homa wood sticks should be twelve finger-breadths long. Mung beans, white glue, and the patron's own garments serve as substitutes for his presence. These items used in the practice hall serve the same purpose as if the patron were personally making offerings and reverently gazing upon the deity. For each emplacement: one iron vajra-stake and one copper wheel. The stake should be eight and a half finger-breadths or longer, in a single-pronged form with distinct ridges. The lower portion of the stake should fit into the center hole of the wheel. The wheel is one cubit wide with four inner spokes and eight outer prongs, each protruding as a triple-pronged half vajra head. The center of the wheel has a hole to receive the iron stake. Two homa ladles are needed for the copper ritual. For each emplacement: one banner of Acala and one banner of the directional deva-king. When troops are on the march, these banners lead the vanguard, with the general following behind. When not on the march, they should be installed at the place of the image and kept from defilement.

"When performing the homa, at both the beginning and end, cast three ladles of ghee and carefully observe the fire's appearance. If there are inauspicious signs concerning the land or people, portending calamity or misfortune, dispel them according to the teaching. If evil signs appear and no remedial action is taken, within three years there will certainly be a corresponding disaster. Otherwise, perform great merit-making; wherever relief is needed, it should be done without delay.

"When performing the homa, if the fire swirls clockwise with flames rising straight upward, or produces the sounds of music, bells, and chimes, or takes on auspicious forms, this is a sign of great good fortune. If the fire moves to the left and takes the form of inauspicious birds or beasts, or makes the sound of 'ha, ha,' then that land also faces calamity: sentient beings will freeze and starve, floods and droughts will come at wrong times, and people will die of famine. One should perform the śānti method to dispel this. If during the homa the fire produces fog, dark clouds, and murky gloom spreading downward, and the fire in the hearth goes out so that the offering cannot be completed, that land also harbors hidden plots and treachery. One should perform the abhicāra method to dispel this. For the above four kinds of auspicious and inauspicious signs, observe with the utmost care and detail, and dispel each according to its sign.

"For the śānti rite, use milk-sap wood; in the hearth mark the wheel seal; food and garments should be white. For the puṣṭi rite, use white sandalwood; in the hearth mark the banner seal; food and garments should be red. For the vaśīkaraṇa rite, cast lotus flowers into the fire (if unavailable, substitute mixed flowers); in the hearth mark the lotus seal; food and garments should be pink. For the abhicāra rite, cast chinaberry wood, thorns, honey-locust spines, and poisons; food and garments should all be black.

"If a gold-wheel-turning king performs the emplacement for all sentient beings under the four heavens, it protects within ten thousand yojanas. A silver-wheel king protects within eight thousand yojanas. A copper-wheel king protects within sixty yojanas. An iron-wheel king protects within three thousand yojanas. For the sentient beings of one's own land, the emplacement protects within one hundred yojanas. At a distance of one thousand *li* in each direction from the royal city, and beyond one hundred *li* from the city, select a convenient location for the emplacement, provided it does not exceed the proper directional position.

"The emplacement ritual, maṇḍala, images, and all materials used have now been briefly explained. You sentient beings who have aroused the mind of awakening, for the sake of all sentient beings, study and practice the homa according to the teaching, including the hearth design and arrangement of images. When performing the abhicāra rite, the target's name must not be openly displayed; it must be done in secret so that those in the vicinity do not know. Only the supervisor should know. The medicinal materials must be prepared in advance and carried personally, ready for immediate use."

This secret method is not permitted for monastics. The one who practices this method must personally bow to all the devas when making offerings. Because monastics hold the Buddha's prohibitory precepts, it makes people uneasy to receive bows from them, and thus the merits sought will not be accomplished. Those who violate this will incur great transgression. For this reason, it is not transmitted to monastics. This method was received by Vajrabodhi Tripiṭaka from his great master in India. It must not be transmitted freely, and is permitted to be bestowed only upon laypeople. Practitioners should keep it most secret. In the West, this method is greatly valued. Even when it is to be practiced, it is kept secret so that other lands do not know of its existence. Thus in the West, it has been transmitted from generation to generation without interruption. Furthermore, the West is guarded about its methods; they circulate only the image-form. Among the various divisions, this is considered the greatest protection, and most do not know that there exists such a method for pacifying and protecting the home and state. Vajrabodhi Tripiṭaka bestowed this ritual method upon the practitioner Wang Guang.

### Methods of the Eight Directional Deva-King Banners for Acala's Destruction of Enemies, with the Enemy-Subduing Mantras of the Eight Directional Devas

**East**: Make a white banner. On the banner, paint Indra Deva-king holding a single-pronged vajra in the right hand with the left hand on the hip, mounted on a six-tusked white elephant. The banner-bearer recites Indra's enemy-subduing mantra:

*Oṃ indrāya devādhipataye hūṃ phaṭ svāhā.*

When marching eastward, this banner leads. Visualize Indra leading immeasurable retinues of devas bearing various weapons to charge the enemy host.

**Southeast**: Make a yellow banner. On the banner, paint Agni Deva with four arms, mounted on a blue ram, body covered in flames. The right hands display the fearlessness-granting gesture and hold a rosary; the left hands hold a sage's staff and a water-flask. The banner-bearer recites Agni's enemy-subduing mantra:

*Oṃ agnaye ṛṣy-adhipataye hūṃ phaṭ svāhā.*

When marching southeast, this banner leads. Visualize Agni leading immeasurable fire-sages bearing various weapons to charge the enemy host.

**South**: Make a black banner. On the banner, paint Yama Deva mounted on a water-buffalo, holding a Yama-staff in the right hand with the left hand on the hip. The banner-bearer recites Yama's enemy-subduing mantra:

*Oṃ yamāya pareta-adhipataye hūṃ phaṭ svāhā.*

When marching south, this banner leads. Visualize Yama leading immeasurable ghost-armies bearing various weapons to charge the enemy host.

**Southwest**: Make a somber-colored banner. On the banner, paint the Lord of Rākṣasas mounted on a lion, holding a sword in the right hand with the left hand on the hip. The banner-bearer recites the Lord of Rākṣasas' enemy-subduing mantra:

*Oṃ tridiye rākṣasa-adhipataye hūṃ phaṭ svāhā.*

When marching southwest, this banner leads. Visualize the Lord of Rākṣasas leading immeasurable rākṣasas bearing various weapons to charge the enemy host.

**West**: Make a red banner. On the banner, paint Varuṇa Deva mounted on a tortoise, holding a serpent-lasso in the right hand with the left hand on the hip. That deva has seven nāga-heads atop his head, shaped like serpents. The banner-bearer recites Varuṇa's enemy-subduing mantra:

*Oṃ varuṇāya nāga-adhipataye hūṃ phaṭ svāhā.*

When marching west, this banner leads. Visualize Varuṇa leading immeasurable venomous nāgas bearing various weapons to charge the enemy host.

**Northwest**: Make a blue banner. On the banner, paint Vāyu Deva mounted on a deer, holding a banner in the right hand with the left hand on the hip. The banner-bearer recites Vāyu's enemy-subduing mantra:

*Oṃ vāyave vaikha-cāru-adhipataye hūṃ phaṭ svāhā.*

When marching northwest, this banner leads. Visualize Vāyu leading immeasurable garuḍa-kings bearing various weapons to charge the enemy host.

**North**: Make a yellow banner. On the banner, paint Vaiśravaṇa Deva-king mounted on a ghost, holding a jeweled stūpa in the right hand with the left hand on the hip. The banner-bearer recites Vaiśravaṇa's enemy-subduing mantra:

*Oṃ kubera yakṣa-adhipataye hūṃ phaṭ svāhā.*

When marching north, this banner leads. Visualize Vaiśravaṇa leading immeasurable yakṣa armies bearing various weapons to charge the enemy host.

**Northeast**: Make a light-colored banner. On the banner, paint Īśāna Deva mounted on a yak, holding a trident in the right hand with the left hand on the hip. The banner-bearer recites Īśāna's enemy-subduing mantra:

*Oṃ īśānāya bhūta-adhipataye hūṃ phaṭ svāhā.*

When marching northeast, this banner leads. Visualize Īśāna leading immeasurable ghost-generals and warriors bearing various weapons to charge the enemy host.

The great general himself recites the mantra of Acala Vidyā-rāja, visualizing his own body as having become Acala Vidyā-rāja. He further visualizes Acala Vidyā-rāja commanding the eight directional deva-kings: "Each of you lead your immeasurable armies to destroy the rebellious enemies!" The great general recites Acala Vidyā-rāja's mantra without cease. If one performs this method, the enemy forces will certainly be annihilated without any doubt. The mantra of the Holy Acala Vidyā-rāja:

*Namaḥ samanta-vajrāṇāṃ caṇḍa-mahāroṣaṇa sphoṭaya hūṃ traṭ hāṃ māṃ.*

When encamped, the eight banners are placed outside, each in its proper direction surrounding the army. Wherever the soldiers make camp, the eight banners are also erected, each in its proper direction. The banner-bearers burn benzoin incense and offer pure foods to the banner of their respective direction. The great general rests at the center and burns the finest agarwood incense, also offering pure food. Make a stone image of Acala Vidyā-rāja twelve finger-breadths tall, and paint the enemy's form and write his name upon it. Place this stone image on top to suppress it. Before long, the enemy will be destroyed.
