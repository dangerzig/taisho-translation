# 人本欲生經
## Sūtra on the Origin of Beings' Desire for Rebirth

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0014

"Thus, Ānanda: craving conditions seeking; seeking conditions gain; gain conditions deliberation; deliberation conditions desire for enjoyment; desire for enjoyment conditions the going forth in pursuit. Through craving as condition, one becomes unwilling to relinquish and clings; through clinging as condition, one comes to have a household; through having a household as condition, one comes to guard and protect. From guarding and protecting as a basis, Ānanda, come weapons and staves; from weapons and staves come quarrels and disputes, deceitful speech, oppression of high and low, all manner of slander and wrongdoing; altogether, these harmful and wicked phenomena.

"If originally, Ānanda, there were nothing to protect and nothing to guard, and there were no guarding at all; without any guarding, Ānanda, could there be weapons and staves, quarrels and disputes, deceitful speech, oppression of high and low, all manner of slander and wrongdoing; altogether, these harmful and wicked phenomena?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda: this being the arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition; weapons and staves, quarrels and disputes, deceitful speech, oppression of high and low, all manner of slander and wrongdoing, altogether these harmful and wicked phenomena arise through guarding. Because of guarding, Ānanda, come weapons and staves, quarrels and disputes, deceitful speech, oppression of high and low, all manner of slander and wrongdoing, altogether these harmful and wicked phenomena. Thus all this is simply great suffering arising through the accumulation of the five aggregates. Having a household as condition brings about guarding. Therefore this is said; you should understand from this, Ānanda, that guarding arises through the condition of having a household. If having a household had no causal condition and there were no grasping, through what causal condition could there be a household? If having a household had no causal condition whatsoever, could there be a household? Could there be weapons and staves, quarrels and disputes, deceitful speech, oppression of high and low, all manner of slander and wrongdoing, altogether these harmful and wicked phenomena?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, from this arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition, guarding comes to exist. Through guarding, and from the household, Ānanda, guarding comes to exist. Through the condition of avarice and unwillingness to relinquish, the household comes to exist. From this causal condition this arises; you should understand from this causal condition, Ānanda, that the household arises through the condition of avarice and unwillingness to relinquish. If avarice and unwillingness to relinquish did not exist, Ānanda, there would also be no grasping. With no grasping, could there be avarice and unwillingness to relinquish? If avarice and unwillingness to relinquish did not exist at all, Ānanda, could there be a household?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "There could not."

"Thus, Ānanda, arising from this, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition; the taking possession of a household arises. Because of avarice and unwillingness to relinquish, Ānanda, the household comes to exist. From the going-forth toward grasping as condition, Ānanda, avarice and unwillingness to relinquish come to exist; therefore this is said, and from this causal condition this arises. Thus you should understand from this causal condition, Ānanda: from the impulse to grasp comes grasping, and from this grasping comes avarice and unwillingness to relinquish. If there were no impulse to grasp, Ānanda, could there be grasping? And through what causal condition could one grasp? Without any impulse to grasp at all, Ānanda, could there be avarice and unwillingness to relinquish?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, arising from this, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition, avarice and unwillingness to relinquish come to exist. Through the going-forth toward grasping; thus also the going-forth. Through existence as condition, Ānanda, avarice and unwillingness to relinquish come to exist. Through the condition of desire and lust, Ānanda, the going-forth comes to exist. Therefore this is said; you should understand from this causal condition, Ānanda, that the going-forth arises through the condition of desire and lust. If desire and lust did not exist, Ānanda, there would be no causal condition, and through what causal condition could desire and lust arise? Without any desire and lust at all, Ānanda, could there be any going-forth?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, arising from this, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition, the going-forth comes to exist because there is desire and lust. Therefore, through desire and lust, Ānanda, the going-forth goes forth, having gone forth. Through going-forth comes desire and lust. Therefore this is said; you should understand from this causal condition that desire and lust arise through the going-forth. If there were no going-forth, Ānanda, there would also be nothing to cause going-forth. Without any going-forth at all, Ānanda, could there be desire and lust?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, from this arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition, desire and lust come to exist. Going-forth as condition brings about desire and lust. Through gain, Ānanda, the going-forth comes to exist. This is what is said. You should understand from this causal condition that the going-forth arises through the condition of gain. If there were no gain and no seeking, Ānanda, through what causal condition could there be seeking? Without any gain at all, Ānanda, could there be any going-forth?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, from this arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition; the going-forth is because of gain, and because of gain the seeking goes forth; through the condition of seeking, gain comes to exist. Therefore this is said; you should understand from this causal condition that gain arises through the condition of seeking. If seeking had no causal condition, Ānanda, through what causal condition could there be seeking? And through what causal condition could one seek? Without any seeking at all, Ānanda, could any gain be seen?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, from this arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition, gain comes to exist and seeking comes to exist. Through seeking, Ānanda, gain comes to exist; through craving, seeking comes to exist. Therefore this is said; you should understand from this that seeking arises through craving. If there were no craving, Ānanda, there would be no seeking, no causal condition for seeking, and no craving. Without any craving at all, Ānanda, could there be any seeking?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, from this arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition: through craving, seeking comes to exist; through seeking, craving comes to exist. Now, Ānanda, there is craving for sensual pleasures and there is also craving for existence. Both of these meet at feeling; there is a causal condition for feeling. Ānanda, if asked about this, say: 'Yes.' 'What is the causal condition for it?' Say: 'It arises through the condition of contact.' From this causal condition, Ānanda, you should understand that feeling arises through the condition of contact. If, Ānanda, the eye does not make contact, and there is nothing for it to contact, and the eye cannot make contact; without any eye-contact at all, Ānanda, could there be eye-contact? Could anything arise through eye-contact; whether pleasant, painful, or neither pleasant nor painful?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, from this arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition, eye-contact brings about feeling. Eye-contact as condition, Ānanda, brings about awareness of feeling through the eye. Likewise for the ear; likewise for the nose; likewise for the tongue; likewise for the body. When the mind does not make contact, Ānanda, and there is nothing for the mind to contact, and there is no causal condition for mind-contact; without any mind-contact at all, Ānanda, could there arise through the condition of mind-contact any feeling, any pleasure, any pain, or what is neither painful nor pleasant?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda: this being the arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition; feeling brings about contact; mind-contact as condition, Ānanda, brings about feeling. If asked: 'Is there a causal condition for contact?' Reply: 'There is.' 'What does contact have as its causal condition?' Reply: 'Name-and-form is its condition.' You should understand from this, Ānanda, that contact arises through the condition of name-and-form. If what is present in a place can be encountered and received in that place, then the body of name-and-form comes to be assembled. If, Ānanda, what is present in a place and what can be encountered and received in that place did not exist, could contact and name-and-form exist?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"If, Ānanda, what is present in a place and what can be encountered and received in that place did not exist, and name-and-form thus did not exist, could there be any meeting in contact?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Without any name-and-form at all, Ānanda, could there be contact? Could contact arise?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda: this is the arising, this is the foundation, this is the accumulation, this is the causal condition; from this comes contact, from this comes name-and-form. Name-and-form as condition, Ānanda, brings about contact. If asked: 'Is there a causal condition for name-and-form?' Reply: 'There is.' 'What is the causal condition for name-and-form?' It is said: 'Consciousness is its condition.' You should understand from this causal condition, Ānanda, that name-and-form has consciousness as its condition. If consciousness, Ānanda, did not descend into the mother's womb, could name-and-form accompany the semen and become established?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"If consciousness, Ānanda, having descended into the mother's womb, could not remain and departed, could name-and-form be obtained and established?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Consciousness, Ānanda, is the basis. If a boy or a girl were already destroyed, already deceased, and did not exist, could name-and-form be obtained, grow, and be fulfilled as it should?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, from this arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition, name-and-form comes from consciousness; consciousness is the condition, Ānanda, for name-and-form. If asked: 'Is there a causal condition, Ānanda, for consciousness?' Reply: 'Yes.' 'Through what causal condition does consciousness arise?' 'Consciousness arises through the condition of name-and-form.' You should clearly understand from this causal condition, Ānanda, that consciousness has name-and-form as its condition. If, Ānanda, consciousness could not become established in name-and-form; if consciousness could not abide and could not grow and increase; could birth, old age, death, suffering, and their accumulation arise and be brought about?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, from this arising, this being the foundation, this being the accumulation, this being the causal condition: consciousness brings about name-and-form, and name-and-form as condition brings about consciousness. This is: consciousness is the condition for name-and-form, and name-and-form is the condition for consciousness. Here is where designation stops, here is where meeting stops, here is where the root of disputation is revealed; through wisdom one should not grasp at it.

"Through how many causal conditions, Ānanda, does one take feeling as the self?"

Ānanda said: "This teaching has its basis in the Buddha; this teaching has the Buddha as its rightful source. I take refuge with the Buddha as my basis. I pray the Buddha will explain it, so that through what the Buddha teaches I may receive understanding and benefit."

The Buddha said: "Listen, Ānanda! Well said, well said! Receive it carefully and hold it in mind; the Buddha will now explain."

The Venerable Ānanda responded: "Yes, I will hear it from the Buddha."

The Buddha then said: "Some, Ānanda, view feeling as the self. Others do not view feeling as the self, but see the body as the dharma that experiences feeling and regard this as the self. Others view the body as one thing and feeling as another; not seeing the body as the self, and not seeing the dharma of feeling as the self either; yet seeing the dharma of feeling and reckoning it as not-self, they only regard the body itself as the self.

"For those, Ānanda, who regard feeling as the self, one should address them thus: 'Venerable friend, feeling is of three kinds: there is pleasant feeling, there is painful feeling, and there is neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling. Seeing these three kinds of feeling, which feeling should be regarded as the self?'

"When there is pleasant feeling, Ānanda, at that time the other two feelings have already ceased; painful feeling and neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling. At that time there is only the experience of pleasure. Pleasant feeling, Ānanda, is impermanent, subject to cessation through suffering. When pleasant feeling, Ānanda, has ceased, it departs from the body and is no longer reckoned to be in the body.

"It is thus. When painful feeling, Ānanda, is being experienced, at that time the other two feelings have ceased; pleasant feeling and neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling. At that time there is only the experience of painful feeling. Painful feeling, Ānanda, is impermanent, of the nature to be exhausted through suffering. When painful feeling, Ānanda, has been exhausted, the body no longer experiences or knows it. At that time, Ānanda, one does not experience painful feeling, does not experience pleasant feeling, and does not experience that other feeling.

"At that time the two feelings have ceased; feeling too is suffering. Yet at that time one only experiences neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. Neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, Ānanda, partakes of the dharma-nature; it is impermanent, subject to exhaustion through suffering. When neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, Ānanda, has been exhausted, there should be no self, yet one retains this notion.

"Some, Ānanda, who are practicing the path, take these impermanent phenomena of feeling and view them as the self. Others, Ānanda, who are practicing the path, dispersing themselves among pleasant, painful, and neither-pleasant-nor-painful feelings, view the self through their own perceptions. Thus, Ānanda, through causal conditions it is not appropriate to make feeling into the self or to view the self in feeling.

"For those, Ānanda, who do not take feeling as the self but take the body as the dharma that experiences feeling; one should address them: 'If, Venerable friend, there were no experience of feeling and nothing that experiences, could this still exist?' At that time, Ānanda, a bhikṣu who does not reckon feeling as the self; should this dharma of experiencing feeling still be regarded as the self?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Thus, Ānanda, through these causal conditions it is also not appropriate, nor should it be permitted: that the body, though taking feeling to be absent, is regarded as the self, with the body as merely the dharma that experiences feeling.

"For those, Ānanda, who neither take feeling as the self, nor see feeling as not-self, nor see the body as experiencing feeling, nor see the dharma of feeling and take it as the self; but rather think 'I,' without being aware that this body is the self; one should address them: 'You who entirely reckon the self as something that does not experience feeling; could a self still exist?' At that time, a bhikṣu for whom feeling is not the self, the body also does not experience feeling, the dharma of feeling is also not the self; the self would be only a body that is unaware. When observing the self thus, could a self still exist?"

Ānanda said: "No."

"Through these causal conditions, Ānanda, it is not appropriate to make what does not feel into the self, nor to make the body the experiencer, nor to make the dharma the self, nor to make an unaware body the self. Thus, Ānanda, all making of feeling into a self has already been seen through by feeling itself. In how many ways, Ānanda, does one who is practicing the path neither make feeling into a self nor view a self?"

Ānanda replied: "This teaching originates from the Buddha's instruction; I also take it from the Buddha. I pray the Buddha will explain it. When the Buddha has explained it, the disciples will receive it, and this explanation will benefit through understanding."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Hear this, receive this, receive it carefully, receive it deeply, hold it in mind; I will now explain it." Thus did the Venerable Ānanda hear from the Buddha.

The Buddha then said: "There are bhikṣus, Ānanda, who do not make feeling into the self, do not see feeling as the self, do not take the body as the experiencer, do not reckon the dharma of feeling as the self, do not view self-view as the self, and do not proceed from any notion of 'there is a self,' nor, having viewed this, regard it as the self through self-views. Having thus seen, one no longer brings about rebirth in the world. Having no longer taken up the world, one no longer grasps to bring about the world; one no longer sorrows. Having no longer any sorrow, one attains the unconditioned and crosses over the world. One then knows for oneself: 'It is exhausted; birth, old age, sickness, death, and sorrow are finished; the practice is fulfilled; what needed to be done has been done; one will not return to existence in the world.'

"Up to this point, Ānanda, one who is practicing the path does not take feeling as the self and employs skillful means, and also does not view self-views as the self. Through how many causal conditions, Ānanda, does one who is practicing the path take form as the self?"

Ānanda replied: "This teaching originates from the Buddha; the instruction also comes from the Buddha. I pray the Buddha will explain it. When the Buddha has explained it, the disciples will receive it, and this explanation will benefit through understanding."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Hear this, receive this, receive it carefully, receive it deeply, hold it in mind; I will now explain it." Thus did the Venerable Ānanda hear from the Buddha.

The Buddha then said: "There are those, Ānanda, who are practicing the path and take form of limited extent as the self. There are those, Ānanda, who are practicing the path and do not take form of limited extent as the self; they take form of unlimited extent as the self, while taking only the limited formless as the self. There are others, Ānanda, who are practicing the path and take neither limited form, nor unlimited form, nor the limited form as the self. There are others, Ānanda, who are practicing the path and take neither limited form, nor unlimited form, nor limited formless as the self; they take only the unlimited formless as the self.

"For those, Ānanda, who are practicing the path and take limited form as the self in the present; Ānanda, those who practice the path and take limited form as the self: when their body is destroyed in death, they again perceive an image resembling a body. This is not a counterpart engaging in counterpart-practice, yet what is thus attained is just as it is. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path and take limited form as the self engage in effortful reckoning. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path and take limited form as the self produce the binding of defilements.

"For those, Ānanda, who practice the path and do not take limited form as the self but reckon unlimited form as the self; Ānanda, those who practice the path and reckon unlimited form as the self: when the body is destroyed in death, they again manifest an image resembling a body. This is not a counterpart engaging in counterpart-practice, yet what is thus attained is just as it is. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path reckon the self as unlimited form. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path make unlimited form into the self and produce the binding of defilements.

"For those, Ānanda, who practice the path and take neither limited form nor unlimited form, reckoning only the limited formless as the self in the present; Ānanda, those who practice the path and do not reckon limited form as the self: when the body is destroyed in death, they again perceive an image resembling a body. This is not a counterpart engaging in counterpart-practice, yet what is thus attained is just as it is. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path, through the limited formless, reckon what is thus arrived at as the self. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path produce the binding of defilements through the limited formless.

"For those, Ānanda, who practice the path and take neither limited form, nor unlimited form, nor limited formless, nor unlimited formless; taking as the self what falls here in the present; Ānanda, those who practice the path and take neither limited form, nor unlimited form, nor limited formless, nor unlimited formless, reckoning what falls here as the self: when their body is destroyed in death, they again perceive an image resembling a body. This is not a counterpart engaging in counterpart-practice, yet what is thus attained is just as it is. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path, through the unlimited formless, reckon what is thus arrived at as the self. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path cause the binding of defilements through the formless.

"Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path reckon what is thus arrived at as the self. Through how many causal conditions, Ānanda, does one who is practicing the path not make form into the self?"

Ānanda replied: "This teaching originates from the Buddha; the instruction also comes from the Buddha. I pray the Buddha will explain it. When the Buddha has explained it, the disciples will receive it, and this explanation will benefit through understanding."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Hear this, receive this, receive it carefully, receive it deeply, hold it in mind; I will now explain it." Thus did the Venerable Ānanda hear from the Buddha.

The Buddha then said: "There are those, Ānanda, who practice the path and do not make form into the self, do not reckon form as the self; neither unlimited form, nor limited form, nor reckoning unlimited form as the self.

"For those, Ānanda, who practice the path and do not make limited form into the self, and do not reckon this as the self in the present; Ānanda, those who practice the path and do not make limited form into the self, also do not fall into reckoning this as the self: when their body is destroyed in death, they no longer perceive an image resembling this body. They are not a counterpart in counterpart-practice; thus this does not exist. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path do not take the limited formless as the self and do not reckon it as this self. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path, through the limited formless, do not cause the body to be bound by defilements.

"For those, Ānanda, who practice the path and do not take unlimited form as the self, nor make form into the self in the present; Ānanda, those who practice the path and do not take unlimited form as the self, nor reckon this as the self: when their body is destroyed in death, they no longer perceive an image resembling a body. They are not a counterpart; thus and thus this does not exist. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path do not take unlimited form as the self and do not fall into the reckoning of a self. Thus, Ānanda, up to this point those who practice the path do not take unlimited form as the self and do not cause binding through defilements.

"For those, Ānanda, who practice the path and do not take the limited formless as the self, and do not fall into the reckoning of a self in the present; Ānanda, those who practice the path and do not take the limited formless as the self, and do not fall into the reckoning of a self: when their body is destroyed in death, they no longer perceive an image resembling a body. They are not a counterpart; thus and thus this does not exist. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path do not take the limited formless as the self and do not fall into the reckoning of a self. Thus, Ānanda, up to this point those who practice the path, through the limited formless, do not cause binding through defilements.

"For those, Ānanda, who practice the path and do not proceed with the unlimited formless as the self, nor fall into the reckoning of this self in the present; Ānanda, those who practice the path and do not take the formless, Ānanda, as the self, nor fall into the reckoning of a completed self: when their body is destroyed in death, this body no longer retains such reckoning. They are not a counterpart; thus and thus this does not exist. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path do not proceed with the unlimited formless as the self and do not fall into the reckoning of this self. Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path do not proceed with the unlimited formless and do not cause binding through defilements.

"Up to this point, Ānanda, those who practice the path do not proceed with the unlimited formless as the self, neither completing a self nor falling into the reckoning of this self. There are also these seven stations, Ānanda, where consciousness can abide, and two bases from which liberation is obtained through practice.

"There is the realm of form: therein are beings with various bodies and various perceptions; such as humans and certain devas. This is the first station of consciousness.

"There is the realm of form: therein are beings with one body and various perceptions; the devas known as the Brahmā heavens, dwelling in their primordial abode of long life. This is the second station of consciousness.

"There is the realm of form: therein are beings with one body and various perceptions; the devas known as the Radiant Sound [Ābhāsvara]. This is the third station of consciousness.

"There is the realm of form: therein are beings with one body, one form, and one perception; the devas known as the Universally Pure [Śubhakṛtsna]. This is the fourth station of consciousness.

"There is the formless realm: therein, having entirely surpassed perceptions of form and with manifold perceptions ceased, one dwells with the mind established in the reception of the wisdom of boundless space. The devas are known as those who dwell in the practice of the wisdom of space [Ākāśānantyāyatana]. This is the fifth station of consciousness.

"There is the formless realm: therein, having entirely passed through the practice of space and gone beyond boundless consciousness, one dwells with the mind established in the reception of wisdom. The devas are known as the sphere of the wisdom of consciousness [Vijñānānantyāyatana]. This is the sixth station of consciousness.

"There is the formless realm: therein, having entirely passed beyond the sphere of the wisdom of consciousness, one dwells with the mind established in the reception of the wisdom of nothingness, gone beyond the boundless. The devas are known as those who receive the wisdom of nothingness [Ākiṃcanyāyatana]. This is the seventh station of consciousness.

"What, Ānanda, are the two bases from which liberation is obtained through practice? There are those who practice the path through the causal condition of form, so that they no longer experience perception; the devas known as the Non-Percipient Ones [Asaññasatta]. This is the first base from which liberation is obtained through practice. There are those who practice the path through the causal condition of the formless, having entirely passed beyond nothingness to receive neither-perception-nor-non-perception; they also have limited perception established in practice; the devas known as Neither-Perception-nor-Non-Perception [Nevasaññānāsaññāyatana]. This is the second base from which liberation is obtained through practice.

"Now, Ānanda, regarding that first station of consciousness; it involves the practice of form, practicing through causal conditions, with various bodies and various perceptions, called humans and those in one place. If, Ānanda, one who is practicing the path has already known this station of consciousness, and also knows this consciousness and its arising, and knows its cessation, and knows its pleasure, and knows the suffering experienced therein, and knows the way of escape from it; when one has such knowledge, Ānanda, is this station of consciousness something that a practitioner of the path should seek, aspire to, or move toward?"

Ānanda said: "No."


"As for the second station of consciousness, Ānanda: it pertains to practitioners on the path conditioned by the realm of form; various bodies with a single perception; the devas called Brahmakāyika, who dwell in the foremost primordial abiding place of long life. If, Ānanda, a practitioner on the path knows this station of consciousness for what it is, knows the arising of this station of consciousness, knows its cessation, knows its pleasure, knows its suffering, and knows the escape from it; when one knows it thus, Ānanda, should a practitioner seek, desire, or take up this station of consciousness as a dwelling?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"As for the third station of consciousness, Ānanda: it pertains to practitioners on the path conditioned by the realm of form; a single body with various perceptions; the devas called Ābhassara [Streaming Radiance]. If, Ānanda, a practitioner on the path knows this station of consciousness for what it is, knows the arising of this station of consciousness, knows its cessation, knows its pleasure, knows its suffering, and knows the escape from it; when one knows it thus, Ānanda, should a practitioner seek, desire, or take up this station of consciousness as a dwelling?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"As for the fourth station of consciousness, Ānanda: it pertains to practitioners on the path conditioned by the realm of form; a single body of form with a single perception; the devas called Subhakiṇha [Universal Purity]. If, Ānanda, a practitioner on the path knows this station of consciousness for what it is, knows the arising of this station of consciousness, knows its cessation, knows its pleasure, knows its suffering, and knows the escape from it; when one knows it thus, Ānanda, should a practitioner seek, desire, or take up this station of consciousness as a dwelling?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"As for the fifth station of consciousness, Ānanda: it pertains to practitioners on the path conditioned by the formless realm; having completely transcended all form perceptions, with earth perceptions subsided, abiding in the reception of boundless-space awareness; the devas called [Sphere of] Boundless Space. If, Ānanda, a practitioner on the path knows this station of consciousness for what it is, knows the arising of this station of consciousness, knows its cessation, knows its pleasure, knows its suffering, and knows the escape from it; when one knows it thus, Ānanda, should a practitioner seek, desire, or take up this station of consciousness as a dwelling?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"As for the sixth station of consciousness, Ānanda: it pertains to practitioners on the path conditioned by the formless realm; having completely transcended the sphere of boundless space, abiding in the reception of boundless-consciousness wisdom; the devas called [Sphere of] Boundless Consciousness. If, Ānanda, a practitioner on the path knows this station of consciousness for what it is, knows the arising of this station of consciousness, knows its cessation, knows its pleasure, knows its suffering, and knows the escape from it; when one knows it thus, Ānanda, should a practitioner seek, desire, or take up this station of consciousness as a dwelling?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"As for the seventh station of consciousness, Ānanda: it pertains to practitioners on the path conditioned by the formless realm; having completely transcended the sphere of boundless consciousness, the boundless [state of] non-use already relinquished, abiding in the received wisdom-action of nothingness; the devas called [Sphere of] Nothingness. If, Ānanda, a practitioner on the path knows this station of consciousness for what it is, knows the arising of this station of consciousness, knows its cessation, knows its pleasure, knows its suffering, and knows the escape from it; when one knows it thus, Ānanda, should a practitioner seek, desire, or take up this station of consciousness as a dwelling?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"As for the first domain of liberation, Ānanda: it pertains to practitioners on the path conditioned by the realm of form; having no perception and receiving nothing; the devas called Non-Perceivers [Asaññasatta]. If, Ānanda, a practitioner on the path knows this domain of liberation for what it is, knows the arising of this domain, knows its cessation, knows its pleasure, knows its suffering, and knows the escape from it; when one knows it thus, Ānanda, should a practitioner seek, desire, or take up this domain of liberation as a dwelling?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"As for the second domain of liberation, Ānanda: it pertains to practitioners on the path conditioned by the formless realm; having completely transcended nothingness through wisdom, abiding in a received state that surpasses non-perception yet has not departed from perception; the devas called [Sphere of] Neither-Perception-Nor-Non-Perception. If, Ānanda, a practitioner on the path knows this domain of liberation for what it is, knows the arising of this domain, knows its cessation, knows its pleasure, knows its suffering, and knows the escape from it; when one knows it thus, Ānanda, should a practitioner seek, desire, or take up this domain of liberation as a dwelling?"

Ānanda replied: "No."

"If, Ānanda, a practitioner knows and sees thus, yet claims not to know and not to see; such fetters exist in that person. At such a time one would assert that things are permanent; at such a time one would assert that things are impermanent; at such a time one would assert that the world has a beginning; at such a time one would assert that the world has no beginning; at such a time one would assert that one who has attained the Way is reborn after death; at such a time one would assert that one who has attained the Way does not die; disputing whether existing or not, whether transcending the world or subject to death; all this arises from such fetters.

Thus, Ānanda, regarding these seven stations of consciousness and two domains of liberation: when a practitioner has seen with the wisdom of the noble truth, has been liberated through the mind, and has attained liberation; this is called, Ānanda, the practitioner's liberation through wisdom, without clinging.

There are also, Ānanda, eight places of liberation. What are the eight? Having form, one observes form: this is the first place of liberation. Not perceiving form internally, one observes external form: this is the second place of liberation. Contemplating the thirty-six objects of the pure body, abiding in received observational practice: this is the third place of liberation. Having completely transcended all form perceptions, with earth perceptions extinguished, not attending to the manifold perceptions, abiding in the full reception of boundless-space awareness; the devas called [Sphere of] Boundless Space: this is the fourth place of liberation. Having completely transcended the sphere of boundless space, abiding in the full reception of boundless-consciousness wisdom; the devas called [Sphere of] Boundless Consciousness: this is the fifth place of liberation. Having completely transcended the sphere of boundless consciousness, perfecting the wisdom-action of nothingness and non-use; the devas called [Sphere of] Nothingness Without Use: this is the sixth place of liberation. Having completely transcended non-use through wisdom, abiding in the full reception of neither-perception-nor-non-perception; the devas called [Sphere of] Perception: this is the seventh place of liberation. Having completely transcended neither-perception-nor-non-perception, with the cessation of perception and feeling, the body thoroughly abiding in complete quiescence in full reception: this is the eighth place of liberation.

When, Ānanda, a practitioner achieves liberation from the seven stations of consciousness and two domains of liberation, together with these eight places of liberation; with such wisdom, having further seen and completely restrained and relinquished, having attained liberation, and with this foundational merit the body abides in complete quiescence; this is called, Ānanda, the practitioner's liberation through both practices, without clinging."

Thus spoke the Buddha. Ānanda received these teachings and practiced accordingly.
