# 觀經
## Sūtra on Contemplation

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2914

Author unknown.

---

### Contemplation of the Buddhas of the Ten Directions

The method of contemplating the buddhas of the ten directions. Sit and contemplate the east. A vast, expansive radiance appears. One sees only a single buddha, sitting in full lotus, raising his hand and teaching the Dharma. Observe clearly with the mind: the radiant marks and excellences are vividly distinct. Constantly keep this in mind, not allowing external conditions to distract. If other thoughts arise, gather the attention and bring it back.

When you see in this way, gradually increase to ten buddhas, then see ten buddhas. Increase to a hundred buddhas, then a thousand buddhas, then immeasurable, boundless buddhas. If they are near, then extend further and broader. One sees only the radiance of all the buddhas connecting with one another. Observe clearly with the mind. Having attained this, further contemplate the southeast in the same manner as above. Once accomplished, extend to the west and all the way to the ten directions, likewise.

Having already seen buddhas in every direction teaching the Dharma and giving prophecies, then at one time contemplate the buddhas of the ten directions. In a single moment of concentration, one universally sees them all. When the settled mind is accomplished, within concentration one sees the buddhas of the ten directions, who all teach the Dharma to oneself. The net of doubts is entirely removed, and one attains the patient endurance of the unborn.

If due to past karmic conditions one does not see the buddhas, one should for one day and one night practice the sixfold repentance, exhortation, sympathetic joy, and dedication. Gradually one will come to see the buddhas. Even if the buddhas do not teach the Dharma, this person's mind will attain peace and joy, free from all afflictions.

### The Five Gates of Meditation

The *Sūtra on the Essentials of the Five Gates of Meditation* says: the essential method of seated meditation has five gates. First is mindfulness of breathing. Second is contemplation of impurity. Third is the meditation of loving-kindness. Fourth is contemplation of dependent origination. Fifth is mindfulness of the Buddha.

Mindfulness of breathing and contemplation of impurity, together with contemplation of dependent origination: these three gates involve internal and external realms. Mindfulness of the Buddha and the meditation of loving-kindness focus on external realms.

The reason for the five gates is to correspond to sentient beings' ailments. If one has a predominantly scattered mind, teach mindfulness of breathing. If one has predominantly craving and attachment, teach contemplation of impurity. If one has predominantly anger, teach the meditation of loving-kindness. If one clings predominantly to the self, teach contemplation of dependent origination. If one has a mind that is sluggish and sinking, teach mindfulness of the Buddha.

Then, with single mind, contemplate the Buddha. When contemplating the Buddha, one should do so with utmost sincerity. Contemplate the Buddha's marks and excellences, discerning them clearly one by one. Afterward, close the eyes and recall them in the mind. If the image is clear, then open the eyes and observe with the utmost mind until it is vivid. Then return to sitting, correct the posture, and keep the mind focused as before, as though facing the true Buddha with vivid clarity and no difference.

Then report to the teacher: "Keeping my mind focused, I see the Buddha with no difference." The teacher says: "Return to your seat and focus the mind on the forehead. With single mind, recite the Buddha's name." At that time, a buddha-image will appear on the forehead. From one to ten, and then to a boundless number.

If the practitioner sees many buddhas emerging from the forehead, and they go far from the body and then return, this indicates a person of the Great Vehicle. For people of the three vehicles, the buddhas that emerge return near the body, and the ground turns golden. All these buddhas cause the ground to become flat as the palm and pure as a mirror. Contemplate one's own body as bright and pure like the ground. This is called the realm of buddha-mindfulness samādhi.

Having attained this realm, report to the teacher. The teacher says: "This is a good realm. This is called the initial gate of contemplation."

Then focus the mind on the heart. Afterward, contemplate the Buddha, and one sees all the buddhas emerging from the heart, holding lapis lazuli staffs in their hands. From the two ends of the staffs, light-flames of varying sizes emerge for the three vehicles. After they emerge, the last buddha holds the staff, stands upright in the heart, and then the last buddha turns around and re-enters. The previously departed buddhas all return and follow in. For a person of the lesser vehicle, once all enter, it stops. For a person of the Great Vehicle, once all have entered, they emerge from all the pores of the body, filling the four seas, rising above to the summit of existence, and descending below to the wind-base.

When this radiance is complete, it returns to the person's body like pure lapis lazuli. The reason the radiance returns to the person's body is to demonstrate the realm of heroic vigor and swift power. When the marks and excellences are thus, report to the teacher. The teacher says: "This is called the station of all-mindfulness. Because it can give rise to all concentrations, it is called the station of mindfulness. The staff is the mark of concentration. The radiance is the mark of wisdom. This is the realm of an inner ordinary being."

Then one is told to abandon the previous two contemplations and focus the mind on the navel. With single mind, contemplate the navel. Before long, one perceives a moving sensation at the navel. Observe carefully without confusion. One sees something within the navel, like a hawk's egg, its color bright and white. Report to the teacher again. The teacher says: "Observe further where it is."

Upon further contemplation, there is a lotus flower with a lapis lazuli stem and a golden platform. On the platform sits a buddha in full lotus. The first buddha is within the navel. Again there is a lotus flower, and upon it another buddha sitting in full lotus. In this way they successively emerge, extending all the way to the edge of the great ocean. The last buddha re-enters the navel of the second buddha. The second buddha re-enters the navel of the third buddha. In this way they successively return until they reach the person's navel.

The Buddha causes each buddha to enter the practitioner's navel. Then, from all the pores of the practitioner's body, lotus flowers emerge in a circle, filling the sky, like hanging jeweled necklaces. All these lotus flowers that are born then return into the navel. The practitioner's body becomes soft, light, and joyful. One sees one's own body bright and pure, like the color of mixed jewels. Report to the teacher. The teacher says: "Excellent! This is the mark of accomplished concentration."

Then, told to further contemplate within the navel, one sees five auspicious marks at the crown of the head. Upon contemplating them, one sees a buddha within the radiance, sitting in full lotus. Upon further contemplating the buddha, one sees things within: from the buddha's mouth emerge various lotus flowers, which spread and fill the great earth. Upon further contemplating the five-colored buddha, one sees five lions emerge from the navel. They eat the emerging lotus flowers and then return into the five-colored light. When the lions in the buddha's navel have entered, the five-colored light and the buddha enter the practitioner's crown. This is called the mark of the concentration of the lion's vigorous advance.

The practitioner observes the light entering the buddha's body, and the practitioner's body becomes golden. One sees something within the navel, bright and pure like the sun and moon. Upon seeing this, report to the teacher. The teacher says: "Contemplate further." One then sees buddhas emerge from beneath the armpits and from the waist, four buddhas in all. Each buddha emits immeasurable sunlight, bright and pure. Through the sunlight, one sees the colors of the four continents, above to the summit of existence, below to the wind-base, all vividly clear, as though seen in the palm of one's hand, without any obstruction. This is called "the liberation of purity."

Upon seeing this, one again sees the four buddhas return through the places from which they emerged. After they enter, one further sees radiant flames: what entered in front comes out behind, what entered behind comes out in front, what comes out on the left enters on the right, and what comes out on the right enters on the left. Having completed this, one sees one's own body bright and pure, with water on four sides perfectly filled with pure radiance. This is called "buddha-mindfulness samādhi in the realm of the fourth dhyāna."

### The Method of Contemplating White Bones

First contemplate the body's skin, flesh, sinews, and veins all decaying away, until only white bones remain connected. Their color is like mother-of-pearl snow, and their radiance is likewise. If one cannot see them, consider the following parable: it is like a person with leprosy whom the physician tells must drink blood that is the same color as milk in order to be cured. The family has a silver vessel filled with blood and gives it to the patient to drink, saying: "Your illness will be cured." The sick person says: "Blood I certainly will not eat." They tell him: "It is white milk. Can you not see that the substance inside is entirely white? It is because of your karmic transgressions that you see blood. Just imagine it as milk. Do not think of it as blood." After seven days in this way, it indeed transforms into milk. How much more so with what is truly white bone: how can one fail to see it?

When one sees the bone-person, within the bone-person one's mind of arising and ceasing continues in succession like a thread stringing pearls. What is seen in this way, and the contemplation of the external body, are likewise. If the mind persists with diligence without slackening, it is like drilling for fire and seeing smoke, or digging a well and reaching mud: obtaining water is not far off. If the mind is calm, then in walking, standing, sitting, and lying down, one always sees white bones, vividly clear, like water that is clear allows one to see one's reflection, but when turbid, one cannot see it.

### Buddha-Mindfulness Samādhi

The Buddha is the Dharma-king. He can enable people to attain various kinds of wholesome dharmas. Therefore the meditator should first be mindful of the Buddha. Being mindful of the Buddha can cause immeasurable transgressions to be extinguished. If a person is mindful of the Buddha, the Buddha is also mindful of that person. It is like a king who is mindful of a person: enemies cannot encroach upon or harm that person. Being mindful of the Buddha is likewise: evil dharmas dare not afflict one.

Some say: "The Buddha is no longer in the world. How can one be mindful of him?" No confidence that a person possesses surpasses this. One should contemplate a fine buddha-image as though seeing the true Buddha. First, from the fleshy uṣṇīṣa and the ūrṇā mark between the brows, descend to the feet, then return to the uṣṇīṣa, carefully observing each mark. Then return to a quiet place, close the eyes, recall and focus the mind on the image, not allowing other conditions. If other thoughts arise, gather them and bring them back. The mind observes of its own accord. When one can see as desired, this is called the concentration of contemplating the conditioned.

One should make this reflection: "I did not go, and the image did not come. That I see the Buddha is surely due to the mind being settled and the thought being fixed."

Having attained this contemplation of the Buddha, then further advance to contemplate the living body, and one will be able to see it as though face to face, without any difference.

If the thought-mind scatters and engages with many unwholesome dharmas, one should be like a wet-nurse watching a child, not allowing it to commit evil. If the mind refuses to stay, then rebuke it: "The sufferings of birth, old age, illness, and death constantly press upon you. If born in heaven, one delights in exquisite pleasures and has no means to govern the mind. If one falls into the evil destinies, the fear and suffering allow no wholesome mind to arise. Now, encountering the Buddha's teaching, one should be single-mindedly mindful of the Buddha." Having rebuked oneself in various ways, the mindful mind, in walking, standing, sitting, and contemplating, will constantly be able to see the Buddha.

Afterward, further advance to the living-body dhyāna-concentration. Progressing successively, one easily enters the living-body contemplation. The Dharma-contemplation: since the contemplation of the image has been accomplished with the mind following characteristics, one then gathers the attention, enters concentration, and is able to see. One should base the mind on the image and, through mindfulness, contemplate the living body thus: "As when he sat beneath the bodhi-tree, radiance shone from his head, and his marks were extraordinary." Or: "As when in the Deer Park he taught the four truths for the five bhikṣus." Or: "As when on Vulture Peak he emitted great radiance and taught prajñā for the great assembly." Using whichever place, keep the mind focused on it, not allowing it to scatter. Once the mental image has stabilized, one will see the Buddha. The entire body will be filled with joy, pervading the four limbs. It is like one who is hot obtaining coolness, or one who is cold encountering a warm room. No worldly joy can be compared to it.

The Dharma-body contemplation: having seen the Buddha's living body in empty space, one should, based on the living body, contemplate the inner dharma-body: the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the eighteen exclusive dharmas, great compassion, and immeasurable wholesome karma. It is like a person who first sees a golden vessel and then sees the jewel inside. The reason is that the dharma-body is truly wondrous, its divine wisdom without compare, without near or far, without difficulty or ease. Worlds without limit are all present before the eyes. There is not a single dharma it does not know, not a single dharma it does not comprehend. Therefore the practitioner should concentrate single-mindedly, not allowing the mind to scatter. If the mind has other conditions, gather them and bring them back.

Furthermore, when any being's life comes to its end, at the moment of death, all the sense-faculties first fail, as though falling into a great pit, with terror reaching up to the Brahmā heavens. The boundless thieves of death can only be rescued by the Buddha's power. The Buddha raises up various kinds of human, heavenly, and nirvāṇa joys.

Furthermore, all buddhas, life after life, have always for the sake of all sentient beings not spared their own bodies and lives. For example, when Śākyamuni Buddha was a prince and went out and saw a person with leprosy, he immediately ordered the physician: "You must obtain fresh human blood for him to drink, and then he will be cured." The prince said: "Such a person is hard to find. One death, one life: this is not acceptable." He then offered his own body to the patient, enabling the illness to be cured. The Buddha does the same for all sentient beings.

The Buddha's kindness is deeper than that of a father or mother. Even if all people were divided into one part and two parts, within that part one should be mindful of the Buddha and should not have other thoughts. Through various kinds of merit like this, one follows mindfulness and acts accordingly. If this mindfulness is accomplished, one severs all fetters. One can even attain the patient endurance of the unborn. If one does not attain concentration, one will be reborn in the six desire-heavens, the most honored and foremost. Afterward, one will be reborn before all buddhas.

### Contemplation of Amitāyus Buddha

The contemplation of Amitāyus has two kinds of practitioner.

For the practitioner of dull faculties: first, contemplate a spot one inch on the forehead, with no skin, seeing only red bone. Keep this thought-after-thought in the mind, not allowing other conditions. If other thoughts arise, gather them and bring them back. Once accomplished, cause the red bone, measuring one square inch, to turn white as snow. Once it is thus, contemplate one's entire body as entirely white bones, also the color of snow.

Once accomplished, further contemplate the body's bones transforming to lapis lazuli color, with radiance clear and pure, contemplating both interior and exterior transparently. Having seen this, further cause the lapis lazuli body to emit sunlight, from near to far, pervading Jambudvīpa with nothing seen but radiance.

Then gather the light back into the body. Once it has fully entered, emit it again as before. In all contemplative practices, proceed from easy to difficult, from little to much. After emitting sunlight from within the body, within the light contemplate Amitābha Buddha. That buddha's body emits great radiance, seated facing west. Carefully observe each mark, then contemplate the entire body sitting in full lotus, like a mountain of purple gold. Keep the mind focused on the wondrous marks, not allowing other conditions. If other thoughts arise, gather them and bring them back. Facing the Buddha in sitting, with no difference: before long, one will be able to see him.

For the practitioner of sharp faculties: one can directly contemplate and create sunlight. Then contemplate the radiance of the dharma-realm. Within the light, contemplate, and one can immediately see the Buddha.

If the practitioner wishes to be reborn in the pure land, one should make this contemplation. Then contemplate the true characteristics of all dharmas thus: "The world is like a dream, like an illusion, all without reality. It is only through inverted and deluded thoughts that afflictions arise and one receives the retribution of transgressions. It is like a person seeing small children quarreling over tiles, stones, earth, and wood, and becoming angry and vexed." Contemplating the world is likewise.

One should, with great compassion and vows, resolve to liberate all sentient beings. Constantly subdue the mind and cultivate the two kinds of patience, namely patience toward sentient beings and patience toward dharmas.

Patience toward sentient beings: if sentient beings as numerous as the sands of the Ganges harbor evil minds toward oneself, one does not give rise to anger. When various kinds of reverence are shown, one does not give rise to delight. Furthermore, contemplate sentient beings as without before or after. If there were a before, then there would be no causes and conditions. This means there is no beginning. If there is no beginning, then there is also no middle or end. Contemplating in this way, one falls into the two extremes of eternalism and annihilationism. Using the path of security and peace to contemplate all sentient beings, one does not give rise to wrong views. This is called patience toward sentient beings.

Patience toward dharmas: one should contemplate all pure dharmas as ultimately empty, with the mind unobstructed and able to endure. This is called patience toward dharmas. Those who have newly aroused the mind, even if they have not yet attained this patience toward dharmas, should practice in this way. Furthermore, contemplate all dharmas as ultimately empty, yet toward sentient beings constantly arouse great compassion. Whatever wholesome deeds there may be, without exception dedicate them all, vowing to be reborn in the land of Amitāyus Buddha.
