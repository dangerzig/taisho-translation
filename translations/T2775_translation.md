# Notes on the Brief Preface Preceding the Vimalakīrti Commentary

## *Wéimó Shū Shì Qián Xiǎoxù Chāo* (維摩疏釋前小序抄)

Taishō 2775

Author unknown. Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a sub-commentary (chāo) on the prefatory essay (xiǎoxù, "brief preface") to a major Vimalakīrti Sūtra commentary. The preface being explicated appears to be the one composed by the Tang-dynasty Tiāntái-affiliated exegete Zhànrán or a related figure, which introduces a commentary on the Vimalakīrti Sūtra (Wéimójié Suǒshuō Jīng, T475, Kumārajīva's translation). The sub-commentary methodically glosses the preface phrase by phrase, covering: (1) the historical introduction of Buddhism to China, beginning with Emperor Míng of the Hàn dynasty's dream of a golden figure and the arrival of Kāśyapa Mātaṅga and Dharmaratna (Zhú Fǎlán) with sūtras and images, followed by the construction of the White Horse Monastery and translation of the Sūtra of Forty-Two Sections; (2) the early Wèi and Jìn dynasty translations, which are described as "coarse in language and halting in principle" due to translators' incomplete mastery of Chinese and limited understanding of the Mahāyāna "great contemplation"; (3) the arrival of Kumārajīva in Cháng'ān in 401 CE (Hóngshǐ 3rd year), his translation of the major Mahāyāna texts (the Mahāprajñāpāramitā, the Zhōnglùn, the Shí'èr Mén Lùn, and the Bǎi Lùn), followed by his re-translation of the Vimalakīrti and Lotus sūtras; and (4) the significance of the four great disciples of Kumārajīva (Sēngzhào, Dàoshēng, Dàoróng, and Sēngruì). The sub-commentary also glosses Sēngzhào's preface to the Vimalakīrti commentary, explaining the Tiāntái categories of "original ground" (běn) and "traces" (jì) as applied to the sūtra's teaching on the inconceivable.*

---

## Part One: The Arising of the Teaching

In explaining the preface, there are two divisions: first, clarifying the sequential arising of the teaching; second, explaining the reason for composing the commentary. Within the first, the reason for first clarifying the sequence is this: since the teaching arose in the West [India] in the order of "first the three [vehicles], then the one [vehicle]," and as the Dharma was transmitted to the East [China], it likewise proceeded from the shallow to the deep. The metaphor of entering the ocean can make this clear. The phrase "subtly beginning" refers to this.

The sequential account is in three sections: the first four sentences narrate the source and arising of the teaching; the second section (beginning with "When it reached...") describes how the ancient translations did not yet achieve full clarity; the third section (beginning with "From the Qín [era]...") shows that [Kumārajīva's] sūtra achieved the true principle.

### The Introduction of Buddhism to China

"In the past, during the reign of Hàn Míng[dì], the Dharma-teaching first flowed [to this land] from the region west of the Onion [Mountains]." This refers to the Yǒngpíng era of Emperor Míng of the Hàn dynasty [58-75 CE]. Kāśyapa Mātaṅga (*Jiāshè Móténg*) and Dharmaratna (*Zhú Fǎlán*), coming from the kingdom of Yuèzhī, first brought sūtras and images across the Onion Range (the Pamirs) to transmit them to this country. Because they came from the region of the Onion Range, it says "first flowed from the west of the Onion."

"They first erected stūpas and images; translation was still obstructed." "First" means "beginning." They established stūpas and images. "Stūpa" is the Sanskrit *stūpa*, translated as "shrine" (*miào*). "Images" means likenesses carved, molded, or painted in the semblance of the Buddha. "Obstructed" means "blocked," signifying that [fuller transmission] had not yet been achieved. When the holy teaching first flowed [eastward], ordinary minds had long been in darkness. To transmit the profound purport and gain people's trust was indeed difficult. Therefore, they first erected stūpas and images and translated the Sūtra of Forty-Two Sections, so that people would know that the cultivation of causes and effects has its proper place. This was a gradual beginning of transmission. Because stūpas and images came first, the text says "first erected"; because broader translation had not yet occurred, it says "still obstructed."

As the *Zhōushū Yìjì* [Records of Marvels from the Zhōu Dynasty] states: the World-Honored One appeared in Central India, born in the city of Kapilavastu. At the time of his birth, the earth trembled in six ways, rivers flooded, and wells overflowed. A five-colored radiance penetrated the Taiwei [constellation] and spread across this land. This was in the twenty-fourth year of King Zhāo of Zhōu, the *jiǎyín* year [1028 BCE]. King Zhāo asked: "What does this portend for the realm?" Sū Yóu answered: "At present, nothing. But after a thousand years, his teaching will reach here." [The King] thereupon inscribed a stone record and buried it before the Southern Suburban Temple.

Twenty-seven years later, King Zhāo died and King Mù succeeded. In the fifty-second year of Mù's reign, the *rénshēn* year [949 BCE], the earth again trembled six times. Rivers flowed backwards. Black clouds arose on all sides. Wild beasts howled. A white light appeared in the west. King Mù, perplexed, asked the Grand Scribe Hù Duō, who answered: "This is the sign that a sage of the western land has entered nirvāṇa."

[The sub-commentary continues with accounts of Liú Xiàng finding Buddhist scriptures in the imperial archives, and the definitive narrative of Emperor Míng's dream and the dispatch of envoys to India in 67 CE, the arrival of Kāśyapa Mātaṅga and Dharmaratna with scriptures on a white horse, and the founding of the White Horse Monastery in Luòyáng.]

### The Wèi and Jìn Translations

"When it reached the Wèi and Jìn, [translation] was first opened and propagated." "Wèi" here refers to the [Cáo] Wèi [220-265 CE]. "Jìn" here refers to the Western Jìn [265-316 CE]. "First opened" means initial dissemination of the teaching.

"Yet the sūtras produced [in this period] were still coarse in language and halting in principle." "Coarse" means distant [from the original sense]; "halting" means difficult [to penetrate]. As in the translation of the *Dharmapadavadāna*: "People see the heavenly; people's principle..." Although this conveys something of the text, it loses the literary grace. And as in the translation of this [Vimalakīrti] sūtra: "Not coming, yet appearing as [both] humiliated and arriving; not seeing, yet appearing as seeing." Though the language has literary form, it loses the principle. Both are cases of coarse language and halting principle.

"This was because the scholars had not yet attained the great contemplation," and "the transmitters lacked fluency in regional languages." "Great contemplation" means the correct contemplation of the Mahāyāna: the realm extinguished and mind stilled, without characteristics, without name. Without name, the path of language is cut off. Without characteristics, the activity of the mind is extinguished. As Dharma Master [Sēng]zhào said: "The mouth wishes to speak, but words fail. The mind wishes to grasp, but thought expires." Contemplation of impermanence, suffering, and emptiness is the "small"; the middle-way correct contemplation is the "great."

"The foreign monks who first arrived had not [mastered] the regional languages." Because they lacked fluency in Chinese, the sūtra texts were coarse and stilted.

"Since the great Dharma had only just begun to trickle [into this land], one could not yet judge its gains and losses." The great Dharma is the Mahāyāna. "Trickle" means gradually seeping, still shallow. In the Wèi-Jìn period, the teaching had not long been transmitted. It was like the first seepage of a sweet spring, still thin and shallow. The translations had qualities one might call gains and qualities one might call losses. Like a fine jade only partially illuminated, or a bright pearl half-flawed: therefore one could not yet judge their gains and losses.

### Kumārajīva's Translation

"From the third year of the Hóngshǐ era of the [Later] Qín [dynasty], in winter, [Kumārajīva] entered the pass." The [Later] Qín refers to the dynasty of Yáo Xīng, with its capital at Cháng'ān [401 CE]. Previously, Fú Jiān of the Former Qín had sent the general Lǚ Guāng to conquer Kuchā and bring back Kumārajīva. After Fú Jiān's defeat and death, Lǚ Guāng established his own kingdom (the Later Liáng) in Gūzāng. When Yáo Xīng succeeded, in the third year of Hóngshǐ [401 CE], Kumārajīva arrived in the capital. Yáo Xīng received him with the highest courtesy, treating him as a national preceptor.

"He first translated the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā*, the *Dazhìdù Lùn*, and then the *Zhōnglùn*, the *Shí'èr Mén Lùn*, and the *Bǎi Lùn*." The *Zhōnglùn* is the Madhyamaka-śāstra (*Zhōng Lùn*). The *Shí'èr Mén* is the Dvādaśamukha-śāstra. The *Bǎi* is the Śata-śāstra.

The purpose of translating in this sequence is explained: by first steeping [the audience] in the ultimate principle [through the Prajñā and Madhyamaka texts], and then re-translating this sūtra [the Vimalakīrti] and the Lotus Sūtra, the text and principle of the new translation became supremely precise and penetrating, with "no gap between them" (*wú jiānrán*).

"Precisely because [Kumārajīva] first steeped [the students] in the ultimate principle, wandering masterfully in the true teaching, the re-translation of this sūtra achieves words that are precise and a principle that arrives [at reality]." The previous four translations were imperfect; Kumārajīva's re-translation (the fifth) was definitive.

## Part Two: The Reason for Composing the Commentary

The sub-commentary then turns to the reason the commentary was composed. There are two sub-sections: first, noting that present-day transmission has lost the true purport (the "distant reason"); second, praising the ancient [Kumārajīva-school] masters as having arrived at the truth (the "proximate reason").

"In recent days, those who transmit and study [the sūtra] are mostly careless about the Way but esteem learning, taking 'breadth' as the measure, gazing at heterodox ends." They neglect the deep principle and prize the accumulation of doctrinal information, taking prolixity for breadth and missing the true point. As the *Lúnyǔ* says: "To pursue heterodox ends is truly harmful." "Heterodox ends" means divergent paths that do not converge on the same destination.

"The Guānzhōng [Cháng'ān] masters' earlier compositions are concise in language and deep in purport; they will serve posterity, but some may find the brevity difficult to penetrate." Kumārajīva's four great disciples in particular (Sēngzhào, Dàoshēng, Dàoróng, and Sēngruì) all profoundly penetrated the true vehicle. Their compositions are terse yet profound. This is not their deficiency; rather, it is because later ages have grown dim in understanding.

## Part Three: On Sēngzhào's Preface

The sub-commentary then turns to Sēngzhào's preface to the Vimalakīrti commentary, divided into four sections: (1) a general account of "original ground" and "traces"; (2) a specific discussion of the arising of the teaching; (3) the reason for the re-translation; and (4) the purpose of the commentary.

### Original Ground and Traces

"Vimalakīrti's Inconceivable Sūtra": this names both the person [Vimalakīrti] and the dharma [the inconceivable]. Though the person is specific to this sūtra, the sūtra itself applies universally. What the Mahāyāna reveals is textually diverse yet identical in principle. Therefore "the inconceivable" is not limited to a single teaching.

In general, the inconceivable has two aspects: (1) the "original ground" (*běn*), and (2) the "traces" (*jì*). This establishes conventional names to reveal the reality of true characteristics.

"It exhausts the subtlety and fully transforms: this is the designation of the wondrous and transcendent." "Exhausting the subtlety" refers to penetrating the dharma-body's wondrousness; "fully transforming" refers to completing the response-traces' spiritual transformations. As in the chapter "Seeing Akṣobhya Buddha": the first part, on contemplating the Tathāgata, clarifies the original ground; the latter part, on fetching the Abhirati world, clarifies the traces. The "Gate of Non-Duality" clarifies the original ground; the "Fragrant Meal" chapter and the "Inconceivable" chapter clarify the traces.

"Its purport is profound and abstruse." Its meaning is deep and far-reaching.

"It cannot be fathomed by language or image." Without image, it cannot be fathomed by image. Without name, it cannot be fathomed by language.

"The Way surpasses the three emptinesses and is not what the two vehicles can discuss." The "Way" is the true principle. "The three emptinesses" are the Hīnayāna's three gates of liberation (emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness), not the Mahāyāna's [understanding of them]. The great Mahāyāna principle of one characteristic surpasses the realm of the two vehicles' three emptinesses, and therefore the two vehicles cannot discuss it.

"It transcends the domain of enumeration." The "enumerable things" are the three categories [of dharmas], the four characteristics, and so on. The principle of the Dharma, being profoundly empty, is beyond what the three categories can encompass or the four characteristics can change.

"It extinguishes the realm of the minded." Whatever the mind can grasp has characteristics. Since the Dharma is without characteristics, the mind's grasping naturally ceases.

"Vast and boundless, it is unconditioned yet there is nothing it does not do." Unconditioned: the principle of the unconditioned pervades the dharma-realm. Responding to stimuli and pervading all: therefore there is nothing it does not do. The unconditioned is the original ground; "nothing it does not do" is the traces.

### Holy Wisdom and Holy Body

"Thus, holy wisdom has no [discriminative] knowledge, yet illuminates all things." "Holy wisdom" is the non-discriminative wisdom of the sage. Because it is free from characteristic-grasping discrimination, it is called "no knowledge." Yet because it is free from defilement, its subtle illumination is all the more brilliant. Though without knowledge, it illuminates all things.

"The dharma-body has no image, yet manifests in myriad distinct forms." "No image" refers to the dharma-body as the original ground: all buddhas of the ten directions share one dharma-body. "Myriad distinct forms appearing in response" is the response-body as traces.

"The supreme music has no words, yet the wondrous texts spread everywhere." The "supreme music" is the teaching. Without words is the principle; the wondrous texts spreading is the expediency. The sūtra says: "In these nameless, characterless [things], provisional names and characteristics are used for teaching. Therefore, throughout the day one names yet does not name; throughout the day one speaks yet does not speak."

"The hidden mechanism is without guile, yet its movement accords with events." "Hidden mechanism" means subtle, spontaneous response. "Without guile" means responding without calculating intention. Like a clear mirror that has no mind yet reflects every form: the sage's mind, though without deliberation, responds to beings without error.

### The Function

"Therefore it can universally bring order to all regions, opening beings to their tasks, benefiting all under heaven, while for me there is non-action." "Opening beings" means guiding sentient beings to know the tasks of cultivation. "Benefiting all under heaven while for me there is non-action": benefiting beings through manifested [response], yet responding only when stimulated, hence "for me there is non-action."
