# 五苦章句經
## Sūtra of Verses on the Five Sufferings

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0741

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhú Tánwúlán (竺曇無蘭).

---

The World-Honored One said: "In the three realms and five destinies, birth and death are unceasing. There are altogether five sufferings. What are the five sufferings? The first is the suffering of the heavenly beings, the second is the suffering of the human realm, the third is the suffering of the animal realm, the fourth is the suffering of the hungry ghosts, and the fifth is the suffering of hell.

"What is the suffering of the heavenly beings? From the first heaven up to the twenty-eighth heaven, excluding the heaven of the non-returner, all are attained by those who uphold the five precepts, practice the ten wholesome deeds, and cultivate the four dhyānas. Because they lack the wisdom-mind of the Way, they undergo birth, old age, sickness, and death. Some do not live out their full heavenly lifespan, for their destiny varies according to what they did in past lives, and lifespans are longer or shorter accordingly. The heavenly beings face two great calamities: first, the exhaustion of lifespan; second, the exhaustion of the kalpa. The exhaustion of the kalpa has three causes: first, great fire; second, great wind; third, great water. The exhaustion of lifespan has seven signs: first, the light around the neck is extinguished; second, the flowers on the head wilt; third, the complexion changes; fourth, dust appears on the garments; fifth, sweat issues from the armpits; sixth, the bodily form becomes gaunt and thin; seventh, flies alight, and one naturally departs from one's original seat. When the water calamity strikes, a great flood arises reaching as far as the fifteenth heaven, and everything within it is annihilated. When the wind calamity strikes, the great Vairambhaka wind arises from all four directions, blowing Mount Sumeru and all the famous mountains so that mountains crash against mountains, grinding them to fine dust, and nothing remains. When the fire calamity strikes, seven suns appear simultaneously, standing still without moving, burning and destroying heaven and earth until all is like molten gold, and everything in the desire realm is annihilated. Even the highest four heavens, though their lifespan is eighty billion four hundred million kalpas, must all eventually die and fall into the eight evil realms. This is the first suffering.

"The second is the suffering of the human realm. There are hundreds and thousands of varieties. Humans are truly wearied. From slaves, servants, beggars, and people of low status, through the middling ranks of the wealthy and noble, up to emperors and wheel-turning sage-kings, all undergo birth, old age, sickness, and death, hunger and thirst, cold and heat, pain and distress, worry and affliction, calamity and misfortune. There may be soldiers and bandits, prison and execution, burning by fire and drowning in water, falling from heights and being crushed, struck by bricks, stones, knives, or staves, trampled by runaway carts or bolting horses, attacked by enemies and robbers. They harm one another in every way; their manners of death are myriad. All sentient beings who have not escaped the three realms share in these. This is the second suffering.

"The third is the suffering of the animal realm. Flying insects and creeping creatures, things that walk on legs and things that breathe, birds and beasts, up to elephants, nāgas, and the king of the golden-winged birds: all are animals. They too suffer hunger and thirst, cold and heat, worry and hardship. The strong subdue the weak; they devour one another. Some are slaughtered, hunted, trapped in nets, and their flesh is offered to people. The variations are myriad and cannot be fully described. This is the third suffering.

"The fourth is the suffering of the hungry ghosts. There are nine kinds of hungry ghosts. The first type has a body one league in length, with a throat as narrow as the eye of a needle. When they walk, their joints and bones crack with a sound like five hundred carts. Flames shoot from their throats, burning them from within. If they see flowing water and approach, it immediately dries up, and they cannot swallow a single mouthful. If they manage one swallow, it transforms into pus and blood, or into boiling excrement, or into molten copper. Their throats swell naturally, and the burning liquid passes through them below, searing everything without exception. Until their offenses are exhausted, their bodies naturally reconstitute, and so it continues. All of them were formerly human beings who made their livings through violence and oppression, intimidation and coercion, without reason or principle, who were miserly and greedy, eating alone. Therefore they receive this retribution. This is the fourth suffering.

"The fifth is the suffering of hell. Iron cities and cauldrons of boiling liquid, forests of swords and mountains of knives, iron pillars and molten copper, pus and blood, freezing ice, boiling excrement and salt water, chariots of bamboo-leaf fire, furnaces of coals and fire-nails, sixteen poison-thorns. Crows, magpies, cunning dogs, quails, and vulture-birds whose beaks are entirely of hard iron fly into people's mouths and pierce through inside and out, devouring the five organs. North, south, east, and west, there is no place of refuge. The prisons of bitter, poisonous punishment number altogether eighteen. All who receive punishment, regardless of rank, undergo it according to the gravity of their evils. Some complete their term in one kalpa or half a kalpa; some cannot be released sooner. When their punishment is finished, they are reborn in the world to receive further retribution. This is the fifth suffering.

"The eight evil places are: first, hell; second, the hungry ghost realm; third, the animal realm; fourth, the borderlands; fifth, the long-life heavens; sixth, though one obtains a human body, one is blind, deaf, mute, or crippled in hands or feet and cannot listen to or receive the teaching; seventh, though one obtains a human body with all six faculties intact and worldly intelligence and eloquence, one studies worldly texts and believes in wrong and inverted views, makes sacrifices to ghosts and demons, or engages in slaughter and hunting, giving free rein to the mind, committing fraud and deception in myriad ways, not believing in the Three Treasures. From this, in the next life one returns to hell, entering from darkness into darkness, with no time of liberation. Should one occasionally become human, one still does not believe in what is right, does not honor the Three Treasures, and slanders the holy Way. Eighth, being born in a place where the Buddha has already departed. These are the eight evils, also called the eight difficulties.

"The three evil paths are the home of all sentient beings. Briefly obtaining human form, briefly being reborn in heaven: these are like being a guest. The days of the visit are few, and the days of returning home are many. Students should reflect on this and strive with diligent effort, for it is possible to escape from suffering. A human body is hard to obtain; complete faculties are hard to have; eloquence of speech is hard to achieve; intelligence is hard to come by; long life is hard to acquire; a wise teacher is hard to encounter; right faith is hard to maintain; a great aspiration is hard to generate; the sūtras are hard to hear; a Tathāgata is hard to meet. There is a tree in the world called the udumbara, which has fruit but no flowers; only when a Tathāgata appears in the world does it produce flowers. Having already obtained a human body, with the six faculties complete, with eloquence and intelligence, with an extended lifespan, having encountered a wise teacher, having generated the bodhisattva aspiration, possessing right faith without regression, having fully heard the sūtras, and meeting a Tathāgata's age: all of these come from past practices. Those who are endowed with merit go from brightness to brightness, following the Tathāgata's traces, accumulating practices without ceasing, arriving at the place of awakening. Do not damage these roots and lose your former efforts! A single loss of the aspiration for the Way can set one back for kalpas. Be careful, be careful! All sentient beings are perpetually in a great prison surrounded by twelve layers of walls, fenced with three layers of thorny hedges, with six sword-wielding bandits constantly lying in wait. To escape from within this prison is extremely difficult, extremely difficult!

"What is the great prison? It is the three realms. What are the twelve layers of walls? They are the twelve links of dependent arising. What are the three layers of thorny hedges? They are the three poisons. What are the six sword-wielding bandits? They are the six sense faculties. Having generated the aspiration for the Way, one should fully maintain the precepts, the four immeasurable minds of great compassion, the six perfections, mindfulness of breathing, the thirty-seven factors of awakening, all dhyānas, samādhis, and gates of dhāraṇī. Equalizing all dharmas, the mind neither rises nor falls, without thought and without aspiration, one emerges through the three gates of liberation, attains the three methods of cure, distinguishes the three directions, comprehends the three penetrating wisdoms, and is neither bound nor freed. Not seeking heavenly or human honor, not moved by the position of a wheel-turning king, not fearing the suffering of offenses, not counting the toil, with one's aspiration directed at all beings without any pride, understanding the emptiness of the three realms, not habituating to the three forms of existence: this is called escaping the twelve-layered great prison. Knowing the arising and cessation of the twelve links of dependent arising and being able to sever the root of delusion: this is called escaping the twelve layers of walls. Knowing that lust, anger, and delusion, the three defilements, have no origin, and the mind no longer clings: this is called uprooting the three layers of thorny hedges. Clearly understanding the six sense faculties as all without beginning or end, like a plantain tree, and the mind not bound in attachment: this is called separating from the six sword-wielding bandits.

"One must first understand that there is no self and no other, and that nothing whatsoever is done. Yet nothing is not done: the meritorious deeds one performs do not weary one even over billions of kalpas. It is like a bird flying through empty space, leaving no footprints. One acts without traces, and none can see it. Not associating with offensive matters or evil conditions even so much as a hair's breadth: this is what it means for one who has generated the bodhisattva aspiration to be able to cross over the ocean of suffering. The household is a prison; wife, children, wealth, and precious jewels are fetters and chains; clinging through love and attachment is a heavy burden."

The Buddha addressed his disciples: "All good men and good women! Having left home, you have escaped the prison. Having abandoned wives and children, you have shed the chains. How can you not let go of the heavy burden?"

The renunciants said: "We carry no burden!"

The Buddha said: "You renunciants cling to 'I' and 'mine' and are attached to the body and to lifespan: this is your heavy burden. Seeking only offerings and accumulating possessions: this is your heavy burden. Not getting along with fellow students and instead befriending laypersons: this is your heavy burden. Being proud of your clan and lineage, conceited and arrogant: this is your heavy burden. Relying on your wisdom to look down on the foolish and disdain others: this is your heavy burden. Being obstinate and willful, not accepting others' admonitions: this is your heavy burden. Eating without moderation, drinking alcohol and craving flavors: this is your heavy burden. Not having proper Dharma robes and wearing worldly clothing: this is your heavy burden. Appearing proper outwardly but harboring flattery and deceit within: this is your heavy burden. Not restraining the six sense faculties, breaking precepts and indulging desires: this is your heavy burden. Levying taxes on the populace to build temples: this is your heavy burden. Making offerings to ghost-mothers and praying to spirits for blessings: this is your heavy burden. Exploiting the Buddha's Dharma to practice spells and heal illness: this is your heavy burden. Betraying the assembled sages and violating the four grave prohibitions: this is your heavy burden. Wandering without a fixed abode and not returning to the monastery: this is your heavy burden. Those who do not set down their burdens will hereafter enter hell."

The Buddha said: "There is a great white elephant of tremendous strength that can move mountains, gouge out valleys, uproot trees, and crush boulders. The elephant's strength is matchless. Yet if someone ties a single hair around its foot, the elephant stumbles and cannot move."

The Buddha told his disciples: "You should understand this parable and reflect on it well! If a worthy person living in a household practices the Way, grows weary of worldly possessions, and perceives the suffering, emptiness, and non-self of all things, always wishing to leave the body, renounces home, wife, and children to follow a wise teacher and receive the Dharma robes: on the day of departure, wife and children weep with longing, their cries pitiful, their words heartrending. If the worthy person sees this and the heart falters, if the mind turns and changes, deceived by wife and children, and the aspiration to leave home is lost, then it is like the hair binding the elephant, unable to move, and one will long suffer decline!"

The Buddha said: "Of all strongmen, none surpasses the mind. The mind is the enemy; it always deceives and misleads. The mind seizes hell, the mind seizes the hungry ghost realm, the mind seizes the animal realm, and the mind seizes heaven and the human realm. Whatever form one takes is all the doing of the mind. One who can subdue the mind for the Way possesses the greatest strength. I have struggled with the mind for numberless kalpas. Only now have I become a Buddha, standing alone in the three realms. All of this is the doing of the mind. Of all fragrances, none surpasses sandalwood. Its fragrance is immeasurable, and its value exceeds that of Jambudvīpa gold. Moreover, it heals the sick. If someone has been poisoned and has a headache and fever, grinding sandalwood powder and applying it, or taking it internally, the illness is immediately cured. All sentient beings wish to obtain it. If someone has a large sandalwood tree and bundles it as firewood to sell, no one buys it. When the Buddha was in the world, the sūtras he taught enabled people to attain the Way, and without exception all were liberated. After his parinirvāṇa, the twelve divisions of the canon remain in the world, amounting to volumes upon volumes, yet no one reads them. This too is like sandalwood bundled as firewood: no one buys it. Of all foul trees, none surpasses the ilanang. Its stench is so toxic that people loathe the sight of it and dread its smell. The ilanang and the sandalwood grow in four combinations. What are the four? First, a sandalwood tree surrounded by ilanang. Second, an ilanang tree surrounded by sandalwood. Third, sandalwood forming a grove with sandalwood alone. Fourth, ilanang surrounding ilanang. What does sandalwood surrounded by ilanang mean? When the head of a household has right faith and practices the Way, but the wife and family do not follow the teaching, holding wrong and inverted views, making sacrifices to ghosts and demons, and not obeying instructions: this is called sandalwood surrounded by ilanang. What does ilanang surrounded by sandalwood mean? When the head of a household believes in wrong and inverted views and makes sacrifices to ghosts and demons, but the wife, children, daughters-in-law, and entire household have right faith in the Three Treasures, do not miss the eight fasting days, practice generosity and virtue, and do not neglect the six perfections; when the head of household scolds and forbids them, but they do not follow the instruction and practice in secret: this is called ilanang as master with sandalwood surrounding it. What does sandalwood forming a grove of sandalwood mean? When the head of a household practices the Way and the entire household follows the teaching without disagreement, all having right faith in the Three Treasures with minds in harmony: this is called sandalwood forming a grove of sandalwood. What does ilanang surrounding ilanang mean? When the head of a household believes in wrong and inverted views, fully practices the ten evils, makes sacrifices to ghosts and demons, and the entire household slaughters with one accord and shared delight: this is called ilanang surrounding ilanang. These four combinations of conditions are all due to differences in past-life mental dispositions, which cause disharmony. Therefore the Vinaya sūtras clarify conditions and the obtaining of offenses and merit. When a sacrificing household kills living beings, ghosts will not partake, and one should not associate with them or eat their food and drink. When entering mountains and marshes and seeing birds and beasts gathered to eat, one should never startle or disturb them or cut off their food. When seeing the slaughter of pigs and sheep, hunting and trapping, or the execution of criminals, one should not watch but should avoid and depart. If one truly cannot avoid it, one should generate great compassion and make a bodhisattva vow: 'When I attain buddhahood, may food and drink in my land arise naturally, with none of these evil conditions!'"

It was said: "In former times, when a queen entrusted incense but did not give it to the butcher's wife, the conditions of offense from birth and death created mutual entanglement. Turning through conditions, sometimes offense leads to merit, sometimes merit leads to offense. When offense and merit meet, there are two kinds of planted fruit. The mind generates conceptions that become the seeds of action. Because there are root afflictions, one later receives the resultant fruit. For this butcher's wife, offense led to merit, but later through mutual encounters she would inevitably be reborn to plant roots of suffering. Therefore the incense was not given to her.

"Father and son, husband and wife, brothers, family, acquaintances, and servants: these relationships arise from five conditions. What are the five? First, being enemies; second, being creditors; third, repaying debts; fourth, fulfilling past vows; fifth, being true friends. What does being enemies mean? Father and son, husband and wife, brothers, kin, acquaintances, and servants meeting and killing one another: this is being enemies. What does being creditors mean? Parents accumulate wealth and the children squander it: this is being creditors. What does repaying debts mean? Children earn wealth to support and provide for parents: this is repaying debts. What does fulfilling past vows mean? In a past life, having formed the aspiration to be family, they meet with joy and goodwill, and with deep mutual respect: this is fulfilling past vows. What does being true friends mean? In past lives, through conditions of the Way and Dharma, they served one another. Later, meeting again, they illuminate the Dharma for each other, progress diligently, and their aspirations are in harmony: this is being true friends.

"In former times, in the household of Anāthapiṇḍada, there were five conditions of meritorious virtue. What are the five? First, timeliness; second, bodily example; third, spoken instruction; fourth, equal provision; fifth, harmony and compliance. What is timeliness? Not missing the six daily periods of paying respect: this is timeliness. What is bodily example? When the head of household rises, all in the house, great and small, follow: this is bodily example. What is spoken instruction? When the head of household wishes to undertake meritorious works, first informing the household and all follow the instruction: this is spoken instruction. What is equal provision? Food, clothing, and provisions are shared equally, including for servants: this is equal provision. What is harmony and compliance? Superiors and subordinates follow one another without disagreement: this is harmony and compliance. Through these five merits, the servants, cattle, horses, livestock, and even flying insects and creeping creatures in the household were all reborn in heaven upon death. Moreover, any person's household, passing travelers, or birds and beasts that passed over the residence: upon death, they too were all reborn in heaven. Because in the household of the elder, every speaking being within the gate recited the Dharma, and the sound of sūtras never ceased. Those who heard the sound enter their ears, and all rejoice: the mind is the root, and therefore they are reborn in heaven. This is also due to the elder's past vow. From numberless kalpas past, his spoken word was sincere, he never deceived or demeaned others, he never associated with evil conditions, and his merit was purely good. Through the power of the great bodhisattva vow, it was made to be so. When the three calamities strike the boundary of heaven and earth and everything within it is annihilated, it does not reach that land. When the great kalpa ends and within a single buddha-field, wherever there are a hundred billion Mount Sumerus and a hundred billion Iron-Ring Mountains, everything is annihilated, but it does not reach that buddha-land. Thus the buddha-lands of all buddhas in the ten directions are of infinite space, infinite sentient beings, infinite buddha-lands, boundless space, boundless sentient beings, and fathomless great thousandfold world-systems. Even if the Tathāgata were to fill them all and, with a lifespan of billions of kalpas, could not finish describing that sentient beings have a beginning or an end. The Tathāgata's wisdom comprehends all sentient beings as bottomless; therefore it is said that the perfection of wisdom is bottomless, and sentient beings are bottomless."

It was said: "The Buddha further taught: 'There are four kinds of birth: first, womb-birth; second, egg-birth; third, moisture-birth; fourth, transformation-birth.' This is spoken by way of classification to inform all beings of the various types. All sentient beings of the three realms and five destinies, everything that exists, are all transformation-born. Therefore it is said that all things are like a transformation, like a dream, like a shadow, like an echo, like the moon reflected in water, and there is no maker. First understand this principle, and only then can one practice the Way. The Way too is like a transformation; all is without origin, without creation, without making, without beginning, without end. Beginners who hear this are startled and doubtful. Those who are startled and doubtful have three conditions: first, their past merit is insufficient; second, they have not found a wise teacher; third, they have not been diligent in studying the sūtras. Relying on their own ideas and clinging to self and mine, they pursue name and form and crave profit. Their conduct is flattering and deceitful, without genuine faith. Such people cannot approach the deep patience with the unborn nature of all dharmas."

It was said: "Emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness are the essentials of the Way. The wisdom-Way takes emptiness as supreme; study takes the unconditioned as primary. These three statements should not be taught to beginning students. Hearing that there is nothing whatsoever, they loosen their minds, cease to cultivate precepts, and regard nothing as an obstacle. In each of the six perfections they become lax and negligent, saying: 'All is empty; what is there to do?' Their mouths speak of emptiness, but their conduct is in the realm of existence. Falling into the four inversions, they therefore lack merit. Bodhisattvas should not let them hear about the patience of the unborn dharma. A good spiritual friend who wishes to teach a beginner should gradually teach step by step, explaining the workings of Māra and how to guard against them, explaining the conditions of birth and death and the suffering of offenses, making the five destinies clearly known, and establishing faith in the workings of offense and merit, making everything perfectly clear. Only then may one speak of the Way.

"In former times, King Prasenajit competed with the Buddha in wisdom. The Buddha told the king: 'If you were to grind the ocean to make ink and fell all the trees to make brushes, and write down what I know as sūtras, the ocean water would be exhausted and the tree branches all used up, but my sūtras would not be finished.' The reason for this is that the Buddha possesses the three penetrating wisdoms, and past, present, and future are all comprehended without exception. The Buddha's sūtras are exceedingly numerous, measured by the vastness of space. The Buddha's wisdom is vast and deep, with the unconditioned as its source. What the sūtras expound is inconceivable; some passages are intricate and difficult to understand. Broadly speaking, there are six things by which one can know the essentials: first, the right Way; second, skillful means; third, the ultimate teaching; fourth, encouragement; fifth, merit and virtue; sixth, precepts and prohibitions. What is the right Way? Teaching that there is no beginning, no creation, no making, nothing whatsoever, nothing from which things arise, no practice and no attainment, natural suchness: this is the right Way. What are skillful means? Transforming in unlimited ways, sometimes appearing and sometimes dwelling, entering into each type of being, creating conditions, teaching as is timely, not matching fixed phrases, simply working to transform and liberate: these are skillful means. What is the ultimate teaching? Pointing out the workings of offense and merit: 'Do this and receive this; all is brought about by one's own actions; nothing is arbitrarily given.' The matter is clear and evident: this is the ultimate teaching. What is encouragement? Opening the minds of the uninstructed, showing them what guards and benefits them, increasing their lifespan and fortune, obtainable in this very life: this is encouragement. What is merit and virtue? The six boundless perfections that govern the six sense faculties, controlling and guarding the gates of the senses, enabling one to attain heaven, the human realm, or the status of a wheel-turning sage-king, with everlasting happiness without limit: this is merit and virtue. What are precepts and prohibitions? Guarding speech, restraining the mind, the body not killing, not stealing, not committing sexual misconduct, not deceiving, honoring parents, and not becoming intoxicated. The suffering of the three evil paths cannot be endured for long: these are precepts and prohibitions. First understand these principles, and only then can one practice the Way. It is like handling a net: first grasp the main cord, and all the meshes will be straight. If one does not understand how to hold the main cord and first tries to sort out the individual meshes, they become tangled and entangled with no end. Study is the same: if one does not comprehend the essentials and hears what the sūtras say without understanding skillful means, unable to make distinctions, people criticize and dispute with each other, each clinging to their own position, giving rise to angry thoughts, losing the original meaning and forgetting the principle, rejecting what is right and pursuing what is wrong. Such students merely echo one another, chasing after sounds without correcting each other. Those who recognize the truth are few; those who fall away are many. Such people bear the name of student in vain!"

It was said: "The Four Noble Truths are: first, the truth of suffering; second, the truth of accumulation; third, the truth of cessation; fourth, the truth of the Way. All sentient beings do not perceive this suffering but take suffering as happiness, seeking peace and comfort within the midst of offense and suffering. Quack physicians make false claims and confuse people's minds, saying: 'What you do can produce results in this very life!' Students who hear this follow eagerly. Hearing the moderate and balanced words of truth, which are contrary to what they wish, they refuse to accept them. Therefore it is said: 'Right speech sounds like its opposite; who can accept it?' Furthermore, they do not know accumulation. Those who know accumulation die and dare not act again. Furthermore, they do not know cessation. Those who know cessation die and dare not act again. Furthermore, they do not know the Way. Those who know the Way, upon hearing the Way, can practice it. Among all worldly people, doing evil is easy and doing good is hard. Among all students, doing good is easy and practicing the Way is hard. Practicing the Way may even be easy, but understanding the Way is hard. Explaining the Way is easy, but practicing it is hard. Therefore it is said: exceedingly difficult, exceedingly difficult!"

It was said: "The Tathāgata's sūtras, precepts, and Vinaya amount to altogether eight hundred forty million scrolls, serving as the remedy for all beings, treating the body, speech, and mind, healing birth, old age, sickness, and death. The teaching for sentient beings has two essentials. What are the two? First, do this and receive this. Second, do not do this and you will not receive this. As the Buddha has said: 'In the three realms and five destinies, the defilement, suffering, and affliction of offenses all arise from action. Nothing comes arbitrarily; it is not bestowed by heaven, nor by ghosts and spirits, nor by emperors, nor by parents, nor by renunciants or brahmins. The offenses and merits one creates follow one like a shadow follows form, like an echo follows sound, never missing even by so much as a hair!'"

The Buddha said: "In former times, I was King Yama, and I possessed vast, universal compassion. All who had fallen into the prison of punishment, the king showed them everything. The king said: 'Why have you come to this place?' The offenders replied: 'When we died, we did not know where our deeds would lead us. All the evils naturally pursued and sent us here. We beg the king to have compassion and pardon our offenses!' The king said: 'What evil did you all commit?' The offenders replied: 'When we were alive, we were unfilial to our parents, we killed, stole, committed sexual misconduct, lied, drank alcohol, fought, brandished weapons, and used our power to oppress good people. We slandered the holy Way. The evils we committed are too many to fully describe! Moreover, we trusted evil teachers who sacrificed to ghosts and spirits, saying it would bring blessings. We slaughtered animals and made offerings to the spirits. We now confess and repent of all our past evils!' The king said: 'When you were in the world, I sent five messengers to inspect the realm and warn you. Why did you not heed their teaching?' The offenders said: 'When we were alive, we truly never saw or heard them!'

"The king said: 'Listen carefully! I will explain for you. The five messengers: the first is this: in the world, when a woman carries a child for ten months, her body becomes ill from it. When the time of delivery comes, father and mother are terrified. Having been safely delivered, one passes from death to life. Nursed and cradled, the wet is exchanged for the dry, and they eagerly await the child growing up, worrying endlessly. Have you seen this or not?' The offenders said: 'We have seen it!' The king said: 'This is my first messenger. The second is this: in the world, there are old people whose complexions have deteriorated, whose hair is white and teeth have fallen out, whose eyes are dim and ears are deaf, whose flesh is wizened and skin is shrunken, who walk hunched over. Have you seen this or not?' The offenders said: 'We have seen it!' The king said: 'This is my second messenger. The third is this: in the world, there are sick people, enfeebled and bedridden, with a hundred pains all coming at once, and fine food turning repulsive. Have you seen this or not?' The offenders said: 'We have seen it!' The king said: 'This is my third messenger. The fourth is this: in the world, there are dead people. The wind of the sword severs the veins and uproots the life-root. The body lies stiff and straight. Before ten days have passed, the flesh decays and blood flows, the body bloats and rots and stinks, and nothing of value remains. While alive they loved each other; in death they all find each other repulsive. Have you seen this or not?' The offenders said: 'We have seen it!' The king said: 'This is my fourth messenger. The fifth is this: in the world, those who commit crimes are bound and sent to prison, placed in stocks and chains, flogged and whipped, subjected to the five tortures. They are executed in the public square, or have their hands and feet cut off, burned by fire, chopped by the axe, beheaded and displayed, or dragged through the five punishments. Have you seen this or not?' The offenders said: 'We have seen it!' The king said: 'This is my fifth messenger.'

"The king further told the offenders: 'Having seen all this, you should have reflected that your own body would also pass through birth, old age, sickness, and death, and that if you committed crimes, you too would suffer such retribution. Why were you not filial to your parents, humble and respectful to elders, with compassion foremost in your heart? What you would not wish for yourself, do not inflict on others. There are wise and virtuous people in the world from whom you should have sought instruction. Taking refuge in the Three Treasures, devoting your heart to the Way, restraining desire and stopping craving: it is possible to cross over suffering. What you have done, you must now receive. I do not wrong you!' The offenders said to the king: 'When we were alive, we truly labored so bitterly that we had no time to do good!' The king told the jailers: 'Take them away to the place of extreme suffering.'

"The jailer's name was Pāng, with an ox-head and human hands, two feet with ox hooves, and tremendous strength that could move mountains. He held a steel trident with three prongs. With one thrust he impaled hundreds of thousands of offenders and cast them into the cauldron. The cauldron was forty leagues in both length and width, naturally holding them so they did not fall out. Until their offenses were exhausted, they were kept from dying. From the mouth to the bottom took a hundred years, and from the bottom back to the top also took a hundred years. This is called the place of extreme suffering. The offenders underwent punishment, and their bitter and poisonous suffering continued through all eighteen stations. Among them, those whose offenses were completed and who were due for release, the king again showed them and said: 'Now go. Whether you are to be born as a child in a human household, you should be mindful of filial piety and repay the kindness of your parents. In the prime of life, you should endure evil and do good, have sincere faith in the Three Treasures, uphold precepts and follow the Way, and cultivate all merits. Do not again commit evil and return to this place. As for hell, it never summons anyone. Reflect well on this for yourselves!' The offenders rejoiced and all exclaimed: 'Long live the king!'"

The Buddha said: "Of those who hear the Dharma and are sometimes believing and sometimes not, who waver in doubt and advance and retreat, returning to wrong views, all have just emerged from hell. Because they received King Yama's teaching but their root of faith is shallow and slight, it is so for them. Even so, whatever merit they create will never be in vain. The Buddha's vast compassion also does not forget them. It is only that the passage of kalpas delays them. After a very long time, they too will eventually cross over to liberation."

At that time the Buddha told Ānanda: "Receive this sūtra, uphold it, recite it, read it, and explain it widely for others. Quickly let it spread far, so that the Dharma's grace flows forth to future generations."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "I shall receive and uphold it! What is this sūtra called, and how should it be practiced?"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! This sūtra is called 'Purifying the Veil of Offenses and Delighting in the Buddha-Dharma,' and its other name is 'The Bestowal of the Inconceivable Light Bodhisattva's Path of Resolve.' You should uphold it! If good sons and good daughters were, for the entirety of their lives, to make offerings to the Tathāgata according to what is appropriate and comfortable, with heavenly flowers piled as high as Mount Sumeru scattered upon the Buddha, along with the finest incenses, anointing fragrances, mixed fragrances, silk canopies, streamers, and banners, humbly and reverently presented, persevering without slackening: even this would not compare with good sons and good daughters who receive this sūtra, uphold it, recite it, explain it widely for others, and follow its teachings in practice. The merit and blessings of such practice surpass those offerings by billions upon billions of times!"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! One should always make offerings to the Tathāgata through the Dharma. If you wish to pay reverence to the supreme great sage, you should receive this sūtra, uphold it, recite it, explain it for others, and accord with the Dharma scrolls."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the venerable Ānanda, and the entire assembly of asuras and people of the world, hearing what the Buddha had taught, all rejoiced, paid reverence, and departed.

> When heavenly merit is exhausted, one falls to become an insect on an ox's neck.
> It is like a great farmer whose harvest is abundant and rich,
> But who only eats and never plants again: when the grain is gone, hunger follows.
> Consuming merit is just the same: when merit is exhausted, one falls into the realm of offense.

> A human body is exceedingly hard to obtain, and complete faculties are also exceedingly hard.
> A hundred kalpas upon a hundred kalpas: only then does one become human.
> Losing the precepts, one loses the basis of being human, all because of clinging to conditions.
> Not knowing satisfaction, one receives suffering as long as the ages.

> Flying insects and creeping creatures: their spirits share a single source.
> Because of committing theft, borrowing without the thought of returning,
> Accepting what is entrusted and then denying it, and butting others with the head,
> One turns and turns within the animal realm, and the suffering is hard to describe in full.

> The Buddha spoke of the suffering of hungry ghosts: nothing but the affliction of hunger and thirst.
> They wander east and west seeking food and drink but never hear the sound of water or grain.
> Their bodies are one league long, naked, with hair wrapped around them.
> All because they were miserly and ate alone, they have fallen into the city of black ropes.

> Between the two Iron-Ring Mountains, how dark, how dark it is.
> The spirit-consciousness falls within, never seeing the light of sun or moon.
> Turning and turning, they cannot see each other, only hearing the cries of anguish.
> Every kind of terrible sound, the pain of suffering pierces the heart.

> Having finally been born as a human, one must receive bodily retribution.
> Blind, deaf, mute, and afflicted, crippled and unable to walk,
> Though the Dharma of liberation exists, one cannot listen to or receive it.
> Through the long night one suffers this, turning like a cartwheel.

> Though one is born with complete faculties, handsome, eloquent, and intelligent,
> Wrong views lead to inversion, and one does not believe in the Buddha's sūtras.
> Perhaps engaging in slaughter, hunting, and trapping, indulging in wine and pleasures and desires,
> One sinks and meets King Yama. Only when punishment arrives does terror come.

> In the borderlands there is no righteousness. Parents and children devour each other.
> Family members sell one another, becoming slaves and servants to others.
> Constantly driven and put to labor, every movement met with blows,
> Though one has a human form, one shares the company of animals.

> Those of pure worldly goodness who have no teacher or Dharma
> Will be born in the long-life heavens, without form but with consciousness alone.
> Though their lifespan may be extended, the three evil paths are near neighbors.
> Afterward they become earthworms, with mud and sand as their food and drink.

> Because of dwelling in the eight difficult places, it is hard to become human again.
> It is like a blind turtle in the ocean trying to find the hole in a floating log.
> Those who died first fall into the Sumeru River. By the time they arrive, it has already passed.
> Meeting the Dharma that has already vanished, one is inevitably born in a place where the Buddha has departed.

> The ship of Dharma is about to break apart. Contemplate and enter the sweet dew.
> Diligence in recitation is the great effort; a good spiritual friend is the teacher.
> Diligent effort is the great strength; the light of wisdom surpasses the sun.
> The sweet dew dissolves all poisons and can also eliminate the five aggregates.

> If a person has faith and abides in the Buddha's teaching and precepts,
> Then the Way opens and becomes smooth, for the gate of sweet dew has been opened.
> The sound of the sweet dew has gone forth; the three realms are universally illuminated.

> The great essential Way has already been opened; one need only walk it with a correct mind.
> Single-mindedly directed in all places, practice the Way without stopping midway.
> The human mind is like a balance: one should always restrain and hold it in check.
> Contemplation of calm and insight: this is the light of the world.
> With palms joined, bow the head: in the three realms, all pay homage to the Buddha.
