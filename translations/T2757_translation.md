# 華嚴經疏卷第三
## Commentary on the Avataṃsaka Sūtra, Fascicle Three

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2757

Translated from the Chinese. Silla, compiled by Wŏnhyo (元曉).

---

### The Tathāgata's Radiance Awakening Chapter

In this chapter, the Tathāgata emanates light that universally illuminates the ten directions, causing the great assembly to eliminate the obstructions of darkness and awaken to the fact that the Tathāgata's body pervades the dharmadhātu. For this reason it is called the "Radiance Awakening Chapter." This answers two questions, the meaning of which is as explained previously.

Moreover, this buddha-light extinguishes all doubts and delusions and removes the multitude of calamities and difficulties. For this reason, it answers those two phrases and further inaugurates the original stage.

Within the faith-mind section, among the two subdivisions, the first raises the fruit that is attained in order to generate the mind of aspiration and delight. From "Having already, in what precedes" onward is the second, where, in relation to the practices to be cultivated, the intention of progressive advancement is generated. Within this, there are four chapters, classified into two divisions. The first two chapters dispel doubts and difficulties in order to generate faith and understanding. The latter two chapters directly explain the virtues of practice so as to encourage progressive cultivation.

Within the first, there are also two. The first chapter dispels doubts; the next chapter resolves difficulties. "Resolving difficulties" means that regarding the Dharma, which is hard to understand, various difficulties arise. "Dispelling doubts" means that regarding the Buddha, in whom one does not yet have faith, various doubts arise.

How do these doubts arise? The previous two chapters stated that the Buddha's name and title pervade all worlds throughout the ten directions. Thereupon doubt arises: "Is it because the Buddha's body pervades that his fame follows in pervasion? Or is his body confined to this world, with only his fame pervading? If only his fame pervades but his body does not, how can the retribution of bodily karma be narrow while the fruit of verbal karma is broad, when originally both karmas were cultivated in immeasurable measure? If, like his fame, his body also pervades, why do we only see the Buddha in this assembly's world?"

To dispel this doubt, this chapter has come. Such is the general meaning.

Next, we explain the text. The text has two parts. First, it clarifies the place from which the light issues. Second, it reveals the place that the light illuminates.

In the first part, it says: "From the wheel-marks on both feet, he emitted a hundred billion rays of light." The wheel-marks are the thousand-spoked wheel marks. They symbolize the faith-practice's initial arousal. When one first gives rise to the ten minds, they increase to hundreds and thousands. Śamatha and vipaśyanā are jointly exercised, and one enters the rank of Worthy-Chief. Therefore the light issues from the wheel-marks on both feet.

From "universally illuminating" onward is the place illuminated by the light. Proceeding from near to far, through ten levels of progressive increase, this symbolizes that for one whose practices are just beginning, progressive cultivation and advancement necessarily complete the full rank. Here it directly reveals that the Buddha's body pervades everywhere, and therefore the fruition-retribution of both bodily and verbal karma is equally boundless, equally pervading the dharmadhātu.

Within the first level, there are two divisions. First, a narrative of the place illuminated by the light and the events seen. Second, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva speaks verses praising the Buddha. Within the first, there are also two: first, the light illuminates a hundred billion worlds; second, the Buddha and the great assembly are universally seen. The remaining nine levels follow the same textual structure.

In the praise of the Buddha, there are also two parts: first, one location is explained; then the rest are given by analogy. Within the verses there are also two. The first two verses, based on the name of the eastern buddha, praise the Buddha's characteristic of "non-movement." The latter eight verses, drawing on the name of Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, reveal the wisdom of non-abiding and non-attainment.

Within the first, there are also two. The first verse reveals by negation; the second verse clarifies by affirmation.

The meaning of revelation by negation: if one knows the Tathāgata as freed from bondage and departed from fullness, then one says the Tathāgata has movement and departure. Since there is something attained, this is not the pure eye.

The meaning of clarification by affirmation: if one knows the Tathāgata contemplates all dharmas as entirely without existence, then one accords with the Tathāgata's wisdom of non-movement. Since there is nothing in which one abides, one swiftly becomes a buddha.

The latter eight verses contain four pairs.

In the first pair: the first verse penetrates the conventional as unreal; the next verse enters the ultimate as non-dual.

In the second pair: first, regarding persons there is no abiding; second, regarding dharmas there is lasting attainment.

In the third pair: first, regarding dharmas one transcends existence and non-existence; second, regarding the Buddha there is neither decrease nor increase.

In the fourth pair: first, through the gate of non-attainment regarding persons and dharmas, one transforms sentient beings; second, through the gate of unobstructed mutual interpenetration of one and many, one obtains fearlessness. Because all dharmas enter into one dharma, within the one, the immeasurable is understood. Because one dharma enters into all dharmas, within the immeasurable, the one is understood. The reason they can mutually interpenetrate is that they alternately serve as mirror-reflections for each other, arising not as real but as reflections, and therefore without obstruction. This is called the Dharma-gate of Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva.

In the second set of verses, there are also two parts. The first two verses, drawing on the name of the southern buddha, praise the Dharma-gate of the fire of wisdom. The latter eight verses, based on the name of that bodhisattva, reveal the Dharma-gate of Awakening-Chief.

Within the first, there are two. The first verse sees all sentient beings in the darkness of delusion and causes them to seek the supreme light of wisdom. The second verse inwardly cherishes the irreversible fire of wisdom and outwardly turns the supreme wheel of light.

The latter eight verses form four pairs. In the first pair: the first has great vows as chief, awakening beings from the sleep of birth and death; the second has compassion as its heart, subduing the hostile army of māras. In the second pair: inwardly attaining deep wisdom-awakening, able to destroy all afflictions; outwardly striking the drum of the correct Dharma, alerting the lands of the ten directions. In the third pair: below, in all forms of existence, one does not grasp or attach; above, in all the buddhas, one gives rise to joyous mindfulness. In the fourth pair: to save sentient beings, one accepts suffering through vast kalpas; to protect the buddha-dharma, one does not spare one's body or life. This is the Dharma-gate of Awakening-Chief Bodhisattva's self-enlightenment and awakening of others.

In the third set of verses, there are also two parts. The first verse, drawing on the name of the western buddha, praises the practice of pure-wisdom Dharma. The following nine verses, based on the name of Wealth-Chief Bodhisattva, illuminate the manifestation of various merits and the Dharma-gate of enriching and benefiting all sentient beings.

Within the first, there are two: the upper half is awakening to purity; the lower half is mind-purity. This is called the practice of pure-wisdom Dharma.

In the fourth set of verses, there are also two parts. The first nine verses, drawing on the name of the northern buddha, praise the Dharma-gate of complete majestic power and wisdom. The final verse, based on the name of Jewel-Chief Bodhisattva, reveals the Dharma-gate of refined-gold purity.

Within the first, there are also two. The first verse gives a general indication of the completeness of inner and outer majestic power. The upper half is inner realization of majestic power; the lower two lines are outer manifestation of majestic power. The next eight verses separately display four pairs of complete majestic power.

First pair: self-awakened, free from dust: virtue. Transforming others, universally arousing: majestic power. Second pair: without aggregates, free from suffering: majestic power of inner liberation; outer liberation: virtue. Third pair: still yet constantly active: majestic power. Active yet constantly still: virtue. Fourth pair: knowing the extremely profound: virtue. Universally seeing and broadly delivering: majestic power.

In the fifth set of verses, there are also two parts. The first five verses, based on the fifth bodhisattva, Virtue-Chief, illuminate his merits of self-benefit and benefiting others. The following five verses, drawing on the name of the fifth buddha, Bright-Wisdom, praise his illumination of inconceivable realms.

Within the first, there are also two. The first verse: below, transforming the multitudes of beings with the virtue of compassion. The following four verses: above, seeking the buddha-way with the virtue of progressive cultivation.

The latter five verses are also divided into two. The first two verses illuminate the contemplation of the true-characteristic realm of body and mind. The following three verses penetrate the inconceivable realm of immeasurable quantity.

In the sixth set of verses, there are two parts: six verses of seven characters and five verses of five characters.

The seven-character verses have two parts. The first two verses, based on the sixth bodhisattva, Eye-Chief, reveal by negation the wondrous forms seen by the pure eye. The following four verses, drawing on the southeastern buddha's name, praise the realm of profound and wondrous ultimate wisdom.

The five-character verses also have two parts. The first two verses, based on Eye-Chief's name, praise the Buddha's Dharma-gate of pure-eye sovereignty. The following three verses, drawing on that buddha's name, praise the profoundly deep realm of ultimate wisdom.

In the seventh set of verses, there are also two parts. The first two verses, drawing on the southwestern buddha's name, praise the Buddha's supreme wisdom-awakening. The following eight verses, based on the bodhisattva Vigor-Chief's name, reveal his progressive entry into the realm of the Buddha's wisdom.

Within the first, there are also two: the first praises the supreme wisdom of self-enlightenment and awakening others; the second praises the supreme virtue of being without defilement and without destruction.

The latter eight verses form four undisclosed pairs. First pair: entering the Buddha's wisdom and entering the Dharma-ocean. Second pair: correctly turning the Dharma-wheel and correctly contemplating the Buddha's Way. Third pair: hearing the Buddha's voice and seeing the Buddha's body. Fourth pair: grasping existence without grasping, and opening. Fifth pair: below, the analogy above, the Dharma. Sixth pair: outside, the metaphor within, the Dharma. These last two pairs use the gate of inferential reasoning to establish the meaning of the Buddha's body pervading everywhere.

In the eighth set of verses, there are twenty verses, divided into two parts. The first ten verses, drawing on the northwestern buddha's name, praise the skillful means of sovereign wisdom. The latter ten verses, based on the bodhisattva Dharma-Chief's name, praise the wisdom-power of accordance with the meaning of the Dharma.

Within the first, there are five pairs. First pair: great wisdom's sovereignty and great virtue's accomplishment. Second pair: the awakened nature's broad contemplation and the transcendence of characteristics' deep delight. Third pair: regarding existence, without obstruction; regarding emptiness, without attachment. Fourth pair: truly understanding differentiation and delighting in contemplating equality. Fifth pair: encompassing all wisdom and accomplishing the supreme intention. These five pairs of ten gates of skillful means are all differentiations of sovereign wisdom.

Next, the praise of Dharma-wisdom also has five pairs. First pair: deep Dharma followed and known, reaching everywhere. Second pair: according with one mind, able to reach the profound realm. Third pair: remembering the time and season, understanding success and failure. Fourth pair: what is known is differentiated, and the ability to understand is skillful means. Fifth pair: contemplating the three times and awakening to equality. All five pairs belong to the Dharma-gate of Dharma-Chief.

In the ninth set of verses, there are also two parts. The first ten verses, drawing on the lower-direction buddha's name, praise the Dharma-gate of Brahmā-wisdom. The latter ten verses, based on Wisdom-Chief Bodhisattva's name, praise his Dharma-gate of the wisdom-lamp that dispels darkness.

Within the first, there are two: two verses give a brief indication, and eight verses provide a detailed explanation. Within the brief indication, there are also two: the first clarifies the Brahmā-practice; the second reveals the Brahmā-voice.

"Brahmā-practice" means that when Brahmā manifests transformations among humans, he takes on the form of difficult practices, appearing like a skeleton. One should know that difficult practice is precisely Brahmā-practice.

The eight verses of detailed explanation reveal four gates. First, two verses on the Dharma-gate of transcending arrogance: eliminating the suffering and difficulty of birth and death. Second, two verses on the Dharma-gate of non-negligence: eliminating worldly negligence. Third, two verses on the Dharma-gate of proclaiming the non-self teaching: extinguishing sentient beings' attachment to self. Fourth, two verses on the Dharma-gate of great vows' universal nourishment: extinguishing sentient beings' blazing fire. These are all gates of the Brahmā-practice.

The latter ten verses also have two parts. The first two verses briefly indicate the wisdom-gate. The first verse raises the darkness that is eliminated; the following seven verses reveal the lamp that eliminates it.

The eight verses of explanation also have four gates. First, two verses on the Dharma-gate of the ship ferrying those adrift. Second, two verses on the Dharma-gate of the bridge rescuing those who are sinking. Third, two verses on the Dharma-gate of skillful means extracting suffering. Fourth, two verses on the Dharma-gate of true wisdom bestowing happiness.

In the tenth set of verses, there are also two parts. The first four verses, drawing on the upper-direction buddha's name, praise the Dharma-gate of the wisdom that subdues enemies. The latter sixteen verses, based on the bodhisattva Worthy-Chief's name, broadly explain the Dharma-gate of Worthy-Chief's excellent abilities.

Within the first, there are two. The first two verses directly reveal the ten powers of excellent wisdom that subdues enemies. The next two verses indicate the excellent virtue of the fame and renown of subduing enemies.

From "universally for sentient beings" onward is the second, the Dharma-gate of Worthy-Chief. Within this, there are two: the first four verses are the excellent ability of transforming sentient beings below; the latter twelve verses are the excellent ability of seeking the Buddha-Way above.

From "able to ask the buddhas of the ten directions" onward is the second, seeking the Buddha-Way above. The twelve verses form six pairs. First pair: asking the Buddha and seeing the Buddha. Second pair: able to teach and able to transform. Third pair: hearing the Buddha's voice and seeing the Buddha's body. Fourth pair: grasping existence without grasping, and opening. Fifth pair: below, the analogy above, the Dharma. Sixth pair: outside, the metaphor within, the Dharma. These last two pairs use the gate of inferential reasoning to establish the meaning of the Buddha's body pervading everywhere.

The above ten levels of light universally illuminating reveal that the Buddha's form-body is everywhere without omission. The ten levels of verse-praise of the Buddha's merit reveal that the Buddha's inner virtue is also all-pervading without omission. Within this, the bodhisattvas' virtues are also revealed, showing that all the bodhisattvas, following the Buddha, are able to accord with the Buddha. The doubts that had arisen about the Buddha have now been entirely dispelled. This is complete.
