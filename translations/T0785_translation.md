# 得道梯橙錫杖經
## Sūtra of the Monk's Staff as Steps to Attaining the Path

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0785

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

At that time the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus: "All of you should receive and uphold the monk's staff. Why? The Buddhas of the past held the monk's staff, the Buddhas of the future will hold the monk's staff, and the Buddhas of the present also hold this staff. Just as I today, having become the Buddha, the World-Honored One, also hold this staff that should be held. The Buddhas of the past, future, and present have taught their disciples to hold the monk's staff as well. Therefore I today, having become the Buddha, the World-Honored One, following the Dharma of all Buddhas, teach you likewise. All of you should now receive and uphold the monk's staff. Why? This monk's staff is called the Staff of Wisdom, and is also called the Staff of Virtue. It reveals and displays the wisdom of the sages, and so is called the Staff of Wisdom. It serves as the foundation for the practice of merit and virtue, and so is called the Staff of Virtue. Such a staff is the emblem of the sages, the clear mark of the worthy, the true banner of the Dharma-path, and the standard that establishes mindfulness and meaning. Therefore all of you should uphold it according to the Dharma."

At that time the Elder Kāśyapa rose from his seat, arranged his robes, bared his right shoulder, joined his palms, knelt, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why is it called the monk's staff? How should it be received and upheld? We pray the World-Honored One will expound and explain it, and we shall practice accordingly."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Listen carefully and reflect well! I shall explain it for you.

"The word *xī* in 'monk's staff' means 'light.' By relying on this staff, one removes afflictions and escapes the three realms; therefore it is called 'light.' *Xī* means 'bright.' The person who holds this staff attains the brightness of wisdom; therefore it is called 'bright.' *Xī* means 'not turning back.' The person who holds this staff can escape the three existences and is never again defiled; therefore it is called 'not turning back.' *Xī* means 'awake.' The person who holds this staff awakens to suffering, emptiness, and the fetters of the three realms, clearly comprehending the four truths and the twelve links of dependent arising; therefore it is called 'awake.' *Xī* means 'not arrogant.' The person who holds this staff cuts off the karma of arrogance; therefore it is called 'not arrogant.' *Xī* means 'separating.' The person who holds this staff separates from the five desires, severs the fetter of craving and attachment, disperses and destroys the aggregates, distances himself from the five hindrances, aspires toward nirvāṇa, and relinquishes conditioned karma; therefore it is called 'separating.' *Xī* means 'gathering.' The person who holds this staff gathers the treasures of morality, concentration, and wisdom of all the Buddhas, and obtains liberation; therefore it is called 'gathering.' *Xī* means 'accomplishing.' The person who holds this staff accomplishes the Dharma-treasury of all the Buddhas, practicing as taught without any deficiency, completing everything fully; therefore it is called 'accomplishing.'"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "The meanings of the word *xī* are vast and numerous and cannot be fully stated. For now you should receive and uphold it thus."

Kāśyapa addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Since the monk's staff has such meanings, how is it the Staff of Wisdom, and how does it establish the standard of mindfulness and meaning? We pray the World-Honored One will explain."

The Buddha said: "This monk's staff is for those who cultivate wisdom, who broadly practice extensive learning, who understand worldly and world-transcending matters, who distinguish good and evil, the conditioned and unconditioned, the tainted and untainted, whose understanding is unobstructed and whose wisdom is accomplished; therefore it is called the Staff of Wisdom.

"It is for those who uphold the precepts, who practice patience and dhyāna concentration, who are single-minded and undistracted, who constantly cultivate merit without a moment's laziness, as if extinguishing a fire on their head; therefore it is called the Staff of Virtue.

"The person who takes up and holds this staff internally possesses the sixteen practices (namely, the four truths: suffering, the origin, cessation, and the path; the four immeasurables: loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity; the four dhyānas: the first, second, third, and fourth dhyāna; and the four formless attainments: the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, and the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception), and is also complete in the thirty-seven practices (namely, the thirty-seven factors of the path to awakening). Within these dharmas one clearly distinguishes and realizes directly in one's own person, not following mere sounds. Within these dharmas one freely roams, entering the liberation-gates of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, at ease and without difficulty: such a one is called a sage. Having such virtue within, one holds the monk's staff outwardly, indicating that this person surely possesses the virtue of a sage: morality, concentration, patience, and wisdom; the three knowledges and six supernatural powers; and the eight liberations, all fully complete. Marking this person, others look up with reverence; therefore it is called 'the emblem of the sages.'

"As for 'the clear mark of the worthy': having the nature of wisdom within, one is called a worthy person. This mark clearly indicates that the person has the nature of wisdom within, cultivates the foundation of merit and virtue, advances in the Dharma, and accomplishes a wholesome mind; therefore it is called 'the clear mark of the worthy.'

"This person will before long accomplish wisdom, enter the unconditioned, abide in peaceful tranquility, attain the peace and joy of nirvāṇa and the path of the ultimate truth; therefore it is called 'the true banner of the Dharma-path.'

"As for 'the standard that establishes mindfulness and meaning': this staff has three compartments. Seeing the three compartments stacked, one is mindful of the suffering and affliction of the three evil paths, and cultivates morality, concentration, and wisdom. One is mindful of the three calamities of old age, sickness, and death, and eliminates the three poisons of greed, anger, and delusion. One is mindful of the impermanence of the three realms, places faith in and reveres the Three Jewels, eliminates the three evils, severs the three outflows, purifies the three karmas, and aspires to accomplish the three knowledges, enter the three liberations, attain the three mindfulnesses, and penetrate the three wisdoms. Therefore three compartments are established, stacked upon one another.

"Furthermore, the four prongs are for cutting off the four kinds of birth, being mindful of the four truths, cultivating the four immeasurables, entering the four dhyānas, purifying the four formless attainments, understanding the four foundations of mindfulness, firmly establishing the four right efforts, and attaining the four bases of supernatural power. Therefore four edges are established.

"The five compartments in the middle shaft are for cutting off the suffering and affliction of cyclic existence through the five destinies, cultivating the five faculties, fulfilling the five powers, removing the five hindrances, dispersing the five aggregates, and attaining the fivefold body of the Dharma. Therefore the number five is established.

"The twelve rings are for being mindful of the twelve links of dependent arising, penetrating them without obstruction, and practicing the twelve-gate meditation, causing the mind to be free from affliction.

"The three compartments and four edges together make seven, for being mindful of the Tathāgata's Dharma of the seven factors of awakening and accomplishing the seven noble treasures.

"The eight divisions of the entire staff are for being mindful of the eightfold noble path, attaining the eight liberations, and eliminating the eight difficulties. Therefore the number eight is used. In brief, the meanings of the monk's staff are thus! You should uphold it well."

Kāśyapa addressed the Buddha: "So it is, World-Honored One! As the holy Dharma teaches."

At that time Kāśyapa again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Is this Dharma the same for the Buddhas of the three times?"

The Buddha said: "Having a staff is the same, but the form differs. Some have four prongs, some have two prongs, while the number of rings is the same. I today use four prongs and twelve rings in my teaching. Two prongs were established by Kāśyapa Tathāgata, causing sentient beings to be mindful of the two truths, conventional truth and ultimate truth, and thereby establishing their meaning."

At that time, after the World-Honored One had spoken this Dharma, the Elder Kāśyapa, the assembly of twelve hundred, and all the great assembly rejoiced greatly, received it reverently, and practiced accordingly.

---

As a general principle: the upper platform represents heaven, the lower platform represents earth, the four limbs represent the four heavenly kings, and the twelve rings represent the twelve links of dependent arising. It encompasses heaven and earth, devas and humans, above and below; nothing is not fully contained therein. Whenever one gives rise to loving-kindness, the myriad practices are broadly generated, and nothing fails to take this as its standard; good roots have already been planted.

The method of holding this staff is as follows. One holds it between heaven and earth, placing it beneath the left armpit and hooking it with the little finger, keeping both ends level and not tilted. When sounding it, one sounds it continuously, not allowing the sound to cease. The sound should be even, fine, and harmonious, always as if at the start. If at the start there is no sound, then throughout the entire round one should make no sound. If at the start there is sound, then throughout the entire round one should always make it sound. The sound should also be even, not sometimes coarse and sometimes fine. A monk places it by the left foot; a nun places it by the right foot. It must not touch the ground. If a patron does not come out, one goes to at least three houses and at most seven houses. If one does not receive food, one must not go to more; to exceed this is not the practice of a mendicant. If within this limit one receives food, one hangs the staff on a tree and must not let it touch the ground. If there is no tree, one may place it on the ground on a flat surface, not allowing it to tilt. When sleeping, one places the staff aligned with the body, behind the bed, exactly even with the body, not forward or back. When traveling or resting, the head of the staff should always face the sun and must not be reversed or turned away. Holding this staff is holding the body of the Buddha; the myriad practices are all contained within it, for it holds heaven and encompasses earth.

And one contemplates the twelve links of dependent arising for the protection of the entire body. If the staff tilts, all the myriad things also tilt; if it is level and upright, all sentient beings are caused to dwell in peace and the unconditioned. If the lower platform touches the ground, the beings of the three evil paths suffer ever more acutely; if it does not touch the ground, the beings of the three evil paths are thereby delivered. If it is held upside down, it goes against the world and also causes the practitioner's mind to be confused and disordered; if one can hold it in the correct direction, both self and others benefit. If one holds it thus, one immediately attains the dignified deportment of entering and leaving, guarded and aided, and afterward attains the fruit, quickly accomplishing perfect awakening.

The rules of deportment for holding the monk's staff comprise twenty-five matters.

The ten rules for holding the monk's staff: First, because there are insects on the ground. Second, because of old age and infirmity. Third, because of the alms-round. Fourth, one must not hold the staff and swing it forward and back with the hand. Fifth, one must not carry the staff on the shoulder. Sixth, one must not lay it horizontally across the shoulders with the hands hanging from both ends. Seventh, when entering or leaving and seeing a Buddha image, one must not allow it to make a sound. Eighth, the staff must not be brought into the assembly. Ninth, one must not carelessly bring it to the back of a house. Tenth, the staff must not go out after midday.

There are five further rules: First, when invited on a journey with overnight stay, one may go out after midday. Second, when visiting the home of the sick, one may go out after midday. Third, when sending off one who has passed away, one may go out after midday. Fourth, when invited by non-Buddhist practitioners, one may go out after midday. Fifth, one must not point the staff at people or use it to draw characters on the ground.

There are five further rules: First, when all three teachers go out together, one must not carry the staff and follow on one's own. Second, when four people travel together, apart from the senior monk, the others must not all carry staffs. Third, when arriving at a patron's gate, one should properly maintain dignified deportment. Fourth, upon entering a patron's gate, one shakes the staff three times; if after three repetitions the patron does not come out, one proceeds to another house. Fifth, when the patron comes out, one should hold the staff in the center of the left elbow.

There are five further rules: First, the staff should always be in one's own room, not apart from the body. Second, one must not let the head touch the ground. Third, one must not allow mold to grow on the staff. Fourth, one must polish and wipe it daily. Fifth, when one is about to go out with the staff, one should receive it from a novice; if there is no novice, a layperson may also hand it over.

> The four prongs of the monk's staff correspond to the four truths,
> The rings correspond to the twelve links of dependent arising,
> The center proclaims the meaning of the Middle Way.
> The upper head corresponds to the summit of Sumeru,
> The second level corresponds to Mount Sumeru,
> The central wood corresponds to empty space,
> The lower ferrule corresponds to the base of Sumeru.

The Dharma of the śramaṇa is to comprehend emptiness and attain the path, and holding this staff to awaken all sentient beings in the world. All you bhikṣus! Practice this with your whole heart.

## Alternate Rules for Holding the Monk's Staff

The monk's staff has twenty-five rules of deportment. The twenty-five rules for holding the monk's staff are: First, because of snakes and insects. Second, because of old age. Third, because of the alms-round. Fourth, when entering or leaving and seeing a Buddha image, one must not allow the head to make a sound. Fifth, one must not bring the staff into the assembly. Sixth, after midday one must not go out again with the staff. Seventh, one must not carry it on the shoulder with the hands hanging from both ends. Eighth, one must not lay it horizontally across the knees with both ends hanging. Ninth, one must not swing it forward and back with the hand. Tenth, one must not bring it to the back of a house. Eleventh, one must not carry the staff and follow behind the three teachers when they have already gone out with a staff. Twelfth, when four people travel together, if one person already carries a staff, the others must not carry one as well. Thirteenth, when arriving at a patron's house, one must not set down the staff apart from the body. Fourteenth, upon arriving at someone's gate, one shakes it three times; if the patron does not come out, one should proceed to another house. Fifteenth, when the patron comes out, one should hold the staff in the left elbow and carry it. Sixteenth, when in a room, one must not let it touch the ground. Seventeenth, one should keep it near one's own bed. Eighteenth, one should regularly take it and wipe it. Nineteenth, one must not allow mold to grow on the head. Twentieth, when about to travel, one should receive it from a novice or layperson. Twenty-first, when visiting the home of the sick and staying overnight, one may go out with the staff in the evening. Twenty-second, when seeing off one who has passed away at a distance, one may go out with the staff in the evening. Twenty-third, when invited on a distant journey with an overnight stay, one may go out with the staff in the evening. Twenty-fourth, when going far to welcome someone, one may go out with the staff in the evening. Twenty-fifth, one should always keep it near one's person, and must not point it at people or use it to draw characters on the ground.
