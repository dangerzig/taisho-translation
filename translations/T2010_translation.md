# Inscription on Faith in Mind

## *Xìnxīn Míng* (信心銘)

Taishō 2010

By Sēngcàn (*Sēngcàn*, 僧璨), Third Patriarch of Chan, Sui dynasty.

> The supreme way is not difficult;
> It only abhors discrimination.
> Just do not hate or love,
> And it will be thoroughly clear.
>
> A hairsbreadth of difference,
> And heaven and earth are set apart.
> If you wish it to appear before you,
> Do not hold to for or against.
>
> The conflict of for and against:
> This is the disease of the mind.
> Without recognizing the dark principle,
> It is useless to practice stillness.
>
> It is complete like great space,
> With nothing lacking, nothing in excess.
> Truly, because of grasping and rejecting,
> You cannot see it as it is.
>
> Do not pursue conditioned existence,
> Do not abide in forbearing emptiness.
> In oneness and equality of mind,
> All self-vanishes of itself.
>
> When you try to stop activity to achieve stillness,
> The very effort fills you with activity.
> As long as you remain in one extreme or the other,
> You will never know oneness.
>
> Those who do not penetrate oneness
> Fail on both paths.
> Banish existence and you drown in existence;
> Follow emptiness and you turn your back on it.
>
> The more you talk and think,
> The further you are from the way.
> Cut off talk, cut off thinking,
> And there is nowhere you cannot go.
>
> Return to the root and gain the meaning;
> Chase after appearances and lose the source.
> A moment's turning of the light within
> Surpasses the emptiness that came before.
>
> That prior emptiness was transformed:
> It was entirely due to deluded views.
> No need to seek the true;
> Just put an end to views.
>
> Do not abide in dualistic views;
> Take care not to pursue them.
> The moment there is right and wrong,
> The mind is lost in confusion.
>
> The two arise from the one,
> But do not cling to the one either.
> When the one mind does not arise,
> The myriad dharmas are without fault.
>
> Without fault, without dharmas,
> No arising, no mind.
> The subject is extinguished along with its object;
> The object sinks away along with its subject.
>
> The object exists because of the subject;
> The subject exists because of the object.
> If you wish to know both,
> They are originally one emptiness.
>
> In one emptiness both are the same,
> Equally containing all forms.
> Not seeing fine or coarse:
> How could there be any bias?
>
> The great way is broad,
> Neither easy nor difficult.
> But those with narrow views are full of doubt:
> The more they hurry, the slower they go.
>
> Clinging to it, you lose measure;
> The mind enters a crooked path.
> Let it go naturally,
> And the essence neither goes nor stays.
>
> Accord with your nature and merge with the way;
> Wander at ease, free from vexation.
> Bound thoughts diverge from the real;
> Sinking into darkness is unwholesome.
>
> What is unwholesome wearies the spirit.
> What use is there in nearness or distance?
> If you wish to travel the one vehicle,
> Do not dislike the six sense-objects.
>
> The six sense-objects are not evil;
> They are the same as true enlightenment.
> The wise person does nothing;
> The fool binds himself.
>
> Dharmas are not different from dharmas;
> The deluded mind clings to what it loves.
> Using mind to grasp at mind:
> Is this not a great mistake?
>
> Delusion gives rise to stillness and turmoil;
> Awakening has no good or bad.
> All dualities
> Come from one's own discriminating mind.
>
> Dreams, illusions, empty flowers:
> Why trouble yourself grasping at them?
> Gain and loss, right and wrong:
> Let them all go at once.
>
> If the eyes never sleep,
> All dreams cease of themselves.
> If the mind makes no distinctions,
> The myriad dharmas are of one suchness.
>
> The essence of one suchness is profound;
> Unmoving, one forgets all entanglements.
> Contemplating all dharmas equally,
> One returns to the natural.
>
> Their reason exhausted, they cannot be compared.
> Stop activity, and there is no activity;
> Activity stopped, there is no stopping.
> When both are no longer so,
> How can the one remain?
>
> At the ultimate point, at the final limit,
> No rules or standards exist.
> Make the mind equal and at rest,
> And all striving ceases.
>
> Doubts are cleansed away entirely;
> Right faith is adjusted and straightforward.
> Nothing whatsoever remains;
> Nothing to remember.
>
> Empty, bright, self-illuminating,
> No effort of the mind is needed.
> This is not a place of thought;
> Consciousness and feeling cannot fathom it.
>
> In the true suchness of the dharma-realm,
> There is no other, no self.
> If you wish to accord with it quickly,
> Just say "not two."
>
> In not-two, all is the same;
> Nothing is not included.
> The wise ones of the ten directions
> All enter this principle.
>
> This principle is neither hurried nor drawn out.
> One thought is ten thousand years;
> There is nowhere it is not present.
>
> The infinitely small is the same as the large,
> When the boundaries of delusion vanish.
> The infinitely large is the same as the small,
> When the limits of the view are unseen.
>
> Being is none other than non-being;
> Non-being is none other than being.
> If it is not like this,
> You need not hold to it.
>
> One is all;
> All is one.
> If you can just be like this,
> Why worry about not being complete?
>
> Faith and mind are not two;
> Not-two is faith in mind.
> The path of words is cut off;
> It is not of the past, the future, or the present.
