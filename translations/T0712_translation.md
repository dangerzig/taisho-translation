# 佛說大乘稻芉經
## The Mahāyāna Sūtra of the Rice Seedling

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0712

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling on Vulture Peak Mountain in the city of Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty monks and bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

At that time, the Elder Śāriputra went to the place where the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya was walking in meditation. Having arrived, they exchanged greetings and sat together on a flat rock. Then the Elder Śāriputra addressed the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya, saying: "Maitreya! Today the World-Honored One, observing the rice seedlings, told the monks this: 'You monks! Whoever sees causes and conditions sees the Dharma. Whoever sees the Dharma can see the Buddha.' Having said this, he fell silent. Maitreya! Why did the Sugata speak thus? What does it mean? What are causes and conditions? What is the Dharma? What is the Buddha? How does seeing causes and conditions enable one to see the Dharma? How does seeing the Dharma enable one to see the Buddha?"

After these words were spoken, the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya answered the Elder Śāriputra, saying: "The Buddha, the Dharma King, the Perfectly Enlightened One, told the monks: 'Whoever sees causes and conditions can see the Dharma; whoever sees the Dharma can see the Buddha.' What are causes and conditions? Causes and conditions means: because this exists, that exists; because this arises, that arises. That is to say, conditioned by ignorance there are volitional formations; conditioned by volitional formations there is consciousness; conditioned by consciousness there is name-and-form; conditioned by name-and-form there are the six sense-bases; conditioned by the six sense-bases there is contact; conditioned by contact there is feeling; conditioned by feeling there is craving; conditioned by craving there is grasping; conditioned by grasping there is becoming; conditioned by becoming there is birth; conditioned by birth there are old age and death, grief, lamentation, suffering, sorrow, and distress. Thus arises this entire mass of pure and extreme great suffering. Within this, when ignorance ceases, volitional formations cease; when volitional formations cease, consciousness ceases; when consciousness ceases, name-and-form ceases; when name-and-form ceases, the six sense-bases cease; when the six sense-bases cease, contact ceases; when contact ceases, feeling ceases; when feeling ceases, craving ceases; when craving ceases, grasping ceases; when grasping ceases, becoming ceases; when becoming ceases, birth ceases; when birth ceases, old age and death, grief, lamentation, suffering, sorrow, and distress cease. Thus ceases this entire mass of pure and extreme great suffering. This is the teaching of causes and conditions spoken by the World-Honored One.

"What is the Dharma? It is the Noble Eightfold Path: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration. This Noble Eightfold Path, together with its fruit and nirvana, is what the World-Honored One has taught and named the Dharma.

"What is the Buddha? One who knows all dharmas is called the Buddha, because through the eye of wisdom and the Dharma-body, one sees and practices the dharmas of bodhi, both those of training and beyond training.

"How does seeing causes and conditions lead to seeing the Dharma? As the Buddha taught, if one can see the dharma of causes and conditions as permanent, without a life-span, free from life-span, of the nature of reality, free from confusion, unborn, unarisen, uncreated, unconditioned, unobstructed, without an object, quiescent, without fear, not subject to seizure, and not of a quiescent characteristic, then one can likewise see the Dharma as permanent, without a life-span, free from life-span, of the nature of reality, free from confusion, unborn, unarisen, uncreated, unconditioned, unobstructed, without an object, quiescent, without fear, not subject to seizure, and not of a quiescent characteristic. Through attaining right knowledge, one can realize the supreme Dharma and, through the unsurpassed Dharma-body, see the Buddha."

The question was asked: "Why is it called 'causes and conditions'?"

The answer: "Having causes and having conditions is called causes and conditions, not being without causes and conditions. Therefore it is called the dharma of causes and conditions. The World-Honored One taught the characteristics of causes and conditions in brief: given such a cause, such a fruit is produced. Whether tathāgatas appear in the world or do not appear, the nature of dharmas, the abiding nature of dharmas, the fixed nature of dharmas, the nature of accordance with causes and conditions, the nature of suchness, the nature free from confusion, the nature free from alteration, the nature of reality, the nature of the actual limit, the nature free from falsity, the nature free from inversion: these are all taught in this way. This dharma of causes and conditions arises from two kinds of conditions. What are the two? Accordance with inherent causes and accordance with conditions. These are further twofold: external and internal.

"What is the external dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with inherent causes? From a seed a sprout is born; from a sprout a leaf is born; from a leaf a stem is born; from a stem a node is born; from a node an ear of grain is born; from an ear a flower is born; from a flower a fruit is born. If there is no seed, a sprout cannot be born, and so on up to: if there is no flower, a fruit cannot be born. When there is a seed, a sprout is born; just so, when there is a flower, fruit is born. The seed does not think: 'I can produce a sprout.' The sprout does not think: 'I was born from a seed.' And so on up to: the flower does not think: 'I can produce fruit.' The fruit does not think: 'I was born from a flower.' Yet, because there is a seed, a sprout is born; just so, because there is a flower, fruit can come to be. One should thus observe the meaning of the external dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with inherent causes.

"How should one observe the meaning of the external dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with conditions? It is because of the combination of six elements. What six elements combine? Earth, water, fire, wind, space, and time combine, and the external dharma of causes and conditions arises. One should thus observe the meaning of the external dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with conditions. What is the earth element? That which supports the seed. What is the water element? That which moistens the seed. What is the fire element? That which warms the seed. What is the wind element? That which stirs the seed. What is the space element? That which does not obstruct the seed. What is the time element? That which causes the seed to change. If these conditions are absent, a seed cannot produce a sprout. If the external earth element is not lacking, and likewise water, fire, wind, space, and time are not lacking, then when all combine, at the time the seed dissolves, the sprout is born. Here, the earth element does not think: 'I can support the seed.' The water element does not think: 'I can moisten the seed.' The fire element does not think: 'I can warm the seed.' The wind element does not think: 'I can stir the seed.' The space element does not think: 'I can keep the seed unobstructed.' The time element does not think: 'I can cause the seed to change.' The seed also does not think: 'I can produce a sprout.' The sprout also does not think: 'I was born from these conditions.' Yet, with these conditions, when the seed dissolves, the sprout is born. And when the flower is present, the fruit comes to be. That sprout is not self-made, not made by another, not made by both together, not made by Maheśvara, not made by temporal change, not born from inherent nature, and not produced without cause. Yet, through the combination of earth, water, fire, wind, space, and time elements, when the seed dissolves, the sprout is born. Therefore one should thus observe the meaning of the external dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with conditions.

"One should observe the external dharma of causes and conditions through five characteristics. What are the five? Not permanent, not annihilated, not transferred, from a small cause producing a great fruit, and resembling its kind. How is it not permanent? Because the sprout and the seed are each distinct. The sprout is not the seed. It is not the case that when the seed is destroyed the sprout comes to be; nor is it that it arises without destruction. When the seed is destroyed, the sprout is born; therefore it is not permanent. How is it not annihilated? It is not the case that the former seed first perishes and then the sprout arises; nor is it that the sprout arises without the seed's dissolution. The seed also dissolves, and at that very moment, like a scale dipping and rising, the sprout comes to be; therefore it is not annihilated. How is it not transferred? Because the sprout is different from the seed; the sprout is not the seed; therefore it is not transferred. How does a small cause produce a great fruit? From a small seed, a great fruit is produced; therefore a small cause produces a great fruit. How does it resemble its kind? Whatever type of seed is planted, that same type of fruit is produced; therefore it resembles its kind. Thus should one observe the external dharma of causes and conditions through five characteristics.

"Likewise, the internal dharma of causes and conditions also arises from two kinds of conditions. What are the two? Accordance with inherent causes and accordance with conditions. What is the meaning of the internal dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with inherent causes? Beginning with ignorance conditioning volitional formations, and so on up to birth conditioning old age and death. If ignorance does not arise, volitional formations also do not exist; and so on up to: if there is no birth, old age and death do not exist. Thus, because there is ignorance, volitional formations arise; and so on up to: because there is birth, old age and death come to be. Ignorance does not think: 'I can produce volitional formations.' Volitional formations do not think: 'I was born from ignorance.' And so on up to: birth does not think: 'I can produce old age and death.' Old age and death do not think: 'I exist because of birth.' Yet, because there is ignorance, volitional formations arise; and because there is birth, old age and death come to be. One should thus observe the meaning of the internal dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with inherent causes.

"How should one observe the matter of the internal dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with conditions? It is because of the combination of six elements. What six elements combine? Earth, water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness combine. One should thus observe the matter of the internal dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with conditions. What is the characteristic of the internal earth element? That which constitutes the solid and firm parts of the body is called the earth element. That which enables the body to cohere is called the water element. That which digests food and drink within the body is called the fire element. That which produces inhalation and exhalation within the body is called the wind element. That which produces the hollow spaces within the body is called the space element. The five sense-consciousnesses together with the defiled mental consciousness, like a bundle of reeds, which can bring about the sprout of name-and-form in the body: this is called the consciousness element. If these conditions are absent, the body cannot be formed. If the internal earth element is not lacking, and likewise water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness are not lacking, then when all combine, the body is produced. That earth element does not think: 'I can make the body firm and solid.' The water element does not think: 'I can make the body cohere.' The fire element does not think: 'I can digest food and drink within the body.' The wind element does not think: 'I can produce inhalation and exhalation.' The space element does not think: 'I can produce hollow spaces within the body.' The consciousness element also does not think: 'I can bring about the sprout of name-and-form in the body.' The body also does not think: 'I was born from these conditions.' Yet, when these conditions are present, the body is produced. That earth element is not a self, not a being, not a life-possessor, not one who is born, not a youth, not an agent, not male, not female, not a eunuch, not a sovereign, not 'mine,' and not anything else. Likewise, the water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness elements are not a self, not a being, not a life-possessor, not one who is born, not a youth, not an agent, not male, not female, not a eunuch, not a sovereign, not 'mine,' and not anything else. What is ignorance? With regard to these six elements, giving rise to a perception of unity, a perception of combination, a perception of permanence, a perception of solidity, a perception of imperishability, a perception of pleasure, and perceptions of a being, a life, one who is born, a nurturer, a person, a youth, an agent, a self, and 'mine': the various forms of unknowing that arise in this way constitute ignorance. Because there is ignorance, craving, aversion, and delusion arise in relation to objects. Because craving, aversion, and delusion arise in relation to objects, this is ignorance conditioning volitional formations. That which discerns objects is called consciousness. The four appropriated aggregates that arise together with consciousness constitute name-and-form. The sense-faculties on which name-and-form depends are called the six sense-bases. The conjunction of three things is called contact. That which experiences contact is called feeling. Attachment to feeling is called craving. The increase of craving is called grasping. From grasping, karma that gives rise to becoming is produced, and this is called becoming. The aggregates born from that cause are called birth. When the aggregates have matured, it is called old age. When the aggregates disintegrate, it is called death. The inner attachment, grasping, and affliction at the approach of death is called grief. The words and cries of lamentation that arise from grief are called lamentation. What the five sense-consciousnesses experience as pain is called suffering. The intentional mental consciousness experiencing pain is called sorrow. All such accompanying afflictions are called distress. Great darkness is called ignorance. Creating is called volitional formations. Discerning is called consciousness. Mutual dependence is called name-and-form. Serving as gateways for arising is called the six sense-bases. Contact is called contact. Experiencing is called feeling. Thirsting is called craving. Grasping is called grasping. Producing further becoming is called becoming. Producing the aggregates is called birth. The maturation of the aggregates is called old age. The disintegration of the aggregates is called death. Grieving is called grief. Lamenting is called lamentation. Afflicting the body is called suffering. Afflicting the mind is called sorrow. Disturbing is called distress. Furthermore, not comprehending the true nature, inverted unknowing, is called ignorance. Thus, because there is ignorance, three kinds of formations can be produced: meritorious formations, demeritorious formations, and imperturbable formations. From meritorious formations, meritorious-formation consciousness arises: this is ignorance conditioning volitional formations. From demeritorious formations, demeritorious-formation consciousness arises: this is volitional formations conditioning consciousness. From imperturbable formations, imperturbable-formation consciousness arises: this is consciousness conditioning name-and-form. When name-and-form increases, that which can accomplish through the gates of the six sense-bases is name-and-form conditioning the six sense-bases. From the six sense-bases, the six groups of contact arise: this is the six sense-bases conditioning contact. From the corresponding types of contact arise the corresponding types of feeling: this is contact conditioning feeling. Having discerned feeling, giving rise to craving and attachment: this is feeling conditioning craving. Knowing, and then giving rise to craving and attachment, not wishing to part from pleasing forms and pleasures, and generating longing and desire: this is craving conditioning grasping. Having generated longing and desire, creating karma of body, speech, and mind for further becoming: this is grasping conditioning becoming. The aggregates born from that karma: this is becoming conditioning birth. After birth, the aggregates maturing and disintegrating: this is birth conditioning old age and death. Therefore, these twelve branches of the dharma of causes and conditions are mutually causes and mutually conditions. They are not permanent, not impermanent, not conditioned, not unconditioned, not without causes, not without conditions, not having reception, not exhausting, not decaying, not ceasing. From beginningless time they have flowed like a rushing torrent without interruption. And yet, these twelve branches of the dharma of causes and conditions are mutually causes and mutually conditions, not permanent, not impermanent, not conditioned, not unconditioned, not without causes, not without conditions, not having reception, not exhausting, not decaying, not ceasing, and from beginningless time have flowed like a rushing torrent without interruption.

"There are four factors that can encompass the twelve links of causes and conditions. What are the four? Ignorance, craving, karma, and consciousness. Consciousness, by its nature as a seed, serves as cause. Karma, by its nature as a field, serves as cause. Ignorance and craving, by their nature as afflictions, serve as cause. Among these, karma and afflictions produce the seed of consciousness. Karma can serve as the field for the seed of consciousness. Craving can moisten the seed of consciousness. Ignorance can plant the seed of consciousness. If these conditions are absent, the seed of consciousness cannot be formed. Karma does not think: 'I now serve as a field for the seed of consciousness.' Craving does not think: 'I now moisten the seed of consciousness.' Ignorance does not think: 'I now plant the seed of consciousness.' The seed of consciousness also does not think: 'I am produced from these conditions.' Yet the seed of consciousness depends on the field of karma, moistened by craving, and planted in the manure of ignorance, and in the place of birth, entering the mother's womb, it can give rise to the sprout of name-and-form. That sprout of name-and-form is not self-made, not made by another, not made by both together, not created by Maheśvara, not produced by temporal change, not born from inherent nature, not dependent on an agent, and not produced without cause. Yet, when father and mother come together at the appropriate time, and when other conditions combine at the appropriate time, without a self and without 'mine,' like empty space, in those dharmas that are like illusions, when causes and conditions are not lacking, the seed of consciousness, depending on its place of birth, enters the mother's womb, and can bring about the appropriating seed of consciousness.

"The sprout of name-and-form: just as eye-consciousness arises from five conditions when they are complete. What are the five? Depending on the eye, form, light, and space, and from these the arising of attention: eye-consciousness is produced. The eye serves as the support for eye-consciousness; form serves as the object of eye-consciousness; light serves to reveal; space serves to be unobstructed; and attention serves as thought. If these conditions are absent, eye-consciousness does not arise. If the internal eye-base is not lacking, and likewise form, light, space, and attention are not lacking, then when all combine, eye-consciousness is produced. The eye does not think: 'I now serve as the support for eye-consciousness.' Form does not think: 'I now serve as the object of eye-consciousness.' Light does not think: 'I now serve to reveal for eye-consciousness.' Space does not think: 'I now serve as the unobstructed condition for eye-consciousness.' Attention does not think: 'I now serve as thought for eye-consciousness.' Eye-consciousness also does not think: 'I arise from these conditions.' Yet, with these conditions, eye-consciousness is produced. The remaining sense-faculties should be understood in the same way.

"Thus, there is no dharma that transfers from this world to another. Yet, because causes and conditions are not lacking, karmic fruit also manifests. It is like a clear mirror in which a face-image appears. Although the face-image does not transfer into the mirror, because causes and conditions are not lacking, the face-image appears. Likewise, nothing ceases here and arises in another place; because causes and conditions are not lacking, karmic fruit manifests. It is like the disk of the moon, which travels forty-two thousand yojanas from here. The form of the moon appears in a small water-filled vessel. The moon does not transfer from there to the vessel of water. Yet, because causes and conditions are not lacking, the moon also appears. Likewise, nothing ceases here and is born in another place; because causes and conditions are not lacking, karmic fruit manifests. It is like fire: if causes and conditions are not complete, it cannot burn; when causes and conditions are complete, it can burn. Likewise, in dharmas without a self, without a self and without 'mine,' like empty space, depending on those dharmas that are like illusions, when causes and conditions are not lacking, the seed of consciousness, entering the mother's womb at its place of birth, can bring about the sprout of name-and-form, born from karma and afflictions. Therefore one should thus observe the matter of the internal dharma of causes and conditions in accordance with conditions.

"One should observe the internal dharma of causes and conditions through five characteristics. What are the five? Not permanent, not annihilated, not transferred, from a small cause producing a great fruit, and resembling its kind. How is it not permanent? The aggregates at the time of death on this side are each distinct from the aggregates at the time of birth on that side. The dying aggregates are not the birth aggregates; the dying aggregates also cease and the birth aggregates also come to manifest; therefore it is not permanent. How is it not annihilated? It is not the case that, dependent on the dying aggregates' dissolution, the birth aggregates come to be; nor is it that they arise without the dying aggregates' dissolution. The dying aggregates also dissolve, and at that very moment, like a scale dipping and rising, the birth aggregates come to be; therefore it is not annihilated. How is it not transferred? Because sentient beings, from a non-belonging-class place, can be born into a belonging-class place; therefore it is not transferred. How does a small cause produce a great fruit? Performing a small karma, one experiences a great maturation; therefore a small cause produces a great fruit. As the cause that was made attracts that fruit, it resembles its kind. Therefore one should observe the dharma of causes and conditions through five characteristics.

"Venerable Śāriputra! If there is someone who, with right wisdom, constantly observes the dharma of causes and conditions as taught by the Tathāgata, seeing it as without a life-span, free from life-span, of the nature of reality, free from confusion, unborn, unarisen, uncreated, unconditioned, unobstructed, without an object, quiescent, without fear, not subject to seizure, inexhaustible, and not of a quiescent characteristic; not existing, empty, deceptive, without solidity, like a disease, like a boil, like an arrow, faulty, impermanent, suffering, empty, and without self: then one will not speculate about the past, thinking, 'Did I have a birth in the past? Did I not have a birth?' One will not speculate about the future, thinking, 'Where will I be born in the future?' Nor will one speculate about the present, thinking, 'What is this? How is this? What am I making? Where do these beings come from? Having ceased here, where do they go?' One can also eliminate all the various views of śramaṇas and brahmins of the world, namely: self-view, being-view, life-span view, person-view, views of the extraordinary, views of auspiciousness, views of opening and closing. Through thorough understanding, like a palmyra tree, one clearly severs all roots. In future ages, one realizes the dharma of non-arising and non-ceasing.

"Venerable Śāriputra! If there is someone who is fully endowed with the patience of non-arising (anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti) and can thoroughly discern this dharma of causes and conditions, then the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One, Accomplished in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone One, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One shall bestow upon that person the prediction of supreme, perfect enlightenment (anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi)."

When the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya had finished speaking these words, Śāriputra, together with all the devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and others of the world, having heard the Dharma spoken by the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya, accepted it with faith and put it into practice.
