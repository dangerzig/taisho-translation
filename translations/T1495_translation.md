# 善恭敬經
## Sūtra on Wholesome Reverence

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1495

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, translated by Jñānagupta (闍那崛多).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling in the Jewel-Adorned Hall, the place where the Tathāgata originally practiced. At that time, the World-Honored One, together with great bhikṣus, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, and immeasurable hundreds of thousands of millions of billions of the fourfold assembly, was surrounded on all sides, gathered together to teach the Dharma.

At that time, the elder Ānanda rose from his seat, adjusted his robes, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his ten fingertips in palms together toward the Buddha, and with body and mind reverent, addressed the Buddha, saying: "World-Honored One! The Tathāgata constantly says that those of much learning have great merit. If one further establishes others in a place of much learning, World-Honored One, how much merit does that good man obtain?" Having said this, he remained silent.

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the elder Ānanda, saying: "Ānanda! Since you have asked me, on account of this meaning I shall explain it for you with a simile. Why? Because the wise understand meaning through similes.

"Ānanda! Suppose that in the entire three-thousand great-thousand world-system, all the trees, grasses, herbs, and plants, whether small or large, down to those as small as one finger-breadth across, growing from the earth, were each furnished with branches, leaves, flowers, fruits, and seeds, all fully complete. Ānanda! Among all those trees, the very smallest, one finger-breadth across, would produce flowers and fruits, the number of which would be like the sands of one Ganges River. The grasses and trees of two finger-breadths, like those of one finger-breadth in having branches, leaves, flowers, fruits, and seeds, would have a number of flowers, fruits, and seeds like the sands of two Ganges Rivers. In this manner, proceeding in sequence, those growing from the earth at three finger-breadths would likewise have branches, leaves, flowers, and fruits in number like the sands of three Ganges Rivers.

"Ānanda! What do you think? Could anyone count the number of those trees?"

Ānanda said: "No, World-Honored One."

At that time, the Buddha further addressed Ānanda: "Someone might still be able to count the flowers, fruits, and seeds above a single finger-breadth. But a good man or good woman who teaches others even a single verse of four lines, explaining it for them without seeking reward, arising a mind of compassion and pity, even teaching them until they attain the fruit of arhatship, and who further thinks: 'By what skillful means can I cause many sentient beings, through the power of this Dharma-gift, to attain the fruit of stream-entry, up to the fruit of arhatship, and even to arouse the mind of bodhi?'; who, out of compassion, teaches others even a single verse of four lines, explaining, analyzing, and elucidating it for them: if one were to compare this merit with the number of the merit in the previous simile, this merit would not be reached by even one hundredth part, one thousandth part, one hundred-thousandth part, one billion-thousandth part, one kalā fraction, one simile fraction, or one upaniṣad fraction. The blessings of such men and women cannot be weighed or measured.

"Ānanda! Those men and women gain abundant roots of goodness, even establishing others in much learning, and further teaching others even a single verse of four lines."

At that time, after the World-Honored One had spoken these words, the elder Ānanda again addressed the Buddha, saying: "How rare, World-Honored One! That the World-Honored One should make such a statement! How rare, Bhagavān! That the Tathāgata should make such a statement! World-Honored One! Those good men and good women who receive the Dharma, with regard to that Dharma and to the Dharma-teacher, what kind of reverential mind should they cultivate?"

Having said this, the Buddha addressed Ānanda: "Ānanda! Do not ask me about such a matter. Why? Because sentient beings of the present have no mind of respect for the Dharma."

Ānanda again earnestly addressed the Buddha: "Excellent, World-Honored One! I thirst for the Dharma. Within this Dharma I give rise to deep reverence. Learning the Dharma in accord with the Dharma, since I became the World-Honored One's attendant, I have never heard such a teaching as this. World-Honored One! From now on I shall cultivate such a reverential mind. As the World-Honored One commands, I will not go against the sacred teaching."

At that time, Ānanda again addressed the Buddha, saying: "World-Honored One! In the final age that follows, there will be good men and good women who, with regard to the various dharmas, may have the appearance of thirsting and reverently esteeming them, yet have only words. For the sake of clothing and food, for the sake of gain and offerings, from poor and lowly circumstances they shave their heads and leave home. They say: 'I am capable of the Dharma,' and they do indeed seek the Buddha's Dharma. World-Honored One! Yet those beings have no mind to practice the Dharma. They display the marks of baseness, and such people give rise to base minds. World-Honored One! I ask this question for my own sake. How should we abide? How should we practice?"

Having said this, at that time the Buddha addressed the elder Ānanda, saying: "Ānanda! If there are good men and good women who delight in the Dharma and wish to study and recite it, those beings should go to their upādhyāya or ācārya. Having arrived, they should ask about the Buddha's Dharma. Whatever they delight in and are capable of being taught, they should request reliance. Whether the teacher has ten rains or twelve rains, out of esteem for the Dharma, they should request reliance. Why? Although the Tathāgata once said in the past, 'A bhikṣu of five rains need not seek reliance,' yet the student who has reverence before the teacher is thereby able to serve the Dharma. For this reason, one should seek reliance. Why? Because that person wishes to study the Buddha-Dharma.

"Ānanda! The upādhyāya and ācārya should establish reliance for that person as follows: 'Will it be thus? Do I permit you? Have you gained benefit? Do I instruct you? You must be carefully obedient. Do not be heedless. You must train diligently. It is thus given.' If a bhikṣu has obtained the full dharmas, then that one is able to grant reliance to another. If one can thus analyze the Dharma-phrases and grant reliance, one is called a reliance-teacher.

"If a bhikṣu, even of a hundred rains, cannot skillfully comprehend these Dharma-phrases, that one too should seek reliance from another. Why? If one cannot understand oneself, how much less can one serve as a reliance-teacher for others! Even if a senior bhikṣu of a hundred rains cannot comprehend the secret matters of the śramaṇa sons of the Śākyas, that person should, for the sake of the Dharma, request reliance. Even a senior bhikṣu of a hundred rains who does not comprehend the vinaya should also request reliance.

"If there are bhikṣus who receive the Dharma from another, those bhikṣus should, toward that teacher, give rise to a mind of esteem, honor, and reverence. When about to receive the Dharma, while before the teacher, one must not laugh lightly, must not show the teeth, must not cross the feet, must not look at one's feet, must not move the feet, must not tap the foot. The ankles should be neat and even, not tilting high or low. Before the teacher, one must not ascend a high seat. Unless the teacher raises a question, one must not speak arbitrarily. Whatever task is assigned, one must not disobey. One must not stare at the teacher's face. Remain three cubits from the teacher. When told to sit, sit; do not disobey the instruction. Having been properly seated, one should give rise to a mind of lovingkindness toward the teacher. When a disciple wishes to receive the Dharma, one should kneel before the teacher and first recite what one has already learned. After reciting, if there is doubt, one should first inform the teacher. If permission is seen to be granted, then one may request a resolution.

"At that time, when the student has received the Dharma, placing the right knee on the ground, taking the teacher's feet in both hands, one should single-mindedly bow one's head to the place where the teacher stands. If the ground is level and proper, one should then pay respect. If the ground is narrow or uneven, one should step back and stand until the teacher passes to level ground, and there request the Dharma. Having reached level ground and bowed at the teacher's feet, one should retreat walking backward for ten cubits' distance. Having bowed from afar, one may return as one wishes.

"Furthermore, the disciple should think: 'The teacher is behind me, observing whether I do right or wrong. I should not be heedless. If I can come back soon and appear before the teacher to request resolution of doubts, that is good. If I cannot come, I should know the proper time.' Three times a day one should attend upon and pay respects to the teacher. If during the three periods one does not attend upon and pay respects, the teacher should discipline that person according to the Dharma.

"Furthermore, when a disciple goes to visit the teacher and arrives at the teacher's place but does not find the teacher, one should leave a clod of earth, a piece of wood, or some grass as a sign of having visited. If one sees the teacher inside the room, at that time the student should give rise to utmost sincerity, circumambulate the room three times, bow one's head to the teacher, and only then return. If one does not find the teacher, all tasks should cease and nothing should be done, except for using the lavatory.

"Furthermore, a disciple must not use coarse language toward the teacher. If the teacher rebukes, one should not retort. For the teacher's sitting and reclining bed, one should first spread and wipe it clean, free of dust, insects, and ants. From the time the teacher sits or lies down until the teacher rises, one should practice recitation.

"At that time, when the student sees the sun in the east, one should go to the teacher's place. Having ascertained the proper time, one should frequently visit the teacher's side, inquire about needs, and ask what tasks should be done. One should say to the teacher: 'Will you enter the village?' If the teacher wishes to enter the village, one should first offer the teacher's kaṣāya. Before doing so, one should wash one's hands. After washing, one should take one's own cloth and wipe one's own hands. Before the teacher, with body and mind composed, holding the robe in both hands, one should kneel and present it, offering it reverently according to the Dharma, settled in one's place. Afterward, one should offer water for washing the hands and face, first offering the inner garment worn against the body, and then offering the remaining garments customarily used. Toward the teacher, one should cultivate such a mind of reverence.

"Furthermore, a disciple must not spit or blow the nose before the teacher. When walking within the monastery, out of reverence for the teacher, one must not cover both shoulders with the kaṣāya, and must not cover the head. Where the teacher walks back and forth, one should always sweep and clean. When the weather is hot, three times a day one should fan the teacher. Three times one should offer water and assist with bathing. Furthermore, three times one should offer cool drinks. One should know the proper time and beg food for the teacher. Whatever the teacher is engaged in, one should exert one's full bodily strength to assist. One should take the teacher's bowl, wash it, and make it clean. If the teacher gives it to be washed, one should wash the teacher's bowl first and then one's own. If given to wash, one washes; if not given, one should not ask again. Why? Because there is a reason for this.

"Ānanda! There are bhikṣus who should think: 'In the past, the Tathāgata's bowl was washed by no one. Those who study the Buddha should serve themselves.' Nevertheless, the Tathāgata permitted this: when the weather is hot, one should prepare cool water; when cold, one should prepare warm water. Whatever is needed should all be fully provided. When in the teacher's presence, one must not chew a tooth-stick. Before other people, one must not speak of the teacher's faults. If one sees the teacher from afar, one should immediately rise and go to greet them.

"Ānanda! Whoever has a teacher, from whomever one has learned even a four-line verse, whether by listening, reading, questioning, or consulting about even a single four-line verse, that person is one's teacher. The student should constantly give rise to a mind of reverence and esteem toward the teacher. If one does not do so, one is called disrespectful and is also not called one who abides in right practice. If one speaks of the teacher's faults before others, that person cannot take me as teacher. Why? Ānanda! That person, having no respectful mind, does not love the Buddha. That person, having no practice, how much less loves the Dharma? That person, having no respect, will certainly not love the Dharma. That greatly evil person also does not love the Sangha and is not counted among the Sangha. Why? Because that foolish person does not practice the right practice. Ānanda! All the words the Buddha speaks are for those who practice."

At that time, having heard the Buddha's teaching, the elder Ānanda wept with tears, wiped them with his hand, and said: "World-Honored One! In the future world, there will be few sentient beings who abide in this practice. World-Honored One! We shall practice such a practice. I now shall abide in such a practice. World-Honored One! If a bhikṣu does not give rise to a reverential mind toward the teacher or upādhyāya and speaks of their merits and faults, what retribution will that person receive in the future world?"

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "If in the future world there are bhikṣus who do not give rise to reverence toward the teacher or upādhyāya and who speak of the merits and faults of teacher and sangha, those people are neither stream-enterers nor ordinary persons. Those foolish people should be disciplined as follows. Why? Ānanda! Even if the teacher truly has faults, one must not speak of them, how much less when there are none!

"Ānanda! If there are bhikṣus who are disrespectful toward their teacher, I say there is a separate small hell named 'Beating and Striking,' and they will fall into it. Having fallen there, they will have one body with four heads, the entire body ablaze, resembling a mass of fire, emitting great fierce flames, blazing without cease, burning again and again. In that hell there are insects named 'Hooks'; those poisonous insects constantly devour the tongue-faculty.

"Then those foolish people, relinquishing that body, will be born among animals and receive the form of a wild beast, the body of a jackal, or the body of a wolf. When people see them, they cry out loudly, shouting 'Wolf!' or shouting 'Jackal!' Ānanda! Those foolish ones, all because in the past they reviled and insulted their teacher and upādhyāya, are therefore disliked by all who see them. Because of the fault of their tongue-faculty in the past, they constantly eat excrement and urine.

"Relinquishing that body and though born in the human realm, they are always born in borderlands. Born in borderlands, they abandon all matters of meritorious qualities and are fully endowed with evil dharmas, separated from all wholesome dharmas. Though they obtain a human body, their skin does not resemble that of a human being. They cannot fully possess human form and complexion. They do not resemble their father or mother, and their parents detest them. Having obtained a human body, they are constantly slighted, slandered, and humiliated, separated from the Buddha and World-Honored One, forever without wisdom. After dying from there, they quickly fall back into the midst of hell. Why? Ānanda! If there are people who, toward the instructing teacher, the teacher who grants freedom, the teacher who instructs in the Dharma-practice, or the teacher who instructs in the true practice, give rise to disrespect, they receive this heavy calamity. Ānanda! Those foolish ones further receive immeasurable and boundless dharmas of suffering and affliction.

"Ānanda! If from another one has heard even a single four-line verse, or copied or written it on bamboo or silk, whatever the names may be, for however many kalpas, one should take that upādhyāya or ācārya and carry them on one's shoulders, or sometimes carry them on one's back, or sometimes bear them upon one's head, always bearing them as one walks. Furthermore, one should bring all manner of musical instruments and make offerings to this teacher. Ānanda! Even doing such things, one still cannot fully repay the teacher's kindness, nor is it called deep reverence for the teacher, let alone reverence for the Dharma! To do such things constitutes reverence for the teacher. Ānanda! Only with immeasurable and boundless offerings can one be worthy to make offerings to the teacher!

"Ānanda! In the future world, many bhikṣus, having obtained this sūtra, will give rise to a disrespectful mind toward their teacher and upādhyāya, will have no right practice, and will constantly speak of the teacher's and upādhyāya's faults. Ānanda! I say those foolish people will experience the greatest suffering and in the future world will certainly fall into the evil destinies. Ānanda! I have spoken to you, I have told you: while the Tathāgata is in the world, I have already finished explaining to you, 'This is the right path; this is not the right path.' In the future world, whatever practice those bhikṣus follow, they will be reborn in such a place and receive such retribution. Therefore, Ānanda, I instruct you all to constantly practice reverence.

"Ānanda! If there are good men and good women who can give rise to a mind of reverence and esteem, they will obtain such supreme and superior dharmas, namely: loving and revering the buddhas and world-honored ones, respecting and esteeming the sūtra-Dharma, and deeply loving and revering the Sangha. They will enter this order."

After the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the elder Ānanda and the great assembly, having heard the Buddha's teaching, joyfully received it and put it into practice.
