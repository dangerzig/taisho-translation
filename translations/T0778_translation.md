# 佛說菩薩內習六波羅蜜經
## Sūtra of the Bodhisattva's Internal Practice of the Six Pāramitās, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0778

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by Yan Fotiao (嚴佛調)

---

The Buddha said: "Those who wish to study the bodhisattva path should begin from these: first, counting; second, following; third, stopping; fourth, contemplating; fifth, returning; and sixth, purifying."

The Buddha said: "First, counting corresponds to the dāna-pāramitā. One who counts the breaths enables the spirit to ascend to the heavens. This is giving the spirit within one's own body, and through it one can attain the fruit of srotāpanna, sakṛdāgāmin, anāgāmin, arhat, or pratyekabuddha, or accomplish Buddhahood. This is the internal dāna-pāramitā: through giving one attains deliverance."

The Buddha said: "Second, following corresponds to the śīla-pāramitā. The intention follows the mind, going out and coming in together. There are no wrong thoughts and the mind does not waver. This constitutes not violating the precepts of the path. This is the internal śīla-pāramitā: through not violating the precepts one attains deliverance."

The Buddha said: "Third, stopping corresponds to the kṣānti-pāramitā. When the mind desires lust, anger, or resentment, one can endure and not act upon it. When the mouth desires rich and fine flavors, and the body desires subtle and pleasant touch, one controls the mind, endures, and does not indulge. This is the internal kṣānti-pāramitā: through patience one attains deliverance."

The Buddha said: "Fourth, clear contemplation corresponds to the vīrya-pāramitā. Internally one contemplates the three bodies. Externally one contemplates the ten thousand things: all will decay and perish, nothing endures permanently. One no longer gives rise to greed. Turning toward the path, one contemplates the unconditioned, constantly discerning without laziness. This is the internal vīrya-pāramitā: through vigor one attains deliverance."

The Buddha said: "Fifth, returning corresponds to the dhyāna-pāramitā. One severs the six sense-entries and returns from the five aggregates. What are the six sense-entries? Form entering the eyes is decline; sound entering the ears is decline; scent entering the nose is decline; taste entering the mouth is decline; subtle touch entering the body is decline; excessive thinking causing the mind to decline. These are the six entries, also called the six declines, and they are also related to the five aggregates. What are the five aggregates? The aggregate of form, the aggregate of feeling, the aggregate of perception, the aggregate of formations, and the aggregate of consciousness. These are the five aggregates. One returns to the body and guards its purity, severs seeking, and contemplates emptiness. This is the internal dhyāna-pāramitā: through guarding one-pointedness one attains deliverance."

The Buddha said: "Sixth, purifying corresponds to the prajñā-pāramitā. One knows that all people and all things will perish. The mind is not directed toward birth-and-death; desire and craving are severed; the mind is pure and clean; and wisdom is accomplished. This is the internal mahā-prajñā-pāramitā: through wisdom one attains deliverance."

The question was asked: "What is dāna? What is śīla? What is kṣānti? What is vīrya? What is dhyāna? What is prajñā? And what is pāramitā?"

The Buddha said: "Dāna means giving. Śīla means upholding the precepts. Kṣānti means patience. Vīrya means vigor. Dhyāna means abandoning evil. Prajñā means wisdom. Pāra means crossing over from birth-and-death to deliverance. Mita means boundless. These are the six pāramitās."

The question was asked: "Why are there exactly six pāramitās?"

The Buddha said: "Because people have lust, anger, resentment, and delusion. Giving is practiced to remove evil greed. Upholding the precepts is to remove lust and anger. Patience is to remove resentment and rage. Vigor is to remove laziness. One-pointed concentration is to remove a disordered mind. Wisdom is to remove delusion. Because one wishes to eliminate these six things, one practices the six pāramitās."

The Buddha said: "People have six hidden thieves and robbers. To sever these evils, the dāna-pāramitā is the master that governs the body; the śīla-pāramitā is the master that governs the eyes; the kṣānti-pāramitā is the master that governs the ears; the vīrya-pāramitā is the master that governs the nose; the dhyāna-pāramitā is the master that governs the mouth; and the prajñā-pāramitā is the master that governs the mind."

The question was asked: "Why does the body correspond to the dāna-pāramitā?"

The Buddha said: "If someone asks for one's head, one gives the head. If someone asks for one's eyes, one gives the eyes. If someone asks for one's flesh, one gives the flesh. One casts one's body to a hungry tiger. This is giving, and therefore it belongs to the dāna-pāramitā."

The question was asked: "Why do the eyes correspond to the śīla-pāramitā?"

The Buddha said: "The eyes do not follow after form, and the mind does not give rise to disordered thoughts. This constitutes upholding the precepts, and therefore it belongs to the śīla-pāramitā."

The question was asked: "Why do the ears correspond to the kṣānti-pāramitā?"

The Buddha said: "The ears hear harsh and evil sounds yet do not give rise to anger. This constitutes patience, and therefore it belongs to the kṣānti-pāramitā."

The question was asked: "Why does the nose correspond to the vīrya-pāramitā?"

The Buddha said: "The nose knows the breath going out and coming in, always guarding it without departing. This constitutes vigor, and therefore it belongs to the vīrya-pāramitā."

The question was asked: "Why does the mouth correspond to the dhyāna-pāramitā?"

The Buddha said: "The mouth does not curse, does not speak divisively, does not speak falsely, and does not speak frivolously. This constitutes stillness, and therefore it belongs to the dhyāna-pāramitā."

The question was asked: "Why does the mind correspond to the prajñā-pāramitā?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "All of you who practice the path should always clearly understand and know all the impurities and defilements, and the purity that is naturally so. Neither arising nor ceasing, one completely severs all the roots. Once the roots are severed, they cannot grow again. Those who practice the path should generate the mind of equality, broadly delivering all beings, establishing the bridge of the Dharma, causing all to enter the gate of the Dharma, and widely proclaiming the teaching: without beginning, without end; without form, without sound; without boundary, without limit; without above, without below. In establishing this teaching one should give from within the originally-without. In upholding the Dharma one should employ it so that, as the Tathāgata sought the path, it rests within the mind. If the mind and intention are not right, the path also does not arise. Establish one's practice within the originally-without. When impurities and defilements have been removed, inside and outside are pure. From purity one sees clarity, and thereby the naturally-so manifests; this is the purity of emptiness. Pure and yet purer, empty and yet emptier, empty with nothing whatsoever: this is the path. The essential nature of the path is without anything to depend upon. Above there is nothing to grasp; below there is nothing to stand upon; to the left there is nothing to pull; to the right there is nothing to hold. It stands naturally, with purity as its foundation. The emptiness of emptiness: this is called nirvāṇa. Within existence, to have nothing that exists: this is existence. Within non-existence, to not be nothing: this is non-existence. Within attainment, to have nothing that is attained: this is attainment."

The first is the Bodhisattva of Initial Aspiration; the second is the Bodhisattva Who Upholds the Grounds; the third is the Bodhisattva Who Should Practice; the fourth is the Bodhisattva Born of Noble Lineage; the fifth is the Bodhisattva Who Cultivates to Completion; the sixth is the Bodhisattva Who Ascends in Practice; the seventh is the Bodhisattva of Non-Retrogression; the eighth is the Bodhisattva of the Youthful Truth; the ninth is the Bodhisattva Who Has Completed the Course of Birth; and the tenth is the Bodhisattva Awaiting Succession.
