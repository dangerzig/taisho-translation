## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra of the Great Teaching on Arising, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the village of the Kurus, together with an assembly of bhikṣus. At that time, the Venerable Ānanda was dwelling alone in one place, and in the watches of the night a thought arose in his mind: "The dharmas of dependent arising are exceedingly profound in meaning and difficult to comprehend. Only the Buddha, the Blessed One, endowed with right and complete knowledge, is able to expound them well." Having thought thus, when morning came he departed from his dwelling place and came to where the Buddha was. Having arrived, he paid homage with his head at the feet of the Blessed One, exchanged greetings, and withdrew to stand to one side. He then addressed the Buddha, saying: "Blessed One! I was dwelling alone in one place, and in the watches of the night this thought arose in my mind: the dharmas of dependent arising are profound and difficult to understand. I pray that the Blessed One would expound them for me."

At that time, the Blessed One addressed Ānanda: "Just so, just so. Those dharmas of dependent arising are profound and subtle, difficult to see, difficult to understand, and difficult to contemplate and examine. Only the noble ones endowed with skillful wisdom can discern them; they are not comprehended by those who are foolish. Why is this? Foolish sentient beings, dying in this world and being reborn in other worlds, cycling in this manner through saṃsāra — all do so because they fail to understand the dharmas of dependent arising. Ānanda! Know that all dharmas arise successively through causes and conditions; therefore the cycle of rebirth cannot be severed.

"As for the dharmas of dependent arising: what is called aging-and-death — conditioned by birth, aging-and-death arises; were birth absent, how could aging-and-death exist? It is thus that through the condition of birth, things arise in succession. What is called aquatic beings give rise to aquatic beings; flying birds give rise to flying birds; the various classes give rise to their respective classes; and so on up to human beings giving rise to human beings. Through these conditions, the various sentient beings arise in dependence upon one another. Ānanda! Know that this dharma of birth is a false dharma and is not ultimate. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, aging-and-death exists; thus aging-and-death is also not ultimate.

"Furthermore, what is the condition for birth? What is called existence (bhava) is the condition. Because of that existence, the dharma of birth arises; were existence absent, how could birth be obtained? Therefore the dharma of existence, as has been described, causes the various realms of rebirth to arise successively without cessation. Ānanda! Know that this dharma of existence is a false dharma and is not ultimate. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, the dharma of birth arises; thus birth is also not ultimate.

"Furthermore, what is the condition for existence? What is called grasping (upādāna) is the condition. Because of grasping, the dharma of existence arises; were grasping absent, how could existence be obtained? Ānanda! Know that this dharma of grasping is a false dharma and is not ultimate. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, the dharma of existence arises; thus existence is also not ultimate.

"Furthermore, what is the condition for grasping? What is called craving (tṛṣṇā) is the condition. Because of craving, grasping arises; were craving absent, how could grasping exist? Ānanda! Know that conditioned by craving, seeking arises; conditioned by seeking, acquisition occurs; through acquisition the mind becomes unsettled; through unsettledness there is no contentment; through inner discontentment, delighted greed arises; conditioned by greed, the view of self arises; with the arising of the view of self, clinging occurs; conditioned by clinging, the mind becomes scattered; through scatteredness, false speech, disputes, quarrels, and violence with knives and clubs arise; through these causes and conditions, various unwholesome deeds are thereby committed. All such deeds arise through scatteredness; without scatteredness, these deeds would not arise.

"What is the condition for this dharma of scatteredness? Clinging is the condition. Because of clinging, the mind becomes scattered; were clinging absent, how could scatteredness be obtained? What is the condition for this dharma of clinging? The view of self is the condition through which clinging arises; were the view of self absent, there would be no clinging. What is the condition for this dharma of the view of self? Delighted greed is the condition through which the view of self arises; were delighted greed absent, there would be no view of self. What is the condition for this dharma of delighted greed? Inner discontentment is the condition. Through discontentment, delighted greed arises; if the inner mind has contentment, delighted greed does not arise. What is the condition for this dharma of inner discontentment? Unsettledness is the condition. Through unsettledness, there is no contentment; if the mind is settled, contentment arises. What is the condition for this dharma of unsettledness? Acquisition is the condition. Through acquisition, there is unsettledness; were there nothing acquired, the mind would be settled. What is the condition for this dharma of acquisition? Seeking is the condition. Through seeking, acquisition occurs; were there no seeking, there would be no acquisition. Thus all these dharmas arise successively because craving and seeking mutually condition each other. Know that the dharma of craving is of two kinds: craving for sensual pleasures (kāma-tṛṣṇā) and craving for existence (bhava-tṛṣṇā); from these two dharmas all faults arise. Ānanda! Know that this dharma of craving is a false dharma and is not ultimate. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, the dharma of grasping arises; thus grasping is also not ultimate.

"Furthermore, what is the condition for craving? What is called feeling (vedanā) is the condition. Conditioned by feeling, the dharma of craving arises; were feeling absent, how could craving exist? Ānanda! Know that this dharma of feeling is a false dharma and is not ultimate. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, the dharma of craving arises; thus craving is also not ultimate.

"Furthermore, what is the condition for feeling? What is called contact (sparśa) is the condition. Conditioned by contact, the dharma of feeling arises; were contact absent, how could feeling exist? Conditioned by eye-contact, various feelings arise inwardly: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling; likewise conditioned by ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, and mind-contact, various feelings arise inwardly. All such feelings arise conditioned by the dharma of contact. Ānanda! Know that this dharma of contact is a false dharma and is not ultimate. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, the dharma of feeling exists; thus feeling is also not ultimate.

"Furthermore, what is the condition for contact? What is called the six sense bases (ṣaḍāyatana) is the condition. Conditioned by the six sense bases, the dharma of contact exists; were the six sense bases absent, how could contact exist? Ānanda! Know that this dharma of the six sense bases is false and is not ultimate. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, the dharma of contact arises; thus contact is also not ultimate.

"Furthermore, what is the condition for the six sense bases? What is called name-and-form (nāmarūpa) is the condition. Conditioned by name-and-form, the six sense bases arise; were name-and-form absent, how could the six sense bases exist? This name-and-form refers to the accumulation of the dharma of form together with the dharmas of mind and so forth. This name-and-form and the dharma of consciousness mutually condition each other and arise together in union — this is name-and-form. Ānanda! Know that the dharma of name-and-form is a false dharma and is not ultimate. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, the six sense bases exist; thus the six sense bases are also not ultimate.

"Furthermore, what is the condition for this dharma of name-and-form? What is called the dharma of consciousness (vijñāna) is the condition. Because of the dharma of consciousness, name-and-form exists; were the dharma of consciousness absent, how could name-and-form exist? As for this dharma of consciousness: upon first taking birth, it dwells in the mother's womb depending upon the kalala; the dharma of consciousness, being fully present, neither increases nor decreases. Through the conditions of consciousness, the various aggregates (skandhas) arise, and thus name-and-form becomes complete and fully formed. Know that this consciousness and that name-and-form mutually condition each other and arise.

"Furthermore, know that the condition for this consciousness is precisely name-and-form; therefore consciousness is the condition for name-and-form, and name-and-form is the condition for consciousness. Through this, the fruit of suffering arises; with the arising of the fruit of suffering, aging-and-death succeeds and continues. Through this accumulation, this cause, this arising, this condition, the fruit of suffering is a false dharma and is not ultimate. Through such dependent arising: consciousness conditions name-and-form, name-and-form conditions the six sense bases, the six sense bases condition contact, contact conditions feeling — thus is assembled the one great mass of suffering."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "You should now know that all speech and the path of speech, non-speech and the path of non-speech, what is generated and the path of generation — these two kinds all do not depart from name-and-form. Ānanda! If one understands thus, one abides in the view of equanimity; this is called penetrating the dharmas of dependent arising. This dharma of dependent arising is the fundamental dharma of all Buddhas, the eye of all Buddhas, the very place to which all Buddhas turn."

At this time, the Venerable Ānanda offered this praise: "Well said, Blessed One! You have well expounded this dharma, bringing benefit and joy to me and to all the bhikṣus."

At that time, the Blessed One addressed Ānanda: "I will now expound for you the dharma of non-feeling. You should listen carefully and receive it well, attending with proper intention, mindful recollection, and contemplation. Ānanda! Know that apart from the mark of self there is no dharma of feeling. Why is this? If the dharma of self exists, the dharma of feeling arises in accordance with it; but through penetrating the understanding that the dharma of self is empty, what receiver of feeling could there be? Ānanda! When both self and the dharma of feeling have entirely ceased, there is nothing whatsoever; one abides in the view of equanimity.

"Ānanda! The dharma of feeling is of three kinds: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. As for pleasant feeling: what is called the one who feels and the dharma that is felt — if one can penetrate the understanding that these two are dharmas of cessation and destruction, then there is no pleasant feeling; thus there is no dharma of feeling at all. Why is this? Know that pleasant feeling is an impermanent dharma; when pleasant feeling ceases, one departs from the mark of self; the mark of self being absent, what could constitute a receiver?

"Furthermore, as for painful feeling: what is called the one who feels and the dharma that is felt — if one can penetrate the understanding that these two are dharmas of cessation and destruction, then there is no painful feeling; thus there is no dharma of feeling at all. Why is this? Know that painful feeling is an impermanent dharma; when painful feeling ceases, one departs from the mark of self; the mark of self being absent, what could constitute a receiver?

"Furthermore, neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling is likewise. What is called the receiver and the dharma of feeling — there are these two; if one can penetrate the understanding that these two are dharmas of cessation and destruction, then with regard to suffering, to pleasure, and to neither-suffering-nor-pleasure, the three dharmas are equalized, and there is nothing whatsoever that is felt. Why is this? These three dharmas of feeling are all impermanent and ultimately without substance; when this feeling ceases, one departs from the mark of self; the mark of self being absent, how could there be a receiver?

"Ānanda! What do you think of this? Know that all feelings arise from the mind; because the mind undergoes no turning, there is inwardly no receiver; because dharmas have no substance, there is outwardly nothing that is felt. Therefore, Ānanda! To know thus and abide in the view of equanimity — to abide in this view is to have penetrated the dharma of non-feeling. This dharma of non-feeling is the fundamental dharma of all Buddhas, the eye of all Buddhas, the very place to which all Buddhas turn."

At this time Ānanda again offered praise: "Well said, Blessed One! You have well expounded this dharma! Having heard it, we believe, understand, and receive and uphold it."

At that time, the Blessed One addressed Ānanda: "I will now expound for you the dharma of no-self. You all should listen carefully and receive it well, attending with proper intention, mindful recollection, and contemplation. Ānanda! Understanding that feeling has no substance, one departs from the view of self; having departed from the view of self, one abides in the view of equanimity; abiding in this view, the marks are equalized; through equalization, nothing whatsoever arises in the world; having understood non-arising, one attains: 'My birth is exhausted, the holy life has been established, what needed to be done has been accomplished, I shall not receive another existence.' Ānanda! Know that when the view of self does not arise and one abides in the view of equanimity, one thus attains the complete liberation of the mind; there is no knowing, no seeing, and nothing to be gained; departing from all conceptual thinking, one fully penetrates gaining, non-gaining, and neither-gaining-nor-non-gaining.

"Having thus penetrated, regarding speech and the path of speech, non-speech and the path of non-speech, what is generated and the path of generation — all of these are without knowing and without seeing. Having thus penetrated, one departs from the view of self, abides in the view of equanimity, and knows as it truly is. This is called penetrating the dharma of no-self. This is the fundamental dharma of all Buddhas, the eye of all Buddhas, the very place to which all Buddhas turn."

At this time, Ānanda, having heard what the Buddha had said, again offered praise: "Well said, Blessed One! You have well expounded this dharma! Having heard it, we believe, understand, and receive and uphold it."

At that time, the Blessed One addressed Ānanda: "I will now expound further for you. You should listen carefully and receive it well, attending with proper intention, mindful recollection, and contemplation. Ānanda! Know that no mark of form is to be obtained; departing from all self-clinging, one knows as it truly is; having so known, one contemplates this body as destructible, insubstantial, and unworthy of attachment. Through such contemplation, one departs from all marks of form and does not give rise to self-clinging; when the mark of self has ceased, one understands that this body is destructible and insubstantial; thus one attains abiding in the view of equanimity. Abiding in this view, one penetrates the understanding that all the aggregates are empty; with the aggregates being empty, in what could self and the mark of form be seen?

"Furthermore, Ānanda! Know that the places where consciousness dwells are seven in kind, and the places where consciousness does not dwell are two in kind.

"The seven abodes of consciousness are as follows: There are beings with form who have various bodies and various perceptions — this is the first abode of consciousness. There are beings with form who have various bodies but one perception — namely, the gods of the first dhyāna — this is the second abode of consciousness. There are beings with form who have one body but various perceptions — namely, the gods of the second dhyāna — this is the third abode of consciousness. There are beings with form who have one body and one perception — namely, the gods of the third dhyāna — this is the fourth abode of consciousness. There are beings without form, where in all those realms all perceptions of form are transcended and there is only boundless space — namely, the gods of the sphere of boundless space (ākāśānantyāyatana) — this is the fifth abode of consciousness. There are beings without form, where in all those realms the perception of space is transcended and there is only boundless consciousness — namely, the gods of the sphere of boundless consciousness (vijñānānantyāyatana) — this is the sixth abode of consciousness. There are beings without form, where in all those realms boundless consciousness is transcended and there is nothing whatsoever — namely, the gods of the sphere of nothingness (ākiñcanyāyatana) — this is the seventh abode of consciousness.

"Ānanda! The two places where consciousness does not dwell are as follows: There are beings with form — namely, the non-percipient gods (asañjñisattva) — this is the first place where consciousness does not dwell. There are beings without form, where in all those realms beyond the sphere of nothingness there is neither perception nor non-perception — namely, the gods of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception (naivasañjñānāsañjñāyatana) — this is the second place where consciousness does not dwell."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "Thus, beings with form who have various bodies and various perceptions — this is the first abode of consciousness. You bhikṣus! Should understand this as it truly is; in walking, sitting, and speaking, you should always praise these dharma gates and expound them in detail for the benefit of others. Likewise, up through the seventh abode of consciousness and the two non-abodes of consciousness and their dharma gates — similarly, in walking, sitting, and speaking, always praise what the Buddhas have taught, give rise to pure faith, and understand as it truly is. Those who so understand attain the fruit of the arahant liberated by wisdom (prajñāvimukta).

"Furthermore, Ānanda! Know that there are eight dharma gates of liberation (aṣṭa-vimokṣa). What is called: if one internally has form and observes external form — this is the first liberation. If one internally has no form and observes external form — this is the second liberation. If one directly realizes pure liberation with the body — this is the third liberation. If, having attained purity, one transcends all perceptions of form and observes boundless space without limit — when this contemplation is accomplished — this is the fourth liberation of the sphere of boundless space. If one departs from the sphere of boundless space and contemplates consciousness — consciousness being also boundless — when this contemplation is accomplished, this is the fifth liberation of the sphere of boundless consciousness. If, having departed from the sphere of boundless consciousness, one contemplates that all is without anything whatsoever — when this contemplation is accomplished, this is the sixth liberation of the sphere of nothingness. If, having departed from the sphere of nothingness, one contemplates the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception — when this contemplation is accomplished, this is the seventh liberation of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. If, having departed from the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, one causes feeling and perception to cease and abides in samādhi, having directly realized this with the body — this is the eighth liberation of the cessation of feeling and perception. These are called the eight dharma gates of liberation."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "You should now know that what I have previously set forth — the seven abodes of consciousness, the two non-abodes of consciousness, and the eight dharma gates of liberation — you bhikṣus should understand as I have said, as things truly are; you should always rejoice and offer praise, and furthermore should practice in accordance with right principles. Those who fully and thoroughly penetrate these dharma gates attain the twofold liberation of the arahant fruit (ubhatobhāgavimukta)."

At that time, having expounded this sūtra, the Blessed One concluded. The Venerable Ānanda and all the bhikṣus were filled with great joy; with faith they received it and put it into practice.
