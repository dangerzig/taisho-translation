# 彌沙塞部和醯五分律
## The Five-Part Vinaya of the Mahīśāsaka School

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1421

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Buddhajīva (佛陀什) and Zhu Daosheng (竺道生)

---

## Part One: The Bhiksu Pratimoksa

### Chapter 1: The Parajika Rules

The Buddha was in the country of Suraiva, together with a great assembly of five hundred *bhiksus*. They went to the town of Vairanjya and stayed beneath the trees of a grove. In that town there was a brahmin named Vairanjya, to whom King Prasenajit had granted the town as a fief. He heard that the Buddha, of the Sakya clan, had gone forth from home to study the Way and had attained the states of *Tathagata*, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, Endowed with Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Tamer of Men, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One; that he universally knew the thoughts and intentions of all beings in the world; and that he taught the true Dharma, good in the beginning, middle, and end, good in meaning and good in flavor, complete in the marks of the pure *brahmacarya* practice. With his disciples he had come to visit this town. The brahmin exclaimed, "Excellent! I wish to see the Buddha!" He then went out surrounded by five hundred attendants to the place where the Buddha was. From afar he saw the World-Honored One beneath the trees, his faculties calm and settled, his radiance extraordinary. Filled with joy and delight, he descended from his carriage, proceeded on foot, stood and offered greetings, then sat to one side. At that time the World-Honored One taught him the wonderful Dharma, instructing, benefiting, and delighting him. Having heard the Dharma with joy, the brahmin said to the Buddha, "I pray that the Buddha and the *Samgha* will accept my offering of support for the three months of the rains retreat!" The Buddha said, "My community is large, and you hold different beliefs, different views, different pleasures, and worship different things." The brahmin again said, "Though I hold different beliefs, views, pleasures, and worship, I do not consider the World-Honored One's community too large." He made this request three times, and the Buddha accepted. The brahmin then rose from his seat, circumambulated to the right, and departed, returning home to prepare offerings for the rains retreat.

At that time, Mara Papiyan had this thought: "Now the brahmin has invited the Buddha and the *Samgha* for the three months of the rains retreat. I shall cast a spell to confuse his mind." Having thought this, he came and enchanted the brahmin. Under Mara's spell, the brahmin entered the inner palace to enjoy the five sensual pleasures, and ordered his gatekeeper: "I shall now take my pleasure within the palace for three months. No matter what happens outside, good or bad, do not report it to me!" He completely forgot that he had invited the Buddha and the *Samgha*.

At that time, the people of that country followed heterodox paths. There were no monasteries or lecture halls in the towns. North of the city was a mountain with clean forests and streams, and the Buddha and the great assembly settled there for the rains retreat. There was a famine in the land, and alms were difficult to obtain. When the *bhiksus* entered the town to beg, they received nothing at all. At that time, a horse trader from the country of Pari had driven five hundred horses there at the beginning of summer. Finding the town cool and the water and grass abundant, he stopped to feed and rest his horses. The *bhiksus* went to the horse trader and stood silently before him. The horse trader had faith in the Buddha and compassion for the *bhiksus* who had obtained nothing from begging, and he said, "I have only horse fodder. If you can eat it, I will reduce the horses' share by half and give you one measure each, enough to sustain your bodies and enable you to practice the Way." The *bhiksus* said, "The Buddha has not permitted us to eat from the horses' share." They reported this to the Buddha. The Buddha, on account of this matter, assembled the *bhiksu samgha*, and with various praises of fewness of desires and contentment, told the *bhiksus*: "From now on, you are permitted to eat from the horses' share." At that time, Ananda took the Buddha's portion, had someone prepare it as parched grain, and offered it to the World-Honored One. The other *bhiksus* hulled and cooked theirs to eat.

At that time, the Venerable Maudgalyayana, while in a quiet place, had this thought: "In this country, alms are difficult to obtain. I should take those who have attained supernatural powers to Uttarakuru to eat the naturally growing rice there." Having thought this, he rose from his seat, went to the Buddha, prostrated at his feet, stood to one side, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I just had this thought: in this country alms are difficult to obtain. I should take those who have attained supernatural powers to Uttarakuru to eat the naturally growing rice."

The Buddha told Maudgalyayana: "You might be able to do this, but what about the ordinary *bhiksus*?"

Maudgalyayana said to the Buddha: "I can carry them there with my supernatural powers."

The Buddha said: "Stop, stop! Though you have such supernatural powers, what would you do about the karmic consequences from past lives? And what about ordinary *bhiksus* in the future?" Maudgalyayana accepted the teaching and remained silent.

At that time, the Venerable Sariputra, while in a quiet place, had this thought: "Among the Buddhas of the past, which Buddhas' *brahmacarya* did not long endure? And which Buddhas' *brahmacarya* did long endure?" Having thought this, he rose from his seat, went to the Buddha, prostrated at his feet, stood to one side, and said to the Buddha: "I just had this thought: among the Buddhas of the past, which Buddhas' *brahmacarya* did not long endure, and which did long endure?"

At that time the Buddha praised Sariputra: "Excellent, excellent! What you have contemplated is good, and what you ask is also good. Sariputra! The *brahmacarya* of the Buddhas Vipassin, Sikhin, and Vessabhu did not long endure. The *brahmacarya* of the Buddhas Krakucchanda, Kanakamuni, and Kasyapa did long endure."

Sariputra said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, for what reason did the *brahmacarya* of three Buddhas not long endure, while the *brahmacarya* of three Buddhas did long endure?"

The Buddha told Sariputra: "The three Buddhas did not extensively teach the Dharma to their disciples, did not establish precepts, and did not recite the *Pratimoksa*. After those Buddhas and their disciples passed into *nirvana*, disciples of various names and clans who had gone forth quickly destroyed the *brahmacarya*. It is like a tray of scattered flowers placed at a crossroads: when the four winds blow, the flowers scatter in all directions. Why? Because there is no thread to hold them together. So too, Sariputra, because those three Buddhas did not extensively teach the Dharma, did not establish precepts, and did not recite the *Pratimoksa*, the *brahmacarya* did not long endure.

"Furthermore, Sariputra, the three Buddhas Krakucchanda, Kanakamuni, and Kasyapa extensively taught the Dharma to their disciples without weariness, namely: *sutras*, *geyas*, predictions, *gathas*, *udanas*, *nidanas*, *jatakas*, *vaipulyas*, accounts of unprecedented events, *avadanas*, and *upadeshas*; they established precepts and recited the *Pratimoksa*. After those Buddhas and their disciples passed into *nirvana*, although disciples of various names and clans went forth, the *brahmacarya* was not quickly destroyed. It is like various flowers strung together with thread and placed at a crossroads: when the four winds blow, they cannot be scattered. Why? Because the thread holds them together. So too, Sariputra, because those three Buddhas extensively taught the above Dharma, the *brahmacarya* did long endure."

Sariputra said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if the *brahmacarya* does not long endure because of not extensively teaching the Dharma, not establishing precepts, and not reciting the *Pratimoksa*, I pray that the World-Honored One will extensively teach the Dharma, establish precepts, and recite the *Pratimoksa* for the disciples! Now is the proper time."

The Buddha said: "Enough. I myself know the right time. Sariputra, this community of mine is pure and has not yet produced any unprecedented unwholesome dharmas. Among this community, even the least has attained the fruit of *srotapanna*. Buddhas and *Tathagatas* do not establish precepts for disciples before unwholesome dharmas have arisen. In this community, there are none who, because of relying on much learning, because of gain and fame, because of many desires, or because of displaying supernatural powers and being known to gods and humans, have given rise to the defilements."

When the three months of the rains retreat were completed, the World-Honored One told Ananda: "Come, Ananda, let us go to the brahmin Vairanjya's place." Ananda accepted the instruction, arranged his robes, and followed the Buddha to the brahmin's gate. At that time the brahmin was on a high terrace enjoying the five sensual pleasures. Seeing the World-Honored One from afar, he suddenly remembered and recalled what he had done. He rushed down, wiped the seats clean to receive the Buddha, prostrated with his five limbs on the ground, and repented, blaming himself: "I am a foolish person! I invited the Buddha for the rains retreat and then failed to provide offerings. It was not that I changed my mind, nor that I lacked provisions; I simply became confused and forgot. I pray that the World-Honored One will accept my repentance!"

The Buddha said: "You are indeed foolish! You invited the Buddha and the *Samgha* and then failed to provide offerings. It is fitting that you should repent! I and the community now accept your confession." He also told the brahmin: "In my noble Dharma, one who knows how to confess increases in wholesome qualities."

[The brahmin requested the Buddha to stay for one month; the Buddha declined three times, saying he must now travel. The brahmin then offered a farewell feast, which the Buddha accepted. The next day, the brahmin provided a meal and offered cloth and sandals as rains-retreat gifts. The Buddha, after praising fewness of desires and contentment, permitted the *bhiksus* to accept rains-retreat gifts. The brahmin rejoiced, and the Buddha spoke a verse of blessing:]

> "Of all shrines to the gods, fire worship is the foremost;
> Of all heterodox schools, Savatthi is the foremost;
> Of all people, the wheel-turning king is the foremost;
> Of all flowing waters, the great ocean is the foremost;
> Of all luminaries, the sun and moon are the foremost;
> Of all beings in heaven and earth, the Buddha is the foremost field of merit."

The World-Honored One, having spoken this verse and further taught the Dharma, rose from his seat and went toward the country of Samkasya. Traveling from place to place, he later arrived at Vaisali and stayed at the Kutagarasala (peaked-roof hall) beside the Monkey Pool, where he was honored, revered, praised, and supplied by the four assemblies of *bhiksus*, *bhiksunis*, *upasakas*, and *upasikas*, as well as kings, ministers, *sramanas*, and brahmins.

#### The First Parajika: Sexual Intercourse

At that time, the elders of the Kalandaka village came to the city on business and heard that the Buddha was at the Kutagarasala. They all went to the Buddha and saw the World-Honored One teaching the Dharma to a countless assembly. Among them was a householder's son of the Kalandaka clan named Sudinna, who, hearing the Dharma with joy, thought: "As I understand what the Buddha has taught, one who lives at home is bound by attachments and affections and cannot practice the *brahmacarya* to its fullness for the whole of one's life. Going forth is without attachment, like open space. Would it not be better for me to go forth from household life and practice the Way?"

[Sudinna requested ordination; the Buddha asked if his parents had consented; they had not. Sudinna returned home and pleaded with his parents, who refused three times. He then fasted for six days until his relatives and friends persuaded his parents to consent, on the condition that he visit them from time to time.]

Sudinna returned to the Buddha, who said: "Welcome, *bhiksu*! Practice the *brahmacarya*. I have well taught the Dharma for the ending of all suffering." When the Buddha said this, Sudinna's hair and beard fell away of themselves, the *kasaya* robe appeared on his body, an alms bowl appeared in his hand, and he became a *sramana* who had received full ordination.

[Not long after his ordination, during a famine, Sudinna brought *bhiksus* to his home village. His parents urged him to return to lay life; he refused. His parents then brought his former wife, who was in her fertile period, and begged him to produce an heir to continue the family line. Sudinna, moved by their pleas about ancestral rites and royal laws regarding succession, consented and had sexual relations with his former wife three times, resulting in a conception. A deity of great power from the Tusita heaven was reborn in the womb.]

At that time the earth deities announced to the sky deities: "The Kalandaka son has done an unprecedented deed among the unprecedented *Samgha*!" The sky deities told the Four Great Kings, the Four Great Kings told the Trayastrimsa gods, and so the message was passed up to the Brahma heaven.

His wife, when her months were full, bore a son of great intelligence, who was named "Continuing the Line." When he grew up, he went forth and attained *arhatship*.

At that time Sudinna, having committed this evil, reproached himself: "I have lost my good fortune! How could I, having gone forth in the Buddha's true Dharma, fail to thoroughly practice the *brahmacarya*?" He became emaciated and haggard, barely alive. The other *bhiksus* saw him and asked: "You formerly had a good complexion; why are you now so haggard? Could it be that you are unhappy with the *brahmacarya* and have committed an evil offense?" He answered: "I have committed an evil offense; that is why I am thus!" They asked: "What offense have you committed?" He answered: "I had sexual intercourse with my former wife." The *bhiksus* said: "What you have done is unwholesome, impure, contrary to the *sramana*'s way, and not in accord with the Path!" They rebuked him with various reproofs as the Buddha has taught, and then brought him before the Buddha and reported the matter.

The Buddha, on account of this matter, assembled the *bhiksu samgha*. It is the constant way of all Buddhas: they ask knowing the answer and sometimes do not ask; they ask at the right time and do not ask at the wrong time; they ask when it is beneficial and do not ask when it is not. It is the constant way of all Buddhas: five hundred *vajra* spirits attend them on each side, and if the Buddha asks three times and one does not answer truthfully, one's head will split into seven pieces. The Buddha asked: "What do the *bhiksus* wish to say?" The *bhiksus* reported the matter again. The Buddha asked Sudinna: "Did you truly do this?" He answered: "Truly, World-Honored One." The Buddha said: "You foolish man! What you did is unwholesome, impure, contrary to the *sramana*'s way, and not in accord with the Path! This cannot cause those without faith to gain faith, and causes those with faith to regress. Have you not heard me condemn desire, the perception of desire, the thought of desire, the fever of desire in various ways, and praise the cutting off of desire, the abandonment of the perception of desire, the removal of the thought of desire, the extinguishing of the fever of desire? I have constantly said that desire is like a heap of dry bones, a great fire pit, a sharp knife, a sharp arrow, a poisonous snake, a poison, an illusion, a dream that deceives people. How could you commit this great evil?" The Buddha continued with extensive reproofs, then told the *bhiksus*: "For ten benefits, I now establish a precept for the *bhiksus*. From now on, this precept should be recited thus:

**"'If a *bhiksu* engages in sexual intercourse, he incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

[A second case followed: a forest-dwelling *bhiksu* was found to have had intercourse with a female monkey. The rule was then expanded:]

**"'If a *bhiksu* engages in sexual intercourse, even with an animal, he incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

[A third case followed, involving *bhiksus* who said they should practice lay and heterodox customs. The Buddha rebuked this. Then the case of Sundara Nanda arose, who practiced all manner of evil while in robes. The Buddha declared him to be no *bhiksu* and ordered the *samgha* to perform a formal act of expulsion (*nasana-kamma*) against him. The final formulation:]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, having accepted the training rules shared with fellow *bhiksus*, without weakness in his precepts and without renouncing them, engages in sexual intercourse, even with an animal, that *bhiksu* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

[The text then provides detailed word-by-word definitions:]

"*Bhiksu*" means: a mendicant *bhiksu*, a *bhiksu* who wears robes of muted color cut into pieces, a *bhiksu* who destroys evil, a true *bhiksu*, a resolute *bhiksu*, a *bhiksu* who sees faults, a *bhiksu* ordained with one statement, with two statements, with three statements, a *bhiksu* ordained by "Welcome," and a *bhiksu* ordained by the proper procedure of a *jnapticaturthakarma*. These are called "*bhiksu*."

"Shared training" means: as the Buddha has taught, "For the whole of one's life, one does not transgress; this is the shared training."

"Weakness in precepts without renouncing" and "renouncing precepts without weakness" and their combinations are then defined at length. [The text enumerates many invalid ways of renouncing precepts: while asleep, while intoxicated, while insane, while mentally disturbed, toward non-beings, by letter, by gesture, in jest, etc. Only a clear verbal renunciation to a person who understands constitutes valid renunciation.]

"Engaging in sexual intercourse" means: unchaste conduct, slothful conduct, conduct like dogs, contemptible conduct, the joining of two bodies with the emission of impurity.

"*Parajika*" means: it is called "a fallen dharma," "an evil dharma," "a dharma of severing the head," "a dharma that is not of a *sramana*."

"Expelled from the community" means: as when formerly a layperson, one cannot share in the training, the *karman*, or the recitation of the *Pratimoksa* with the *bhiksus*.

The Buddhas and World-Honored Ones, who well teach the true Dharma, also give apt similes. They say that one who commits sexual intercourse is like a needle whose eye is broken and cannot be used again; like a person whose life has ended and cannot be revived; like a stone that is shattered and cannot be joined again; like the heart of a *tala* tree that is cut and cannot grow again.

[Detailed case law follows, specifying the offense for intercourse with three kinds of beings (human, non-human, animal), three types of female, three types of male, eunuchs, those without sex organs, and those with both organs; specifying three orifices; specifying offenses in various states (sleeping, intoxicated, insane, dead, partially consumed); specifying offenses for the one who is coerced; specifying lesser offenses for use of fingers or external objects.]

*Bhiksunis* also incur *parajika*. *Siksamanas*, *sramaneras*, and *sramaneris* incur *duskrta* and are expelled.

Those who are not in offense: those with a deranged mind, a disordered mind, a mind impaired by illness, and those acting for the first time before the rule was established. These four are not in offense. In all subsequent precepts below, the same applies and will not be repeated.

#### The Second Parajika: Theft

The Buddha was in Rajagrha. At that time there was a *bhiksu* named Dhaniya, a potter's son, who had built a grass hut on Mount Isigili. [When woodcutters repeatedly destroyed his hut, he built a fired-brick house of great beauty. The Buddha reproached him for harming living creatures in the firing process and ordered the house destroyed. Dhaniya then obtained lumber by falsely claiming the king had given permission. He took the city's defensive timbers. When caught, he was brought before the king, who sent him to the Buddha for judgment.]

The Buddha rebuked him extensively and then told the *bhiksus*: "For ten benefits, I establish a precept. From now on, this precept should be recited thus:

**"'If a *bhiksu* steals property worth five coins or more, he incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

[Further cases led to expanding the rule to include both inhabited and uninhabited areas, and to its final formulation:]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, in an inhabited or uninhabited place, with thieving intent takes what is not given, such that a king or minister would seize, bind, kill, or banish him, saying "You are a thief! You are a fool! You are ignorant!" that *bhiksu* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

[Detailed definitions follow for "inhabited place," "uninhabited place," "thieving intent," "taking what is not given," "king," "minister," "seizing," "binding," "killing," "banishing," "thief," "fool," and "ignorant." Then follows an extensive enumeration of types of theft: underground objects, objects on the ground, objects in the air, villages, shops, fields, gardens, houses, vehicles, burdens, boats, ponds, deposits, highway robbery, guiding thieves, joint theft, tax evasion; with detailed graduated penalties for each.]

Four kinds of taking heavy objects without offense: thinking it is one's own, taking with the owner's consent, borrowing temporarily, and taking without thieving intent.

#### The Third Parajika: Killing a Human Being

The Buddha was in Vaisali. At that time the World-Honored One taught the *bhiksus* the contemplation of impurity, saying it yields great fruit and benefit. The *bhiksus* all practiced it and became deeply disgusted with and ashamed of the body. [Some killed themselves, others killed each other, and some sought out a *candala* named Migalandika to kill them. Mara appeared and encouraged the *candala*, who then went from room to room offering to "liberate" *bhiksus*. In a single day he killed as many as sixty.]

The World-Honored One, arising from *samadhi*, noticed the *Samgha* had diminished. Ananda explained the circumstances. The Buddha assembled the *Samgha*, rebuked the *bhiksus*, and told them: "If one kills oneself, one incurs a *thullaccaya* offense." He then taught the practice of *anapanasmrti* (mindfulness of breathing).

[Through a series of progressive cases involving providing weapons to the suicidal, instructing hunters to kill, and praising death to the sick, the rule was progressively expanded to its final formulation:]

**"'If a *bhiksu* kills a human being or one resembling a human being, whether by his own hand, or by providing a weapon or poison, or by instructing another to kill, or by instructing one to kill oneself, or by praising death, saying "Alas! What use is a wretched life? Death is better than living," with such intent, and the other thereby dies, in these various circumstances, that *bhiksu* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

"Resembling a human being" means: from the time of entering the mother's womb through the forty-ninth day. After that, one is fully called "a human being."

[Detailed case law follows for various means of killing: by hand, by weapon, by poison, by commissioning, by letter, by sign, by gesture, by indirect speech, by summoning ghosts, by pitfalls, by rigged seats, by sorcery. Graduated penalties are specified for killing non-humans (*thullaccaya*) and animals (*payantika*).]

#### The Fourth Parajika: Falsely Claiming Superhuman Attainments

The Buddha was in Vaisali. [During a famine, *bhiksus* by the Vaggumuda River conspired to falsely praise each other's spiritual attainments to impress lay supporters and obtain better food. When the Buddha learned of this, he severely rebuked them, comparing them to the five worst thieves in the world, the greatest being one who falsely claims superhuman attainments.]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, without knowing and without seeing superhuman dharmas or the fullness of noble attainments, claims "I know thus, I see thus," and later, whether questioned or not, seeking to clear himself and become pure, says "I claimed to know when I did not know, claimed to see when I did not see; I spoke falsely and deceptively," excluding cases of *adhimana* (overestimation), that *bhiksu* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

[Definitions of "superhuman dharmas" (all *dhyanas*, liberations, *samadhis*, *samapattis*, the noble path and its fruits), "fullness of noble attainments," and related terms follow. Eight types of false claims that incur *parajika* are enumerated. Four, eight, and sixteen types of "non-noble speech" and "noble speech" concerning seeing, hearing, cognizing, and knowing are detailed.]

### Chapter 2: The Samghavasiesa Rules

#### 1. Intentional Emission of Semen

The Buddha was in Sravasti. At that time the elder Udayin, burning with the fire of desire, became emaciated and barely alive. He emitted semen by hand and attained ease. [He taught this method to another *bhiksu*, who rebuked him. The matter was reported to the Buddha.]

**"'If a *bhiksu* intentionally emits semen, except in a dream, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

"*Samghavasiesa*" means: this offense has a remainder; there are still conditions under which it can be treated. One has a refuge and can seek its removal within the *Samgha*.

[Ten types of semen are listed. Ten motivations for intentional emission are enumerated. Five bodily means are specified. Detailed graduated offenses for various combinations of mental intent, bodily action, and emission/non-emission follow.]

#### 2. Touching a Woman's Body

[Udayin, unable to emit semen (now prohibited), devised touching women visitors in his room. A brahmin's wife was molested. The matter was widely condemned and reported to the Buddha.]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, overcome by desire with altered mind, touches a woman's body, whether grasping her hand, grasping her hair, or grasping any part of her body, rubbing against her for the pleasure of contact, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

[Detailed case law on various combinations of perception (female/male/eunuch/sexless/dual-sexed), clothing/unclothing, and degrees of contact follow, with graduated offenses.]

#### 3. Lewd Speech to a Woman

[Udayin, forbidden from touching, instead spoke lewd words to women visitors, asking obscene questions about their bodies and conjugal relations. The matter was reported to the Buddha.]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, overcome by desire with altered mind, speaks lewd words to a woman in accordance with sexual conduct, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

[Seven types of lewd speech are enumerated: disparagement, praise, solicitation, entreaty, questioning, counter-questioning, and instruction.]

#### 4. Soliciting Service to One's Own Body

[As above, with Udayin now soliciting women to provide sensual services to himself. The rule:]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, overcome by desire with altered mind, speaks in the presence of a woman praising the offering of sexual service to his own body, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 5. Acting as a Go-Between

[The *bhiksu* Candaka acted as a matchmaker, conveying messages between men and women for the purpose of marriage or liaison.]

**"'If a *bhiksu* acts as a go-between, conveying a man's intentions to a woman and a woman's intentions to a man, whether for marriage or for temporary union, even for a single encounter, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 6. Building a Hut

[A *bhiksu* built a hut without the *Samgha*'s permission, in an unsuitable location, obstructing pathways.]

**"'If a *bhiksu* builds a hut by begging for materials himself, without a designated site, without the *Samgha*'s approval, exceeding the proper measurements, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 7. Building a Large Dwelling

[As above, for a larger dwelling requiring a patron's support.]

**"'If a *bhiksu* builds a large dwelling with a patron's support, without the *Samgha*'s approval of the site, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 8. Groundlessly Accusing Another Bhiksu of Parajika

**"'If a *bhiksu*, acting improperly, out of malicious anger, groundlessly accuses another *bhiksu* who is free of *parajika* offenses, wishing to destroy his *brahmacarya*; and this *bhiksu* later, whether questioned or not, says "This matter was groundless; I spoke out of anger," he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 9. Using a Contrived Pretext to Accuse Another of Parajika

**"'If a *bhiksu*, seizing upon some other matter as a pretext, accuses another *bhiksu* of a *parajika* offense, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 10. Schism

[The case of Devadatta's attempts to split the *Samgha* and the Buddha's response. The rule establishes a three-part admonition process.]

**"'If a *bhiksu* endeavors to split the harmonious *Samgha*, or persists in taking up an issue conducive to schism, the *bhiksus* should admonish that *bhiksu* thus: "Venerable one, do not endeavor to split the harmonious *Samgha*!" If, after being admonished up to three times, he desists, it is well; if he does not desist, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 11. Supporting a Schismatic

**"'If *bhiksus*, whether one, two, or three, being partisans of that *bhiksu*, say "Do not admonish that *bhiksu*! He speaks Dharma, he speaks Vinaya!" the *bhiksus* should admonish those *bhiksus*. If after three admonitions they desist, it is well; if not, they incur *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 12. Corrupting Families

[A *bhiksu* who ingratiated himself with lay families through improper means, giving gifts and performing services to maintain their patronage, bringing the *Samgha* into disrepute.]

**"'If a *bhiksu* corrupts families and his conduct is seen to be evil, the *bhiksus* should admonish him. If after three admonitions he desists, it is well; if not, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

#### 13. Refusing to Accept Admonition

**"'If a *bhiksu*, when lawfully admonished, says "Venerable ones, do not speak to me of good or evil! I shall not speak to the Venerable ones of good or evil!" the *bhiksus* should admonish him up to three times. If he desists, it is well; if not, he incurs *samghavasiesa*.'"**

### Chapter 3: The Aniyata (Undetermined) Rules

The Buddha was in Sravasti. At that time, Bhadra Nanda regularly visited a householder's home. In the morning, he would put on his robe, take his bowl, go to the house, spread his sitting cloth, and sit alone in a secluded place with the householder's wife, speaking words of sexual desire.

At that time, Visakha the Deer Mother heard that Bhadra Nanda was sitting alone in a secluded place with the householder's wife, speaking words of sexual desire. She thought: "If the householder returns and sees this, he will certainly develop ill will toward the other *bhiksus*, causing them long-lasting suffering. I should send someone to inform the World-Honored One."

[The matter was reported. The Buddha established the first undetermined rule:]

**"'If a *bhiksu* sits alone in a secluded place suitable for sexual intercourse with a single woman, and a trustworthy *upasika* sees him and describes the situation in terms of one of three offenses (*parajika*, *samghavasiesa*, or *payantika*), and if the *bhiksu* says "It is as the *upasika* says," he should be dealt with according to whichever of the three offenses she describes. This is called an undetermined rule.'"**

The second undetermined rule is the same but for sitting with a woman in an open (non-secluded) place, excluding *parajika*.

### Chapter 4: The Nihsargika-Payantika (Forfeiture-Expiation) Rules

These thirty rules concern offenses involving improper acquisition, retention, or use of material goods, primarily robes and cloth. The goods must be forfeited to the *Samgha* before the offense can be expiated.

#### 1. Keeping Extra Robes

The Buddha was in Sravasti. The World-Honored One had taught the *bhiksus* to keep only three robes, but the Group of Six *bhiksus* wore different robes at each meal time.

[Through progressive cases involving Aniruddha's torn robe and *bhiksus* traveling with heavy robes through mud, the Buddha permitted the *kathina* privilege and the ten-day allowance:]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, after his three robes are completed and the *kathina* has been relinquished, keeps an extra robe for more than ten days, he incurs *nihsargika-payantika*.'"**

#### 2. Sleeping Apart from the Three Robes

[The Group of Seventeen *bhiksus* left robes behind when traveling.]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, after his three robes are completed and the *kathina* has been relinquished, sleeps even one night apart from any of the three robes, except with the *Samgha*'s authorization, he incurs *nihsargika-payantika*.'"**

#### 3. Out-of-Season Cloth

**"'If a *bhiksu*, after his three robes are completed and the *kathina* has been relinquished, receives out-of-season cloth, and if he needs it, he should accept it and quickly make it into a robe. If sufficient, that is well; if insufficient, he may keep it up to one month while expecting to obtain more. If he keeps it beyond that, he incurs *nihsargika-payantika*.'"**

[Rules 4 through 30 follow the same pattern, covering: having a *bhiksuni* wash one's robe; accepting robes from a *bhiksuni*; accepting robes from a non-relative; asking for robes beyond necessity; directing a householder's robe offering; repeatedly soliciting a householder; obtaining cloth above the specified value; obtaining silk; pure black wool; mixed wool; keeping wool beyond the allowed period; a *bhiksu*'s sitting cloth; *kathina* cloth of discarded rags; medicine; gold and silver; various forms of trade and commerce; extra bowls; thread for weaving; requesting a weaver to modify cloth; and other matters of material goods. For each rule, an origin story is given, followed by the rule formulation, definitions, and case law. As the pattern is formulaic, they are abbreviated here.]

### Chapter 5: The Payantika (Expiation) Rules

These ninety-one rules address various offenses requiring simple confession.

#### 1. Deliberate Lying

The Buddha was in Sravasti. At that time there was a Dharma-teaching *bhiksu* named Sarana, who was clever and eloquent. No one among the four assemblies, heterodox practitioners, *sramanas*, or brahmins could match him. He would argue that wrong was right and right was wrong, knowing it was untrue, claiming knowledge when he did not know, and not claiming knowledge when he did know, always using his cleverness to defeat others in debate. When asked whether he genuinely believed his arguments, he admitted: "I truly know they are wrong, but I am ashamed of losing, so I speak falsely!"

**"'If a *bhiksu* deliberately speaks falsehood, he incurs *payantika*.'"**

#### 2. Reviling a Bhiksu

[The Group of Six *bhiksus*, jealous of others' diligent study, reviled their fellows by calling them low-caste, artisans, and evil-doers, disrupting their studies.]

The Buddha told a *jataka*: "In the past, in the city of Taksasila, a brahmin's ox was taunted with insults during a contest and lost its strength. When praised with kind words in a rematch, it won. Even animals respond to disparagement with weakness and to encouragement with strength; how much more so for humans!"

The Buddha then spoke this verse:

> "One should speak pleasing words,
> And never speak what is disagreeable.
> Even animals, hearing kind words, rejoice
> And pull heavy loads up steep hills.
> Thereby they have no rival
> And obtain double gain with delight.
> How much more for human beings,
> Where praise and blame have real effect!"

**"'If a *bhiksu* reviles another *bhiksu*, he incurs *payantika*.'"**

#### 3. Slandering Between Bhiksus

[The Group of Six *bhiksus* carried tales between *bhiksus* to create enmity, disrupting their studies.]

The Buddha told a *jataka* about a lion named Good Fang, a tiger named Good Claw, and a jackal who sowed discord between them with verse. The lion eventually discerned the jackal's treachery.

**"'If a *bhiksu* slanders between *bhiksus* to create discord, he incurs *payantika*.'"**

#### 4. Teaching Dharma to a Woman Beyond Five or Six Sentences

[Bhadra Nanda taught Dharma to laywomen improperly, creating suspicion. Later, a *bhiksu* of great virtue declined to teach a woman who was about to die, strictly following the rule. The Buddha then permitted up to five or six sentences.]

**"'If a *bhiksu* teaches Dharma to a woman beyond five or six sentences, he incurs *payantika*.'"**

[Rules 5 through 91 follow the same pattern. Each has an origin story, a rule formulation, definitions, and case law. Major topics include:

- Rules 5-10: Sleeping arrangements, lodging with women, teaching unordained persons, reporting false attainments
- Rules 11-20: Destroying vegetation, evasive speech, criticizing monastic officials, leaving beds outdoors, removing another's bedding, displacing another *bhiksu*
- Rules 21-30: Teaching a *bhiksuni* without authorization, teaching after sunset, accusing a *bhiksu* of teaching *bhiksunis* for food, giving robes to *bhiksunis*, sewing robes for *bhiksunis*, traveling with *bhiksunis*
- Rules 31-40: Accepting food from non-relatives, eating out of turn, being invited to consecutive meals, accepting more than two or three bowls of food, eating after the meal is complete, inducing another to eat without the leftover-food procedure, eating at the wrong time, storing food
- Rules 41-50: Taking food not offered, entering a village at the wrong time, eating at a military camp, watching military exercises, drinking intoxicants, tickling, playing in water, hitting a *bhiksu*, threatening a *bhiksu*, hiding another's belongings
- Rules 51-60: Destroying living beings, stirring up doubt in another *bhiksu*, eavesdropping on disputing *bhiksus*, refusing to consent to a lawful act, leaving during a proceeding, giving consent then retracting it, whispering, striking a *bhiksu* with the palm, groundless accusations of *samghavasiesa*
- Rules 61-70: Deliberately killing animals, drinking water with living creatures, reopening settled issues, concealing another's serious offenses, ordaining a person under twenty, arranging travel with thieves, traveling with a woman, disparaging the Dharma and Vinaya
- Rules 71-80: Making a *bhiksu* uncertain about the rules, obstructing distribution of robes, causing harm to lay families, wrongly declaring non-offense, entering the king's palace without permission, picking up valuables, entering a village at the wrong time for bathing, using an improperly made *nisidana*
- Rules 81-91: Various offenses concerning *bhiksus*' needles, beds, cotton, rain-bathing, the robe-making season, and other matters.

As these follow the same formulaic pattern, they are abbreviated here with "[as above]" for the recurrent procedural passages.]

### Chapter 6: The Pratidesaniya (Confession) Rules

#### 1. Receiving Food from a Non-Related Bhiksuni

The Buddha was in Sravasti. At that time, the *upasika* Vagara's mother had gone forth and, being of few desires and contentment, attracted many offerings. She gave all the food she collected to *bhiksus*, going hungry herself until she collapsed from weakness in the road. The *bhiksus* were rebuked.

**"'If a *bhiksu*, who is not ill, receives food directly from a non-related *bhiksuni* in the street, that *bhiksu* should confess to the *bhiksus*: "I have committed a blameworthy act. I now confess before the Venerable ones." This is called a confession rule.'"**

#### 2. Not Restraining a Bhiksuni Who Directs Food Distribution

[When *bhiksunis* at a lay feast directed the servers to give extra food to certain *bhiksus*, the remaining *bhiksus* went without.]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, when invited to eat at a layperson's home, and there is a *bhiksuni* who says "Give rice to this *bhiksu*, give soup to this *bhiksu*," the *bhiksus* should tell that *bhiksuni*: "Sister, please stand aside until the *bhiksus* have finished eating." If among the *bhiksus* not even one says this, all those *bhiksus* should confess.'"**

#### 3. Accepting Food from a Designated "Training Family"

[The householder Ghosila's family was impoverished by excessive *bhiksu* visits. The *Samgha* performed a *karman* designating it a "training family" (*sekha-kula*), restricting *bhiksu* visits.]

**"'If a *bhiksu*, who is not ill and has not been previously invited, personally receives food from a family designated by the *Samgha* as a training family, he should confess.'"**

#### 4. Receiving Food in a Dangerous Forest Without Warning

[*Bhiksus* dwelling in the wilderness failed to warn laypersons bringing food that bandits were about, and the laypersons were robbed.]

**"'If a *bhiksu* dwelling in a wilderness place with known dangers, without first keeping watch, receives food directly within the monastery without going outside to receive it, he should confess.'"**

#### 5. Receiving Food from an Unrelated Bhiksuni Without Being Ill

[As above, with additional specification.]

### Chapter 7: The Saiksa (Rules to Be Learned) Rules

These one hundred rules concern proper deportment and etiquette. Each carries the penalty of *duskrta* for ignorance or carelessness, and *payantika* for deliberate contempt.

The Buddha was in Rajagrha. At that time *bhiksus* wore their lower robes improperly: too high, too low, unevenly, like palm leaves, like an elephant's trunk, like a ball, or with fine pleats. Householders ridiculed them.

**"'One should wear the lower robe not too high, not too low, not unevenly, not like a palm leaf, not like an elephant's trunk, not like a ball, and not with fine pleats. This is to be learned.'"**

[The remaining 99 rules follow the same pattern, covering:

- Wearing the upper robe properly
- Proper covering of the body when among laypersons
- Controlled deportment (not laughing loudly, not swinging the arms, not looking about)
- Proper sitting posture
- Mindful walking with eyes cast down
- Not sitting with legs outstretched
- Eating properly (not taking overly large mouthfuls, not opening the mouth before food arrives, not talking with food in the mouth, not throwing food, not licking fingers, not scraping the bowl, not licking the lips)
- Not accepting food in a dirty bowl
- Not teaching Dharma to one with an umbrella, a staff, a weapon, or wearing sandals, or mounted on an elephant, horse, or vehicle
- Not urinating or defecating while standing, onto growing plants, or into water
- Not teaching Dharma to one who is lying down while the teacher is sitting, or to one who is sitting while the teacher is standing

Each rule is introduced with a brief origin story and concluded with the penalties.]

**"'Where does a dispute arise that should be settled by the procedure of recollection (*sati-vinaya*)? In Rajagrha. On account of whom? The elder Dharmika.'"**

**"'Where does a dispute arise that should be settled by the procedure of non-insanity (*amudha-vinaya*)? In Rajagrha. On account of whom? The *bhiksu* Gagga.'"**

**"'Where does a dispute arise that should be settled by confession (*pratijnaya*)? In Sravasti. On account of whom? A certain *bhiksu*.'"**

**"'Where does a dispute arise that should be settled by majority decision (*yebhuyyasika*)? In Sravasti. On account of whom? A group of many *bhiksus*.'"**

**"'Where does a dispute arise that should be settled by the procedure of "spreading grass" (*trna-vastara*)? In Sravasti. On account of whom? A group of many *bhiksus*.'"**

## Part Two: The Bhiksuni Pratimoksa

### Chapter 1: The Bhiksuni Parajika Rules

The Buddha was in Sravasti. At that time the elder Upali asked the Buddha: "World-Honored One, you have established the precept for *bhiksus*: 'If a *bhiksu*, having accepted the training rules shared with fellow *bhiksus*, without weakness in his precepts and without renouncing them, engages in sexual intercourse, even with an animal, that *bhiksu* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.' How should this precept be maintained? Should it be maintained as a single *Samgha* or as two *Samghas*?" The Buddha said: "It should be maintained as two *Samghas*. From now on, this precept should be recited thus:

**"'If a *bhiksuni*, having accepted the training rules shared with fellow *bhiksunis*, without weakness in her precepts and without renouncing them, freely engages in sexual intercourse, even with an animal, that *bhiksuni* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

[The remaining *bhiksu parajika* rules (theft, killing, false claims of attainment) are similarly adapted for *bhiksunis*, with Upali asking and the Buddha replying "maintain as two *Samghas*." Additionally, the *bhiksuni* section adds four uniquely feminine *parajika* rules:]

#### 5. Bodily Contact with a Man (Bhiksuni Parajika)

[The *bhiksuni* Thullananda allowed the young man Siribhadda to massage her body, claiming she did not have sexual intercourse but only permitted bodily contact.]

**"'If a *bhiksuni*, overcome by desire with altered mind, allows a man to touch various parts of her body from below the hairline to above the knees and behind the elbows, that *bhiksuni* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

#### 6. The Eight Conditions

[*Bhiksunis* who allowed men to hold their hands, hold their robes, made appointments with men, walked alone with men, stayed alone with men, spoke alone with men, sat alone with men on a single seat, and physically approached men.]

**"'If a *bhiksuni*, overcome by desire with altered mind, allows a man to hold her hand, hold her robe, makes an appointment with a man, walks alone with a man, stays alone with a man, speaks alone with a man, sits alone on a single seat with a man, and physically approaches a man, fulfilling all eight conditions, that *bhiksuni* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

#### 7. Following an Expelled Bhiksu

[The *bhiksuni* Ucca continued to associate with her brother, the *bhiksu* Channa, after he had been subjected to a formal act of non-seeing by the *Samgha*.]

**"'If a *bhiksuni*, knowing that the *bhiksu Samgha* has lawfully performed a formal act of non-seeing against a *bhiksu*, and the *bhiksus* will not dwell with him, act with him, or speak with him, yet she follows and supports him, and after being admonished three times does not desist, that *bhiksuni* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

#### 8. Concealing a Bhiksuni's Parajika Offense

[The *bhiksuni* Sukkha concealed her companion Bhadra's *parajika* offense during her lifetime, only revealing it after her death.]

**"'If a *bhiksuni* sees another *bhiksuni* commit a *parajika* offense and conceals it, and that other *bhiksuni* later dies, goes far away, is expelled, leaves the order, or undergoes a change of sex, and the first *bhiksuni* then says "I previously witnessed her commit a *parajika* offense," that *bhiksuni* incurs *parajika* and is expelled from the community.'"**

Concealment: each moment from morning to the first watch of the night is a *duskrta*; each moment from the first watch to the appearance of dawn is a *thullaccaya*; at the appearance of dawn, it becomes *parajika*.

### Chapter 2: Bhiksuni Samghavasiesa Rules

[Upali asked the Buddha how to maintain the *bhiksu samghavasiesa* rules for *bhiksunis*. The Buddha said "maintain as two *Samghas*." Rules covering matchmaking, groundless accusations, contrived pretexts, and others are adapted. Additional rules unique to *bhiksunis* address ordaining without permission, going to the other bank of a river alone, etc. As the pattern follows the *bhiksu* rules with gender-appropriate modifications, they are abbreviated here.]

### Chapters 3-8: Bhiksuni Aniyata, Nihsargika-Payantika, Payantika, Pratidesaniya, Saiksa, and Adhikarana-Samatha Rules

[These follow the same pattern as the *bhiksu* rules, with modifications appropriate to *bhiksunis*. Where rules are identical, they are shared. Additional rules unique to *bhiksunis* are included. The formulaic procedural passages are as above.]

## Part Three: The Khandhakas (Procedural Chapters)

### Chapter 1: The Ordination Procedure (Upper Section)

The Buddha was in Rajagrha. He told the *bhiksus* the following account:

"In the past there was a king named Udumma who had four sons by concubines: Illuminating Eye, Keen Eye, Taming Elephant, and Niru. They were all brilliant, far-sighted, and possessed great authority. The chief queen had a son named Long Life, who was dull, ugly, and despised by the people. The queen thought: 'Though my son is the eldest, his abilities do not match the others. The four sons all have great authority, and the kingdom's succession will surely fall to them. What stratagem can I devise to secure the throne for my son?'"

[The queen manipulated the king into banishing the four sons, who crossed the Ganges to the foothills of the Himalayas and established a great kingdom. Years later, the king, hearing of their success, exclaimed three times "My sons are capable!" and from that time they were called the Sakya clan.]

"Niru had a son named Simhahanu. Simhahanu's son was Ghotamaka. Ghotamaka's son was Nihshura. Nihshura had four sons: Suddhodana, Suklodana, Dromodana, and Amritodana. King Suddhodana had two sons: the Bodhisattva and Nanda."

[The text then narrates the Bodhisattva's encounters with old age, sickness, death, and a renunciant through the four gates of the city; his great renunciation; his exchange of garments with a hunter; his cutting of hair; his meeting with King Bimbisara; his attainment of enlightenment under the Bodhi tree; the twelve-fold chain of dependent origination; his initial reluctance to teach; Brahma's request; his search for suitable first hearers; his journey to the Deer Park at Varanasi. This narrative is given in extensive detail, with verses at key points:]

> "All conditioned things are thus; the ascetic first enters meditation.
> Having known this law of conditions, he can remove all doubt.
> All conditioned things are thus; the ascetic first enters meditation.
> He destroys the darkness of Mara, as the sun rises in the sky."

[The narrative continues with the first meal offerings from two merchants, the verses of blessing, the encounter with the Naga king, the first lay disciples, the meeting with Sujata, Brahma's request (with verses), and the Buddha's journey to teach the five ascetics at the Deer Park.]

### Chapter 2: The Uposatha (Fortnightly Observance)

The Buddha was in Rajagrha. At that time, heterodox *sramanas* and brahmins gathered on the eighth, fourteenth, and fifteenth days of the half-month for communal observances and teaching, attracting many followers and supporters. King Bimbisara thought: "If the disciples of the true Dharma also did this, would it not be good?"

The Buddha himself also thought: "I have established precepts for the *bhiksus*, but some have not heard them and cannot recite, study, or remember them. I should now permit the *bhiksus* to hold the *uposatha* recitation of the precepts."

[Through progressive cases, the frequency was fixed at the eighth, fourteenth, and fifteenth days. The content was specified: praise of the Three Jewels, the foundations of mindfulness, the right efforts, the bases of supernatural power, the faculties, the powers, the factors of awakening, the path. The *Pratimoksa* recitation procedure was established:]

"Venerable ones! Today is the fifteenth-day *uposatha* for the recitation of the *Pratimoksa*. Let all listen well and reflect carefully. If anyone has committed an offense, he should confess it. If one has no offenses, one should remain silent. By silence, I shall know that I and the Venerable ones are pure. As in the noble silence, so too shall I and the Venerable ones be. If a *bhiksu*, in such an assembly, even after three announcements, remembers an offense and does not confess it, he incurs the offense of deliberate falsehood. Deliberate falsehood, the Buddha has declared, is an obstruction to the Path; confession brings peace."

[Five types of *uposatha* are defined. Five types of *Pratimoksa* recitation are described. Seven qualifications of a Vinaya-holder are listed. Procedures for handling various irregularities in the *uposatha* assembly are specified.]

## Part Four: Dispute Resolution and Karman Procedures

### Chapter 1: The Resolution of Disputes

The Buddha was in Sravasti. At that time the *bhiksus* frequently quarreled and litigated against one another. *Bhiksus* quarreled with *bhiksus*, *bhiksus* with *bhiksunis*, *bhiksunis* with *bhiksunis*, and *bhiksunis* with *bhiksus*.

At that time Channa sided with the *bhiksunis* against the *bhiksus*, causing disputes to arise where there were none, and to grow where they already existed. The Buddha rebuked this and established the seven methods of settling disputes.

"There are four kinds of disputes: (1) disputes about words (*vivada*), (2) disputes about admonition (*anuvaada*), (3) disputes about offenses (*apatti*), and (4) disputes about procedures (*kiccaa*)."

**The Seven Methods:**

1. **Settlement by Presence (*sammukha-vinaya*)**: The *Samgha* assembles, the disputants are present, and the Vinaya is applied face to face.

2. **Settlement by Recollection (*sati-vinaya*)**: When a *bhiksu* is repeatedly questioned about whether he remembers committing a serious offense and consistently answers "I do not remember," the *Samgha* may grant him the procedure of recollection, so that the questioner may no longer press the matter.

3. **Settlement by Non-Insanity (*amudha-vinaya*)**: When a *bhiksu* states that he does not remember committing a serious offense because he was formerly insane, disordered in mind, or ill, the *Samgha* may grant the procedure of non-insanity.

4. **Settlement by Confession (*pratijnaya*)**: A *bhiksu* confesses his offense before one or more *bhiksus*, who ask if he sees his offense and whether he wishes to repent, then admonish him not to repeat it.

5. **Settlement by Majority Decision (*yebhuyyasika*)**: When a dispute cannot be resolved by other means, voting by ballot is conducted. Detailed procedures are given for lawful and unlawful balloting, with ten criteria for each, and fourteen qualifications for the ballot distributor.

6. **Settlement by "Spreading Grass" (*trna-vastara*)**: When *bhiksus* have quarreled, exchanged insults, and committed physical and verbal offenses, and later wish to reconcile, they may, without investigating the original cause, prostrate before the *Samgha* and perform a collective confession. The origin of the dispute is not re-examined.

7. **Settlement According to the Original Statement (*tatkrtakarana*)**: When a *bhiksu*, repeatedly questioned, first denies committing a serious offense, then admits it, and then retracts his admission claiming he was joking, the *Samgha* judges him based on his original admission.

[Detailed *karman* formulas are given for each method, along with specifications for who may serve as arbitrators and what qualifications they must have.]

### Chapter 2: Karman Procedures

[This chapter covers the formal procedures (*karman*) for dealing with offenses:]

#### Probation (*Manaatta*)

"If a *bhiksu* commits a *samghavasiesa* offense and does not conceal it, the *Samgha* should, by a *jnapticaturthakarma*, grant him six nights of probation (*manaatta*)."

[Detailed procedures follow for: the request for probation, the *karman* formula, the conduct required during probation, what constitutes a break in probation, restarting probation, combining probations, and the rehabilitation (*abbhana*) ceremony.]

#### Penance (*Parivasa*)

[For a *bhiksu* who concealed a *samghavasiesa* offense, penance equal to the period of concealment must precede the six nights of probation.]

#### Rehabilitation (*Abbhana*)

[After completing probation, a *bhiksu* requests rehabilitation before an assembly of at least twenty *bhiksus*. The *karman* formula restores him to full standing in the community.]

#### Other Formal Acts

[Formal acts of censure, suspension, banishment, reconciliation, and the procedures for each are detailed, with *karman* formulas and the conditions under which they may be imposed and lifted.]

## Part Five: Miscellaneous Rules and Historical Accounts

### Chapter 1: Schism (*Samghabheda*)

At that time Devadatta thought for the third time: "I shall now split the *sramana* Gautama's *Samgha* and gain great fame. Everyone will say: 'The *sramana* Gautama has great supernatural power, yet Devadatta was able to split his *Samgha*!'" Having thought this, he told his followers: Asvagupta, Vasuvannin, Rohitaka, Rohitasa, Kokkalika, Khandadeva, and Samuddadatta. Among them, Samuddadatta was the most clever, and he asked Devadatta: "The *sramana* Gautama has great authority. How can his *Samgha* be split?" Devadatta answered: "I shall proclaim five rules within the *Samgha* to be observed for life: (1) not eating salt; (2) not eating butter or milk; (3) not eating fish or meat, saying that if one eats these, wholesome qualities will not arise; (4) always begging for food, saying that if one accepts invitations, wholesome qualities will not arise; (5) during the eight months of spring and summer, sitting in the open, and during the four months of winter, dwelling in a grass hut, saying that if one accepts a dwelling, wholesome qualities will not arise."

[On the fifteenth-day *uposatha*, Devadatta proposed these five rules, conducted a ballot, and five hundred *bhiksus* took the ballots. Only Ananda and one *srotapanna bhiksu* refused. Devadatta then held a separate *uposatha*. The Buddha sent Sariputra and Maudgalyayana to recover the five hundred *bhiksus*. Devadatta, imitating the Buddha's custom, asked Sariputra to teach the Dharma while he rested. He fell asleep. Maudgalyayana displayed supernatural powers while Sariputra taught the Dharma. The five hundred *bhiksus* attained the Dharma-eye and returned to the Buddha. Devadatta's companion Samuddadatta woke him; enraged, Devadatta fell into the great hell in his living body.]

The Buddha spoke this verse:

> "Do not imitate the great dragon; the great dragon cannot be imitated.
> By imitating the great dragon,
> One eats mud and meets a death of suffering."

The Buddha told *jataka* stories illustrating Devadatta's past enmity: the story of two elephants (one that washed its lotus roots and one that did not); the story of the elephant trainer whose jealous student tried to surpass him; the story of the archer Kuhali whose student destroyed five hundred bandits.

[Upali then asked the Buddha to define schism precisely. The Buddha explained: Four things constitute schism: proclaiming the five rules, conducting a ballot, collecting the ballots, and performing separate *Samgha* acts within a single boundary. Eight persons who cause schism with wrong understanding or wrong intention incur an entire *kalpa* in the great hell. Six persons who cause schism with right understanding or right intention do not incur such punishment.]

### Chapter 9: The Council of the Five Hundred

Shortly after the World-Honored One entered *nirvana*, Mahakasyapa was in Vaisali at the Kutagarasala beside the Monkey Pool with a great assembly of five hundred *bhiksus*, all *arhats* except for Ananda. He addressed the *bhiksus*:

"Once, when I was traveling from the country of the Pavarins toward the city of Kusinagara, between the two countries I heard that the World-Honored One had passed into *nirvana*. At that time my heart became confused and I could not compose myself. The village *bhiksus*, *bhiksunis*, *upasakas*, and *upasikas* were all stumbling and leaping, rolling on the ground, crying out in lamentation: 'The world is empty! The eye of the world is extinguished!' At that time Upananda, who had previously been there, stopped the people saying: 'That elder always said: "You should do this, you should not do that; you should learn this, you should not learn that." Now at last we are free from this suffering! We may do as we please without any further restraint. Why are you all weeping together?' When I heard these words, my grief doubled. Although the Buddha has entered *nirvana*, the Vinaya remains. We should encourage one another to compile it together, lest Upananda and his kind establish their own factions and destroy the true Dharma."

The *bhiksus* all agreed, and said to Kasyapa: "Ananda constantly attended the World-Honored One, is brilliant and learned, and holds the entire treasury of the Dharma. He should be included in the number of those who compile the Vinaya." Kasyapa said: "Ananda is still on the path of training (*sekha*). He may follow desire, anger, delusion, or fear. He should not be admitted."

[Ananda, who was in Vaisali teaching the four assemblies, was advised by a meditating *bhiksu* through a verse to practice diligently rather than merely teaching. Hearing also that Kasyapa would not include him in the compilation, Ananda practiced with great effort through the first, middle, and last watches of the night. At the very end of the last watch, exhausted and about to lie down, before his head touched the pillow, his defilements were suddenly and completely destroyed. The *bhiksus* informed Kasyapa, who then admitted him.]

Kasyapa thought: "Where are there sufficient food, beds, and requisites to support the compilation of the Vinaya?" He determined that only Rajagrha was suitable. He announced to the *Samgha*: "These five hundred *arhats* should go to Rajagrha for the rains retreat. No others may go!"

The five hundred *arhats* went to Rajagrha. During the first month of the rains they repaired dwellings and requisites. During the second month they practiced the *dhyanas* and liberations. In the third month they assembled together.

Kasyapa announced to the *Samgha*: "I shall now question Upali on the meaning of the Vinaya in the midst of the *Samgha*." Upali also announced: "I shall now answer Kasyapa's questions on the Vinaya."

Kasyapa asked Upali: "Where did the Buddha establish the first precept?" Upali said: "In Vaisali." "On account of whom?" "On account of Sudinna the Kalandaka son." "For what matter?" "For having sexual intercourse with his former wife." "Were there subsequent revisions?" "Yes. A *bhiksu* had intercourse with a female monkey."

[Kasyapa questioned Upali on each precept of the Vinaya in order: the second (*parajika* of theft, in Rajagrha, on account of Dhaniya), the third (killing, in Vaisali, on account of many *bhiksus*), and the fourth (false claims, in Vaisali, on account of the Vaggumuda River *bhiksus*). He continued through the entire Vinaya.]

Kasyapa then announced to the *Samgha*: "This is the *bhiksu* Vinaya; this is the *bhiksuni* Vinaya. Together they are called the Vinaya Pitaka."

Kasyapa then announced: "I shall now question Ananda on the meaning of the *sutras*." Ananda also announced his readiness to answer.

Kasyapa asked Ananda: "Where did the Buddha teach the Increasing by Ones Sutra? Where did he teach the Increasing by Tens Sutra, the Great Causes Sutra, the Samgiti Sutra, the Fruits of the Ascetic Life Sutra, the Brahmajala Sutra? Which *sutras* were spoken on account of *bhiksus*? Which on account of *bhiksunis*, *upasakas*, *upasikas*, gods, and goddesses?" Ananda answered each according to what the Buddha had taught.

After questioning on all the *sutras*, Kasyapa announced to the *Samgha*: "These are the long discourses; they are now compiled as one collection, called the *Dirgha Agama*. These are the discourses of medium length; they are now compiled as one collection, called the *Madhyama Agama*. These are the miscellaneous discourses taught for *bhiksus*, *bhiksunis*, *upasakas*, *upasikas*, gods, and goddesses; they are now compiled as one collection, called the *Samyukta Agama*. These discourses are arranged from one dharma up to eleven dharmas; they are now compiled as one collection, called the *Ekottara Agama*. The remaining miscellaneous teachings are now compiled as one collection, called the *Ksudraka*. Together these are called the Sutra Pitaka. We have now completed the compilation of the Dharma. From now on, what the Buddha did not establish should not be arbitrarily established; what was already established must not be violated. As the Buddha taught, so should we diligently study."

Ananda then said to Kasyapa: "I personally heard the Buddha say: 'After my *nirvana*, if you wish to abolish the minor and lesser precepts, you may do so.'" Kasyapa asked: "Which do you consider the minor and lesser precepts?" Ananda said: "I do not know." "Why do you not know?" "I did not ask the World-Honored One." "Why did you not ask?" "At that time the Buddha's body was in pain; I feared I would trouble him."

Kasyapa reproached Ananda: "You did not ask about this matter; this is a *duskrta*. You should see your offense and repent!" Ananda said: "Venerable one, it was not that I did not respect the precepts; I feared troubling the World-Honored One and therefore did not dare ask. I do not see any fault in this matter, but out of reverence for the Venerable one, I will now repent."

[Kasyapa further reproached Ananda for six *duskrta* offenses: (1) not asking about the minor precepts; (2) stepping on the Buddha's robe while sewing it; (3) three times requesting the Buddha to allow women to go forth; (4) not requesting the Buddha to remain for a *kalpa* when the Buddha hinted at the possibility; (5) not offering water when the Buddha asked three times; (6) allowing women to pay homage to the relics first. Ananda acknowledged each, saying he saw no fault but out of reverence for Kasyapa would repent.]

Kasyapa then argued: "If we take the *saiksa* rules as the minor precepts, others will say the *pratidesaniya* rules are also minor. If we include the *pratidesaniya*, others will say the *payantika* rules are also minor. If we include the *payantika*, others will say the *nihsargika-payantika* rules are also minor. This would quickly lead to four categories of exception, and nothing could be settled." Kasyapa further said: "If we who do not know what the minor precepts are arbitrarily abolish them, heterodox practitioners will say: 'The disciples of the *sramana* Gautama have a dharma like smoke; while the teacher was present, they all practiced what he established, but after his *nirvana*, they refused to follow it.'"

Kasyapa again announced to the *Samgha*: "We have now completed the compilation of the Dharma. What the Buddha did not establish should not be arbitrarily established; what was already established must not be violated. As the Buddha taught, so should we diligently study."

[The elder Purana arrived from the south with his retinue, having heard about the compilation. He agreed with all the compiled matters except seven points (internal storage of food, internal cooking, self-cooking, etc.) that the Buddha had temporarily permitted during a famine at Vaisali and later re-prohibited. Purana maintained these should remain permitted. Kasyapa argued that the Buddha, as master of the Dharma, had the right to permit and later prohibit. Purana said he would accept everything else but could not practice these seven rules. Kasyapa again announced the principle of not adding to or subtracting from the Buddha's teaching.]

The *bhiksu* Channa in Kausambi was persistently causing trouble. A *bhiksu* reported this to Kasyapa after the rains retreat. Kasyapa instructed Ananda: "Go to Kausambi and, by the words of the Buddha and the *Samgha*, impose the *brahmadanda* (penalty of silence) on him." Ananda went with five hundred *bhiksus*. Channa, hearing of their approach, asked: "Have you come to harm me?" Ananda said: "Rather, to benefit you." He explained the *brahmadanda*: "No *bhiksu*, *bhiksuni*, *upasaka*, or *upasika* may interact or speak with you." Channa, hearing this, fainted and fell to the ground, saying: "Is this not the same as killing me?" Ananda said: "I personally heard the Buddha say: 'You shall attain the Way through me.' Rise, and I will teach you the Dharma." Channa rose and listened. Ananda taught him various wonderful dharmas, and Channa was purified, separated from dust, and in all dharmas attained the eye of the Dharma.

At the compilation, the elder Ajnata Kaundinya was the first senior seat, Purana the second, Dhammiya the third, Dasaka Kasyapa the fourth, Bhadra Kasyapa the fifth, Mahakasyapa the sixth, Upali the seventh, and Aniruddha the eighth. There were five hundred *arhats*, no more and no less. Therefore it is called "The Council of the Five Hundred."

### Chapter 10: The Council of the Seven Hundred

One hundred years after the Buddha's *nirvana*, the *bhiksus* of Vaisali belonging to the Vajjian clan first began ten unlawful practices: (1) storing salt mixed with ginger overnight is pure; (2) eating with two fingers' width of food remaining is pure; (3) sitting down again to eat after finishing is pure; (4) crossing to another settlement to eat is pure; (5) mixing ghee, oil, honey, and rock sugar with curds is pure; (6) drinking *jalogi* wine is pure; (7) making a sitting cloth of any size one wishes is pure; (8) practicing what one formerly practiced as a layperson is pure; (9) seeking permission after the fact is pure; (10) accepting and storing gold, silver, and coins is pure.

Those *bhiksus* regularly, on the eighth, fourteenth, and fifteenth days, set out bowls full of water in crowded places, placed the bowls before them as a sign of auspiciousness, and demanded donations from passersby. Some gave; those who refused were ridiculed. Householders who disapproved said: "*Sramanas* of the Sakya clan should not accept and store gold, silver, and coins!"

At that time the elder Yasa Kakandakaputta, at the Kutagarasala beside the Monkey Pool, told the *bhiksus*: "Do not make such solicitations! I personally heard the Buddha say that unlawful solicitation and giving to unlawful solicitation both incur offense." He also told the laypersons not to give to such solicitations.

[The Vajjian *bhiksus*, after collecting gold and silver, offered Yasa a share. He refused. They imposed a *patisaraniya-kamma* (reconciliation act) on him. Yasa, accompanied by the assigned companion, went to the laypersons and explained the Buddha's teaching on the impropriety of accepting gold and silver, citing the Buddha's discourse to the minister Pearl Topknot in Rajagrha. The laypersons were convinced and offered Yasa their exclusive support. The Vajjian *bhiksus*, enraged, prepared to impose a *ukkhepaniya-kamma* (act of suspension) on Yasa, who flew away to the country of the Pavarins using supernatural powers.]

[Yasa gathered support: sixty forest-dwelling *bhiksus* in Pali (all *Arahants* and disciples of Ananda), then thirty more in Mathura, thirty more in Ahalabhi, the elder Sambhuta on Mount Ahoganga, and finally the elder Revata in Kausambi, who had attained the *samadhi* of loving-kindness and had a great following. All agreed to join in suppressing the ten unlawful practices.]

[The Vajjian *bhiksus* attempted to bribe Revata through his attendant Dhammika, but Revata refused. Revata decided to go to Vaisali and settle the matter there. In Vaisali, the elder Sabbakamin (whose ordination age was 136 years, the most senior in all of Jambudvipa, an *arhat* and disciple of Ananda) was the presiding elder.]

[Revata and Sabbakamin spent the night together in meditation. In the late night, Sabbakamin asked Revata what meditation he had practiced; Revata said loving-kindness. Sabbakamin said this was a "coarse" meditation. Sabbakamin had practiced the emptiness meditation. Revata said: "This is what the great ones practice; why? Because the *samadhi* of emptiness is the dharma of the great person."]

A formal assembly was convened. Each side chose four representatives. The eight arbitrators went to the garden donated by the laywoman Viralaya. Revata questioned Sabbakamin on each of the ten points:

"Is it pure to store salt mixed with ginger overnight?" "It is not pure." "Where was this prohibited?" "In Rajagrha." "On account of whom?" "A forest-dwelling *bhiksu*." "What offense?" "Stored food, *payantika*."

Revata said: "This is the Dharma, this is the Vinaya, this is the Buddha's teaching. What the Vajjian *bhiksus* practice is not the Dharma, not the Vinaya, not the Buddha's teaching. Place the first ballot."

[Each of the ten points was similarly examined and rejected. The ballots were placed one through ten. Then the eight returned to the full assembly, and Revata repeated each question publicly before the entire *Samgha*.]

Revata then announced: "We have now completed the discussion of the Vinaya. What the Buddha did not establish should not be arbitrarily established; what was already established must not be violated. As the Buddha taught, so should we diligently study."

At the time of this Vinaya discussion, the first senior seat was the elder Sabbakamin, with 136 years of ordination; the second was the elder Revata, with 120 years; the third was the elder Sambhuta; the fourth was the elder Yasa, both with 110 years. There were seven hundred *arhats*, no more and no less. Therefore it is called "The Council of the Seven Hundred."
