# 佛說解節經
## Sūtra on Resolving the Knots

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0677

Translated from the Chinese. Chen dynasty, translated by Paramārtha (真諦)

---

### Chapter One: The Inexpressible and Non-Duality

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha, the Bhagavat, was dwelling at Vulture Peak Mountain in the city of Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of ninety-nine thousand bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats whose outflows were exhausted, whose tasks were accomplished, who had cast off their heavy burdens, who had realized their own benefit, who had completely exhausted the fetters of existence, whose minds were well liberated, who were well endowed with mastery, and who were well endowed with śamatha and vipaśyanā. Their names were: Ājñāta-Kauṇḍinya of Pure Livelihood, and so forth, up to Subhūti who dwells in the samādhi of araṇa. Also present were a great assembly of thirty-six thousand bhikṣuṇīs, with Mahāprajāpatī and others, up to the bhikṣuṇī Bhadra-Kapilā, as the foremost. Also present were immeasurable and countless upāsakas and upāsikās, with King Bimbisāra as the foremost. Also present were bodhisattva-mahāsattvas numbering immeasurable hundreds of thousands, the assemblies of bodhisattvas of this Fortunate Kalpa, some dwelling in this land and some having come from other realms. The bodhisattva Maitreya, the one-birth-supplement, the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, and others were the foremost. All had penetrated the deeply profound dharma-nature, were gentle and easy to guide, practiced equality well, cultivated the bodhisattva path, and were true good teachers of all sentient beings. They had attained the unhindered dhāraṇī, turned the wheel of non-retrogression, and had already made offerings to immeasurable buddhas. Such assemblies had all gathered together.

At that time the bodhisattva Yathābhūta-śruta asked the bodhisattva who Resolves the Knots of Profound Meaning: "Son of the Buddha! All dharmas are non-dual. 'All dharmas are non-dual': what does this mean?"

The bodhisattva who Resolves the Knots of Profound Meaning said: "Good man! All dharmas do not go beyond these two, namely, the conditioned and the unconditioned. The conditioned is neither conditioned nor not-conditioned. The unconditioned is neither not-unconditioned nor conditioned."

The bodhisattva Yathābhūta-śruta asked: "Son of the Buddha! How is the conditioned neither conditioned nor not-conditioned? And how is the unconditioned neither not-unconditioned nor conditioned?"

The bodhisattva who Resolves the Knots of Profound Meaning said: "Good man! That which is conditioned: this is the correct teaching, the words and phrases of the Great Teacher. If it is the correct teaching, the words and phrases of the Great Teacher, then it is a designation established by the world, arisen from conceptual discrimination. If this worldly designation arises from conceptual discrimination, then, because of the various discriminations and the statements themselves, it is entirely unestablished. Therefore it is 'not conditioned.'

"Good man! That which is unconditioned falls within the scope of verbal teaching. If there is a dharma apart from both the conditioned and the unconditioned, then that dharma is just as it is, just as it is. If it is so, then can the Great Teacher's verbal teaching be devoid of meaning?"

"It is not devoid of meaning."

"If it has meaning, what is the characteristic of that meaning? It is the inexpressible essence, realized only through the non-discriminating knowing and seeing of the noble ones. In order to enable others to understand this inexpressible essence, the Great Teacher proclaims this verbal teaching, saying 'This dharma is conditioned.'

"Good man! That which is unconditioned: this is the correct teaching, the words and phrases of the Great Teacher. If it is the correct teaching, the words and phrases of the Great Teacher, then it is a designation established by the world, arisen from conceptual discrimination. If this worldly designation arises from conceptual discrimination, then, because of the various discriminations and the statements themselves, it is entirely unestablished. Therefore it is 'neither not-unconditioned.'

"Good man! That which is conditioned falls within the scope of verbal teaching. If there is a dharma apart from both the unconditioned and the conditioned, then that dharma is just as it is, just as it is. If it is so, then can the Great Teacher's verbal teaching be devoid of meaning?"

"It is not devoid of meaning."

"If it has meaning, what is the characteristic of that meaning? It is the inexpressible essence, realized only through the non-discriminating knowing and seeing of the noble ones. In order to enable others to understand this inexpressible essence, the Great Teacher proclaims this verbal teaching, saying 'This dharma is unconditioned.'

"Good man! It is like a skilled illusionist and his apprentice who, at a crossroads, take grass, leaves, wood, stones, and the like, heap them in one place, and then manifest various illusory spectacles: elephants and elephant troops, horses and cavalry, chariots, infantry, maṇi jewels, pearls, coral, gemstones, treasuries, and the like. If there are people who are infants, ordinary beings, foolish and of wrong understanding, unable to discern the illusory nature of the grass and other materials, then when they see and hear, they think: 'These elephants, horses, four kinds of troops, and treasuries truly exist.' Having seen and heard, to the extent of their ability they cling to what they see and hear, and say: 'This is real; what differs from this is not real.' Such people should reconsider carefully.

"But if there are people who are not infants, not ordinary beings, and not of foolish and wrong understanding, who recognize the illusory nature of the grass and other materials, then when they see and hear, they think: 'There are no such things as elephants, horses, and treasuries.' Having seen and heard, to the extent of their ability they do not cling to what they see and hear, and say: 'What I think is real; what differs from this is not real.' Though they follow worldly speech, it is to reveal the true meaning. Such people do not need to reconsider.

"Good man! Likewise, these infants and ordinary beings have not attained the transcendent wisdom of the noble ones' true suchness, and they do not recognize the inexpressible essence of all dharmas. When they see and hear the conditioned and unconditioned among all dharmas, they think: 'These conditioned and unconditioned dharmas truly exist. Why? Because they can be seen and known.' Having seen and heard, to the extent of their ability they cling to what they see and hear, and say as they have seen and heard: 'This is real; what differs from this is not real.' Such people should reconsider carefully.

"But if there are people who are not infants and ordinary beings, who have already seen the truth and attained the transcendent wisdom of the noble ones' true suchness, who have already recognized the inexpressible essence of all dharmas: when they see and hear, they think: 'The conditioned and unconditioned among all dharmas, as they are seen and known, are not truly existent. They are only provisional appearances arisen from conceptual discrimination, like illusory transformations that deceive the ordinary mind. Within them arise the names "conditioned" and "unconditioned" and other various names.' Such people, in accordance with what they see and hear, do not develop attachment and do not say: 'This is real; what differs from this is not real.' Though they follow worldly speech, it is to reveal the true meaning. Such people do not need to reconsider.

"Good man! In this way, noble ones, through their noble knowing and seeing, have already realized the inexpressible essence. In order to enable others to see the true nature of dharmas, they speak these teaching-phrases, saying 'This is conditioned,' 'This is unconditioned,' and so forth."

At that time the bodhisattva who Resolves the Knots of Profound Meaning spoke in verse:

> The Buddha teaches the dharma beyond words,
> non-dual, beyond the reach of ordinary minds.
> The foolish are confused within it,
> clinging to duality and engaged in idle theories.
> Through indecisive and wrong decisions,
> they constantly revolve through the realms of existence.
> The wise, leaving behind what is seen and heard,
> discern the true meaning within.

### Chapter Two: Beyond the Domain of Discursive Thought

At that time the bodhisattva Dharmodgata addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Departing from this sahā world and going east to the farthest extent of the eastern direction, past worlds numbering seventy-seven Ganges-sands, there is a world called Good Renown, where there is a buddha named Vast Good Renown Sugata Abode. At one time I went to that buddha's presence, and there I saw a certain area of land where seventy-seven thousand outsiders, with their teachers at the head, had gathered and were seated, intending to consider the true nature of all dharmas. Those outsiders pondered, measured, analyzed, and established the true nature of all dharmas. Relying on their own studies, they sought the true nature, but none could find it. They gave rise to various attachments, contradicted and disputed one another, even to the point of harmful words. With the swords and clubs of their mouths they wounded and defamed one another, and then they each dispersed. Seeing this, I had this thought: 'How rare! How rare! The World-Honored Buddhas who appear in the world! Through the Buddha's appearance, the profound characteristics of all dharmas, which lie beyond the domain of discursive thought, are penetrated, realized, and fully revealed.'"

When the bodhisattva had finished speaking, the Buddha then said: "So it is, Dharmottara! So it is. The true nature is beyond the domain of discursive thought. I have realized it, and for others I explain it, establishing the correct teaching, revealing and making manifest, rendering its meaning accessible and easy. Why? What I teach as true reality is only what the noble ones themselves have directly witnessed. If it were within the domain of discursive thought of ordinary beings, then self and others alike could witness it. Dharmottara! For this reason, you should know that the true nature is beyond the entire domain of discursive thought.

"Furthermore, Dharmottara! What I teach as true reality is not the domain of characteristics and mental activity. All discursive thought operates within the domain of characteristics and mental activity. For this reason, you should know that the true nature is beyond the domain of discursive thought.

"Furthermore, Dharmottara! What I teach as true reality is inexpressible. All discursive thought operates only through verbal expression. Therefore you should know that the true nature is beyond the domain of discursive thought.

"Furthermore, Dharmottara! What I teach as true reality transcends the four matters, namely seeing, hearing, awareness, and knowing. All discursive thought operates by conditioning these four matters.

"Furthermore, Dharmottara! What I teach as the true characteristic is free from all dispute. All discursive thought belongs to the domain of dispute. For this reason, you should know that the true nature is beyond the domain of discursive thought.

"Dharmottara! It is as if a person, throughout their entire life, ate only bitter flavors. That person then being able, through discursive thought, inference, or recollection, to apprehend the sweet taste of honey and such things: there is no such possibility.

"Furthermore, it is as if a person who constantly delights in the objects of desire, scorched and burned by the heat of desire and craving, were then able, through discursive thought, inference, or recollection, to apprehend the inward bliss of seclusion that does not depend on sense-objects: there is no such possibility.

"Furthermore, it is as if a person who constantly delights in arguments, wrong discussions, and playful chatter were then able, through discursive thought, inference, or recollection, to apprehend the noble silence of concentration: there is no such possibility.

"Furthermore, it is as if a person who constantly delights in and constantly engages in seeing, hearing, awareness, and knowing were then able, through discursive thought, inference, or recollection, to apprehend the place that transcends these four matters, the extinction of the view of self-identity, which is parinirvāṇa: there is no such possibility.

"Furthermore, Dharmottara! It is as if a person who, through constantly hoarding wealth, delights in waging war, were then able, through discursive thought, inference, or recollection, to apprehend the Northern Continent of Uttarakuru, where there is no 'mine,' no hoarding, no mutual conflict, and where this constitutes the happiness of the present life: there is no such possibility.

"Dharmottara! Likewise, such people, being within the domain of discursive thought, could then be able to consider, infer, and recollect that which is beyond the domain of discursive thought: there is no such possibility."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, he spoke again in verse:

> The self-realized dharma without characteristics,
> beyond words, transcending the four matters,
> free from dispute, the universal characteristic of dharmas,
> is beyond the entire domain of discursive thought.

### Chapter Three: Beyond One and Different

At that time the bodhisattva Viśuddhimati addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! This is a correct teaching, exceedingly profound and rare. As the World-Honored One has said, the principle of true reality is subtle and exceedingly profound, difficult to penetrate, namely, it transcends the characteristics of oneness and difference.

"World-Honored One! At one time I saw a certain area of land where a great assembly of bodhisattvas who were cultivating the practice of awakening, being at the stage of aspiration and delight, had gathered and were seated, intending to consider whether the true nature of all dharmas is one with or different from conditioned dharmas. At that time, among the assembly, some bodhisattvas said: 'The true nature is not different from conditioned things.' Other bodhisattvas said: 'The true nature is not one with conditioned things.' Still other bodhisattvas, giving rise to doubt and confusion, not trusting either oneness or difference, said: 'Among these views of oneness and difference, who speaks truly? Who speaks falsely? Which is right practice? Which is wrong practice? Should one hold to oneness? Should one hold to difference?'

"World-Honored One! Seeing this, I had this thought: 'These good men are infants, foolish, without awareness, without understanding, and do not practice in accordance with principle. Why? These good men have not yet penetrated the subtle and exceedingly profound true reality and its relationship with conditioned things and the like, which transcends the characteristics of oneness and difference.'"

When the bodhisattva had finished speaking, the Buddha then said: "So it is, Viśuddhimati! So it is. These good men are infants, foolish, without awareness, without understanding, and do not practice in accordance with principle. The Tathāgata has penetrated the subtle and exceedingly profound true reality and its relationship with conditioned things and the like, which transcends the characteristics of oneness and difference. Why? Viśuddhimati! If one maintains this position and cultivates the contemplation of true reality based on conditioned dharmas, being able to penetrate and realize the principle of true suchness: there is no such possibility. Why? Viśuddhimati! If true suchness were not different from the characteristics of conditioned things, then all ordinary beings should be able to see true suchness. Furthermore, all sentient beings, while still at the ordinary stage, should attain supreme nirvāṇa of peace. Furthermore, all sentient beings, while at the ordinary stage, should also be able to attain supreme awakening.

"If the characteristic of true suchness were different from the characteristic of conditioned things, then all noble ones who have already seen true suchness should not be able to subdue and extinguish the characteristics of conditioned things. Because they do not subdue and extinguish the characteristics of conditioned things, even though they see the noble truths, they cannot be liberated from the bondage of characteristics. If they cannot be liberated from the bondage of characteristics, they also cannot be liberated from the bondage of gross obstructions. If they cannot be liberated from these two kinds of bondage, they cannot attain supreme nirvāṇa of peace without remainder, nor can they attain supreme awakening.

"Viśuddhimati! Because ordinary beings do not see true suchness, while at the ordinary stage they do not attain supreme nirvāṇa of peace, nor can they attain supreme awakening. For this reason, the proposition that true suchness is one with conditioned things is not tenable. If someone says 'True suchness is not different from the characteristics of conditioned things,' then for this reason, you should know that person does not practice in accordance with principle.

"Furthermore, Viśuddhimati! All noble ones, having seen true suchness, are already able to subdue and extinguish the characteristics of conditioned dharmas, not unable to do so. They have already been liberated from the bondage of all characteristics and of gross delusions, not without liberation. Through this twofold liberation, they have already attained supreme nirvāṇa of peace, and they have even attained supreme awakening. Therefore, the proposition that true suchness is different from the characteristics of conditioned things is not tenable. If someone says 'True suchness is different from the characteristics of conditioned things,' then for this reason, you should know that person does not practice in accordance with principle.

"Furthermore, Viśuddhimati! If true suchness were not different from the characteristics of conditioned things, then just as the characteristics of conditioned things fall into the characteristics of delusion, so too the characteristic of true suchness should fall into the characteristics of delusion. Furthermore, Viśuddhimati! If the characteristic of true suchness were different from the characteristics of conditioned things, then true suchness would not be the universal characteristic of all conditioned things. Viśuddhimati! Since true suchness does not fall into the characteristics of delusion, and since it is indeed the universal characteristic of all conditioned things, for these reasons, the proposition that true suchness is both one with and different from conditioned things is not tenable. If someone says 'True suchness is both one with and different from conditioned things,' then for this reason, you should know that person does not practice in accordance with principle.

"Furthermore, Viśuddhimati! If true suchness were not different from the characteristics of conditioned things, then just as the characteristic of true reality is universally undifferentiated among all conditioned things, so too the characteristics of conditioned things should be universally undifferentiated. Therefore, one who cultivates contemplative practice should not, within conditioned things, go beyond seeing, hearing, awareness, and knowing to cultivate the superior contemplation of true reality. Furthermore, if the characteristic of true suchness were different from the characteristics of conditioned things, then for this reason, the mere selflessness and essencelessness of all conditioned things should not be the true reality. Furthermore, at one time the pure and impure categories would each have separate characteristics.

"Viśuddhimati! Because the characteristics of conditioned things are only differentiated, not universal, because contemplative practitioners, within conditioned things, do go beyond seeing, hearing, awareness, and knowing to cultivate the superior contemplation of true reality, because the selflessness and essencelessness revealed in conditioned things is the true reality, and because the pure and impure categories are not at one time each separately characterized: for these reasons, the proposition that true suchness is both one with and different from conditioned things is not tenable. If someone says 'True suchness is both one with and different from conditioned things,' you should know that person does not practice in accordance with principle.

"Viśuddhimati! It is like the white color of a conch shell: it cannot be established as either one with or different from the conch. The red color of gold being neither one with nor different from gold is also like this. It is like the beautiful sound of a vīṇā: it cannot be established as either one with or different from the vīṇā. It is like aloeswood, whose fragrance is delightful: it cannot be established as either one with or different from the aloeswood. And like black pepper, whose taste is pungent and hot: it cannot be established as either one with or different from the pepper. The astringent taste of harītakī is also like this. It is like cotton, whose touch is soft: it cannot be established as either one with or different from the cotton. Butter and ghee being neither one nor different is also like this. It is like the truth that all conditioned suffering, all conditioned impermanence, and all dharmas are without self: these truths of suffering and the rest cannot be established as either one with or different from the dharmas. It is also like craving, hatred, delusion, pride, and the rest, whose characteristics are non-tranquil: they cannot be established as either one with or different from them. Viśuddhimati! In the same way, true suchness and all conditioned things cannot be established as either one or different."

"Viśuddhimati! Such true suchness is subtle and exceedingly profound, difficult to penetrate. I have realized it, and for others I explain it, establishing the correct teaching, revealing and making manifest, rendering its meaning accessible and easy."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, he spoke again in verse:

> True reality and conditioned dharmas
> have no characteristic of being one, different, or both.
> Those who hold to oneness, difference, or both
> are said to practice not in accordance with principle.
> Those who cultivate śamatha
> and vipaśyanā
> are able to be liberated
> from the bonds of characteristics, delusion, and gross obstructions.

### Chapter Four: One Taste

At that time the Buddha addressed Subhūti: "Subhūti! Do you see and know how many sentient beings in the realm of beings have overweening pride and, through this pride, claim their own attainment? Furthermore, do you see and know how many sentient beings in the realm of beings are without overweening pride and do not, through pride, claim their own attainment?"

Subhūti said: "World-Honored One! I see and know that few sentient beings in the realm of beings are without overweening pride and do not, through pride, claim their own attainment. World-Honored One! I see and know that immeasurable, countless, and inexpressible sentient beings in the realm of beings have overweening pride and, through this pride, claim their own attainment.

"World-Honored One! Furthermore, at one time I was dwelling in a remote forest hermitage. Many bhikṣus had gathered together in a hermitage not far from me. One afternoon I saw this great assembly gather together, and each, according to the various dharma-characteristics they had realized, spoke of their own practice and claimed their own attainment. Some bhikṣus, through realizing the aggregates, claimed their attainment: some had realized and seen the aggregates; some had realized and seen the characteristics of the aggregates; some had realized the arising of the aggregates; some had realized the transformation of the aggregates; some had realized and seen the cessation of the aggregates; some had realized the path to the cessation of the aggregates. Just as there were six kinds of realization regarding the aggregates, so too: some bhikṣus, through realizing and seeing the sense-bases, claimed their own attainment, up to the cessation of the sense-bases and the path to their cessation. Some bhikṣus, through realizing and seeing dependent arising, claimed their own attainment, up to the cessation of dependent arising and the path to its cessation. Some bhikṣus realized and saw nutriment. Some bhikṣus realized and saw the Four Noble Truths. Some bhikṣus realized and saw the elements, the differentiation of elements, and the various kinds of elements, up to the cessation of elements and the path to their cessation. Some bhikṣus realized and saw the foundations of mindfulness, the characteristics of the foundations of mindfulness, their antidotes, the path of their antidotes, and their cultivation: realizing the arising of a foundation of mindfulness not yet arisen, realizing the stabilization of a foundation of mindfulness already arisen, its non-forgetting, its increase, and its full perfection, and claiming their own attainment. Just as with the foundations of mindfulness, so too with right effort, the bases of spiritual power, the faculties, powers, factors of awakening, and the noble path: realizing the characteristics of the noble path, its antidotes, the path of its antidotes, and its cultivation, realizing the arising of a path-factor not yet arisen, the stabilization of a path-factor already arisen, its non-forgetting, its increase, and its full perfection, and claiming their own attainment.

"World-Honored One! Seeing this, I had this thought: 'The way these elders, according to the various dharma-characteristics they have realized, claim their own attainment: these elders have overweening pride. Through this pride they claim their own attainment; this is certain.' Why? The dharmas they claim to have realized and seen as described show that these people have not yet been able to discern the one-taste true suchness that pervades all things.

"World-Honored One! As the World-Honored One has said: 'The one-taste true reality pervading all things is subtle and exceedingly profound, difficult to penetrate.' These words are exceedingly rare, an incomparable teaching.

"World-Honored One! If even bhikṣus who diligently cultivate contemplative practice within the World-Honored One's correct teaching find the one-taste true reality pervading all things difficult to penetrate, how much less can the outsiders, who are outside the correct teaching, realize and know the one-taste true reality?"

The Buddha said: "So it is, Subhūti! So it is. The subtle, the most subtle; the profound, the most profound; the hard to see, the hardest to see: the one-taste true reality pervading all things. I have realized it, and for others I explain it, establishing the correct teaching, revealing and making manifest, rendering its meaning accessible and easy. Why? Subhūti! Within the five aggregates, the purified domain is what I call true reality. Subhūti! Within the twelve sense-bases, the twelve links of dependent arising, the four kinds of nutriment, the Four Noble Truths, the elements, the foundations of mindfulness, right effort, the bases of spiritual power, the faculties, powers, factors of awakening, and the eightfold noble path, the purified domain is what I call true reality. This purified domain is equally one-tasted, without any differentiated characteristic, throughout all the aggregates. Just as in the aggregates, so too up to the path-factors: the purified domain is equally one-tasted, without any differentiation. Subhūti! For this reason, you should know that the one-taste true suchness pervades all things.

"Furthermore, Subhūti! If a practicing bhikṣu has already penetrated the true suchness of one aggregate, its selflessness of persons and dharmas, then it is not necessary to further contemplate the true suchness of each of the remaining aggregates. Regarding the twelve sense-bases, the twelve links of dependent arising, the four kinds of nutriment, the Four Noble Truths, the elements, the foundations of mindfulness, right effort, the bases of spiritual power, the faculties, powers, factors of awakening, and the eightfold noble path: if one has already penetrated the true suchness of one of these, its selflessness of persons and dharmas, it is not necessary to further contemplate the true suchness of the remaining path-factors. Apart from the non-discriminating subsequent wisdom, there is no separate contemplation that can follow the truth of true suchness in observing the one-taste true reality pervading all other dharmas. Only through the non-discriminating subsequent wisdom, following the prior non-discriminating wisdom, does one contemplate the one-taste true reality of all dharmas, retaining it until the attainment is won. Subhūti! For this reason, you should know that the principle of true reality pervades all things and has only a single-taste characteristic.

"Furthermore, Subhūti! Just as the aggregates each have differentiated characteristics, and just as the twelve sense-bases, the twelve links of dependent arising, the four kinds of nutriment, the Four Noble Truths, the elements, the foundations of mindfulness, right effort, the bases of spiritual power, the faculties, powers, factors of awakening, and the eightfold noble path each have differentiated characteristics: if the true suchness of all dharmas, the selflessness of persons and dharmas, each had differentiated characteristics, then the true suchness of all dharmas, the selflessness of persons and dharmas, would not be genuine. It would have arisen from causes. If it arises from causes, then it is conditioned. If it is conditioned, then it is not true reality. If it is not true reality, one should seek yet another true reality beyond it. Subhūti! Because this true reality does not arise from causes, is not conditioned, is not untrue, and within it one need not seek a separate true reality. Why? This dharma is permanent. Whether buddhas appear in the world or do not, the dharma-nature, the dharma-realm, and the dharma's abiding all remain permanently. Subhūti! For this reason, you should know that the one-taste true reality is equal throughout all things.

"Subhūti! It is as if, amid the various colors with their differentiations and mutual distinctions, the characteristic of empty space within those colors is without differentiation, without transformation, and has one and the same taste in all places. In the same way, all dharmas are each different from one another. You should know that the one-taste true suchness amid all dharmas is equally without differentiation, and it is also like this."

At that time, when the World-Honored One had finished speaking this sūtra, he spoke again in verse:

> The universal characteristic of dharmas is one-tasted;
> all buddhas teach it as equality.
> Those who hold to differentiations therein
> are people of overweening pride.
> The path that goes against the current of birth and death
> is subtle, profound, and hard to see.
> Because of the covering of desire, defilement, and delusion,
> ordinary people cannot attain it.

At that time the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara placed his right knee on the ground, pressed his palms together reverently, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Today, from the Buddha, I have heard this profound dharma of Resolving the Knots, an experience unprecedented. I receive it upon my forehead and reverently uphold it. World-Honored One! What should this sūtra be called? How should it be upheld?"

The Buddha addressed the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara: "This sūtra is called 'The Correct Teaching of Definitive Meaning.' It is also called 'The Correct Teaching on the Domain of True Reality and Wisdom.' It is also called 'The Correct Teaching on the Foundation of the Stages and Pāramitās.' You should uphold it in this way."

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, eighty thousand bodhisattvas all attained the great vehicle's samādhi of awesome virtue. Immeasurable and boundless assemblies of bodhisattvas attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas in regard to the unborn dharma. Countless sentient beings had their minds liberated from the various streams. Countless sentient beings gave rise to faith and delight in the dharma of the great vehicle.
