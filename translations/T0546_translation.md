# 佛說金耀童子經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Boy Golden Radiance

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0546

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Tiānxīzāi (天息災)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the state of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove at Anāthapiṇḍada's Park.

At that time, the World-Honored One, at the time for meals, put on his robes and, respectfully surrounded by the bhikṣus, entered the great city of Śrāvastī to beg for food in sequence. A certain brahmin came out from Śrāvastī and, meeting the World-Honored One, gazed long at his bearing and then spoke these words of praise: "Gautama! Your face is supreme, golden in color and solemnly beautiful."

The World-Honored One affirmed: "So it is! So it is! The merit I have cultivated has brought me this reward."

The brahmin said: "Gautama! I too have merit in this present life. In my household, a boy has been born whose golden radiance and beautiful bearing resemble yours, Gautama. This has never been seen before.

"Furthermore, Gautama, at the time when the boy was first born, there were other marvelous and auspicious events. At the moment of his birth, his mind was tranquil and all his faculties were keen and bright. In his courtyard, lotus flowers suddenly sprang up. The room was filled with a celestial fragrance, always sweet and abundant. All sentient beings were universally delighted.

"Furthermore, Gautama, these marvels are still not the most extraordinary thing. When the boy was first born, champaka trees sprang up everywhere. These trees bore wondrous champaka flowers of celestial purple-gold color.

"Furthermore, Gautama, even this auspicious sign is not yet the most remarkable. When the boy was first born, celestial golden plates appeared of their own accord, filled with celestial food and drink of a hundred thousand million kinds. No matter how much was eaten from them, they were never exhausted.

"Furthermore, Gautama, even this miraculous response is not yet the most extraordinary. When the boy was first born, his mouth uttered sounds saying that a buddha, a world-honored one, and arhats have appeared in the world, and even in all his movements and pauses his constant thought was of them." After the brahmin had described all these auspicious events of the boy, he said to the Buddha: "Please come to my home to see the boy." The World-Honored One silently consented and went to the brahmin's dwelling. As he was about to enter, an upāsaka said to the Buddha: "Do not enter this house! That brahmin has no faith or reverence for the Buddha-Dharma." The World-Honored One replied: "This brahmin also possesses the root of faith." Having answered the upāsaka, the World-Honored One entered the brahmin's house and saw the boy.

When the boy saw the World-Honored One, he immediately went to take refuge and prostrated fully. The Buddha then pronounced a blessing. In the same way, the bhikṣus also came from the Buddha's side and saw the boy. After the Buddha had pronounced the blessing, he returned to the monastery with the bhikṣus.

As the boy gradually grew up, the king of the state of Śrāvastī, Prasenajit, heard that the brahmin had such meritorious qualities and had fathered this remarkable son. He dispatched envoys bearing abundant flower garlands, sandalwood, precious incense, and other offerings to the brahmin's household, surrounding the boy as they extended the royal invitation. The boy replied: "Wait until I have first gone to the Jeta Grove to pay homage to the World-Honored One, and then I shall enter Śrāvastī to see King Prasenajit."

After the envoys had returned and fully reported these words, King Prasenajit said: "I too shall go to the Jeta Grove to pay homage to the World-Honored One and to see the boy."

At that time, the boy set out for the Jeta Grove. Along the way he met a brahmin who asked: "Where are you going?" The boy answered: "I wish to go to the Jeta Grove to pay homage to the World-Honored One."

The brahmin scolded the boy: "How can one born of a renowned brahmin family deliberately go to see a śramaṇa?"

The boy replied: "If you obtained a treasury of great jewels, would you not want to carry the jewels home? When good fortune comes before you, would you strike it away with a stick?" Having answered, the boy continued on to the Jeta Grove. Arriving at the World-Honored One's abode, he paid homage at the Buddha's feet, sat facing him, and listened to the Dharma. Wondrous lotus flowers from his original birth appeared and grew within the Jeta Grove, their fragrance permeating everything far and wide. Wisdom suddenly arose: "I shall now offer these lotus flowers to the World-Honored One." He further reflected: "When I was first born, champaka trees appeared, which the world had never seen before." At the moment of this thought, champaka trees of their own accord sprang up, bearing champaka flowers of celestial purple-gold color. The boy then cupped champaka flowers in his hands and scattered them upon the World-Honored One. The flowers he scattered remained upon the Buddha's body, adorning it. Some came to rest upon the Buddha's crown, some upon the Buddha's breast, some at the Buddha's feet. Some formed garlands and robes, and in this way he made various kinds of offerings.

The king was astonished and asked the boy: "How do you make offerings with such spiritual power?" The boy answered: "I have created all these adornments within the Jeta Grove."

Then the boy gave rise to supreme wisdom: "My champaka trees, following my aspiration, produce champaka flowers. The flowers grow upon the tree trunk, upon the fruit, upon the branches, and upon the leaves. Some champaka flowers also appear suspended in the empty sky. And in the empty sky above the Jeta Grove there appear golden jeweled bells and chimes."

The boy then paid homage at the World-Honored One's feet and said: "World-Honored One! Please accept my offering of food today for the World-Honored One, all the bhikṣus, the king, and his attendants; may all accept my provisions." The World-Honored One silently accepted the invitation. When the wooden gong was struck and the time had come, the World-Honored One calmly took his seat, and the bhikṣus, the king, and his ministers took their seats in order below him. At that time, the boy gave rise to supreme, subtle reflective wisdom and recalled: "When I was born, golden plates appeared, filled with celestial food. May they now appear before me, that I may bring them to make offerings to the Buddha." Having formed this thought, the golden plates from his birth appeared in accordance with his mind, filled with celestial flavors. The boy then personally took the golden plates and food and made offerings.

When the World-Honored One, the bhikṣus, the king, and the attendants had eaten and were satisfied, the boy Golden Radiance was overjoyed. He paid homage at the World-Honored One's feet and aroused the aspiration for supreme, perfect enlightenment (anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi): "Hereafter may my roots of merit increase and grow. Having sincerely made vows and broadly practiced the giving of the Dharma to rescue sentient beings, only afterward shall I attain buddhahood. Those not yet rescued, I shall rescue. Those not yet at ease, I shall bring ease. Those not yet at peace, I shall bring peace."

At that time, the World-Honored One, because of the boy's arousal of aspiration, taught in sequence the forms of the hells: "That is to say, Avīci Hell, Blister Hell, Cracking Blister Hell, Aṭaṭa Hell, Hahava Hell, Huhuva Hell, Blue Lotus Hell, Red Lotus Hell, Great Red Lotus Hell. Having emerged from these, one enters the eight hot hells. All in sequence are induced by deluded karma. If there is one with wisdom who teaches my Dharma of liberation, those beings shall find coolness."

At that time, as the World-Honored One spoke these words, light of four colors, blue, yellow, red, and white, issued from his mouth. Some of this light went upward into the sky and some went downward into the hells, illuminating the hells of Reviving, Black Cord, Crushing, Wailing, Great Wailing, Blazing Heat, Extreme Blazing Heat, and Avīci, as well as the Blister Hell, Cracking Blister Hell, Aṭaṭa Hell, Hahava Hell, Huhuva Hell, Blue Lotus Hell, Red Lotus Hell, and Great Red Lotus Hell. Where the light went to the hot hells, those beings found coolness. Where it entered the cold hells, those beings found warmth. And those beings gave rise to a superior aspiration: "How did we come to be in this place? When our lives end, may we be reborn in other destinies." Having aroused this aspiration, the World-Honored One produced a transformed light and sent it forth, enabling those beings to see. Having seen it, they thought: "After our lives end in this place, we shall certainly not be reborn in any evil destination. Never before have we seen beings in this place receive such effortless, supreme light, which engenders faith. When our hellish karma is exhausted, each of us shall be born among humans and devas, truly receiving that reward." Where the light illuminated the upper realms of the Four Great King Heaven, Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, Yāma Heaven, Tuṣita Heaven, Nirmāṇarati Heaven, Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven, Brahma Assembly Heaven, Brahma Minister Heaven, Great Brahma Heaven, Limited Light Heaven, Immeasurable Light Heaven, Supreme Light Purity Heaven, Limited Purity Heaven, Immeasurable Purity Heaven, Universal Purity Heaven, Cloudless Heaven, Merit-Born Heaven, Vast Fruit Heaven, Non-Perception Heaven, Non-Affliction Heaven, Non-Heat Heaven, Good Appearance Heaven, Good Vision Heaven, and Akaniṣṭha Heaven, having reached those places, the light produced this sound, proclaiming suffering, emptiness, impermanence, and non-self, and spoke two verses:

> "Issuing light to exhort and guide you:
> Take refuge in the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha.
> Shake off the army of the Māra of death,
> Like an elephant freed from its bonds.
> If one enters this Dharma
> And practices with a resolute, unwavering mind,
> One thereby severs the round of rebirth,
> And all sufferings come to an end."

At that time, the light illuminated the entire great trichiliocosm to rescue sentient beings. This light then returned to the Buddha's body, following behind the World-Honored One.

At that time, when the World-Honored One wished to give a prediction concerning past karma, the light entered behind his body. When he wished to give a prediction concerning future karma, the light entered before his face. When he wished to give a prediction of birth in hell, the light entered from beneath the Buddha's feet. When he wished to give a prediction of birth among animals, the light entered from the Buddha's heels. When he wished to give a prediction of birth among hungry ghosts, the light entered from the Buddha's big toes. When he wished to give a prediction of birth among humans, the light entered from below the Buddha's knees. When he wished to give a prediction of a powerful wheel-turning king, the light entered from the Buddha's left palm. When he wished to give a prediction of a universal wheel-turning king, the light entered from the Buddha's right palm. When he wished to give a prediction of birth in heaven, the light entered from the Buddha's navel. When he wished to give a prediction of śrāvaka enlightenment, the light entered from the Buddha's chest. When he wished to give a prediction of pratyekabuddha enlightenment, the light entered from between the Buddha's brows. When he wished to give a prediction of supreme, perfect enlightenment, the light entered from the crown of the Buddha's head.

At that time, the light emitted by the World-Honored One circled his body three times and entered the World-Honored One's crown.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda joined his palms respectfully and said: "World-Honored One! Light of many colors and a hundred thousand adornments has issued from your mouth, illuminating the ten directions everywhere." He spoke in verse:

> "Right and wrong were long ago cast off,
> All afflictions entirely dispelled.
> In all the world the Buddha is supreme;
> The fruit of excellent causes is not in vain.
> White as conch and lotus,
> Vanquishing Māra, the Buddha reveals his light.
> At that time Māra withdrew of his own accord,
> And serene, wondrous wisdom was established.
> Making the śrāvakas seek buddhahood:
> The Sage's voice is serene and steadfast.
> Like the supreme bull-king,
> He removes all the nets of doubt.
> No enemy can bind him;
> He is like water dissolving salt.
> The Perfectly Awakened One reveals his light:
> To whom does the Buddha give this prediction?
> Those who hear find serenity and joy,
> And this assembly of people rejoices."

The Buddha said: "So it is! So it is, Ānanda! Nothing is without cause and effect. Ānanda, the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One speaks truly. Ānanda, seeing this boy make offerings to me in this way, with roots of merit deeply planted, arousing the aspiration to give the Dharma: after three great incalculable kalpas of cultivating the path of enlightenment, perfecting great compassion and the six pāramitās, when his contemplative practice is complete, he shall attain perfect enlightenment and be called Golden Radiance Tathāgata, endowed with the ten powers, the four wisdoms perfectly complete, and the three secrets and uncommon mindfulness of great compassion. When we ourselves aroused the aspiration in past lives, we too practiced this giving of the Dharma."

At that time, King Prasenajit asked: "World-Honored One! What karmic deeds did this boy perform to obtain such wealth and honor?"

The World-Honored One answered: "In past lives this boy broadly cultivated the causes of meritorious deeds. In the present life he has reaped this reward. Moreover, when this boy planted those karmic causes, his aspiration was resolute and unwavering. Who in this present life can avoid such consequences? Great king, the karma one creates and the rewards one receives are like the earth-element, which is inexhaustible; the water-element, which is inexhaustible; the fire-element, which is inexhaustible; the wind-element, which is inexhaustible. In the same way, the aggregates, elements, and six sense-objects, and the rewards obtained from karma, are inexhaustible. This boy's resolute aspiration planted the causes of merit in past lives, and in this life he has received inexhaustible rewards. The karmic rewards of good and evil deeds are inexhaustible; even after a hundred kalpas, when the karma ripens, one must receive its consequences.

"Great king, in a past age, in the kingdom of Vārāṇasī, there was a king named Śrutasena. He had a prince named Auspicious Secret. At that time the father-king broadly committed unwholesome deeds. When the prince saw his father's wrongdoing, his hair stood on end in alarm and he said to the king: 'I wish to go and cultivate the religious life.' The king said: 'You are my only son. How can I let you go and become a renunciant?' Auspicious Secret said: 'I must leave my father and go to practice.' The boy said: 'Gold and silver, elephants and horses, palace women, treasuries: my mind has no attachment to them and takes no pleasure in using them.' He then cultivated the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment and realized the enlightenment of a pratyekabuddha. Countless hundreds of thousands of humans and devas came to make offerings to him. Others who saw this informed the king: 'The prince has attained such merit.'

"When the king heard this, he wished to see his son. He came forth from the palace with his fourfold army following behind. A poor man saw the king seated upon a magnificent elephant's back, clad in fine garments and adornments, with fragrant ointments applied to his body, a parasol and canopy held above him, and the four divisions of his army surrounding him. The poor man had a flash of wisdom: 'This king's hands and feet, belly, head, face, shoulders, and back are no different from mine. Why does he ride upon a great elephant, clad in fine garments, anointed with fragrant balm, with a parasol above and the fourfold army surrounding him? For my part, through many past lives of miserliness I never practiced giving, which is why I suffer like this in my present life. Being so destitute, unable to give, how can I ever be born among such people?' He asked the king: 'Where is Your Majesty going?' King Śrutasena said: 'I have a son named Auspicious Secret who left home to practice and has realized the enlightenment of a pratyekabuddha. If anyone makes even a small offering to him, they will obtain great fruit in the future.' Having answered the question, the king continued on his way. Then the king suddenly spotted a herd of deer; the king, desiring the deer, pursued them. At that time the poor man carefully considered: 'The king is pursuing the deer. Now is my chance to see the pratyekabuddha.' So the poor man gradually made his way forward, entering a mountain valley, where he saw the pratyekabuddha. His form was majestic and his mind was tranquil. Countless hundreds of thousands of sages surrounded him, and scattered māndārava flowers were heaped up to his knees. The poor man cried out loudly, weeping in great sorrow and regret. Then the hundreds of thousands of sages, having completed their offerings, departed. The poor man reflected: 'With what shall I now make offerings to the pratyekabuddha? Not far from here there is an āmra tree.' The poor man then picked the finest āmra fruit, filled a bowl with it, and offered it to the pratyekabuddha.

"At that time, the pratyekabuddha took the bowl and, like a king of geese, rose into the sky freely, manifesting various supernatural transformations. He descended from the sky and returned to his original seat. The poor man then bowed at the pratyekabuddha's feet and said: 'Eat for my sake, and let me receive the merit.' On the second day, the pratyekabuddha, seeing that the man's mind was pure and awakened, accepted the offering in order to rescue the poor man. The poor man then left the mountain valley.

"King Śrutasena saw the pratyekabuddha in the sky from afar. The king had a flash of wisdom and thought: 'There must be some person of great merit and celestial virtue in that place. I must go quickly to see the pratyekabuddha and that person.' The king then entered the mountain valley. Along the way he saw the poor man coming out of the valley. The king asked: 'Where have you come from?' The poor man answered: 'From this place.' The king said: 'Poor man! Your body is rough and coarse, your hair disheveled, your clothes filthy and unwashed. How can you possibly escape poverty and become rich like me? Truly, you cannot escape your poverty.'

"The poor man took leave of the king. Suddenly he thought: 'How can I obtain a fine piece of land? And abundant, delicious food and drink of every flavor?' Before his thought was finished, his foot struck a round stone, and he stumbled and fell. At that very spot he found an iron vessel filled with gold and jewels.

"The king reached the mountain and saw the pratyekabuddha. He sat before him. After a moment, he said to his son: 'To seek merit, will you accept my offering of a meal tomorrow?' The pratyekabuddha answered: 'Great king, I have already accepted an invitation.' Śrutasena asked: 'Whose invitation have you accepted?' The pratyekabuddha answered: 'A poor man has invited me to accept his offering.'

"The king dispatched an envoy to tell the poor man: 'I have invited the pratyekabuddha to my meal. You should schedule your offering for another day.' When the envoy arrived and delivered the king's message, the poor man refused. The king then went in person to the poor man and said: 'I shall provide the pratyekabuddha's meal. You may make your offering on another day.' He refused. The king said: 'You must change the day.' The poor man said: 'Why should I change the day? I have my own gold and jewels and shall certainly make my offering.' The king said: 'You were a beggar all along. I am a kṣatriya king of consecrated lineage. How can you claim to have gold?' The poor man said: 'If the king does not believe me, I shall show the king the gold.' They went together to the place where the gold had appeared. There was an iron vessel, and when they poured out the gold and jewels, the heap was like a mountain: so high that a person standing on one side could not see the other. The king reflected: 'This man has such merit!' The poor man said: 'The time for my meal-offering is approaching.'

"On the second day, the poor man swept the ground clean, adorned it beautifully, scattered lotus flowers, plucked tree branches and leaves to make fine parasols, and set out the food as offerings. At that time the pratyekabuddha again rose into the sky and manifested various supernatural transformations.

"Then the poor man bowed at the pratyekabuddha's feet and made this vow: 'As I have scattered lotus flowers upon this ground, may I in every life, world after world, obtain celestial, wondrous lotus flowers. As I have made parasols from tree branches for this offering, may I in every life, world after world, obtain champaka trees that produce champaka flowers of celestial purple-gold color. As I have used earthen vessels to hold food in my offering, by the wholesome root of this merit may I in every life, world after world, always obtain golden plates filled with celestial food, such that even if a hundred thousand people eat from them they are never exhausted, and may I encounter a buddha.'"

The Buddha told King Prasenajit: "You should reflect on this. The poor man of that time is now the brahmin's son, the boy Golden Radiance. Through making offerings to the pratyekabuddha, he obtained these roots of merit: boundless happiness, with every aspiration of his heart fulfilled."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, the bhikṣus all received it wholeheartedly and practiced it with joy.
